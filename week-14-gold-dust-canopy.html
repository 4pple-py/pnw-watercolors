Week 14 — PNW Watercolor Series

Gold Dust Canopy

Mid-April morning, bigleaf maples shutting the sky in one furious week. Cedar pollen drifts gold through shafts of light hitting calypso orchids on the forest floor. Spring is greedy now — closing its fist around every gap. The canopy isn't growing so much as conquering, leaf over leaf, while the understory races to bloom before the light runs out.

Primary Trio

New Gamboge

The pollen itself, suspended and sun-caught; transparent, non-granulating, builds warm glazes that feel airborne

Quinacridone Rose

Calypso orchid throats glowing off the forest floor; transparent and staining, pushes violet when it meets the blue

Cobalt Blue

Sky through closing canopy, broken into fragments; granulating and luminous, settles into paper texture like light through leaves

Mixes & Ratios

Pollen Drift

3 New Gamboge : 1 Buff Titanium : ½ Yellow Ochre

Cedar pollen suspended in still air between trunks, gold dust hanging where no breeze reaches.

New Gamboge
Buff Titanium
Yellow Ochre
Wet-in-wet on damp paper. Let New Gamboge lead — it's transparent enough to glow. Buff Titanium adds body without killing the light. Touch Yellow Ochre in last to ground the mix. Don't over-stir; let the ochre granulate where it lands.

Orchid Floor

2 Quinacridone Rose : 1 Cobalt Blue : ½ Perylene Green

Calypso orchids dark-pink and improbable against brown duff, petals like dropped jewelry.

Quin. Rose
Cobalt Blue
Perylene Green
Mix on the palette, not the paper. Quinacridone Rose stains immediately — commit to your stroke. The Cobalt Blue pushes it toward violet; Perylene Green darkens without muddying, giving you the shadow side of the petal. This mix granulates beautifully at higher dilution.

Canopy Shut

3 Sap Green : 2 New Gamboge : 1 Moonglow

Bigleaf maples racing to close the light out, leaves oversaturated and almost too green to believe.

Sap Green
New Gamboge
Moonglow
Layer, don't puddle. Sap Green and New Gamboge together make the radioactive yellow-green of new maple leaves in full sun. Moonglow tempers the mix — adds atmospheric distance and a faint granulation that suggests depth in the canopy. Apply in overlapping washes to build density.

Why These Colors?

This palette is engineered for transparency and glow. New Gamboge and Quinacridone Rose are both fully transparent and staining — they make light feel like it originates from the paper itself, which is exactly what pollen-shaft light does. Cobalt Blue's granulation breaks up washes the way canopy gaps break up sky. Perylene Green and Moonglow handle the darks and atmospherics without deadening the luminosity that the primary trio establishes. Aussie Red Gold and Yellow Ochre are the earth anchors — warm, opaque enough to feel solid, grounding a palette that could otherwise float away in gold dust.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Pollen Shaft Through the Canopy

Subject: A single shaft of morning light cutting diagonally through a closed maple canopy, gold pollen suspended in the beam, the forest floor dark on either side with one or two calypso orchids glowing where the light hits.

The Pollen Drift mix (3 New Gamboge : 1 Buff Titanium : 1/2 Yellow Ochre) is the light beam itself — transparent gold with just enough body to feel airborne. Perylene Green and Moonglow build the dark forest flanking the shaft. The Orchid Floor mix places the calypso orchids exactly where the light lands.

  • Paint the dark forest first: Perylene Green for conifer trunks, Moonglow for the atmospheric murk between them — leave the light shaft as untouched white paper
  • Once the darks are fully dry, glaze the Pollen Drift mix into the shaft area on damp paper — New Gamboge’s transparency lets the paper glow from within
  • Touch Buff Titanium into the shaft while damp for the diffused scatter where pollen is thickest
  • The calypso orchids at the base are small, precise: Orchid Floor mix (Quin Rose + Cobalt Blue + Perylene Green) on dry paper, each bloom a committed stroke
Prompt 2

Bigleaf Maple Canopy From Below

Subject: A worm’s-eye view looking straight up through unfurling bigleaf maple leaves — oversaturated green layered three and four deep, with fragments of Cobalt Blue sky visible through the gaps, and gold pollen drifting across the frame.

The Canopy Shut mix (3 Sap Green : 2 New Gamboge : 1 Moonglow) gives you that radioactive yellow-green of new maple leaves in full sun. Cobalt Blue granulates into the sky fragments, breaking up naturally the way light through leaves actually behaves. New Gamboge pollen drifts across everything.

  • Build the canopy in overlapping washes — first layer Sap Green alone, second layer the full Canopy Shut mix, each pass adding density
  • Reserve irregular shapes of bare paper for the sky gaps, then fill them with Cobalt Blue — its granulation mimics the way sky shimmers through moving leaves
  • The leaves closest to the viewer (lowest in the canopy) should be darkest: add Moonglow to the mix for these
  • Pollen is a final glaze of very dilute New Gamboge drifted across the whole painting after everything else has dried
Prompt 3

Calypso Orchids on the Forest Floor

Subject: A cluster of three to five calypso orchids growing from brown duff and moss, dappled gold light hitting them from above, a dark cedar trunk rising behind.

Quinacridone Rose is transparent and staining — one committed stroke and the orchid glows like dropped jewelry. Cobalt Blue pushes it toward the violet these orchids actually are. Yellow Ochre and Aussie Red Gold build the forest-floor duff. Perylene Green gives you the cedar trunk shadow that makes the pink pop.

  • Paint the forest floor first: Yellow Ochre for the duff, Cascade Green for the moss patches, let them dry
  • Each orchid is the Orchid Floor mix applied in a single decisive stroke — Quinacridone Rose stains immediately, so no second chances
  • The orchid’s distinctive pouch is darker: add more Perylene Green to the shadow side of each bloom while the first stroke is still damp
  • The cedar trunk behind is Aussie Red Gold for the bark catching side-light, with Perylene Green in the deep shadow — nearly black but still chromatic
Prompt 4

Gold Dust Trail

Subject: A forest trail in mid-April — boot-kicked dust hanging gold in the air, bigleaf maple canopy closing overhead, a warbler (Wilson’s or yellow-rumped) perched on a trailside branch at eye level.

Yellow Ochre is the settled trail dust, New Gamboge is the kicked-up gold cloud. The Canopy Shut mix arches overhead. Aussie Red Gold warms the cedar bark on trailside trees. The warbler is a tiny burst of New Gamboge and Cobalt Blue — a bird-shaped exclamation point.

  • Lay in the trail with Yellow Ochre on dry paper — it’s opaque enough to feel solid underfoot
  • The dust cloud is New Gamboge wet-into-wet on damp paper above the trail surface — transparent, weightless, catching light
  • Build the canopy overhead with the Canopy Shut mix in overlapping washes, leaving a few Cobalt Blue sky fragments
  • The warbler is three strokes: New Gamboge body, Cobalt Blue cap (if Wilson’s) or Cobalt Blue with Perylene Green (if yellow-rumped), and a Perylene Green eye dot
Prompt 5

Cedar Trunk and Moss in Side-Light

Subject: A close-up study of a western red cedar trunk — bark lit warm by side-light, moss on the shaded side, pollen dust settled into the bark furrows, canopy leaves framing the top of the composition.

Aussie Red Gold is the cedar bark in warm light — a pigment warm enough to taste. Cascade Green handles the moss on the shaded side, quiet green that never sees direct sun. The Pollen Drift mix settles into bark furrows. Perylene Green deepens the shadow side without going dead.

  • Block in the trunk with Aussie Red Gold for the lit side and Perylene Green for the shadow side — one continuous vertical stroke with a flat brush, warm to cool
  • Bark furrows are dry-brushed Perylene Green over the dried Aussie Red Gold — use the texture of rough watercolor paper to catch the ridges
  • Moss on the shaded side is Cascade Green applied wet-into-wet into the still-damp Perylene Green shadow
  • Dust the Pollen Drift mix (very dilute) across the lit bark surface as a final glaze — the gold settles into furrows naturally
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Moonglow is a convenience mix (Ultramarine Blue, Phthalo Blue, Pyrrol Crimson) that granulates unpredictably — test it before committing to large washes. Buff Titanium is semi-opaque; use it for body and diffusion, not glazing. Perylene Green reads almost black in mass tone but lifts to a dark forest green — useful for shadows that stay chromatic.
