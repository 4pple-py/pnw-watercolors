Week 14 — PNW Watercolor Series

Gold Dust Canopy

Your car is yellow. The puddles have yellow film. The air itself is gold-green. Bigleaf maples, alders, and Doug firs are all releasing pollen at once and the world looks like it’s been shot through a gold filter. Four yellows in this palette and none of them redundant — each one doing a different job in the haze.

Primary Trio

Hansa Yellow Medium

Pure pollen gold — PY97 is transparent, non-granulating, and builds clean glazes without muddying. The bright center of the week’s palette.

Green Gold

The gold-green filter everything’s seen through — PY129 straddles yellow and green without committing to either. Canopy light incarnate.

Quinacridone Gold

Rich amber light pooling in bark furrows and puddle edges — PO49 is transparent and staining, warmer and deeper than any yellow here. The anchor.

Mixes & Ratios

Pollen Cloud

3 Hansa Yellow Medium : 1 Nickel Azo Yellow : ½ Buff Titanium

The airborne gold itself — suspended between trees, too thick to be light and too light to be substance.

Hansa Yellow Med.
Nickel Azo Yellow
Buff Titanium
Wet-in-wet on damp paper. Hansa Yellow Medium leads with pure transparent gold while Nickel Azo Yellow’s green bias pushes the mix toward that sickly-beautiful pollen shimmer. Buff Titanium adds just enough body to suggest particulate matter without killing the glow. Don’t over-mix — let the two yellows separate on the paper. The shift between warm gold and acid green-gold is the whole point.

Canopy Filter

2 Green Gold : 2 Sap Green : 1 Quinacridone Gold

Sunlight strained through three layers of new leaves, gold turned green turned gold again on the way down.

Green Gold
Sap Green
Quin. Gold
Layer this, don’t puddle it. Green Gold and Sap Green together make the translucent yellow-green of backlit bigleaf maple leaves. Quinacridone Gold warms the mix from underneath — apply it to the first wash while still damp and watch it bloom into amber veins. Each layer adds canopy depth. Three washes and you’ve got the full filtered-light effect: bright gold-green at the edges thinning to deep amber-green at the center.

Pollen Water

2 New Gamboge : 1 Raw Sienna : 1 Hooker’s Green : touch French Ultramarine

Standing puddle with that distinctive yellow-green film, part mirror, part pollen trap, part sky.

New Gamboge
Raw Sienna
Hooker’s Green
Fr. Ultra.
This one works best as a controlled wet-in-wet. Lay New Gamboge first for the pollen film, then drop Raw Sienna at the puddle edges where it meets the ground. Hooker’s Green drifts in from one side — the reflection of canopy above. The French Ultramarine touch is sky reflected through a gap: just a fleck, applied last, and it granulates against the warm surround in a way that reads as distance. Let everything mingle but don’t stir.

Why These Colors?

Four yellows, and every one earns its place. Hansa Yellow Medium is pure pollen gold — transparent, clean, the brightest thing in the palette. Green Gold shifts it toward the canopy filter that tints everything this week, yellow that’s already halfway to green. Quinacridone Gold goes the other direction, into amber and warmth, the deep pooling light in bark crevices and puddle edges. Nickel Azo Yellow brings PY150’s green bias — that acid shimmer when pollen catches backlight. Together they cover the full spectrum of gold without a single redundancy. Sap Green and Hooker’s Green give the canopy its structure and shadow. New Gamboge bridges the yellows and greens with its warm transparent body. Buff Titanium scatters the sky into a hazy bright wash. Raw Sienna grounds everything in warm earth. And French Ultramarine — used sparingly — is the rare glimpse of actual sky through the canopy, granulating and distant, a reminder that blue still exists somewhere above all this gold.

Painting Prompts

Early April, everything dusted. These four subjects lean into the gold-green haze and the palette’s four-yellow architecture — each prompt uses a different gold as its anchor.

Prompt 1

Pollen-Dusted Windshield

Subject: A car windshield coated in yellow-green pollen — wiper arcs visible as darker crescents, the world beyond blurred to abstract color, a side mirror catching a gold reflection. The dashboard is a warm dark rectangle below.

Hansa Yellow Medium and Nickel Azo Yellow own this prompt. The Pollen Cloud mix is the windshield coating itself — thick, airborne, almost opaque. Buff Titanium diffuses the background into bright nothing. The wiper arcs are negative space where you see through to Green Gold and Sap Green beyond.

  • Lay the full windshield area with the Pollen Cloud mix (3 Hansa Yellow Med. : 1 Nickel Azo Yellow : ½ Buff Titanium) on damp paper — thick enough to feel like a coating, not a glaze
  • While still damp, lift two crescent arcs with a damp brush for the wiper paths — these cleared areas get a wash of Green Gold and Sap Green to suggest the blurred world beyond
  • The side mirror is a small rectangle of New Gamboge reflection with a Quinacridone Gold edge — warm and metallic
  • Dashboard below is Hooker’s Green darkened with French Ultramarine — a cool dark anchor that makes all that yellow sing by contrast
Prompt 2

Canopy From Below

Subject: Looking straight up through bigleaf maple canopy — new leaves backlit gold-green, three or four layers deep, with fragments of bright sky between them. Pollen drifts visibly through shafts of light. A Douglas fir branch intrudes from one side, dark and sharp against the soft maple.

The Canopy Filter mix (2 Green Gold : 2 Sap Green : 1 Quinacridone Gold) is the entire upper structure. French Ultramarine fills the sky gaps. Hooker’s Green gives the fir branch its darkness. Hansa Yellow Medium drifts across everything as pollen.

  • Reserve irregular shapes of bare paper for the sky gaps first, then build the canopy around them with overlapping washes of the Canopy Filter mix — each layer adds depth
  • Fill the reserved sky gaps with French Ultramarine on dry paper — its granulation reads as atmospheric distance and makes the gold-green canopy feel close by contrast
  • The Douglas fir branch is Hooker’s Green applied on dry paper with a rigger brush — sharp, angular, a geometric intrusion into all those soft maple curves
  • Final pass: very dilute Hansa Yellow Medium glazed across the whole painting for the pollen-in-air effect — it unifies everything under the gold filter
Prompt 3

Pollen on Standing Water

Subject: A trail puddle with that distinctive yellow-green pollen film on its surface — iridescent, slightly broken where a leaf fell through. Reflected canopy and one crack of sky visible beneath the film. Gravel and duff at the puddle edge.

The Pollen Water mix is purpose-built for this. New Gamboge gives the yellow film its warmth while Hooker’s Green and Raw Sienna handle the reflected canopy and muddy edges. French Ultramarine is the sky glimpse. Quinacridone Gold concentrates at the puddle rim where pollen is thickest.

  • Start with the puddle as a single wet shape — lay New Gamboge across the top for the pollen film, then drop Hooker’s Green and Raw Sienna from opposite sides for the reflected canopy and ground
  • While still wet, touch French Ultramarine into one small area for the sky reflection — it granulates against the warm surround and reads as depth beneath the film
  • The break in the pollen film where the leaf fell through is a lifted area revealing the dark water beneath: Hooker’s Green with a touch of French Ultramarine
  • Puddle edges get concentrated Quinacridone Gold and Raw Sienna on dry paper — the pollen collects thickest where water meets shore
Prompt 4

Forest Path in Gold Light

Subject: A forest trail in early April, shafts of gold light angling through the canopy, the air visibly thick with pollen. Alder trunks pale on either side. The path itself is warm and dusty, disappearing into a gold-green haze where the light is thickest.

All four golds work together here. Hansa Yellow Medium is the bright shaft centers. Green Gold is the tinted air. Quinacridone Gold pools in the warm foreground path. Nickel Azo Yellow catches the backlit pollen at the vanishing point. Raw Sienna grounds the trail. Buff Titanium hazes the distance.

  • Paint the dark canopy flanks first — Sap Green and Hooker’s Green for the trees on either side, leaving the central path and light shafts as white paper
  • Once dry, glaze the path with Raw Sienna at your feet transitioning to Quinacridone Gold in the middle distance — warm earth becoming warm light
  • The gold-green haze at the vanishing point is Buff Titanium and Green Gold wet-in-wet, with Nickel Azo Yellow dropped in for that acid-yellow backlit pollen shimmer
  • Light shafts are Hansa Yellow Medium on damp paper, angled diagonally — transparent enough to glow without obscuring the canopy structure behind them
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Four yellows, four different golds: Hansa Yellow Medium (PY97) is pure and transparent, Green Gold (PY129) has inherent green bias, Quinacridone Gold (PO49) goes deep amber, and Nickel Azo Yellow (PY150) brings an acid green-gold shimmer. None are redundant — swap any one out and you lose a dimension of the pollen haze. Buff Titanium is semi-opaque; use it for diffusion and body, not glazing. French Ultramarine granulates beautifully against all four yellows, which is why it earns its spot as the sole cool pigment here.
