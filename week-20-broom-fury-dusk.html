Week 20 — PNW Watercolor Series

Broom Fury Dusk

May 14 through 20. Scotch broom owns every roadside, every clear-cut, every neglected lot. You’re supposed to hate it — invasive, aggressive, choking out native plants — but at dusk, backlit by sunset fire, it’s undeniably gorgeous. Hansa Yellow Deep fury from I-5 to the coast. The beauty of destruction, and nobody can look away.

Primary Trio

Hansa Yellow Deep

Scotch broom at full scream; transparent, warm, slightly orange-leaning yellow that owns every disturbed slope in the PNW right now

Cadmium Orange Hue

Sunset fire behind the broom banks; opaque, punchy, blocks of burning sky that the yellow silhouettes against

Transparent Pyrrol Orange

Sunset glow through broom petals; PO73 transparency means light passes through it the way it passes through actual backlit flowers

Mixes & Ratios

Broom Blaze

3 Hansa Yellow Deep : 1 Transparent Pyrrol Orange : ½ Sap Green

The yellow that took over the highway — so saturated it reads as aggression, so beautiful you slow down anyway.

Hansa Yellow Deep
Pyrrol Orange
Sap Green
Hansa Yellow Deep at full concentration is the point — broom doesn’t do subtle. Transparent Pyrrol Orange warms the shadow side of flower clusters and gives the backlit glow that makes broom look radioactive at dusk. The half-part Sap Green is the stem structure visible in dense stands; drop it in while the yellow is still wet and let it bleed upward. If this mix looks like too much, you’re reading the roadside correctly.

Highway Sunset

2 Cadmium Orange Hue : 1 Alizarin Crimson : ½ Transparent Pyrrol Orange

The sky behind the broom banks — opaque fire grading into deep red, the kind of sunset that makes you pull over on I-5.

Cadmium Orange Hue
Alizarin Crimson
Pyrrol Orange
Cadmium Orange Hue is opaque — it blocks light, builds solid color, gives the sunset its weight. Alizarin Crimson deepens the lower horizon where the sun has already dropped. Transparent Pyrrol Orange bridges the two: apply it between the opaque orange and the crimson while both are damp, and it glows where they meet. The transparency difference between the Cadmium Hue and the Pyrrol Orange is doing real optical work here — one blocks, one transmits. Different jobs, same sky.

Dusk Eats the Road

2 Indanthrone Blue : 1 Burnt Sienna : ½ Lamp Black

The eastern sky and ground merging as night arrives — blue-brown darkness swallowing the clear-cut from behind.

Indanthrone Blue
Burnt Sienna
Lamp Black
Indanthrone Blue is the night that’s coming — not black, not purple, just deep transparent blue with enough red to feel inevitable. Burnt Sienna grounds it literally: this is the dirt road, the exposed clear-cut soil, the shoulder gravel going dark. The half-part Lamp Black drops the value without shifting the hue. Use this mix for everything the sunset isn’t touching. It makes the yellows and oranges scream louder by contrast.

Why These Colors?

This palette splits into three temperature zones: the hot yellows and oranges of broom and sunset (Hansa Yellow Deep, Cadmium Orange Hue, Transparent Pyrrol Orange, Alizarin Crimson), the dry earths of the roadside (Sap Green, Olive Green, Burnt Sienna), and the approaching night (Indanthrone Blue, French Ultramarine, Lamp Black). The two oranges are doing fundamentally different optical work — Cadmium Orange Hue is opaque, building solid sunset mass, while Transparent Pyrrol Orange (PO73) lets light through, which is exactly what happens when you’re looking at backlit broom petals. Olive Green keeps the roadsides dry and unglamorous, because this isn’t lush forest — it’s gravel shoulders and neglected lots. Indanthrone Blue and French Ultramarine handle the sky’s transition from fire to night: Indanthrone at the horizon where warmth still contaminates the blue, French Ultramarine overhead where the sky has already gone cold. Lamp Black is for the silhouettes — power lines, fence posts, tree forms — that give the sunset something to burn against.

Painting Prompts

Four scenes built for this palette — each one leans hard into the yellow-orange fury and the dusk that frames it. The broom is the subject whether you want it to be or not.

Prompt 1

Broom-Choked Roadside at Sunset

Subject: A two-lane PNW highway shoulder completely overtaken by scotch broom, golden banks rising on both sides, the sky behind them going orange-red as the sun drops.

The Broom Blaze mix (Hansa Yellow Deep + Transparent Pyrrol Orange + Sap Green) at full saturation is this exact scene. Highway Sunset behind it, Dusk Eats the Road for the asphalt and eastern sky.

  • Paint the sky first: Highway Sunset mix applied wet-in-wet from the horizon up, grading into French Ultramarine at the zenith
  • The broom banks are Broom Blaze at full concentration — don’t dilute, don’t apologize. Let Transparent Pyrrol Orange glow at the backlit edges
  • The road surface is Dusk Eats the Road at low concentration — a neutral stage for the yellow spectacle on either side
  • Lamp Black fence posts or power poles give the composition vertical structure and something for the sunset to silhouette against
Prompt 2

Single Broom Branch, Backlit

Subject: One branch of scotch broom held up against the sunset — individual pea-shaped flowers glowing with transmitted light, stem and leaves dark against the fire.

This is where the transparency of Transparent Pyrrol Orange earns its place. Hansa Yellow Deep for the flower faces, Pyrrol Orange for the backlit edges, Sap Green and Lamp Black for the stem going dark.

  • Paint the background sky first: Cadmium Orange Hue fading to Alizarin Crimson, let it dry completely
  • The flowers facing you are Hansa Yellow Deep; the edges catching backlight are Transparent Pyrrol Orange applied while the yellow is damp
  • The stem is Sap Green darkened with Lamp Black — it’s a silhouette, so keep it simple and crisp against the glow
  • Leave slivers of the orange sky showing between flowers — the negative space is what makes the backlit effect read
Prompt 3

I-5 Median Strip

Subject: The concrete median of Interstate 5 with scotch broom erupting from every crack and gap, headlights approaching from the north, sunset fading in the west.

Olive Green and Burnt Sienna for the gritty median infrastructure. Broom Blaze for the yellow explosion. Indanthrone Blue and Lamp Black for the approaching headlight darkness.

  • The median barrier is Burnt Sienna mixed with Olive Green — concrete that’s been weathering since the ’90s, stained and cracked
  • Broom erupts upward from the barrier in pure Hansa Yellow Deep, with Transparent Pyrrol Orange where the last sun catches it
  • The highway surface on both sides is Indanthrone Blue at low concentration — wet asphalt reflecting the dying sky
  • Approaching headlights are just preserved white paper with a faint Hansa Yellow Deep halo — let the darkness of Lamp Black around them do the work
Prompt 4

Clear-Cut Hillside Gone Yellow

Subject: An entire clear-cut hillside blanketed in scotch broom from stump line to ridge — the kind of scene that makes ecologists furious and painters reach for yellow.

This is a painting about one color dominating everything. Hansa Yellow Deep, Transparent Pyrrol Orange, and Sap Green give you three registers of invasive gold. The Dusk Eats the Road mix handles the tree line and stumps.

  • Cover the hillside with Hansa Yellow Deep at varying concentrations — heavier at the crest where broom is densest, slightly diluted in the draws
  • While wet, drop Transparent Pyrrol Orange into the sun-facing slopes and Sap Green into the shadow draws where stems show through
  • Stumps and slash are Burnt Sienna and Lamp Black — the evidence of what was cleared to let the broom in
  • The sky should be restrained — French Ultramarine grading to Indanthrone Blue — so nothing competes with the yellow. Let the broom be overwhelming. That’s the ecological truth of it
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Transparent Pyrrol Orange (PO73) is genuinely transparent — it glows when backlit, which is the entire reason it’s here alongside the opaque Cadmium Orange Hue. Different optical jobs. Olive Green tends toward brown at high concentration; keep it dilute for accurate dry-roadside reads. French Ultramarine granulates beautifully in sky washes — let the particles settle. Indanthrone Blue is the most versatile dark in this palette; mixed with Burnt Sienna it replaces black for most shadow work while keeping color alive.
