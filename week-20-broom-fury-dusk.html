Week 20 — PNW Watercolor Series

Broom Fury Dusk

Scotch broom has conquered every roadside — invasive, golden, unapologetic. Elderberry holds its cream plates up like offerings. Dusk past 9 PM stretches the sky into lavender. Spring tipping into summer's arrogance, and the weeds got there first. The longest evenings of the year belong to things that weren't invited.

Primary Trio

Hansa Yellow Deep

Scotch broom bank, aggressive yellow colonizing every cleared slope; transparent, warm, slightly orange-leaning

Cobalt Violet

Lupine spikes in road cuts, the native purple fighting back; granulating, semi-transparent, genuinely violet

Phthalo Blue GS

Deep dusk east-facing sky, the blue that arrives when the sun's attention is elsewhere; transparent, staining

Mixes & Ratios

Broom Blaze

3 Hansa Yellow Deep : 1 Green Gold : ½ Transparent Pyrrol Orange

Invasive yellow so aggressive it hurts your eyes — the color equivalent of a species that grows three feet a season.

Hansa Yellow Deep
Green Gold
Pyrrol Orange
Don't soften this. Hansa Yellow Deep at full concentration is the point — broom doesn't do subtle. Green Gold adds the stem-green undertone visible in dense stands. Transparent Pyrrol Orange warms the shadow side of flower clusters. Apply boldly, let it be garish. If the color looks like too much, it's accurate. This invasive earned its reputation.

Lupine Dusk

2 Cobalt Violet : 1 Phthalo Blue GS : ½ Lavender

Granulating purple spikes against dimming sky — native lupine holding ground in the road cut.

Cobalt Violet
Phthalo Blue GS
Lavender
Cobalt Violet granulates dramatically — heavy pigment particles settle into paper texture, mimicking the way individual lupine flowers cluster on the spike. Phthalo Blue GS deepens the shadow side without neutralizing the violet. Lavender ties the spikes to the sky behind them. Work wet-on-dry for defined flower spikes; the granulation provides detail at the micro level automatically.

Elderberry Cream

3 Buff Titanium : ½ Hansa Yellow Deep : ¼ Cascade Green

Flat-topped flower clusters catching the very last light of a 9 PM dusk — cream going gold.

Buff Titanium
Hansa Yellow
Cascade Green
Buff Titanium dominates because elderberry flowers are fundamentally white-cream with body. They're not translucent — they're opaque, flat plates that catch light. The half-part Hansa Yellow Deep is the last sun warming them. Cascade Green in the shadow beneath each plate gives them dimension. Apply with a flat brush; these flowers are architectural, not wispy.

Why These Colors?

This palette is split between the invasive (Hansa Yellow Deep, Green Gold, Transparent Pyrrol Orange) and the native (Cobalt Violet, Phthalo Blue GS, Cascade Green), with the atmospheric colors (Lavender, Buff Titanium, Indigo) mediating between them. Cobalt Violet is irreplaceable here — no mix of red and blue granulates the same way, and lupine demands granulation. Phthalo Blue GS handles the deep dusk sky because it's the only blue transparent enough to stay luminous at high concentration. Indigo at the tree line is practically black but still reads as night-blue, not as death.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Broom-Choked Roadside at Dusk

Subject: A PNW highway shoulder completely overtaken by scotch broom, golden banks rising on both sides of the road, the sky going lavender at 9 PM.

The Broom Blaze mix (Hansa Yellow Deep + Green Gold + Transparent Pyrrol Orange) at full saturation is this exact scene. Lavender overhead and Indigo at the tree line give you the late-spring dusk that frames it.

  • Paint the sky first: Lavender wash at the top grading into Phthalo Blue GS at the zenith, applied wet-in-wet and left alone
  • The broom banks are Broom Blaze mix at full concentration — don’t dilute, don’t apologize. Let it be garish because it genuinely is
  • Green Gold stems are visible in the dense stands; pull thin vertical strokes through the yellow while it’s still damp
  • The road is Indigo at low concentration with a touch of Olive Green for asphalt — a neutral stage for the yellow spectacle on either side
Prompt 2

Lupine Versus Broom (Native Holds Ground)

Subject: A road cut where native lupine spikes compete with invasive broom — purple and gold side by side, the ecological drama painted in complementary colors.

Cobalt Violet and Hansa Yellow Deep are near-complements. Set them next to each other and they vibrate the way the real plants do when they share a disturbed slope.

  • Paint the broom first (Broom Blaze mix) on the left or upper portion of the slope, then let it dry completely
  • The lupine spikes are the Lupine Dusk mix (Cobalt Violet + Phthalo Blue GS + Lavender) applied wet-on-dry for crisp vertical forms
  • Let Cobalt Violet granulate — the heavy pigment particles settling into paper texture mimic the way individual flowers cluster on a lupine spike
  • Olive Green at the base is the smothered native grass, dilute and defeated, tying the composition to the ground
Prompt 3

Elderberry Catching the Last Light

Subject: A red elderberry in full cream-colored bloom, flat-topped flower plates catching horizontal 9 PM light, dark Cascade Green leaves below.

The Elderberry Cream mix (Buff Titanium + Hansa Yellow Deep + Cascade Green) handles the entire flower head. The architectural, flat-topped shape of elderberry is perfect for a flat brush.

  • Paint the leaf mass first with Cascade Green on dry paper — elderberry leaves are compound and elegant, worth a few careful strokes
  • The flower plates are Elderberry Cream applied with a flat brush in single horizontal strokes — these flowers are architectural, not rounded
  • Add a wash of Transparent Pyrrol Orange to the light-facing edges of the flower plates for the last sun warming them gold
  • The background is the Lavender dusk sky, applied before the elderberry and dried, so the cream plates pop forward against violet
Prompt 4

9:30 PM Tree Line Silhouette

Subject: The moment the tree line becomes a silhouette against the last glow of a late-May dusk — Indigo shapes against Lavender sky, with broom still faintly yellow in the foreground.

Indigo is the last readable silhouette. Lavender and Phthalo Blue GS handle the sky’s gradient from warm horizon to deep zenith. The broom in foreground is Hansa Yellow Deep going dark.

  • The sky is a graded wash: Lavender at the horizon, blending up into Phthalo Blue GS at the top. Apply to wet paper in one pass
  • The tree line is Indigo applied with a flat brush in a single jagged horizontal stroke after the sky is dry — firs and hemlocks as a serrated edge
  • Foreground broom is Hansa Yellow Deep at reduced saturation — the flowers are losing their color in the dimming light, so don’t paint them at full noon intensity
  • A few Cobalt Violet lupine spikes in the foreground catch the last light and add purple notes against the darkening yellow
Prompt 5

The Invasion in Full (Panoramic Broom Hillside)

Subject: An entire hillside blanketed in scotch broom from bottom to top — the kind of scene that makes ecologists wince and painters reach for yellow.

This is a painting about one color dominating everything. Hansa Yellow Deep, Green Gold, and Transparent Pyrrol Orange give you three registers of invasive gold. The composition is a study in yellow.

  • Cover the entire hillside area with Hansa Yellow Deep at various concentrations — heavier at the top, slightly diluted in the distance
  • While wet, drop Green Gold into the shadow areas where dense stems show through the flowers
  • Transparent Pyrrol Orange warms the sun-facing clusters and creates the only value variation in the yellow mass
  • The sky should be restrained — Lavender or dilute Phthalo Blue GS — so nothing competes with the broom. Let the yellow be overwhelming. That’s the ecological truth of it.
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Cobalt Violet is expensive and granulates heavily — embrace the texture, it's the pigment's gift. Phthalo Blue GS will dominate any mix it enters; load conservatively. Lavender is a Daniel Smith convenience color that's difficult to replicate by mixing; its pastel violet with slight granulation is unique. Olive Green tends toward brown at high concentration — keep it dilute for accurate dry-grass reads.
