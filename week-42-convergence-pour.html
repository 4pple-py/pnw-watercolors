Week 42 — PNW Watercolor Series

Convergence Pour

Weather fronts stack up over the coast and slam into each other somewhere above the Chehalis Valley. The sky turns the color of wet slate. Rain doesn’t fall — it arrives, in curtains, in walls, in sheets that erase the middle distance entirely. You can watch it coming across the fields: a dark column moving at highway speed, swallowing fence posts and barns and then you. This is the convergence zone doing what it does best — turning October into a verb.

Primary Trio

Payne’s Gray

The driving rain itself — blue-gray, cool, and endlessly versatile; the workhorse pigment that builds every value from drizzle to deluge

Indanthrone Blue

Deep storm sky at its most dramatic; a brooding, granulating blue-black that separates PNW storms from ordinary overcast

Neutral Tint

The rain curtain between you and the horizon — neither warm nor cool, pure atmospheric obstruction, the color of visibility itself disappearing

Mixes & Ratios

Wall of Water

2 Payne’s Gray : 1 Neutral Tint : ½ Indanthrone Blue

The visible edge of a rain curtain crossing a valley — that moment when you can see the downpour as a solid thing, a wall with an inside and an outside.

Payne's Gray
Neutral Tint
Indanthrone Blue
Payne’s Gray is the structural backbone — it has just enough blue to read as rain rather than smoke, and it dilutes into a huge range of values from near-black downpour to pale drizzle haze. Neutral Tint flattens the mix toward true gray, removing the personality that would make the rain look like sky instead of water. Indanthrone Blue pushes the darkest passages toward that deep blue-black you see when a storm cell is directly overhead. Apply on very wet paper with a large mop brush, tilting the board to let the pigment flow downward in streaks. The gravity does your brushwork for you — rain paints itself if you let it.

Flood Mud

2 Raw Umber : 1 Burnt Sienna : ¼ Payne’s Gray

October field after the third straight day of rain — the ground stops absorbing and starts reflecting, and the mud becomes a mirror with a memory of dirt.

Raw Umber
Burnt Sienna
Payne's Gray
Raw Umber is the color of PNW field dirt when it’s fully saturated — not the romantic brown of dry soil, but the flat, heavy, greenish-brown of ground that can’t take any more water. Burnt Sienna warms specific zones where clay subsoil surfaces through the topsoil, giving the flooded field variation instead of monotony. Payne’s Gray cools the mix just enough that the mud reads as wet rather than merely brown — without it, you’d paint a dry road instead of a flood. Apply in puddles on the paper, letting the pigments mingle unevenly. Drop clear water into drying areas to create backruns that mimic standing water on uneven ground.

Storm Core

2 Indanthrone Blue : 1 Lamp Black : ½ Shadow Violet

The center of the convergence zone, directly overhead — so dark it makes noon look like dusk, so heavy the air pressure changes in your ears.

Indanthrone Blue
Lamp Black
Shadow Violet
Indanthrone Blue has a granulating quality that Lamp Black lacks — the blue particles separate on textured paper, creating the churning, uneven darkness of a real storm cell rather than a flat painted-on black. Lamp Black provides the absolute depth: the value floor that tells the viewer this is the darkest thing in the painting and everything else is relative to it. Shadow Violet is the key to the whole mix — heavy rain in the PNW has a purple cast that photographs capture but painters often miss. It’s the moisture in the air refracting what little light remains. Use this mix sparingly and only at the storm’s zenith. Apply to damp paper and let the Indanthrone Blue granulate naturally.

Why These Colors?

A convergence zone palette needs to do one thing supremely well: paint rain as a visible, physical presence in the landscape. That’s why three primaries are all gray-family pigments. Payne’s Gray anchors every value from pale mist to dark deluge. Neutral Tint strips away color bias to paint the rain curtain itself — that band of reduced visibility that has no hue, only density. Indanthrone Blue pushes the upper sky to storm-dark without resorting to black alone, granulating as it dries to create the turbulent texture of stacked cumulonimbus. French Ultramarine handles the rare moments when a crack in the cloud deck reveals electric blue — the color you see for two seconds before the next cell closes in. Cobalt Blue gives you the rain-wet reflective surfaces: roads, puddles, car hoods, anything that turns into a mirror when the water hits. Raw Umber and Burnt Sienna are the flooded earth — the two-tone mud of valley farmland in October. Lamp Black is reserved for the storm’s absolute center, the value anchor that makes everything else look luminous by comparison. Buff Titanium, semi-opaque and warm, paints the rain streaks catching whatever diffused light survives — those pale diagonal lines that tell you rain is falling rather than just present. Shadow Violet provides the purple-gray atmospheric quality unique to heavy PNW rain — the color that makes October storms look different from storms anywhere else.

Painting Prompts

Rain this heavy demands commitment. Soak your paper, tilt your board, and let gravity be your collaborator — these subjects reward the painter who gives up control and lets the water work.

Prompt 1

Rain Wall Crossing the Skagit Flats

Subject: A visible wall of rain sweeping across flat farmland, erasing barns and treelines as it advances. The near ground is still dry enough to see. The far ground has disappeared. A single road cuts toward the vanishing point.

The Wall of Water mix (Payne’s Gray + Neutral Tint + Indanthrone Blue) does the heavy lifting here, graded from full strength at the storm front to dilute mist at the edges. Buff Titanium streaks mark where rain catches light.

  • Wet the entire upper two-thirds of the paper and apply the Wall of Water mix with a loaded mop brush, letting it flow downward — tilt the board at 20 degrees so gravity creates vertical rain streaks naturally
  • The rain wall’s leading edge is the darkest value: load Indanthrone Blue into the Wall of Water mix here, letting the granulation create turbulent texture at the boundary between rain and clear air
  • While the rain wash is still damp, lift thin vertical lines with a thirsty flat brush to create Buff Titanium rain streaks — these are the lit streaks where diffused light catches falling water
  • The dry foreground is Raw Umber and Burnt Sienna in the Flood Mud mix, but drier and warmer than the rest — this temperature contrast is what sells the approaching storm
Prompt 2

Highway 101 in the Downpour

Subject: A two-lane highway through coastal forest, rain so heavy the road surface becomes a sheet of reflected sky. Headlights approaching through the murk. Douglas firs as dark vertical shapes flanking the road. Spray rising from tires.

Cobalt Blue for the wet road reflections, the Storm Core mix (Indanthrone Blue + Lamp Black + Shadow Violet) for the forest wall on either side. Buff Titanium for headlight glow diffused through rain.

  • The road surface is Cobalt Blue diluted and laid flat — wet roads in rain reflect sky color, which means the road is often lighter than the forest beside it, a counterintuitive value relationship that makes the scene read as true
  • Flanking trees are the Storm Core mix applied as dark vertical masses with no detail — in this rain, you cannot see individual branches, only shapes. Let the Indanthrone Blue granulate to suggest bark texture at the nearest trunks
  • Headlights are negative painting: leave two small areas of dry paper and surround them with Buff Titanium in a soft halo, then grade into Payne’s Gray as the glow fades into rain
  • Tire spray is spattered Neutral Tint and Buff Titanium flicked from a loaded brush — the spatter mimics the randomness of road spray better than any deliberate brushwork
Prompt 3

Flooded Field with Fence Posts

Subject: A Willamette Valley field flooded to standing water, fence posts marching into the distance with their reflections doubling them. The sky and water nearly merge at the horizon. A lone oak at the field’s far edge.

The Flood Mud mix (Raw Umber + Burnt Sienna + Payne’s Gray) for the earth tones visible through shallow water. Payne’s Gray graded with Neutral Tint for the sky-water merge. Shadow Violet for the reflected distance.

  • Where sky meets standing water at the horizon, use Neutral Tint at the same value in both zones — the lack of contrast between sky and water’s surface is what creates the eerie convergence-zone feeling of being inside the rain rather than under it
  • Fence posts are Lamp Black and Raw Umber, painted with a rigger brush as confident single strokes. Their reflections are the same colors but broken and slightly lighter — drag the brush quickly over textured paper to skip and create the rippled reflection effect
  • The standing water shows the Flood Mud mix underneath in the shallow zones and pure Payne’s Gray reflection in the deeper areas — this depth variation gives the flood three-dimensional terrain
  • The lone oak is Shadow Violet mixed with Payne’s Gray, softened at its edges by lifting into the damp sky wash — it should feel like it’s being absorbed into the rain, not standing against it
Prompt 4

Convergence Zone from Above — Mountain Pass View

Subject: Looking down from a Cascade foothill into a valley filled with rain. Multiple storm cells visible as distinct dark columns. Layers of atmosphere between you and the valley floor. Breaks in cloud cover revealing brief patches of French Ultramarine sky.

Every gray in the palette gets layered here — Payne’s Gray, Neutral Tint, and Shadow Violet at different dilutions create the atmospheric perspective. French Ultramarine for the sky breaks. The Storm Core mix for the darkest cells.

  • Work in distinct layers from background to foreground: the farthest rain cells are Neutral Tint at 10% strength, middle cells are Payne’s Gray at 30%, and the nearest storm column is the full Storm Core mix — this value progression creates depth through atmosphere alone
  • Sky breaks are French Ultramarine applied with small, decisive strokes between cloud masses — these are the only pure-color moments in the painting and they need to sing against all that gray
  • The valley floor is barely visible: Raw Umber and Burnt Sienna at extreme dilution, glimpsed through gaps in the rain. Less is more here — the ground is a rumor, not a statement
  • Rain columns are vertical washes of the Wall of Water mix dropped into wet paper and allowed to stream downward — paint each cell separately, letting each dry slightly before adding the next, so they overlap but remain distinct
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Payne’s Gray (PBk6, PB29, PV42) is Daniel Smith’s version — slightly bluer than some brands, which matters for rain. Indanthrone Blue (PB60) granulates beautifully on rough paper, creating storm texture passively. Neutral Tint (PBk6, PV42, PB15) is a convenience mix but earns its place by being truly neutral — no color bias means no accidental warmth or coolness in your rain curtains. Shadow Violet (PV15, PBk6) provides that specific purple-gray cast unique to heavy PNW precipitation. Buff Titanium (PW6:1) is semi-opaque by nature, which makes it ideal for suggesting light diffused through rain.
