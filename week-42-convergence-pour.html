Convergence Pour

Convergence zone locked over the lowlands, dumping rain that turns to sleet at 1,200 feet. Eagles circle back to the rivers like rumors confirmed. The sky cannot hold what it carries, and neither can the ground — every ditch is a creek, every creek a river, and the chanterelles are drowning in their own abundance.

Mixes & Ratios

Chanterelle Gill

1 Olive Green : 2 Yellow Ochre

Golden folds under hemlock canopy — the chanterelle’s secret architecture, radiating from stem to cap edge.

Olive Green
Yellow Ochre
Yellow Ochre dominates with warm opacity while Olive Green pulls toward forest floor. Apply with small round in radiating strokes from stem outward.

River Steel

2 French Ultramarine : 1 Lamp Black

Swollen current carrying sky — the river as mirror and muscle, reflecting nothing but its own weight.

French Ultramarine
Lamp Black
French Ultramarine’s granulation with Lamp Black’s cool density produces steely blue-black. Lay wet on rough paper and tilt to flow directionally.

Eagle Perch

1 Burnt Sienna Light : 1 Lamp Black : touch Buff Titanium

Wet bark under talons — the branch worn smooth by years of raptor landings.

Burnt Sienna Lt
Lamp Black
Buff Titanium
Mix on palette for warm charcoal. Apply to branch shape, touch Buff Titanium to upper edge while wet. Let it bloom.

Why These Colors?

The convergence zone is about collision. French Ultramarine and Burnt Sienna Light are the classic granulating complement pair. Olive Green and Yellow Ochre handle the forest floor’s fungal palette without false vibrancy. Lamp Black provides true darks for eagle silhouettes. Lavender granulates into soft violet-grays for sleet haze. Phthalo Green BS is the deep-water note, used sparingly.

Painting Prompts

The convergence zone turns everything into water — these prompts work with that transformation rather than against it.

Prompt 1

Chanterelles in Hemlock Duff

Subject: Golden chanterelles emerging from dark hemlock duff — the forest floor’s quiet gold rush, hidden under dripping boughs.

The Chanterelle Gill mix (Olive Green 1:Yellow Ochre 2) captures the warm opacity of gill folds radiating from stem to cap edge.

  • Paint dark background first with Olive Green and Raw Umber
  • Let dry, paint caps with Yellow Ochre
  • Use Chanterelle Gill mix in radiating strokes
  • Glaze Lavender over far background for rain-haze
Prompt 2

Eagle on Snag Above Swollen River

Subject: Bald eagle hunched on bare snag, steel-gray river below, sleet haze softening everything beyond fifty yards.

River Steel (French Ultramarine 2:Lamp Black 1) gives the water its weight, while Eagle Perch (Burnt Sienna Light 1:Lamp Black 1:Buff Titanium touch) handles the wet bark.

  • Lay sky with Lavender, let it granulate
  • Paint river with River Steel mix on tilted paper
  • Snag gets Eagle Perch mix
  • Paint eagle last on dry paper with hard edges
Prompt 3

Flooded Trail Through Second Growth

Subject: Lowland trail submerged under three inches of water — the path still visible but belonging to the rain now, not to you.

Every color in the palette gets reflected. This is a painting about doubling — the world above water and the world below it, using the same pigments at different dilutions.

  • Paint upper half normally
  • Before lower half dries, paint reflections in diluted versions
  • Use Phthalo Green BS in deepest standing water
  • Break reflections with Buff Titanium horizontal strokes
Prompt 4

Convergence Zone Storm Cell

Subject: Pure sky study of a massive storm cell — no land, no trees, just the architecture of weather doing what it does best over the lowlands.

French Ultramarine and Lavender own the sky. This is a wet-in-wet exercise where the paper does half the work and you do the other half by knowing when to stop.

  • Work entirely wet-in-wet on soaked paper
  • Let Lavender bloom downward for sleet curtain
  • Touch Buff Titanium along foothill line
  • Leave white paper at cloud top edge
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. Burnt Sienna Light is distinct from standard Burnt Sienna — lighter, more transparent. Lavender granulates beautifully into component violets and blues. French Ultramarine and Burnt Sienna Light mixed 1:1 produce granulating grays.
