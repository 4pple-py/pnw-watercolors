Week 3 — PNW Watercolor Series

Convergence Snow

The convergence zone drops snow on places that don’t know what to do with it. Seattle buses slide sideways on Capitol Hill, but in the neighborhoods it’s just quiet — muffled, blue-white, branches bending under weight they weren’t built for. Snow shadows go violet. The world holds its breath.

Primary Trio

French Ultramarine

The blue pooling in every snow shadow — granulating, warm-leaning, settling into paper valleys the way meltwater settles into footprints.

Chinese White

The body of the snow itself — semi-transparent zinc oxide that softens washes without killing them, the way fresh snow softens a landscape.

Cobalt Blue

Where sky meets snow and neither quite wins — granulating, cooler than Ultramarine, with that chalky separation that looks like falling flakes on textured paper.

Mixes & Ratios

Hush Drift

2 Shadow Violet : 1 Cobalt Blue : touch Chinese White

The color of silence after the last bus gives up on the hill — deep violet-blue snow shadow with a milky veil.

Shadow Violet
Cobalt Blue
Chinese Wht
Shadow Violet brings a muted, granulating violet that reads as depth rather than decoration. Adding Cobalt Blue shifts it cooler and introduces a second granulating pigment — the two will separate on textured paper into distinct warm and cool particles, which is exactly what happens in real snow shadows. The touch of Chinese White doesn’t make it opaque; it lifts the value just enough that the shadow still reads as illuminated from within. Mix loosely on the palette and apply in a single pass to wet paper. Let the granulation do the work. This is your go-to for any shadow on undisturbed snow.

Frozen Branch Glaze

1 Raw Umber : 1 Payne’s Gray : trace Lavender

Bare alder branches holding snow they didn’t ask for — bark gone silver-brown under a cold sky.

Raw Umber
Payne’s Gray
Lavender
Raw Umber is the bark; Payne’s Gray is the wet cold sitting on it. Equal parts give you a neutral that leans neither warm nor cool — which is how winter branches actually look against snow. The trace of Lavender is the secret: it shifts the mix just enough toward violet to harmonize with the snow shadows around it, so branches don’t feel pasted on. Apply with a rigger or small round for fine branches. For thicker trunks, use a flat brush and vary the ratio — more Raw Umber on the sunward side, more Payne’s Gray where the bark stays wet.

Convergence Ceiling

2 Cerulean Blue Chromium : 1 Buff Titanium : touch Payne’s Gray

The low cloud deck that means business — opaque, heavy, the particular gray-blue that Seattleites recognize as “actual snow, not just rain.”

Cerulean Blue Chr.
Buff Titanium
Payne’s Gray
Cerulean Blue Chromium is the opaque version of Cerulean — it sits on the paper rather than sinking in, which gives cloud washes a physical weight that transparent blues can’t match. Buff Titanium warms and lightens it to that specific yellow-gray brightness at the cloud edge where snow-light filters through. The touch of Payne’s Gray darkens the mix for the cloud base without muddying it. Apply on damp paper in broad horizontal strokes, varying the Payne’s Gray as you go — more at the bottom of the cloud, less at the top. The opacity means you can layer this over dried washes to build cloud depth.

Why These Colors?

Convergence snow is mostly about blue and white, but the specific blues matter. French Ultramarine granulates warm for shadow structure. Cobalt Blue granulates cool for sky-snow edges. Cerulean Blue Chromium is opaque — it sits on the paper like a snow-loaded cloud sits on the horizon, heavy and physical. Shadow Violet fills the gap between blue shadows and gray tree trunks, giving snow its violet undertone without requiring you to mix purple from scratch. Payne’s Gray and Raw Umber handle everything that isn’t snow — branches, power lines, the dark shapes that give all that white something to be white against. Lavender dissolves distant treelines into atmosphere. Two whites on the palette isn’t redundant: Chinese White is semi-transparent zinc oxide for softening washes; Titanium White is opaque for snow that needs to sit on top of everything. Buff Titanium catches the warm light that sneaks through cloud breaks. Ten colors, but the painting is really about three values: white snow, blue shadow, dark branch.

Painting Prompts

Four scenes built for this palette — each one leans into the narrow value range and blue-violet temperature of lowland PNW snow.

Prompt 1

Seattle Side Street, 6 AM

Subject: A residential side street in the snow before anyone’s driven on it. Parked cars are just mounds. Power lines sag. One porch light is on, casting a warm cone into blue-white nothing.

The Hush Drift mix (Shadow Violet + Cobalt Blue + Chinese White) covers every shadow on every snow mound. Buff Titanium is the porch light’s warm cone. Payne’s Gray handles the power lines and car silhouettes underneath the snow.

  • Reserve white paper for the snow on the cars and street — the shapes are simple mounds, so plan them with a light pencil sketch first
  • Lay the Hush Drift mix into all shadow areas while the paper is still damp, letting it granulate naturally into violet and blue particles
  • Paint power lines with Payne’s Gray using a rigger on dry paper — they need to be crisp against the soft snow
  • Drop Buff Titanium into the porch light cone while the surrounding wash is still slightly damp, so the warm edge bleeds just enough to feel like light diffusing through snowfall
Prompt 2

Frozen Bird Bath

Subject: A backyard bird bath with snow piled on the rim and ice in the bowl. Bare branches frame it overhead. A junco sits on the edge, confused.

Cobalt Blue’s granulation is the ice surface. Raw Umber and the Frozen Branch Glaze mix handle the bare branches overhead. Titanium White can add opaque snow on top of dried branch work. Shadow Violet deepens the shadow inside the bowl.

  • Paint the ice in the bowl with a thin wash of Cobalt Blue on dry paper — let the granulation create the crystalline texture naturally
  • Build the snow rim with reserved white paper, adding Shadow Violet only in the deepest recesses of the piled snow
  • Use the Frozen Branch Glaze mix for overhead branches, then add Titanium White on top for snow sitting on the branches — the opacity matters here, it needs to cover the dark bark
  • The junco is Payne’s Gray with a white belly left as paper — keep it small and simple, just a dark comma with an eye
Prompt 3

Convergence Zone Sky, Looking South

Subject: Just the sky. The convergence zone cloud deck bearing down from the north, with one thin strip of Cerulean visible at the southern horizon. Snow falling from the leading edge.

The Convergence Ceiling mix (Cerulean Blue Chromium + Buff Titanium + Payne’s Gray) is the entire cloud mass. Cerulean Blue Chromium on its own is the thin sky strip. Lavender handles the diffuse zone where snow is actively falling and the cloud dissolves into white-out.

  • Wet the entire sheet and work top to bottom in one pass — this is a speed painting, no going back
  • Load the Convergence Ceiling mix heavy at the top, increasing the Payne’s Gray for the darkest cloud base
  • Leave a dry strip near the bottom and drop in pure Cerulean Blue Chromium — its opacity will hold a clean edge even on damp paper
  • While the cloud wash is still wet, touch Lavender along the leading edge where snow is falling — its component pigments will separate and drift, which is what falling snow looks like at distance
Prompt 4

Bus Stop on Queen Anne Hill

Subject: A bus stop shelter on a steep hill, snow accumulating on the roof and bench. Tire tracks in the slush on the road. The city visible below through falling snow, reduced to smudges of Lavender and Payne’s Gray.

French Ultramarine handles the snow shadows on the shelter roof. The Frozen Branch Glaze mix works for the shelter’s metal frame. Lavender dissolves the downtown skyline into atmosphere. Raw Umber mixed with French Ultramarine gives you the dirty slush in the tire tracks.

  • Establish the city below first with loose Lavender and Payne’s Gray on wet paper — buildings are just vertical smudges, nothing sharp
  • Paint the bus shelter structure with the Frozen Branch Glaze mix on dry paper for hard edges that contrast with the soft city behind
  • Snow on the shelter roof is reserved white paper with French Ultramarine shadows — let the granulation build texture
  • Tire tracks in the road are Raw Umber and French Ultramarine mixed to a granulating gray-brown — apply in parallel strokes that follow the road’s pitch downhill
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. French Ultramarine (PB29) and Cobalt Blue (PB28) are both granulating blues — Ultramarine is warmer and better for shadows, Cobalt is cooler and better for sky-snow edges. Cerulean Blue Chromium (PB36) is the opaque version of Cerulean Blue; it sits on the paper rather than sinking in, which gives cloud washes physical weight. Shadow Violet (PV15/PBk6) is outstanding for snow shadows — it granulates into violet and gray particles that separate on textured paper. Lavender (multiple pigments) is a convenience mix that shifts and separates; use it for atmospheric distance work. Two whites: Chinese White (PW4, zinc oxide) is semi-transparent for softening; Titanium White (PW6) is fully opaque for corrections and snow-on-branch effects. Buff Titanium (PW6:1) reads as warm light on snow. This palette works in a narrow value range — keep most of the painting between values 2 and 5, with branches and silhouettes at 7-8.
