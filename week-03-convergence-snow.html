Week 3 — PNW Watercolor Series

Convergence Snow

Puget Sound convergence zone drops wet snow at 300 feet. Crows mass at dusk — ten thousand pulling toward a single roost, black on lavender. Oregon grape leaves gone burgundy-bronze at the forest edge. The tension: collective warmth sought in the coldest hour.

Primary Trio

French Ultramarine

Snow shadow on the hillside; granulating, warm-leaning blue that settles into paper valleys like snow settles into folds of ground

Alizarin Crimson

Oregon grape leaf edge, cold-stressed to burgundy; transparent, staining, bleeds with intention

Neutral Tint

Crow mass at distance, a single dark shape made of thousands; transparent, cool, erases detail

Mixes & Ratios

Crow Dusk

2 Neutral Tint : 1 Lavender : trace French Ultramarine

Mass roost against dying light — the moment ten thousand crows become one shape, black on violet, pulling east.

Neutral Tint
Lavender
Fr. Ultra.
The trick is layering, not mixing. Lay Lavender as the sky wash first — let it dry completely. Then load Neutral Tint heavy and stamp the crow mass in a single application, no fussing. The trace of French Ultramarine goes into the Neutral Tint on the palette before application, shifting it just cool enough to read as twilight rather than mere darkness. For individual crows at the flock edge, use a rigger with pure Neutral Tint. The Lavender beneath will glow through.

Snow Mud Edge

2 Raw Umber : 1 French Ultramarine

Slush where trail meets shoulder — the dirty margin where snow gives up pretending to be clean.

Raw Umber
Fr. Ultramarine
Both pigments granulate, and together they produce a beautifully gritty neutral — warm enough for mud, cool enough for melt. Mix on the palette to a loose consistency and apply in a single pass. The granulation will separate the warm and cool particles on textured paper, giving you the marbled look of dirty slush without any brushwork. For pure snow shadow without mud, reverse the ratio. For pure mud without snow, drop the Ultramarine entirely.

Grape Leaf Bronze

2 Alizarin Crimson : 1 Transparent Red Oxide

Mahonia foliage cold-stressed to burgundy-bronze — the leaves that traded green for armor weeks ago.

Alizarin Crimson
Transp. Red Oxide
Both pigments are transparent and staining — this mix bites into the paper and stays. Apply to dry paper for the hard, lacquered look of Oregon grape leaves in winter. Alizarin Crimson pulls the mix toward burgundy; Transparent Red Oxide grounds it in bronze. For leaves catching the last horizontal light, increase the oxide. For leaves fully in shadow, add a whisper of Neutral Tint. These leaves are small — use a round brush, sizes 4-6, and paint each one as a single deliberate mark.

Why These Colors?

Convergence zone snow is a drama of value contrast — black crows, white snow, and the bruised lavender sky between them. French Ultramarine’s granulation serves double duty: snow shadow texture and the particulate quality of air thick with falling flakes. Alizarin Crimson and Transparent Red Oxide are both transparent stainers, giving Oregon grape leaves the lacquered, waxy surface they actually have. Neutral Tint is the palette’s workhorse dark — cooler and more transparent than Ivory Black, better for massed shapes at distance. Ivory Black stays on the palette for the one close crow that needs opacity and density. Lavender and Moonglow both contain multiple pigments that separate on paper, which is exactly the behavior you want for a convergence zone sky — unstable, shifting, never quite one color.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Crow Roost at Convergence Dusk

Subject: Ten thousand crows pulling toward a single roost against a lavender snow-sky — the flock resolved to a dark mass at distance, individual birds visible only at the ragged edges.

The Crow Dusk mix (Neutral Tint + Lavender + trace French Ultramarine) was designed for exactly this. Lavender’s shifting granulation gives the snow-bearing sky its bruised instability. Ivory Black is there for the one close bird.

  • Lay the Lavender sky wash first and let it dry completely — the granulation needs time to settle and separate into its component pigments
  • Stamp the crow mass with heavy Neutral Tint in one application — no fussing, no going back. The mass should be a single shape with ragged edges
  • Use a rigger with pure Neutral Tint for individual crows peeling away from the flock edge — each bird is a single flick of the wrist
  • Add one close crow in Ivory Black for the opacity and density that reads as “near” against the transparent distance
Prompt 2

Snow on the Fenceline

Subject: A rural fenceline disappearing into convergence zone snowfall — wet heavy snow on the posts, the wire sagging, the background dissolving into white-gray nothing.

Buff Titanium is the wet snow on the fencepost — semi-opaque and matte, exactly how heavy lowland snow looks. Moonglow dissolves the background treeline. The Snow Mud Edge mix (Raw Umber + French Ultramarine) handles the slush at the base.

  • Reserve the snow on the fenceposts as white paper — plan these shapes first, they’re the painting’s architecture
  • Paint the background with Moonglow on very wet paper, letting it dissolve into formless snowfall — trees should be suggestions, not shapes
  • Once dry, paint the fence posts with Raw Umber, leaving the snow caps as paper white with just a glaze of Buff Titanium for the shadow side
  • The Snow Mud Edge mix goes at the base where posts meet ground — that granulating neutral is the dirty margin between snow and earth
Prompt 3

Oregon Grape at the Forest Edge

Subject: A cluster of Oregon grape leaves gone burgundy-bronze in the cold, catching the last horizontal light at the edge of a snowy clearing. The snow is blue in shadow.

The Grape Leaf Bronze mix (Alizarin Crimson + Transparent Red Oxide) gives you that lacquered, waxy leaf surface. French Ultramarine handles the snow shadows. These are the only warm notes in a cold scene, and they hit hard.

  • Paint the snow first — leave it as white paper with French Ultramarine shadows, letting the granulation create snow texture
  • Paint each Oregon grape leaflet as a single mark with a size 4-6 round, using the Grape Leaf Bronze mix on dry paper for hard, waxy edges
  • For leaves catching horizontal light, increase the Transparent Red Oxide; for leaves in shadow, add a touch of Neutral Tint
  • The forest behind the clearing is Moonglow + Neutral Tint, kept soft and unfocused so the crisp leaf edges pop by contrast
Prompt 4

Convergence Zone Sky Study

Subject: Just the sky — no land, no trees. The convergence zone cloud deck at 4:45 PM, heavy with wet snow, shifting between lavender and gray with one brief gap of Cobalt Blue.

Lavender and Moonglow are both convenience mixes that separate on paper into multiple pigments — that instability IS convergence zone weather. Cobalt Blue is the rare sky gap. Buff Titanium is the brightest cloud edge.

  • Wet the entire sheet and work fast — this is a one-pass painting. Lay Lavender across the upper two-thirds, Moonglow across the lower third
  • While everything is wet, lift a small window with a thirsty brush and drop in Cobalt Blue for the sky gap — keep it small, because these gaps are brief
  • Touch Buff Titanium along the cloud edges where light catches — its opacity will sit on top of the transparent washes
  • Add Neutral Tint to the heaviest cloud base to suggest the snow that’s about to fall — the darkest value should be at the bottom of the cloud, not the top
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Temperature range is decisively cool, with Alizarin Crimson and Transparent Red Oxide as the only warms — and even those are cool-reds. Lavender and Moonglow are both convenience mixes that granulate and separate; embrace that instability for sky work. French Ultramarine and Cobalt Blue are both on the palette — use Ultramarine for snow shadows (warmer, granulating) and Cobalt for sky gaps (cooler, cleaner). Buff Titanium is semi-opaque; reserve it for snow highlights applied last. The entire palette works in a narrow value range — most of the scene lives between values 4 and 7, with the crows at 9 and the snow at 1.
