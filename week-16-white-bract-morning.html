Week 16 — PNW Watercolor Series

White Bract Morning

Dogwood bracts catch the morning mist light like paper lanterns in the forest. Chinese White against Perylene Green darkness. Cobalt Blue mist threading between the trunks. The green is so deep the white glows. Every year you forget how good this contrast is.

Primary Trio

Chinese White

The bract itself — semi-opaque zinc white that glows against dark backgrounds without looking painted-on; the whole week pivots on this pigment

Perylene Green

Deep forest dark that makes white sing; PBk31 is one of the darkest greens available, and that darkness is the point

Cobalt Blue

Morning mist made visible; granulates beautifully on cold-press paper, breaking into the soft particles that are exactly how fog behaves

Mixes & Ratios

Morning Bract

3 Chinese White : 1 Buff Titanium : ¼ Hansa Yellow Light

The warm side of a dogwood bract at 7 a.m., when the first sun hits it and white turns to something almost golden.

Chinese White
Buff Titanium
Hansa Yellow Lt
Chinese White gives the mix body — it sits on the surface instead of sinking in, which is exactly how a bract reads in life: a thing on top of the forest, not absorbed by it. Buff Titanium warms the white without tinting it pink or yellow — just shifts it toward morning. The thread of Hansa Yellow Light is the sun arriving. Apply to dry paper for crisp bract edges. This mix is opaque enough to go over dried green washes if you need to add bracts after the forest is down.

Deep Canopy Dark

3 Perylene Green : 1 Payne's Gray : ½ Cobalt Blue

The forest behind the dogwood, so dark it makes the bracts glow. The green you feel more than see.

Perylene Green
Payne's Gray
Cobalt Blue
Perylene Green is already one of the darkest pigments in the Daniel Smith line — PBk31 is technically a black that reads green. Payne's Gray cools and neutralizes it toward shadow. The half-part Cobalt Blue is the mist working into the dark spaces, giving the shadow atmosphere rather than emptiness. At full concentration this is nearly black; diluted, it separates into green and blue granulation that looks like forest light filtering through canopy layers.

New Leaf Veil

2 Green Gold : 1 Sap Green : ½ Raw Sienna

Late April's newest leaves, still translucent, still more yellow than green. The canopy hasn't filled in yet.

Green Gold
Sap Green
Raw Sienna
Green Gold leads because April leaves aren't fully green yet — they're that translucent yellow-green that makes the whole forest feel backlit. Sap Green steadies it toward an actual leaf color without killing the light. Raw Sienna is the warm undertone of bark showing through thin foliage, the branch you can still see because the leaf hasn't grown large enough to hide it. Glaze this over dried Cobalt Blue mist washes and the layers will create aerial perspective almost by accident.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built on a single contrast: white against dark green. Chinese White (PW4) is semi-opaque zinc — it glows on the paper without looking like correction fluid, which is exactly what dogwood bracts do in the forest. Perylene Green is one of the darkest greens Daniel Smith makes, and that darkness is the stage the bracts perform on. Cobalt Blue granulates into the soft-edged particles that are indistinguishable from morning mist on cold-press paper. Buff Titanium and Hansa Yellow Light warm the bracts toward morning light without overstating it. Sap Green and Green Gold handle the living canopy at two stages of spring growth. Raw Sienna and Transparent Brown Oxide give you bark — warm tones for trunk and branch structure. Payne's Gray ties the mist to the shadows. Ten pigments, one job: making white look luminous by surrounding it with everything that isn't.

Painting Prompts

Four subjects built for this week’s palette — each one leans into that white-on-dark-green contrast that makes dogwood season feel like the forest invented paper lanterns.

Prompt 1

Dogwood Branch Close-Up

Subject: A single dogwood branch at arm’s length — three or four bracts open, the tiny true flowers visible at center, branch structure clear against a soft green background.

The Morning Bract mix (Chinese White + Buff Titanium + Hansa Yellow Light) handles the sunlit bracts. Transparent Brown Oxide draws the branch. Hansa Yellow Light, concentrated, dots the tiny true flowers at center.

  • Paint the soft background first with dilute Sap Green and Green Gold wet-in-wet, then let it dry completely before adding any bract work
  • Each bract has a notched tip — use the Morning Bract mix on dry paper with a small round brush to get those crisp, characteristic indentations
  • The branch is Transparent Brown Oxide with a rigger brush; dogwood branches have a clean, architectural quality that rewards careful line work
  • Leave a sliver of untouched white paper at the brightest edge of each bract — no pigment is as white as the paper itself
Prompt 2

Misty Forest with Dogwood Canopy

Subject: A stand of Douglas fir with dogwood as understory, morning mist threading between trunks, white bracts scattered at mid-height like suspended paper.

Deep Canopy Dark (Perylene Green + Payne’s Gray + Cobalt Blue) builds the fir trunks and deep background. Cobalt Blue alone, highly diluted, is the mist. Chinese White bracts go on last, opaque over the dried dark.

  • Wet the upper third of the paper and float in dilute Cobalt Blue for the mist layer — let it granulate; that texture is the fog
  • Once dry, paint fir trunks with Deep Canopy Dark using a flat brush, vertical strokes, leaving gaps where mist interrupts them
  • The dogwood bracts are Chinese White mixed with a touch of Buff Titanium, applied with a small brush directly over the dark wash — this is where semi-opacity earns its keep
  • Distant bracts should be smaller and slightly bluer (add a breath of Cobalt Blue to the white) to push them back in space
Prompt 3

Single Bract with Morning Dew

Subject: One dogwood bract filling most of the frame, dew drops catching light on its surface, the green of the leaf behind it showing through at thin spots.

Chinese White and Buff Titanium build the bract surface. Sap Green shows through where the bract is thin. Raw Sienna warms the veins. Dewdrops are pure paper white with Payne’s Gray shadow crescents.

  • This is a study in restraint — wash the entire bract shape with very dilute Buff Titanium first, leaving hard white paper only where dewdrops will catch the light
  • While the wash is damp, drop in thin Sap Green where the bract is translucent, letting it bleed softly into the warm base
  • Vein lines are Raw Sienna with a rigger brush after everything is dry — dogwood bract veins curve from center to the notched tip
  • Each dewdrop is a tiny circle of untouched paper with a crescent of Payne’s Gray shadow on the underside; less is more here
Prompt 4

Forest Path with White Overhead

Subject: A trail disappearing into second-growth forest, dogwood bracts forming a loose white canopy overhead, morning light filtering down, ferns lining the path.

The path is Raw Sienna and Transparent Brown Oxide. Perylene Green handles the deep forest walls. Green Gold and Sap Green build the fern understory. Bracts overhead are Chinese White catching the light that Cobalt Blue mist softens.

  • Establish the light source first — mask or preserve white paper where morning sun breaks through the canopy, then build the forest dark around those openings
  • The path narrows to a point; use Raw Sienna thinning to Transparent Brown Oxide as it recedes, letting perspective do the compositional work
  • Ferns are Sap Green pulled in quick downward arcs on either side of the path — paint the gesture, not individual fronds
  • Overhead bracts should vary: some crisp Morning Bract mix on dry paper in the foreground, some soft Chinese White dropped into damp Cobalt Blue mist in the distance
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Chinese White (PW4) is zinc-based and semi-opaque — it won't fully cover darks, but that translucency is what gives it a glow rather than a painted-on look. Perylene Green (PBk31) is technically a black that reads as one of the darkest greens available. Cobalt Blue (PB28) granulates beautifully on cold-press paper, making it ideal for mist and atmospheric effects. Buff Titanium (PW6:1) is a warm opaque white useful for shifting Chinese White toward morning light without adding chroma.
