Week 16 — PNW Watercolor Series

White Bract Morning

Pacific dogwood catches early light in the understory, white bracts hovering like held breath. Gray whales pass offshore, visible only as patience. Warblers move through in waves you hear but can't find. Spring pausing between one spectacle and the next — the quiet week, the inhale before May.

Primary Trio

Nickel Azo Yellow

Warbler flash in the canopy, a soft muted yellow that reads as warmth glimpsed and lost; semi-transparent, gentle

Rose Madder Permanent

Dogwood bract blush-edge, the faintest pink on what looks white; transparent, non-staining, lifts cleanly

French Ultramarine

Deep ocean viewed from a headland, the blue that means depth; transparent, granulating, and warm-leaning for a blue

Mixes & Ratios

Bract Glow

2 Chinese White : 1 Rose Madder Permanent : ¼ Nickel Azo Yellow

Dogwood white flushed warm at center, the part that's not petal but pretends convincingly.

Chinese White
Rose Madder
Nickel Azo
Chinese White provides body — this mix is meant to sit on the paper, not sink in. Rose Madder Permanent tints without staining, so you can lift back to near-white for highlights. The whisper of Nickel Azo Yellow warms the center the way real dogwood bracts warm toward their inflorescence. Apply to dry paper for crisp bract edges.

Headland Gray

3 Payne's Gray : 1 French Ultramarine : ½ Olive Green

Watching for whale spouts in flat light, eyes straining against the gray-blue sameness of ocean and sky.

Payne's Gray
Fr. Ultramarine
Olive Green
Payne's Gray runs cool and neutral — French Ultramarine warms and deepens it, pulling the mix toward ocean rather than concrete. Olive Green is the surprise: it adds the faintly organic, living quality that separates sea-gray from city-gray. Use at high dilution for distant water, concentrated for foreground swells.

Warbler Canopy

2 Sap Green : 2 Nickel Azo Yellow : 1 Cascade Green

Bright tangled green where yellow-rumped warblers disappear the instant you look up.

Sap Green
Nickel Azo Yellow
Cascade Green
Equal parts Sap Green and Nickel Azo Yellow make a canopy green that's alive with spring light — the yellow keeps it from going flat. Cascade Green cools the shadows between leaves. Layer wet-on-dry to build the density of a canopy you can hear birds in but can't see them through. The Nickel Azo Yellow is semi-transparent, so it glazes beautifully over dried green.

Why These Colors?

This is a restraint palette for a restrained week. Rose Madder Permanent is the gentlest red available — non-staining, liftable, barely there — because dogwood bracts are barely pink. Nickel Azo Yellow is similarly quiet, a muted gold rather than a shout. French Ultramarine's granulation gives ocean texture at distance, while Indigo handles the darks with a blue-black that stays chromatic. Chinese White is essential here as a mixing white for the bracts; this is one of the few palettes where opacity is the point. Payne's Gray ties atmosphere to ocean without committing to either.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Dogwood Bracts in the Understory

Subject: A cluster of Pacific dogwood “flowers” (bracts) hovering in the filtered light of a second-growth forest, dark trunks behind them.

The Bract Glow mix (Chinese White + Rose Madder Permanent + Nickel Azo Yellow) was literally designed for this. Against a background of Cascade Green and Sap Green forest, the white bracts will float forward like they do in life.

  • Paint the dark understory first with Cascade Green and Sap Green layered wet-on-dry, then let it dry completely before adding bracts
  • Mix the Bract Glow on the palette and apply to dry paper so the edges stay crisp — dogwood bracts have clean, notched outlines
  • Rose Madder Permanent lifts almost completely, so you can dab back to near-white for highlight edges with a clean damp brush
  • Leave a sliver of untouched white paper at the brightest bract tip for a highlight that no pigment can match
Prompt 2

Whale Watch From the Headland

Subject: A coastal headland view — gray ocean merging with overcast sky, bluff grass in the foreground, and maybe (maybe) a distant spout.

The Headland Gray mix (Payne’s Gray + French Ultramarine + Olive Green) handles the entire ocean-sky merge. Olive Green does the salt-tough bluff grass. Indigo gives you wave troughs.

  • Wet the entire sky-and-ocean area and drop in Payne’s Gray and French Ultramarine, letting them blend where sky meets sea — the lost edge is the point
  • While the wash is still damp, add Indigo in horizontal strokes for wave shadows in the middle distance
  • The bluff grass is Olive Green on dry paper with a fan brush or splayed flat brush for texture
  • If you paint the whale spout, use Chinese White applied with a rigger — one quick vertical mark. Restraint is everything.
Prompt 3

Warbler in the Canopy (Glimpsed and Gone)

Subject: A looking-up view through tangled spring canopy, with one small flash of yellow suggesting a warbler you’ll never quite see.

The Warbler Canopy mix (Sap Green + Nickel Azo Yellow + Cascade Green) builds the tangled green. One tiny dab of concentrated Nickel Azo Yellow is your bird.

  • Work the canopy in three layers: Nickel Azo Yellow wash for backlit areas, then Sap Green wet-on-dry for leaf mass, then Cascade Green for cool shadows
  • Leave small irregular gaps of white paper for sky showing through the canopy — this creates the dappled-light effect
  • The warbler is just a single charged touch of Nickel Azo Yellow, maybe 5mm across, placed off-center in a gap between leaves
  • Add a single branch line with Transparent Red Oxide (madrone) to give the bird something to perch on
Prompt 4

Morning Mist Along the Forest Road

Subject: A gravel forest road disappearing into morning mist, sword ferns lining both sides, dogwood bracts visible as pale shapes in the middle distance.

Payne’s Gray at high dilution is the mist itself. Sap Green handles the fern walls. The dogwood bracts become soft Bract Glow shapes losing their edges in the fog.

  • Wet the upper half of the paper and float in very dilute Payne’s Gray, heavier at the top, fading to nothing at the horizon line
  • Sword ferns are Sap Green pulled downward in quick arcing strokes with a round brush — don’t paint individual fronds, paint the gesture of them
  • The distant dogwood bracts should be Bract Glow mix applied while the mist wash is still slightly damp, so their edges soften and blur
  • The road itself is dilute Payne’s Gray with a touch of Olive Green for gravel. Sometimes the palette teaches you to see differently.
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Chinese White (PW4) is zinc-based and semi-transparent — it won't fully cover darks but creates beautiful opalescent tints. Rose Madder Permanent is the lightfast replacement for true Rose Madder; it lifts almost completely, making it ideal for soft florals. Nickel Azo Yellow may separate slightly in wet washes — this is a feature, not a flaw, for canopy work.
