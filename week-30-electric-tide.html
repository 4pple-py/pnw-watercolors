Week 30 — PNW Watercolor Series

Electric Tide

Peak Perseids overhead, peak Noctiluca below — the sky and sea trade light. Every paddle stroke detonates blue-green fire, and meteors drag white threads above the islands. Opera pink shimmer rides the wavelet crests. Summer is showing off, and it knows the audience is rapt.

Primary Trio

Phthalo Green BS

Full-bloom bioluminescence at peak intensity; transparent and staining beyond rescue

Indanthrone Blue

The dark between glow-strokes, the negative space that makes the light legible; deep, granulating, nearly black at concentration

Opera Pink

The magenta shimmer atop wavelets that shouldn’t exist but does; fugitive and unapologetic about it

Mixes & Ratios

Biolume Fire

3 Phthalo Green BS : 1 Phthalo Blue GS : touch Opera Pink

Full-intensity paddle-stroke glow with magenta crest — the moment your blade enters the water and the water answers with everything it has.

Phthalo Green BS
Phthalo Blue GS
Opera Pink
The Opera Pink touch is critical and dangerous. Too much and you get candy; too little and you miss the magenta crest entirely. Apply the phthalo mix first in a confident stroke, then touch Opera Pink to the leading edge while soaking wet. It will bloom backward into the green — that’s correct. That’s what the light does on a wavelet. Work on pre-dampened dark paper for maximum glow effect.

Perseid Trail

2 Chinese White : 1 Indanthrone Blue

White streak dragged across the dark vault — a rock burning at 130,000 miles per hour, rendered in two pigments and one second of brushwork.

Chinese White
Indanthrone Blue
Mix very lightly — the Indanthrone Blue barely tints the white toward ice-blue. Apply with a rigger brush in a single fast stroke on dry dark paper. Do not go back over it. A meteor either happens or it doesn’t; there are no second takes. The trail should be thin, confident, slightly uneven.

Night Kelp

2 Cascade Green : 1 Lamp Black : touch Phthalo Green BS

Submerged forest catching stray bioluminescent light — dark shapes below the glow line, barely visible but structurally present.

Cascade Green
Lamp Black
Phthalo Green BS
This mix lives in the lower third of the painting, beneath the active glow zone. Cascade Green provides the diffused underwater light; Lamp Black sinks it to depth. The Phthalo Green touch is just enough chromatic kick to keep it from going dead neutral. Apply wet-into-wet over a dark base wash. These shapes should be suggestions, not illustrations.

Why These Colors?

This is the most chromatically extreme palette in the summer arc, and intentionally so — peak bioluminescence is not a subtle event. Phthalo Green BS and Phthalo Blue GS are the highest-chroma transparent pigments available, which makes them uniquely suited to painting light that appears to come from within the water itself. Opera Pink is technically fugitive (it will fade over years), but its particular fluorescent magenta is irreplaceable for the wavelet-crest shimmer. Indanthrone Blue and Lamp Black provide two flavors of dark: granulating deep blue versus flat matte black. Chinese White serves double duty as meteor and water spark. The earth tones — Burnt Sienna, Quinacridone Gold — are present but marginal, because land is not the point this week. Water and sky are.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Full Bioluminescence Paddle Scene

Subject: A kayak on black water, every paddle stroke trailing fire, the bow wave glowing. The Perseids streak above. The full spectacle — sea and sky trading light.

The Biolume Fire mix (Phthalo Green BS + Phthalo Blue GS + touch of Opera Pink) is the main event, and it’s the most chromatically extreme mix in the summer series. Indanthrone Blue and Lamp Black give you two flavors of dark. Chinese White handles both meteor streaks and water sparks.

  • Full dark ground first: Indanthrone Blue for the water (granulating, deep), Carbazole Violet + Lamp Black for the sky (flat, matte, starless between meteors). Let everything dry completely
  • The glow goes on dry dark paper in confident strokes — Phthalo Green BS for the paddle trails, with Phthalo Blue GS graduating outward where the glow diffuses into deeper water
  • Touch Opera Pink to the leading edge of each paddle stroke while soaking wet — it will bloom backward into the green. That backward bloom is exactly what the light does on a wave crest
  • Perseid streaks are Chinese White lightly tinted with Indanthrone Blue, applied with a rigger brush in single fast strokes. One stroke per meteor. No second takes.
Prompt 2

Opera Pink Wavelet Crests

Subject: An extreme close-up of bioluminescent wavelets — the green glow below the surface and the impossible magenta shimmer that rides the crests. Abstract, intimate, the scale of one square foot of water.

Opera Pink exists on this palette for exactly this subject. It’s fugitive and unapologetic — the fluorescent magenta shimmer that Quinacridone Rose can approximate but never truly match. Phthalo Green BS underneath provides the main glow.

  • Base wash of Indanthrone Blue, medium-dark, over the entire sheet. Dry completely
  • Wavelet shapes in Phthalo Green BS — curving, overlapping strokes that suggest water movement. Work in a single pass while the green is wet
  • Opera Pink at the very top of each wavelet crest, touched in while the green is still soaking wet. Too much becomes candy; too little and you miss it. Practice on scrap paper first
  • Cascade Green in the troughs between wavelets for the softer, diffused glow in deeper water. The contrast between bright crests and dim troughs creates the wave rhythm
Prompt 3

Perseid Meteor Over Island Silhouette

Subject: A single bright meteor arcing over a dark island headland, its trail reflected faintly in bioluminescent water below. Lamp Black headland, Carbazole Violet sky, white streak, green water.

The Perseid Trail mix (Chinese White + Indanthrone Blue) gives you the meteor. Lamp Black provides the total-absence silhouette of a new-moon headland. The palette offers two distinct darks — granulating blue water versus flat matte land — and this composition uses both.

  • Sky first: Carbazole Violet at the zenith grading to Indanthrone Blue near the horizon. This is the deep violet-black between constellations
  • The headland silhouette is Lamp Black at full concentration — flat, matte, no detail. It’s total absence of light, and that flatness is what makes it read as solid land against luminous sky
  • Meteor trail: Chinese White barely tinted with Indanthrone Blue, one stroke with a rigger brush on dry sky. Fast and confident. Slightly thicker at the leading end
  • The water below: Indanthrone Blue base with scattered Phthalo Green BS glow marks and one faint echo of the meteor trail in dilute Chinese White reflected on the surface
Prompt 4

Night Blackberry Harvest

Subject: A hand reaching into a blackberry bramble in the dark, guided by feel and memory — the berries invisible except where bioluminescent light from the nearby shore faintly illuminates them. A painting about touch more than sight.

Quinacridone Gold handles the blackberries’ warm undertone (you’re finding them by touch, and they taste warm). Burnt Sienna for the bramble structure. The faint Cascade Green wash from shore-reflected bioluminescence provides just enough light to reveal form without detail.

  • Nearly the whole painting is dark: Indanthrone Blue base with Lamp Black bramble structure laid wet-into-wet so the thorns merge into darkness
  • The berries are Quinacridone Gold at the highlights (where faint shore-glow catches them) grading to Indanthrone Blue in the shadows. They should barely emerge from the dark
  • One edge of the painting gets a faint wash of Cascade Green — the reflected bioluminescence from the bay, providing the painting’s only light source
  • The hand is suggested, not detailed — Burnt Sienna warmth against dark bramble, fingers curled around fruit. The less you show, the more the viewer feels
Prompt 5

Kelp Forest Below the Glow

Subject: The submerged kelp zone beneath the active bioluminescence — dark shapes catching stray light, structural and mysterious. Looking down into the water rather than across it.

The Night Kelp mix (Cascade Green + Lamp Black + touch of Phthalo Green BS) lives in the lower third of any bioluminescence painting. This prompt makes that zone the entire subject.

  • Dark base wash — Indanthrone Blue mixed with Lamp Black, the deepest dark this palette offers. This is the water depth
  • Kelp fronds in the Night Kelp mix, applied wet-into-wet so they’re suggestions rather than illustrations. Vertical, sinuous shapes reaching upward
  • The glow above the kelp zone is Phthalo Green BS at high dilution — a faint, diffused light filtering down from the surface bioluminescence
  • A few bright points of Phthalo Green BS with Chinese White touch where bioluminescent organisms cling to the kelp itself — tiny sparks of life in the deep structure
Notes: All pigments confirmed Daniel Smith. For bioluminescence passages, keep transparency paramount — the glow must read as light from within the paper, not pigment sitting on top of it. Avoid granulating pigments in active glow zones; save Indanthrone Blue’s granulation for the dark water between glows. Opera Pink is fugitive under UV exposure; for archival work, Quinacridone Rose is the closest substitute, but it lacks the fluorescent quality. For this palette specifically, the fugitivity is thematically appropriate. Chinese White: use sparingly. Every white mark is a claim of maximum light — dilute your claims.
