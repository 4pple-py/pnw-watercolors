Week 30 — PNW Watercolor Series

Electric Tide

Low tide on a July afternoon and the rocks are a gallery nobody curates. Every pool is a terrarium of improbable color — scarlet anemone columns, turquoise water so clear it barely exists, purple urchins wedged into cracks, and a single orange sea star draped over a ledge like it owns the place. You crouch at the edge and the whole ocean fits in a bowl.

Mixes & Ratios

Living Pool

2 Phthalo Green BS : 1 Cobalt Teal Blue : ¼ Mayan Blue Genuine

That impossible turquoise-green of a sunlit tide pool — water so clear the color comes from below, not the surface.

Phthalo Green BS
Cobalt Teal Blue
Mayan Blue Gen.
Phthalo Green BS provides the transparent depth — tide pool water gets its color from light passing through to the bottom and reflecting back, and only a fully transparent pigment can replicate that luminosity. Cobalt Teal Blue adds the turquoise shift and opaque granulation that mimics the sandy bottom visible through shallow water. Mayan Blue Genuine is the critical texture ingredient: a Primatek pigment whose mineral particles settle unevenly, creating the subtle variability of real water where sand, rock, and depth all affect color simultaneously. Apply in layers — Phthalo Green BS as a first wash, then float Cobalt Teal Blue into the shallows while still damp. Touch Mayan Blue Genuine at the pool edges where water meets rock.

Anemone Column

2 Deep Scarlet : 1 Opera Pink : ¼ Ivory Black

The fleshy red column of a giant green anemone, half-submerged, half-exposed — alive and pulsing in the shade of a rock ledge.

Deep Scarlet
Opera Pink
Ivory Black
Deep Scarlet is the body of the column — warm, rich red that reads as flesh rather than flower. Opera Pink at the tentacle tips and the column’s upper edge captures the fluorescent quality of anemone tissue under angled light. Ivory Black in the deepest crevices between column and rock, where the animal attaches to its substrate and shadow pools. Work wet-into-wet: Deep Scarlet for the column body, then touch Opera Pink at the top margin while still damp. The pink will bloom downward into the red, creating the gradation that anemone columns actually display. Let the Ivory Black stay at the base only — these animals are darkest where they grip rock.

Kelp Canopy

2 Undersea Green : 1 Phthalo Green BS : ¼ Ivory Black

Bull kelp and sea lettuce layered in the surge channel — translucent green over dark rock, shifting with every wave.

Undersea Green
Phthalo Green BS
Ivory Black
Undersea Green is Daniel Smith’s pre-mixed convenience green specifically formulated for marine subjects — it has a muted, olive-teal quality that reads as living plant rather than the synthetic intensity of straight phthalo. Phthalo Green BS intensifies the thinnest kelp blades where light passes through, adding that translucent glow. Ivory Black anchors the holdfast zones and the dark rock visible between fronds. Paint the kelp fronds with Undersea Green using long, sinuous strokes on damp paper — kelp has no straight lines. Where blades overlap, add Phthalo Green BS to suggest light passing through layered tissue.

Why These Colors?

Tide pools are nature’s paint box, and the palette mirrors that absurd chromatic range. Deep Scarlet for the fleshy red columns of giant green anemones — the warm, saturated red of something alive and attached to rock. Opera Pink for the aggregating anemones that carpet certain ledges in fluorescent pink, a color so unlikely it looks artificial. Cobalt Teal Blue captures that crystal-clear shallow pool turquoise where sunlight hits sandy bottom, and its granulation mimics the shimmer of light on disturbed water. Mayan Blue Genuine is a Primatek pigment whose mineral quality feels genuinely oceanic — ground from the same mineral the Maya used, its granulation creates water effects no synthetic blue can match. Undersea Green for the kelp forests and sea lettuce beds that fill every channel, a muted marine green purpose-built for submerged plant life. Lemon Yellow for sea lemon nudibranchs — those vivid yellow slugs that appear on rocks like dropped candy. Cadmium Orange Hue for ochre sea stars, the signature creature of the PNW intertidal. Phthalo Blue (Red Shade) deepens the water in channels between pools. Ivory Black for wet basalt and mussel beds. Ten colors for a world that uses every one of them without apology.

Painting Prompts

Low tide is the gallery opening. These prompts are built for the palette’s vivid range — every one demands the kind of saturated color that would look wrong anywhere except the intertidal zone.

Prompt 1

The Perfect Pool

Subject: A single tide pool seen from directly above — turquoise water, red anemones dotting the rocky edges, a sea star draped across one side, kelp fronds drifting. The miniature ocean in a stone bowl.

The Living Pool mix (Phthalo Green BS + Cobalt Teal Blue + Mayan Blue Genuine) fills the center. Anemone Column mix (Deep Scarlet + Opera Pink + Ivory Black) dots the margins. Cadmium Orange Hue for the sea star. The full palette in one scene.

  • Map the composition first with pencil — pool edge, anemone positions, kelp fronds, sea star placement. This is a complex scene that benefits from planning
  • Paint the water first using the Living Pool mix, grading from Cobalt Teal Blue where the pool is shallow over sand to deeper Phthalo Green BS where the bottom drops away
  • Anemones go in while the water wash is still slightly damp so their edges bleed into the pool — they’re half-submerged, and hard edges would look wrong
  • The sea star is Cadmium Orange Hue applied on dry paper with defined edges — sea stars sitting on rock above water level have crisp boundaries unlike the submerged creatures
Prompt 2

Anemone Close-Up

Subject: A single giant green anemone filling the frame — green tentacles radiating from a central disc, red-brown column visible below, attached to dark rock. The alien beauty of the intertidal’s most charismatic animal.

Deep Scarlet for the column. Phthalo Green BS for the tentacles — giant green anemones are green because of symbiotic algae living in their tissue. Opera Pink for the oral disc and tentacle tips where fluorescence is strongest.

  • Paint the tentacles as radiating strokes of Phthalo Green BS from the center outward, each one a single confident stroke with a round brush — tentacles taper toward the tips
  • Touch Opera Pink at each tentacle tip while the green is still wet — the fluorescent pink bloom into green mimics the actual color transition of living anemone tissue
  • The column below is Deep Scarlet, applied in a cylinder shape with the shadow side darkened by a touch of Ivory Black — the column is three-dimensional, muscular, alive
  • Surrounding rock is Ivory Black with Undersea Green algae patches — keep the rock simple and dark to let the anemone’s color be the star
Prompt 3

Surge Channel at Low Tide

Subject: A narrow channel between rock shelves, kelp streaming in the current, mussels blackening the walls, turquoise water moving through the gap. The dynamic, flowing intertidal rather than the still pool.

The Kelp Canopy mix (Undersea Green + Phthalo Green BS + Ivory Black) for the streaming kelp. Phthalo Blue (Red Shade) for the deeper, moving water in the channel. Mayan Blue Genuine for the mineral quality of water against rock. Ivory Black for the mussel beds coating the walls.

  • Paint the kelp fronds with long, flowing strokes of Undersea Green on damp paper — every stroke should curve in the same direction to suggest current
  • The water in the channel grades from Cobalt Teal Blue where shallow over rock to Phthalo Blue (Red Shade) in the deepest center — the cool shift suggests depth and movement
  • Mussel beds on the channel walls are Ivory Black applied as dense clusters with dry-brush texture — mussels pack together so tightly they read as a rough, dark mass
  • Drop Mayan Blue Genuine into the water where it meets rock while damp — its mineral granulation creates the visual turbulence of water disturbed by stone edges
Prompt 4

Sea Star and Nudibranch on Rock

Subject: An ochre sea star and a sea lemon nudibranch sharing a rock face at low tide. Orange and yellow against dark wet basalt — two vivid animals in an improbable still life that the tide assembled and will disassemble in six hours.

Cadmium Orange Hue for the sea star’s vivid orange. Lemon Yellow for the nudibranch. Ivory Black for the wet rock. The simplicity of this scene — two creatures, one rock — lets you push the chromatic intensity of each pigment to maximum.

  • The rock face is Ivory Black with a wet sheen suggested by leaving a thin strip of white paper along the top edge where light catches the water film
  • Cadmium Orange Hue at full concentration for the sea star — no dilution. Ochre stars are genuinely this vivid, and the opaque cadmium reads correctly against dark rock
  • Lemon Yellow for the sea lemon nudibranch, with tiny dots of Ivory Black for the tubercles (raised bumps) that cover its body — these details are what make it identifiable as a specific species
  • Let the two creatures almost touch but not quite — the negative space between the orange arm and the yellow body is where the composition lives
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Phthalo Green Blue Shade (PG7) is one of the highest-chroma transparent greens available — use it for water depth and translucent kelp, not for opaque subjects. Cobalt Teal Blue (PG50) is opaque and granulating, ideal for shallow water effects. Deep Scarlet (PR175) is a warm, rich red with good transparency for anemone columns. Mayan Blue Genuine is a Primatek pigment — ground mineral stone — whose granulation creates water-on-rock effects no synthetic blue can match. Opera Pink (BV10:1) is fugitive under UV but irreplaceable for the fluorescent quality of certain marine organisms.
