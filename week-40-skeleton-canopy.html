Week 40 — PNW Watercolor Series

Skeleton Canopy

November strips the pretense. The maples have dropped most of their leaves and now you can see the architecture — the bones of the canopy, every fork and reach and broken limb. A few leaves hang on, crimson and gold holdouts rattling in the wind. More sky shows through every day, cold and pale. The branches are wet and black. The whole forest is becoming a drawing of itself.

Primary Trio

Raw Umber

Bare branch architecture in dry weather; cool, muted brown with a green undertone, the color of dormant wood before rain darkens it

Alizarin Crimson

The last clinging red leaves, translucent against gray sky; cool-toned red with a blue bias, the crimson of leaves that refuse to fall

Yellow Ochre

Last gold leaves and dried grass below; opaque, earthy gold that reads as autumn exhaustion rather than autumn glory

Mixes & Ratios

Wet Branch

2 Raw Umber : 1 Lamp Black : ¼ Payne’s Gray

Rain-darkened branch against gray sky — the limb almost black but still reading as wood, not shadow.

Raw Umber
Lamp Black
Payne’s Gray
Raw Umber is the branch’s identity — even when soaked dark, it keeps the mix reading as wood rather than abstract darkness. Its cool green undertone is exactly right for PNW deciduous bark. Lamp Black pushes the value down to where rain-wet branches actually sit — nearly black but with substance and surface. Payne’s Gray at the edges adds the blue-gray atmospheric haze that wraps around wet wood in November air, the branches not just dark but cold. Use a rigger brush loaded with this mix to draw branches directly — vary pressure to go from thick at the fork to thin at the tip. The mix should be thick enough to hold a calligraphic line but wet enough to bloom slightly at the nodes where branches join.

Last Leaf

2 Quinacridone Gold : 1 Alizarin Crimson : ½ Yellow Ochre

The few leaves still hanging — amber going crimson at the edges, curling but not yet fallen, backlit against bare branches.

Quin. Gold
Alizarin Crimson
Yellow Ochre
Quinacridone Gold is the base — a transparent amber that lets light through the way a real backlit leaf does. Its transparency is essential: these are the last leaves, thin and papery, and they need to glow rather than sit flat. Alizarin Crimson bleeds into the edges and tips where the leaf is dying — the cool red shifts the amber toward the bruised crimson of a leaf that’s been holding on too long. Yellow Ochre adds body and opacity at the stem end, where the leaf is thickest and most connected to the branch. Paint the leaf shape with Quinacridone Gold first, then touch Alizarin Crimson into the edges while still wet and let it creep inward. The bloom pattern will mimic the way color change radiates from a leaf’s margins.

November Sky

2 Neutral Tint : 1 French Ultramarine : ¼ Payne’s Gray

The cold, pale sky that shows through bare branches — not quite blue, not quite gray, the color of a season giving up.

Neutral Tint
French Ultramarine
Payne’s Gray
Neutral Tint is the dominant note — November sky in the PNW is gray first, blue second, and this mix respects that. At full dilution it gives you the flat, featureless overcast that is the default ceiling from October to March. French Ultramarine adds just enough blue to keep the sky from going dead neutral — there is always a memory of blue up there, even on the grayest days. Payne’s Gray at the horizon line adds the blue-gray density where sky meets treeline. Apply as a graded wash — slightly more concentrated at the top, thinning as you come down. The sky needs to recede behind the branch network, so keep it light enough that even dark Wet Branch lines read clearly against it.

Why These Colors?

This palette is about revelation — the tree’s structure emerging as decoration falls away. Raw Umber and Burnt Sienna handle the branch architecture that is the real subject this week: the bones of the forest, every fork and angle suddenly visible. Alizarin Crimson and Yellow Ochre are the holdout leaves, the last color still attached to the skeleton. They’re primaries here because they define the tension of the season — the branches want to be bare, the leaves refuse. Quinacridone Gold captures the amber warmth of leaves backlit against gray sky, glowing rather than just hanging. Hansa Yellow Light is the single bright holdout, the one leaf that somehow stayed vivid when everything around it faded. French Ultramarine is the cold sky now visible through the canopy — more sky every day as more leaves drop, the blue negative space becoming as important as the branches. Payne’s Gray and Neutral Tint are November itself: the gray air, the overcast, the atmosphere that turns the forest into a pencil drawing. Lamp Black earns its place specifically for wet bark — rain-soaked branches go nearly black, and no brown or gray can reach that depth while still reading as wood.

Painting Prompts

The forest is becoming a drawing. These subjects lean into the graphic quality of bare branches against sky, with the last leaves as accents of color in an increasingly monochrome world.

Prompt 1

Looking Up Through Bare Maple

Subject: Standing directly beneath a bigleaf maple, looking straight up. The trunk rises from the bottom of the frame and the branches radiate outward like a cathedral ceiling. A handful of yellow and red leaves cling to the outermost twigs. Cold sky fills the negative space between branches.

The Wet Branch mix (Raw Umber + Lamp Black + Payne’s Gray) builds the entire branch structure. The November Sky mix fills the negative spaces. The Last Leaf mix (Quinacridone Gold + Alizarin Crimson + Yellow Ochre) provides scattered accents of color among the bare limbs.

  • Paint the sky first with the November Sky mix as a flat wash across the entire page — the sky is the background and needs to be fully dry before branches go on top
  • Build branches from thick to thin using the Wet Branch mix on a rigger brush — start at the trunk (thickest, darkest) and work outward, reducing pressure and pigment load as branches get smaller
  • The last leaves are small touches of the Last Leaf mix placed at the very tips of branches — keep them sparse, maybe eight or ten across the entire composition. Each one should feel like a small event
  • Leave some branches with a thin white edge on the sky-facing side by painting slightly inside the branch shape — this suggests backlighting and separates dark branches from dark sky
Prompt 2

Branch Detail — Last Three Leaves

Subject: A single branch crossing the frame diagonally, with three leaves still attached at the end. One is Alizarin Crimson, one is Quinacridone Gold, one is half-gone and curled. The branch is wet. The background is soft gray nothing.

Burnt Sienna and Lamp Black for the wet branch. Each leaf gets its own color from the palette: pure Alizarin Crimson, pure Quinacridone Gold, and the faded one is Yellow Ochre going brown with a Burnt Sienna edge. Neutral Tint for the soft background.

  • The background is Neutral Tint at extreme dilution, applied wet and left to dry with soft, formless edges — no sky detail, just gray atmosphere that pushes the branch forward
  • Paint the branch with Burnt Sienna first, then drop Lamp Black into the underside while still wet — the two-tone effect reads as round, wet wood catching diffuse overhead light
  • Each leaf is a single wet stroke of its assigned color — Alizarin Crimson for the red one, Quinacridone Gold for the amber one. Keep them simple and let the pigment do the work
  • The curled, half-gone leaf is the most complex: Yellow Ochre for the remaining flat surface, Burnt Sienna where the edge has died and curled inward, white paper where the leaf has broken away entirely
Prompt 3

Forest Edge in November Rain

Subject: A row of deciduous trees at a field’s edge, mostly bare with a few holdout leaves. Rain is falling. The trees are dark wet silhouettes against a pale gray sky. Fallen leaves carpet the ground in muted golds and reds. A fence line or path runs along the base.

The full dark side of this palette: Lamp Black and Payne’s Gray for wet tree silhouettes, Neutral Tint for the rain-sky, Yellow Ochre and Alizarin Crimson diluted for the leaf carpet on the ground. Raw Umber for the fence or path.

  • The sky is Neutral Tint in a smooth graded wash, slightly darker at the top — leave some areas barely tinted to suggest where the overcast thins, but keep it uniformly gray. This is not a dramatic sky
  • The tree silhouettes are Lamp Black with Payne’s Gray, applied while the sky is still slightly damp at the treeline so the tops of the trees bleed softly into the sky — rain-blurred edges
  • The ground leaf carpet is Yellow Ochre and dilute Alizarin Crimson dropped randomly into a wet wash of Raw Umber — let the colors mingle and blur. Individual leaves are not the point; the impression of color on the ground is
  • Rain streaks can be lifted with a damp flat brush dragged vertically through the sky area before it fully dries — subtle, pale lines that suggest falling water without being literal
Prompt 4

Single Bright Leaf on Wet Pavement

Subject: One vivid Hansa Yellow Light leaf lying on dark wet pavement or a wooden boardwalk. Rain droplets surround it. The leaf is the only color in the frame — everything else is gray, black, and wet. Intimate, close-up, almost abstract.

Hansa Yellow Light is the star — the brightest pigment in the palette, isolated against Lamp Black and Payne’s Gray. This is the prompt where that single accent earns its place in the palette. Neutral Tint for reflected sky in wet pavement.

  • The wet pavement is Lamp Black at mid-value with Payne’s Gray streaked through — apply to wet paper and let the two pigments separate slightly, creating the mottled surface of wet asphalt or dark wood
  • Leave small circular white-paper spots for rain droplets before painting the pavement — use masking fluid or careful negative painting. These highlights sell the wetness
  • The leaf is Hansa Yellow Light at near-full strength, a small bright shape against all that dark — paint it crisply with dry-brush edges so it sits on top of the surface rather than sinking in
  • Add the leaf’s reflection directly below it with Hansa Yellow Light at half strength, slightly blurred — wet pavement mirrors, and the double-yellow reads as both leaf and surface in one stroke
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Raw Umber (PBr7) has a characteristic cool, greenish undertone that distinguishes it from warmer browns — ideal for dormant wood. Alizarin Crimson (PR83) is a cool-biased red that reads as autumn rather than summer. Quinacridone Gold (PO49) is one of Daniel Smith’s most beloved pigments — a transparent amber with beautiful granulation. Lamp Black (PBk6) is denser and more opaque than other blacks, specifically suited for the near-black of rain-soaked bark. Hansa Yellow Light (PY3) is the brightest yellow in the line, used here as a single vivid accent against November’s gray.
