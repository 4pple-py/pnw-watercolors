Skeleton Canopy

Late October, 5:45 PM. Alder branches claw a sulfur sky as the last light drains west. Licorice fern unfurls electric green on bark the rain just darkened. Decay feeds resurgence, and the forest is trading its ceiling for a floor.

Quinacridone Gold

Last maple leaves catching low sun — transparent, warm, the final act before the branches go bare

Raw Umber

Wet branch skeleton against cloud — granulating, honest, the architecture revealed when leaves leave

Sap Green

Fern colonies claiming bare limbs — licorice fern unfurling electric green on rain-darkened bark

Mixes & Ratios

Slug Trail

2 Raw Umber : 1 Buff Titanium : touch Sap Green

Silvered mucus line across dark duff — banana slug passage, glistening and deliberate.

Raw Umber
Buff Titanium
Sap Green
Lay Raw Umber wet, drop Buff Titanium into the center while damp, barely touch one edge with Sap Green. Let it find its own borders. The Buff Titanium lifts the value just enough to read as silvered mucus against the dark forest floor. Don’t overwork it — slug trails are single-pass events.

Fern Bark

1 Sap Green : 2 Raw Umber

Licorice fern rhizome fused to wet alder — where the plant and the tree stop being separate things.

Sap Green
Raw Umber
Mix on palette first to a warm olive. Apply in a single confident stroke — the rhizome is one continuous line along the branch. Raw Umber’s granulation gives bark-like texture without any additional technique. This is the junction where fern becomes tree becomes fern again.

October Drain

2 Neutral Tint : 1 Cerulean Blue Chromium

Sky five minutes after useful light — the last color draining west, leaving only weight behind.

Neutral Tint
Cerulean Blue Cr.
Cerulean Blue Chromium granulates heavily, settling into textured darks that read as clouds with internal structure. Apply wet and let it pool. The Neutral Tint kills the chroma enough that this reads as dusk rather than twilight — the distinction matters. Let the edges dry unevenly.

Why These Colors?

This palette is engineered around transparency and granulation — the two properties that matter most when painting bare architecture and the things growing on it. Quinacridone Gold and Sap Green are highly transparent stainers that layer without muddying, essential for the glazing this week’s subjects demand. Raw Umber and Burnt Sienna granulate into the paper’s texture, providing bark and leaf-litter effects without brushwork tricks. Neutral Tint provides depth without color bias — pure value, no opinion. Cerulean Blue Chromium’s genuine opacity gives the thin sky between branches real physical presence against the transparent layers beneath. Buff Titanium lifts the overcast glow — warm enough to read as light, opaque enough to sit on top of dried washes.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Licorice Fern Colonnade

Subject: Alder trunks with licorice ferns erupting from the bark, backlit by the last warm light filtering through the thinning canopy.

The Fern Bark mix (Sap Green 1:Raw Umber 2) builds the junction where fern and tree merge, while Quinacridone Gold and Buff Titanium handle the backlit glow.

  • Graded wash of Quinacridone Gold fading to Buff Titanium for the backlit sky behind the trunks
  • Build trunks with Raw Umber — vertical, confident, letting granulation provide bark texture
  • Drop Sap Green wet-into-wet onto trunk shapes where ferns colonize
  • Cerulean Blue Chromium in negative spaces between branches for sky presence
Prompt 2

Leaf Litter Trail

Subject: A muddy trail disappearing into a tunnel of bare branches, ankle-deep in decomposing leaves, one vine maple still burning orange.

Quinacridone Gold, Burnt Sienna, and Raw Sienna dropped loosely onto wet paper build the leaf litter — Transparent Pyrrol Orange reserved for the one deliberate vine maple accent.

  • Wet paper, drop Quinacridone Gold, Burnt Sienna, Raw Sienna loosely — let them mingle
  • Neutral Tint with Raw Umber for branch architecture overhead
  • Save Transparent Pyrrol Orange for one deliberate leaf or vine maple — the accent that proves the rest is muted
  • Leave white paper showing through canopy gaps for sky
Prompt 3

Slug on Nurse Log

Subject: A banana slug crossing a decomposing nurse log — moss textures, silvered trail, the slow commerce of the forest floor.

The Slug Trail mix (Raw Umber 2:Buff Titanium 1:Sap Green touch) builds the mucus line, while the Fern Bark mix handles the log itself.

  • Paint the log first using Fern Bark mix — single confident stroke for the main form
  • Build moss textures by stippling Sap Green and Hooker’s Green while the log is still damp
  • Paint the slug using Slug Trail mix — Buff Titanium center, Raw Umber edges
  • Add Quinacridone Gold as a thin glaze for sidelight catching the slug’s wet surface
Prompt 4

Skeleton Canopy Against Dusk

Subject: Looking straight up through bare branches into a transitioning sky — warm gold at the horizon draining to cold gray overhead, branch architecture in silhouette.

The October Drain mix (Neutral Tint 2:Cerulean Blue Chromium 1) handles the cold upper sky, while Quinacridone Gold provides the warm lower glow — the graded wash is the painting.

  • Work sky first as graded wash: Quinacridone Gold at bottom transitioning to October Drain mix at top
  • Let sky dry completely before painting branches — patience is structural here
  • Raw Umber for thick limbs, Neutral Tint for the finest twigs
  • Resist adding leaves — the skeleton is the point, and any foliage undermines it
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. Quinacridone Gold is discontinued in some lines — stock accordingly if you find it. Cerulean Blue Chromium is genuinely opaque and sits on top of dried layers rather than sinking in, which makes it ideal for painting sky gaps over established branch work. Neutral Tint is a convenience mix that darkens without shifting temperature — it goes where you point it without adding an opinion.
