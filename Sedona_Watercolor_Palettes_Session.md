# Sedona, Arizona Watercolor Palettes — 12 Months

A complete set of twelve 10-color watercolor palettes representing each month of the year in Sedona, Arizona, built exclusively from Daniel Smith watercolors. Each palette includes a primary trio, a full 10-color table, three mixes with ratios, a color-choice explanation, and notes.

## 🎨 January — Cold Fire Mesa

**Mood:** Stark, luminous, and deceptively cold — January in Sedona is the desert pretending to be humble. The red rock buttes blaze their deepest iron-oxide crimson under a winter sun that hangs low and rakes everything with long golden light, but the air bites, frost silvers the sage at dawn, and snow dusts the rimrock of the Mogollon like powdered sugar on terracotta. Oak Creek runs clear and steel-cold between bare cottonwoods whose white trunks glow against ruddy cliffs. Junipers hold dark and stoic on every slope, and the sky — the January sky — is an obscene, depthless cobalt that no one believes until they see it. This is a palette of warm earth against cold air, of fire-colored stone under ice-blue heaven.

### Primary Trio

A warm-dominant split-primary set built to handle Sedona's defining tension — incandescent warm rock against crystalline cool sky — with enough range to reach the dark junipers and pale frost between them.

- 🔴 **Burnt Sienna** — a warm, granulating red-brown; the single most essential Sedona pigment, mapping directly onto the iron-oxide sandstone that defines every view
- 💛 **Raw Sienna** — a warm, transparent golden ochre; captures the sunlit face of rock and the dry winter grass without the opacity that would kill luminosity in washes
- 🔵 **Cobalt Blue** — a true-neutral granulating blue; matches the specific crisp, clean quality of high-desert winter sky and mixes soft violets for shadow without overwhelming the reds

### Mixes & Ratios

**1. 🪨 Cathedral Rock Red**
**Ratio:** 3 Burnt Sienna : 1 Perylene Maroon : ½ Quinacridone Gold
*The iconic Sedona red — not orange, not brown, but that specific deep iron-oxide crimson that glows as if the rock itself is generating warmth*

| Color | Parts |
|-------|-------|
| Burnt Sienna | 3 |
| Perylene Maroon | 1 |
| Quinacridone Gold | ½ |

> Burnt Sienna is already close to the real rock color, but on its own it reads too generically earthy. The Perylene Maroon deepens and cools it just enough to capture the blood-red quality of shadowed sandstone faces, while the Quinacridone Gold pulls the sunlit areas toward that warm glow you see when afternoon light hits Cathedral Rock head-on. Vary the Perylene Maroon for shadow versus sun — more for the north-facing sides, less for surfaces catching direct light. Let the granulation of Burnt Sienna do the work of suggesting rock texture.

**2. 🔷 January Zenith**
**Ratio:** 3 Cobalt Blue : 1 French Ultramarine : ¼ Buff Titanium
*The high-desert winter sky at midday — deeper and more saturated than any coastal sky, with a clarity that comes from 4,500 feet of dry air and no humidity to diffuse the light*

| Color | Parts |
|-------|-------|
| Cobalt Blue | 3 |
| French Ultramarine | 1 |
| Buff Titanium | ¼ |

> Cobalt Blue alone is close but too flat — the French Ultramarine adds a violet warmth near the zenith that the real sky has, and its granulation mingles with the Cobalt's to create a sky that breathes on the paper rather than sitting dead. The whisper of Buff Titanium softens the lower sky toward the horizon where desert haze and distance wash things out. Apply in a single graded wash, more Ultramarine at the top, more Buff Titanium as you approach the mesa line.

**3. 🌲 Juniper Slope**
**Ratio:** 3 Undersea Green : 1 Payne's Gray : 1 Burnt Sienna
*The dark, dusty blue-green of juniper and pinyon covering Sedona's red hillsides — the only green in January, muted and tenacious*

| Color | Parts |
|-------|-------|
| Undersea Green | 3 |
| Payne's Gray | 1 |
| Burnt Sienna | 1 |

> Undersea Green has the right blue-gray undertone for desert conifers — nothing tropical or lush about it. The Payne's Gray darkens without deadening, and the Burnt Sienna is the critical ingredient: it warms and dulls the green into the dusty, reddish-tinged olive that juniper actually reads as against red rock. Without that Burnt Sienna you get Pacific Northwest conifers, not desert ones. Dab with a dry brush for individual tree texture; use a wet wash for distant hillside coverage.

### Why These Colors?

Sedona in January is a study in warm-cool extremes — the landscape is dominated by iron-oxide reds and golds that would read as autumn anywhere else, but the sky, the shadows, and the frost are genuinely cold. I anchored this palette on Burnt Sienna and Raw Sienna because those two pigments together cover the full value range of sunlit sandstone, from pale gold trail dust to deep red cliff face, and both granulate in ways that mimic real rock texture without any effort. Cobalt Blue was the only choice for the sky — Phthalo is too aggressive, Cerulean too chalky, and Ultramarine too violet for the primary sky wash, though French Ultramarine earns its spot as a secondary for shadow work and the deeper zenith. Perylene Maroon is the key to getting Sedona's reds right rather than generic: it pushes Burnt Sienna toward the blood-red that photographs never quite capture. Undersea Green handles the junipers perfectly because it's already a gray-green that belongs in arid landscapes. Monte Amiata Natural Sienna fills the gap between Raw Sienna and Burnt Sienna with a rosy warmth specific to certain sandstone layers. Quinacridone Gold catches the quality of low winter sun on west-facing buttes at golden hour — transparent enough to glaze over dried red washes and make them glow. Buff Titanium and Payne's Gray handle the frost-and-shadow extremes without pulling the palette away from its warm center of gravity.

### Notes

> This palette leans heavily warm by design — January in Sedona is cold in temperature but visually dominated by warm rock and warm light, so the cool colors serve as supporting players rather than leads. The granulation of Burnt Sienna, Cobalt Blue, and French Ultramarine gives you rock and sky texture for free. Every color here mixes cleanly with every other because the palette avoids clashing staining pigments — the reds and blues play well together for shadow purples, and the greens are muted enough to sit naturally against the dominant reds. ## 🎨 February — Thaw on Red Earth

**Mood:** Tentative, brightening, and restless — February in Sedona is winter losing its grip but not yet surrendering. Mornings still crack with frost and the north-facing canyon walls stay in cold blue shadow until noon, but by afternoon the sun is warm enough to sit in and the light has shifted from January's low rake to something more generous, more gold. The first brittlebush shows gray-green rosettes pushing out of rocky slopes. Manzanita bark turns the color of polished mahogany. On rare storm days, snow settles on Bell Rock like a dusting of confectioner's sugar on a rust-red cake, and the contrast is so absurd it looks painted. Oak Creek is at its coldest and clearest, running turquoise over red-brown cobbles. This palette holds the pivot between winter's severity and the first whisper of desert spring.

### Primary Trio

A warmer shift from January's set — still anchored in earth tones but opening toward the brighter, more golden light that February brings to the red rock.

- 🔴 **Quinacridone Burnt Orange** — a transparent, fiery red-orange; captures the way Sedona's rock glows in strengthening February sun without the opacity of cadmiums
- 💛 **Raw Sienna** — a warm, transparent golden ochre; the continuity color from January, still essential for every sandstone value
- 🔵 **Cobalt Blue** — a true-neutral granulating blue; February's sky is just as crisp as January's but slightly warmer at the edges

**2. 🪨 Afternoon Butte**
**Ratio:** 2 Quinacridone Burnt Orange : 2 Raw Sienna : 1 Burnt Sienna

Raw Sienna opens it up toward gold for the sunlit planes, and Burnt Sienna anchors the mid-shadows. Glaze this over a dried Cobalt Blue underpainting for shadow areas that glow with complementary warmth.

**3. 💎 Oak Creek Turquoise**
**Ratio:** 3 Cobalt Blue : 1 Undersea Green : ½ Buff Titanium

The Buff Titanium lifts the value for shallow riffles and adds a hint of milkiness. Paint the creek bed first in dilute Burnt Sienna, let it dry, then wash this mix over it and the red shows through for the cobble effect.

### Why These Colors?

February in Sedona is January with the brightness turned up a notch — the same red rock dominance, but the light is more generous and the contrasts softer. I swapped Perylene Maroon for Quinacridone Burnt Orange as the primary red because February's rock reads more orange-warm than blood-dark, and the transparency of Quinacridone Burnt Orange lets you build luminous glazes that capture strengthening sunlight. Potter's Pink and Lunar Violet are the February-specific picks: Potter's Pink gives you the granulating pink-gray of snow reflecting off red rock and distant mesa haze, while Lunar Violet handles the cool blue-gray shadows that linger in canyon walls. Yellow Ochre fills the gap for drier, more opaque earth tones — trail dust, dormant grass, sandstone faces in flat light. Indigo replaces Payne's Gray for a warmer, richer dark that leans blue rather than neutral, which suits the deeper canyon shadows in February's slightly warmer light. The Undersea Green and Buff Titanium carry over from January because junipers and cottonwood bark don't change with the calendar.

### Notes

> This palette is a half-step warmer and brighter than January's — think of it as the same landscape with the sun five degrees higher and the air five degrees warmer. The granulating trio of Cobalt Blue, Potter's Pink, and Lunar Violet gives you atmospheric effects on autopilot, and the transparent Quinacridone Burnt Orange means you can glaze over dried washes without lifting them. All ten colors are from your Daniel Smith collection.

## 🎨 March — Brittlebush Gold

**Mood:** Bright, awakening, and suddenly vivid — March is when Sedona stops being a study in red and blue and starts throwing gold into the equation. Brittlebush erupts across every south-facing slope in clouds of chrome yellow, globe mallow unfurls tissue-paper orange cups along roadsides, and the barrel cacti put out improbable crowns of waxy red buds. The light has shifted decisively toward spring — warm, directional, and strong enough to bleach the tops of buttes while leaving canyons in violet shadow. Cottonwoods along Oak Creek are still bare but their buds are swelling red-brown, and the creek itself runs high with snowmelt from the Mogollon Rim. The air smells like sage and warm rock. This palette is built for the collision of wildflower gold against Sedona red.

### Primary Trio

A high-chroma set tuned for the yellow-against-red drama of early wildflower season — bright enough for brittlebush gold, warm enough for sunstruck sandstone, and blue enough for the sky that frames everything.

- 🔴 **Pyrrol Orange** — a clean, powerful warm orange; captures globe mallow and the orange face of sunlit sandstone without muddying
- 💛 **New Gamboge** — a warm, transparent golden yellow; the brittlebush color straight from the tube, luminous enough for wildflower washes
- 🔵 **Cerulean Blue, Chromium** — a smooth, opaque sky blue; March's sky is slightly hazier than deep winter, and Cerulean captures that milky warmth at the horizon

### Why These Colors?

March demands chroma. Cerulean Blue, Chromium replaces Cobalt Blue because March's sky has shifted: it's slightly hazier, slightly milkier near the horizon as temperatures rise and thermals begin, and Cerulean captures that quality. French Ultramarine stays for the zenith and shadow work. Sap Green arrives for the first time because March has actual living green in it — new sage growth, brittlebush foliage, irrigated gardens. Quinacridone Red handles the first cactus blooms and the penstemon and paintbrush starting along creek banks. Burnt Sienna and Raw Sienna are Sedona-permanent — you'll never paint this landscape without them.

### Notes

> This is the first high-chroma palette in the Sedona series — January and February were studies in warm earth against cool sky, but March introduces real color competition between wildflowers and rock. The transparency of New Gamboge and Pyrrol Orange is essential because you'll be glazing them over dried rock washes, and opaque yellows would turn to mud. All ten colors are from your Daniel Smith collection.

## 🎨 April — Prickly Pear Bloom

**Mood:** Hot-bright, saturated, and extravagant — April in Sedona is the desert pulling out every color it owns. Prickly pear cacti erupt in waxy magenta, fuchsia, and hot pink blooms that look like they belong in a tropical greenhouse, not pinned to a gray-green pad on a red hillside. Claret cup cacti blaze scarlet in rock crevices. Globe mallow is still going, brittlebush is fading to seed, and the ocotillo — finally — has unfurled its tiny green leaves and tipped each whip-thin branch with a torch of red-orange. The light is intense and high now, flattening shadows at midday but painting everything in warm gold at the edges of the day. Temperatures push toward 80°F and the rock radiates stored heat well past sunset. This palette is built for the improbable clash of hot-pink cactus blooms against iron-red rock under crystalline sky.

### Primary Trio

A high-chroma, warm-dominant trio with enough pink firepower for cactus blooms and enough earth to keep the rock honest.

- 🔴 **Quinacridone Magenta** — a cool, transparent, blazing pink-red; the closest tube color to prickly pear bloom, mixes clean violets and hot pinks
- 💛 **Quinacridone Gold** — a warm, transparent amber-gold; richer and deeper than New Gamboge, matching April's more intense sunlight and aging wildflowers
- 🔵 **Cobalt Blue** — a true-neutral granulating blue; April's sky is still pristine before summer haze, and Cobalt keeps it honest

## 🎨 May — Furnace Light

**Mood:** Blazing, stripped, and monumental — May in Sedona is the landscape boiled down to its essentials. The wildflowers are spent and gone, the greens are retreating to juniper and irrigated gardens, and the rock reasserts total dominance. The sun is high and merciless, flattening everything at midday into a near-white glare on the upper faces of buttes while the shadows go ink-dark and violet. But the magic hours — the first and last two hours of daylight — are the most dramatic of the year: the low-angle light turns the red rock into something that genuinely appears to be on fire, a deep orange-red that photographs can't capture and painters live for. Desert varnish — the dark manganese-oxide streaks on cliff faces — reads almost black against the blazing stone. This palette is built for extreme light and deep shadow on monochrome red earth.

### Primary Trio

A high-contrast, warm-biased set designed for May's defining quality: the widest value range of any month, from white-hot sunlit rock to near-black shadow.

- 🔴 **Quinacridone Burnt Orange** — a transparent, fiery orange-red; captures the specific furnace glow of low-angle light on sandstone
- 💛 **Monte Amiata Natural Sienna** — a warm, granulating rosy earth; the natural mid-tone of Sedona sandstone in flat light, with built-in texture
- 🔵 **French Ultramarine** — a warm-leaning granulating blue; May's shadows are distinctly violet, and Ultramarine mixes the best warm-shadow purples

**2. 🖤 Desert Varnish**

## 🎨 June — Heat Shimmer

**Mood:** Relentless, bleached, and taut — June in Sedona is the desert holding its breath before the monsoon. ## 🎨 July — Monsoon Thunder

**Mood:** Violent, electric, and transformed — July in Sedona is when the sky finally breaks. *The bigtooth maples in Oak Creek Canyon at peak color — red shading to orange to gold, the full autumn spectrum in a single tree*

| Color | Parts |
|-------|-------|
| Pyrrol Scarlet | 3 |
| Quinacridone Burnt Orange | 1 |
| New Gamboge | 1 |

> Bigtooth maples don't turn a single color — they run through the full warm spectrum, sometimes on a single leaf. Start with a New Gamboge wash for the overall canopy, then charge Quinacridone Burnt Orange into the wet paint for the mid-tones, and finally touch Pyrrol Scarlet into the most advanced areas. Let them mingle on the paper — the bleeding and mingling IS the foliage effect. Every tree is different; vary the proportions wildly.

**3. 🏔️ Canyon Corridor**
**Ratio:** 3 Indigo : 1 Sap Green : ½ Burnt Sienna
*The dark, cool backdrop of the canyon walls behind the flaming foliage — Douglas fir, ponderosa pine, and shadowed red rock forming a receding mass of blue-green dark*

| Color | Parts |
|-------|-------|
| Indigo | 3 |
| Sap Green | 1 |
| Burnt Sienna | ½ |

> The autumn foliage is the star, but it only works against a properly dark backdrop. This mix gives you the fir-and-pine mass that lines Oak Creek Canyon above the deciduous trees — dark, blue-green, and slightly warm from the red rock showing between trunks. Indigo provides the depth, Sap Green the life, and Burnt Sienna the connection to the red-rock landscape. Keep it loose and subordinate — this mix exists to make the gold and red sing.

### Why These Colors?

October is the month where Sedona's palette most closely mirrors a New England autumn — but it's happening against red rock instead of gray granite, and the contrast is extraordinary. New Gamboge and Quinacridone Gold together cover the full range of cottonwood foliage from peak gold to deep amber. Pyrrol Scarlet handles the bigtooth maples and the red end of the turning spectrum — it's warm enough to read as foliage rather than rock, which matters in a landscape where everything red could be geology. Quinacridone Burnt Orange bridges the gap between gold and red. Indigo replaces Payne's Gray for the canyon darks because it has the blue-green lean that evergreen forest backgrounds need. Sap Green stays for the persistent greenery — junipers, oaks still holding, canyon fir. Perylene Maroon handles the deep darks and the finished, brown-maroon leaves on the ground.

### Notes

> October is the postcard palette — the one that sells calendars and fills Instagram. But it earns its drama honestly: the yellow-gold foliage against red rock against blue sky is genuinely that vivid, and the transparency of New Gamboge and Quinacridone Gold lets you capture the backlit quality that makes it magical. ## 🎨 November — Bare Bones

**Mood:** Quiet, stripped, and luminous — November in Sedona is October's fire burning down to embers. The cottonwoods have dropped most of their leaves, revealing white-bark skeletons against red cliffs. A few trees still hold gold at the tips, and the carpet of fallen leaves along Oak Creek is a patchwork of amber and brown. The crowds have thinned. The light is low and long all day now, the sun never climbing high enough to flatten the landscape, so every hour has the quality of golden hour. The first hard frosts silver the canyon floor, and the rock takes on a deeper, more somber red as if settling in for winter. Junipers and cypresses stand out sharply as the only living green. This palette is about the quiet beauty of the landscape with its decorations stripped — just rock, bark, earth, and sky.

### Primary Trio

A muted, warm-dominant set for the subdued beauty of late autumn — lower chroma than October, deeper values, and more earth than fire.

- 🔴 **Burnt Sienna** — a warm, granulating red-brown; November rock in its most honest state, neither bleached by summer nor lit by golden-hour drama
- 💛 **Yellow Ochre** — an opaque, earthy yellow; fallen leaves, dry grass, the dusty warmth of late-autumn trails
- 🔵 **Cobalt Blue** — a true-neutral granulating blue; November's sky is still clear and deep but cooler-toned, winter approaching

The Burnt Sienna pulls it toward the rock tones, and the Buff Titanium lifts the value for the general atmosphere of warm light filtering through dry air. **2. 🪵 Bare Cottonwood Skeleton**

### Why These Colors?

November needs earth tones more than fire tones. Raw Umber enters the series for the first time to handle bare bark, wet leaf litter, and the brown end of the earth-tone spectrum that Burnt Sienna doesn't reach. Monte Amiata Natural Sienna fills the mid-earth range with its beautiful granulation. Undersea Green is the sole green representative — November's living vegetation is limited to the evergreens. French Ultramarine and Payne's Gray handle the cool shadows and darks, and Buff Titanium is essential for the pale cottonwood bark that becomes a major visual element once the leaves are gone.

### Notes

It asks you to paint the landscape without any of its usual showmanship: no blazing sunsets, no wildflower drama, no storm theater. Just rock, bark, earth, and sky, lit by the most forgiving light of the year. All ten colors are from your Daniel Smith collection.

## 🎨 December — Solstice Red

**Mood:** Cold, clear, and elemental — December in Sedona returns the landscape to its fundamentals. The cottonwoods are bare, the grasses are dormant gold, the junipers hold their dark-green post, and the red rock reasserts itself as the total visual anchor. But December has a quality no other month shares: the sun is at its absolute lowest, and the light it throws is the warmest, most amber-gold of the year. For two hours at dawn and two hours at dusk, the buttes and mesas burn with a deep, concentrated red-orange that makes January's golden hour look pale. Snow returns to the rimrock and occasionally dusts the valley floor, and the contrast of fresh white against iron-red against deep blue is the defining image of Sedona winter. The air is glass-clear and cold, the stars are violent overhead, and at the solstice the sun sets behind Thunder Mountain in a stripe of molten copper. This palette closes the year where it began — with rock and sky and the extremes between them.

### Primary Trio

A deep, warm-anchored set for the darkest month's extraordinary light — maximum warmth at the edges of the day, maximum cool in the shadows and sky.

- 🔴 **Burnt Sienna** — a warm, granulating red-brown; the year ends where it began, with Sedona's essential pigment
- 💛 **Raw Sienna** — a warm, transparent golden ochre; December's low sun turns everything it touches to gold, and Raw Sienna captures that quality
- 🔵 **French Ultramarine** — a warm-leaning granulating blue; December shadows are long, deep, and violet-toned, and Ultramarine mixes the best shadow purples

> December's solstice sunset is the most dramatic lighting event in Sedona's year — the sun is so low and the light passes through so much atmosphere that it arrives at the rock face as pure amber-red. The Quinacridone Burnt Orange is the fire — transparent, glowing, almost molten. Burnt Sienna provides the rock-specific red beneath it, and Raw Sienna softens the edges where direct light transitions to reflected. Glaze this over a completely dried painting in one courageous wash — speed and confidence matter because you can't rework transparent glazes.

Buff Titanium provides the base value, Moonglow adds the pink-violet warmth for reflected red-rock light (its granulation separates into warm and cool components that mimic this effect perfectly), and the French Ultramarine cools the shadow side. Leave pure white paper only for the most directly sunlit snow edges.

### Why These Colors?

December comes full circle — Burnt Sienna and Raw Sienna anchor the palette just as they did in January, because the fundamental Sedona truth is iron-oxide rock under desert sky and that doesn't change with the calendar. But the blue shifts from Cobalt to French Ultramarine as the primary because December's long shadows are distinctly violet, not neutral, and you need Ultramarine's warm lean to get that right. Moonglow returns for the first time since July's storms — but now it's painting cold dawn mist and snow reflections instead of thunderheads. Quinacridone Burnt Orange takes the warm-accent slot for the most extreme golden-hour moments of the year. Perylene Maroon and Lamp Black handle the deep darks that December's low sun creates — the shadows are as dramatic as the highlights. Cobalt Blue stays as a secondary for the zenith sky, and Undersea Green does its year-round juniper duty. Buff Titanium earns its spot once again for the snow and frost that frame the red-rock drama.

### Notes

> December's palette is a mirror of January's — similar colors, similar concerns, but with the shadows leaning more violet (French Ultramarine instead of Cobalt Blue as primary) and Moonglow added for atmospheric effects. If January is about surviving winter, December is about celebrating its beauty. The solstice light is Sedona's finest, and this palette is tuned to capture it.
