Week 34 — PNW Watercolor Series

Petrichor

Three months of dry and suddenly the sky remembers what it’s for. The first drops hit sun-baked asphalt and dusty trail dirt and the smell rises like something buried coming back to life. Petrichor — the blood of stones. Every surface darkens by a full value in minutes. The earth exhales oils it has been holding all summer, and for one hour the whole Northwest smells like the word “relief” would smell if it had a body.

Primary Trio

Raw Umber

Wet earth at first contact; transparent, cool-leaning brown, the color of soil darkening as water finds it

French Ultramarine

The arriving rain sky; deep, granulating blue that separates into particles like rain separating from cloud

Burnt Sienna

Earth darkened by water; warm and transparent, the color soil turns when rain saturates it fully

Mixes & Ratios

Wet Earth Exhale

2 Raw Umber : 1 Burnt Sienna : ½ Transparent Brown Oxide

The smell made visible — earth going from bone-dry to rain-dark in real time, releasing summer.

Raw Umber
Burnt Sienna
Trans. Brown Oxide
Raw Umber is the structural earth — transparent, slightly cool, it reads as soil before it reads as color. Burnt Sienna warms the mix toward the ruddy clay subsoil that PNW rain exposes as it darkens the surface layers. Transparent Brown Oxide is the glazing agent: applied over the first two while they’re still damp, it deepens the value without adding opacity, replicating the way water darkens earth by filling air pockets between soil particles. The transparency of all three pigments is critical — opaque earths would read as paint on dirt, not as dirt getting wet. Apply Raw Umber as the first wash to dry paper, then immediately drop Burnt Sienna into the areas where clay shows through, and finally glaze Transparent Brown Oxide over everything while the surface is still damp. The wet-into-wet bleeding replicates rain spreading across soil.

First Rain Sky

2 French Ultramarine : 1 Payne’s Gray : ½ Indanthrone Blue

The sky that means it this time — not the teasing overcast of July, but the real thing arriving from the Pacific.

French Ultramarine
Payne’s Gray
Indanthrone Blue
French Ultramarine granulates heavily, and that granulation is the texture of rain clouds — the pigment particles separate on paper the way moisture separates in cloud formations, clusters and gaps and unevenness. Payne’s Gray neutralizes the blue toward the gray-violet of real PNW rain sky, which is never purely blue but always has that gray-lavender quality. Indanthrone Blue adds the weight — the darkest, most serious blue in the palette, it sinks into paper valleys and builds the heavy bottoms of cumulonimbus where the rain is actually falling. Apply to wet paper and tilt the board so the Indanthrone settles toward the bottom of the cloud mass. Let the French Ultramarine granulate undisturbed. The texture does the work.

Dry-to-Wet Edge

2 Raw Sienna : 1 Yellow Ochre : ¼ Sedona Genuine

The border where rain hasn’t reached yet — dry summer gold surrendering to the advancing dark of wet.

Raw Sienna
Yellow Ochre
Sedona Genuine
Raw Sienna is the dry earth baseline — warm, golden, the color the ground has been all summer. Yellow Ochre brightens it toward the sun-bleached grass and dusty trail surface that hasn’t been rained on yet. Sedona Genuine is the magic here: a PrimaTek pigment ground from actual sedimentary stone, it granulates with a mineral roughness that no synthetic pigment can replicate, reading as the gritty, mineral surface of sun-baked earth. Paint this mix for the dry areas, then bring the Wet Earth Exhale mix right up to its edge on wet paper — the dark wet mix will bleed into the dry-area mix exactly the way rain darkens soil from one edge. That bleeding border is the painting’s most important passage. Don’t touch it. Let water do what water does.

Why These Colors?

Petrichor is a transition — summer to fall, dry to wet, holding on to letting go. The palette is built around that crossing. Raw Umber, Burnt Sienna, and Transparent Brown Oxide are three stages of the same earth getting wet: cool and dry, warm and saturated, deep and transparent. All three are fully transparent, which matters because opaque earth tones read as paint sitting on a surface, while transparent earths read as the surface itself changing. Sedona Genuine earns its spot through material honesty: ground from actual stone, its granulation mimics the mineral grit of real soil in a way no synthetic pigment can. Raw Sienna and Yellow Ochre represent what’s left of summer — the dry patches, the bleached grass, the dusty trail surface that hasn’t been touched yet. French Ultramarine and Indanthrone Blue handle the arriving sky, one granulating like cloud texture, the other sinking deep like rain weight. Payne’s Gray bridges sky and earth in the middle values where rain blurs the horizon. Hooker’s Green is the first response — within hours of the first fall rain, PNW vegetation visibly greens up, and this palette needs that note of renewal amid all the earth and cloud.

Painting Prompts

The first rain is the most paintable moment in the PNW year — every surface changes value, every color deepens, and the contrast between wet and dry writes the composition for you.

Prompt 1

Trail Going Dark — Rain Arriving

Subject: A packed-earth hiking trail through mixed forest, rain just arriving. The near ground is still dry and pale. Twenty feet ahead, the trail surface has darkened two full values. The rain line is advancing toward the viewer. Trees on either side, sky heavy above.

The Dry-to-Wet Edge mix (Raw Sienna + Yellow Ochre + Sedona Genuine) for the near ground, the Wet Earth Exhale mix (Raw Umber + Burnt Sienna + Transparent Brown Oxide) for the darkened trail ahead. The bleeding border between them is the subject.

  • Paint the dry trail first with the Dry-to-Wet Edge mix and let the Sedona Genuine granulate undisturbed — this mineral texture reads as sun-baked grit and sets up the contrast with the smooth wet surface ahead
  • While the dry trail is still damp at its far edge, bring the Wet Earth Exhale mix in from the distance — let the two mixes bleed into each other at the rain line. Do not paint this edge. Let it happen
  • The sky is the First Rain Sky mix, heavy at the top with Indanthrone Blue, lighter near the horizon where the rain hasn’t fully closed in yet
  • Trees flanking the trail are Hooker’s Green darkened with Payne’s Gray — they should read as dark masses framing the trail’s value shift, not as detailed foliage
Prompt 2

Rain on Sun-Baked Rock

Subject: A close-up of exposed sandstone or basalt, first raindrops hitting the surface. Each drop creates a dark circle on the pale rock, some drops merging, some isolated. The rock surface is half-wet, half-dry, the pattern random and beautiful.

Sedona Genuine is the star here — its mineral granulation mimics actual stone surface. Raw Sienna for dry rock, Transparent Brown Oxide glazed over for wet areas, French Ultramarine reflected in the water pooling in surface depressions.

  • Lay the entire rock surface with Raw Sienna mixed with Sedona Genuine on rough paper — the Sedona granulation plus the paper tooth creates a convincing stone texture before you paint a single raindrop
  • Individual raindrops are small circles of clean water placed on the dry rock surface, then touched with Transparent Brown Oxide — the pigment darkens only the wetted area, exactly like real rain on rock
  • Where drops merge, connect them with a damp brush and let the Transparent Brown Oxide flow between — pooling rain follows gravity and finds the low spots in the rock surface
  • Tiny reflections of sky in the largest pools are French Ultramarine at its most dilute — just enough blue to read as reflected sky in standing water, not enough to look like paint
Prompt 3

Dry Grass Receiving Rain

Subject: A meadow of sun-bleached summer grass, the first rain bending the stalks and darkening them from gold to amber. In the background, the tree line is already wrapped in rain mist. A few green shoots visible at the base of the grass — the first response.

Yellow Ochre for dry grass tops, Raw Sienna for the stalks darkening, Burnt Sienna where rain has fully saturated. Hooker’s Green for the tiny new shoots at ground level. Payne’s Gray and French Ultramarine for the misty tree line.

  • Start with Yellow Ochre dry-brush strokes for the grass — upward flicks with a fan brush or rigger, leaving lots of white paper between stalks. This is the dry summer baseline
  • While the Yellow Ochre strokes are still slightly damp, mist the upper portion of the painting with a spray bottle — actual water hitting dry pigment replicates rain hitting dry grass more honestly than any brushwork can
  • As the spray darkens and bends the Yellow Ochre strokes, drop Raw Sienna and Burnt Sienna into the wettest areas — let gravity pull the darker pigments downward along the grass blades
  • Hooker’s Green at the base of the grass stalks, tiny upward marks, represents the first green response to rain — keep these small and sparse, the green revival is just beginning
Prompt 4

Rain Arriving Over the Valley

Subject: A wide valley view from a high point — the far side already curtained in rain, the near slopes still dry and golden. The rain curtain is a visible wall of gray-blue advancing across the landscape. Dry farmland in the foreground, wet dark fields in the distance.

The full earth-to-sky range of this palette in one panoramic scene. First Rain Sky for the clouds, Dry-to-Wet Edge for the near landscape, Wet Earth Exhale for the distant fields, Indanthrone Blue and Payne’s Gray for the rain curtain itself.

  • The rain curtain is a vertical wash of Payne’s Gray and Indanthrone Blue, soft-edged on both sides, painted on damp paper so it bleeds into the landscape — rain curtains have no hard edges from a distance
  • The far valley beyond the rain is a flat wash of the Wet Earth Exhale mix, muted and low-contrast — everything seen through rain loses saturation and detail
  • The near foreground stays warm and detailed: Raw Sienna and Yellow Ochre with Sedona Genuine granulation for the dry farmland, each field a slightly different value to show patchwork agriculture
  • The most powerful passage is the line where dry land meets rain curtain — let the warm Dry-to-Wet Edge mix and the cool Payne’s Gray bleed into each other without touching the brush to the join. This is what watercolor does better than any other medium
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Raw Umber (PBr7) and Burnt Sienna (PBr7) are both transparent iron oxides that layer without muddying — essential for building wet-earth depth. Transparent Brown Oxide (PR101) is one of Daniel Smith’s most transparent earth tones, perfect for glazing over other earth colors to darken without covering. Sedona Genuine is a PrimaTek pigment ground from actual Sedona sandstone — its granulation is unique and irreplaceable. French Ultramarine (PB29) granulates heavily, creating cloud and rain texture naturally. Indanthrone Blue (PB60) is the deepest, most serious blue in the Daniel Smith line, sinking into paper valleys like rain sinking into earth.
