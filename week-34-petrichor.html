Petrichor

First rain hits hot pavement and dry duff simultaneously — the whole forest exhales. Chanterelles push through needle litter overnight, egg-yolk gold. Last bioluminescence traces ghost through one final warm evening. Salal berries hang dark as bruises. The drought breaks and the land drinks like it forgot how.

Mixes & Ratios

Petrichor Duff

2 Raw Sienna : 1 Transparent Brown Oxide : touch Perylene Green

Hot forest floor receiving first drops — coin-sized dark circles appearing on pale dry duff as individual raindrops land.

Raw Sienna
Tr. Brown Oxide
Perylene Grn
Paint dry duff with Raw Sienna first, let it dry completely. Then drop water loaded with Transparent Brown Oxide onto the dry wash in scattered circles — each drop is a raindrop, darkening the duff on contact. The Perylene Green touch goes into a few drops to suggest needle color bleeding. Let the drops bloom naturally. Do not touch them. Rain doesn’t adjust itself.

Chanterelle Cap

2 Quinacridone Burnt Orange : 1 Hansa Yellow Medium

Egg-yolk flush of an overnight fruiting body — both pigments transparent, glowing on white paper like a chanterelle in morning light.

QBO
Hansa Yellow Med
Both transparent, so the mix glows on white paper like a chanterelle in morning light. QBO dominates the cap center; Hansa Yellow Medium lightens toward the wavy margin. Paint the cap convex — darker warm center, lighter yellow edge — to capture the way chanterelles cup and funnel. For the gill ridges underneath, use pure dilute Hansa Yellow Medium with a fine brush.

Rain Curtain

1 French Ultramarine : 2 Payne’s Gray : 1 Chinese White

Visible wall of precipitation crossing the sound — a gray column connecting cloud to water, opaque and obscuring.

Fr. Ultramarine
Payne's Gray
Chinese White
The opacity of Chinese White is essential — rain curtains are opaque. They obscure what’s behind them. Mix all three and apply in a vertical wash, darker at the top (more Payne’s Gray), lighter at the bottom (more Chinese White where rain diffuses near the water surface). French Ultramarine’s granulation will separate slightly from the Payne’s Gray, creating the uneven texture of visible precipitation. Spray water droplets onto the wet wash for rain-streak effects.

Why These Colors?

Petrichor is a sensory event, and this palette is calibrated to the visual component of that experience. French Ultramarine’s granulation creates the broken, dramatic quality of first-rain skies — it separates from other pigments in wet washes the way rain clouds separate from clear sky. Quinacridone Burnt Orange is the precise hue of fresh chanterelles, not the red-orange of old ones. Perylene Green goes dark enough to represent rain-soaked conifers without needing to add black. Carbazole Violet handles salal berries — nearly opaque at full concentration, it creates the bruise-dark clusters that hang heavy in September rain. Phthalo Green BS remains for one last bioluminescent ghost, the season’s final transparency before autumn commits to opacity.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

First Drops on Forest Duff

Subject: The forest floor at the exact moment the first rain arrives — coin-sized dark circles appearing on pale dry duff as individual drops land, the smell of petrichor practically visible.

The Petrichor Duff mix was engineered for this moment. You paint dry duff, then literally drop loaded water onto it to mimic rain darkening the ground.

  • Paint the dry duff with Raw Sienna first and let it dry completely — this is the before
  • Drop water loaded with Transparent Brown Oxide onto the dry wash in scattered circles; each paint-drop is a raindrop, darkening on contact
  • Touch Perylene Green into a few drops to suggest needle color bleeding into the wet spots
  • Do not touch the drops after they land. Let them bloom naturally. Rain doesn’t adjust itself, and neither should you
Prompt 2

Chanterelle Emerging from Needle Litter

Subject: A single fresh chanterelle pushing through dark, rain-dampened needle litter — egg-yolk gold against brown duff.

The Chanterelle Cap mix is the precise hue of a fresh fruiting body — both pigments are transparent, so the mix glows on white paper.

  • Paint the duff surroundings first with the Petrichor Duff mix, leaving the mushroom shape as white paper
  • QBO dominates the cap center; Hansa Yellow Medium lightens toward the wavy margin
  • For the gill ridges underneath, use pure dilute Hansa Yellow Medium with a fine brush
  • Perylene Green darkened duff immediately around the mushroom creates the rain-soaked context
Prompt 3

Rain Curtain Crossing the Sound

Subject: A visible wall of precipitation approaching across the strait — a gray column connecting cloud to water.

The Rain Curtain mix creates an opaque, textured column that obscures what’s behind it.

  • Apply the mix as a vertical wash: darker at the top, lighter at the bottom
  • French Ultramarine will granulate and separate slightly from the Payne’s Gray — feature, not bug
  • Spray clean water droplets onto the wet wash for rain-streak effects
  • The foreground shore and background behind the curtain should be distinctly different values
Prompt 4

Salal Berries in Rain

Subject: A cluster of salal berries hanging heavy and dark as bruises, with a single rain-bead balanced on a glossy leaf below.

Carbazole Violet at full concentration creates the near-black, bruise-heavy berry clusters. Chinese White handles the single point of reflection.

  • Carbazole Violet at full concentration is essentially black with a violet bias — test on scrap
  • Build the berries individually: small circles of concentrated Carbazole Violet, each with a tiny white highlight reserved
  • Perylene Green for the leaves, rain-darkened and glossy; leave a thin white edge on each leaf for the wet shine
  • Chinese White for the rain-bead: one single opaque dot on the lowest leaf
