Week 23 — PNW Watercolor Series

Rose Solstice

Nootka roses open along every fence line and forest edge — simple five-petal pink, golden center, that clean wild-rose scent that stops you mid-step. Early June warmth has the quality of a held breath. The light lingers low and gold, and Rosa nutkana doesn’t care whether anyone notices. It blooms because that’s what June is for.

Wild rose petals, NR9 granulating on the paper the way actual petals crinkle at their edges; liftable, fading, true

Quinacridone Gold

Solstice golden warmth, stamen clusters, the long amber light of early June evenings pooling on every surface

Clean warm light through translucent petals, the bright edge of a rose leaf in full sun; transparent and eager

Mixes & Ratios

Nootka Petal

2 Rose Madder Permanent : 1 Potter's Pink : ¼ Buff Titanium

The exact pink of a Nootka rose petal held to the light — granulating, mineral, not remotely polished.

Rose Madder
Potter's Pink
Buff Titanium
Rose Madder Permanent (NR9) provides the core petal hue and that signature granulation — pigment settling into paper valleys the way real petals crinkle. Potter’s Pink (PR233) is the secret: it’s a mineral pigment, slightly gritty, and it makes the pink feel like a wild thing rather than a garden rose. Buff Titanium softens the mix just enough to suggest the translucency where a petal thins near its edge. Apply wet and generous. These are not precious flowers; paint them loose.

Stamen Gold

2 Quinacridone Gold : 1 Hansa Yellow Light : ¼ Burnt Sienna

The dense golden boss at a wild rose’s center — pollen-heavy, warm, the part the bees care about.

Quin. Gold
Hansa Yellow
Burnt Sienna
Quinacridone Gold granulates warmly, giving the stamen cluster its textured, pollen-dusted quality. Hansa Yellow Light lifts the mix into bright warmth — these stamens catch the sun. Burnt Sienna grounds the gold at the anther tips where the color deepens. Use a fine brush and dab rather than stroke; stamens are points, not lines. Drop this into the center of a still-damp Nootka Petal wash and let the pigments negotiate their own border.

Rose Leaf Shadow

2 Sap Green : 1 Green Gold : ½ Cobalt Blue

The serrated leaves that frame every bloom — warm in the sun, cool beneath, always slightly glossy.

Sap Green
Green Gold
Cobalt Blue
Sap Green provides the base — the deep, slightly cool green of Rosa nutkana’s compound leaves. Green Gold warms the sunlit side; wild rose leaves are never one flat green but shift from warm to cool across their surface. Cobalt Blue cools the shadow underneath and its granulation suggests the fine-toothed leaf edges. Paint the leaves in pairs along the stem, varying the Green Gold ratio — more warmth on the south-facing leaves, more Cobalt Blue underneath. Leave a thin dry edge between leaf and stem to suggest that waxy sheen.

Why These Colors?

Rose Madder Permanent is the palette’s heart — NR9 granulates on the paper with exactly the texture of a crinkled Nootka petal, and it lifts cleanly, which is how wild roses behave in life: here and then gone. Potter’s Pink adds the mineral roughness that separates a wild rose from anything cultivated — it’s earthy, slightly opaque, unbothered. Quinacridone Gold handles the golden stamen centers and doubles as the long amber light of early June evenings; it granulates warmly, tying petal-center to sunset. Hansa Yellow Light is the clean warmth shining through translucent petals. Sap Green and Green Gold together create the compound-leaf architecture — warm in sun, cool in shade. Deep Scarlet sits at the margins, hinting at rose hips just beginning to swell. Cobalt Blue provides the sky context and contributes granulation to shadows. Burnt Sienna grounds everything to the fence rails and the earth. Buff Titanium is the soft atmosphere of a June morning before the heat sharpens — it gives the whites somewhere warm to land.

Painting Prompts

Four subjects built for this palette — each one leans into the pinks, golds, and greens you already have mixed and the Nootka roses blooming right now outside your door.

Prompt 1

Wild Rose on a Fence Line

Subject: A weathered split-rail fence with Nootka roses tangled along the top rail — five-petal blooms in various stages, some fresh pink, some fading to near-white, a few petals on the ground. Late afternoon, warm light raking across the rails.

The Nootka Petal mix was made for this — Rose Madder Permanent’s liftability lets you create fading petals by rewetting and lifting while damp. Burnt Sienna handles the weathered fence rails. Quinacridone Gold is the evening light itself.

  • Paint the blooms with the Nootka Petal mix at high dilution, then lift individual petals with a damp brush while still wet — Rose Madder Permanent practically wants to leave, which is exactly how wild roses age
  • Lay Burnt Sienna over dry fence rails with a dry-brush stroke to catch the grain of weathered wood, then glaze Quinacridone Gold over the sunlit side
  • Use the Rose Leaf Shadow mix for the foliage tangled along the rail, varying the Green Gold ratio for leaves catching the light
  • Drop Buff Titanium into the background wash while damp to create the soft warm atmosphere of a June afternoon
Prompt 2

Single Bloom Close-Up with Golden Center

Subject: A single Nootka rose filling most of the paper — five pink petals open flat, the golden stamen boss in the center pollen-heavy and bright. One bee, maybe. The kind of painting where you can almost smell the rose.

Nootka Petal mix for the petals, Stamen Gold for the center. Potter’s Pink adds the mineral grit at the petal base where color concentrates. This is a painting about two things: pink and gold.

  • Wet the entire petal area first, then float in Rose Madder Permanent — let it granulate and settle naturally, which gives you that crinkled-petal texture without any brushwork
  • While the petals are still damp, drop Potter’s Pink at the petal base near the center — it will bloom outward with a gritty mineral quality that reads as the deeper pink of a real rose’s throat
  • Apply the Stamen Gold mix with a fine brush using stippling marks, not strokes — stamens radiate as points from the center
  • Leave white paper between petals for the gaps where light comes through; a Nootka rose is an open, simple flower with space between its five petals
Prompt 3

Rose Hip Forming

Subject: A branch tip where the petals have just fallen and the hip is beginning to swell — still green at the base, blushing red at the crown, with the dried stamen remnants still attached like a tiny brown crown. The transition from flower to fruit.

Deep Scarlet is finally the star — it’s been waiting at the edge of this palette for exactly this subject. Sap Green handles the still-green base. Burnt Sienna for the dried stamens clinging on top.

  • Paint the hip with Sap Green at the base, then wet-blend into Deep Scarlet at the crown — let the transition be gradual, the way an actual hip blushes from bottom to top
  • The dried stamen crown on top is Burnt Sienna with a fine rigger brush — tiny radiating lines, slightly irregular, some broken
  • Use Green Gold on the sepals that splay out around the hip’s base; they catch the light differently than the leaves
  • Add one or two faded Rose Madder Permanent petals still clinging to the hip — nearly transparent, barely there, a ghost of the bloom that was
Prompt 4

Rose-Covered Trail Edge at Golden Hour

Subject: A forest trail curving away into shadow, with Nootka roses blooming along the sunny edge where the canopy opens. Golden hour light catches the blooms and the dust on the trail. The forest side is cool and green; the rose side is warm and pink.

The full palette working together — Cobalt Blue and Sap Green in the forest shadow, the pink and gold mixes in the sunny margin, Buff Titanium for the dusty trail, Burnt Sienna for the earth.

  • Divide the painting diagonally: cool forest on one side (Cobalt Blue + Sap Green wet-into-wet), warm rose-edge on the other (Rose Madder Permanent + Quinacridone Gold glazes)
  • The trail itself is Buff Titanium with Burnt Sienna in the tire ruts or footprints — keep it warm and soft, the color of summer dust
  • Paint the roses as loose pink marks along the trail edge using the Nootka Petal mix — they should read as a mass of bloom, not individual flowers, at this scale
  • Glaze Quinacridone Gold over the entire sunlit half after everything is dry — this unifies the warm side and creates that golden-hour amber that makes June evenings in the PNW feel like they could last forever
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Rose Madder Permanent (NR9) granulates and lifts almost completely — use this to your advantage for fading petals, but protect finished areas from accidental rewetting. Potter’s Pink (PR233) is a mineral pigment with a gritty, slightly opaque character uncommon in pinks — it’s what keeps these from looking like garden roses. Deep Scarlet bridges the gap between the rose petals and the hips forming at the branch tips. Green Gold’s warm green is a near-perfect match for Nootka rose leaves in direct sun. Buff Titanium is semi-opaque; use it sparingly or it will deaden washes beneath it.
