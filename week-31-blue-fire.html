Blue Fire

Every wake shimmers. You drag your hand through the water and wear a glove of light. Pearly everlasting silvers the slopes above, wasp-buzz fills the hot afternoon, but night owns this week — boat wakes write cursive across the strait. Peak bioluminescence demands surrender to awe.

Mixes & Ratios

Wake Cursive

2 Phthalo Blue GS : 1 Phthalo Green BS : touch Chinese White

The bright line a kayak bow writes on black water — fluid, continuous, illegible, and gorgeous.

Phthalo Blue GS
Phthalo Green BS
Chinese White
Use a rigger brush or the tip of a round. Single continuous stroke on dark pre-washed paper — the wake is one gesture, not a built-up effect. The Chinese White touch goes at the bow point, where disturbance is maximum. Let the line thin and fade toward the stern. Speed matters: paint as fast as the kayak moves.

Silver Slope

1 Hansa Yellow Light : 1 Potter’s Pink : 1 Chinese White

Pearly everlasting colony in late afternoon — silver-white flower heads catching light on a tawny hillside.

Hansa Yellow Lt
Potter’s Pink
Chinese White
Potter’s Pink granulates heavily, which is exactly right for the dusty, fuzzy texture of pearly everlasting on a dry slope. Mix loosely — let the three pigments separate on the paper rather than homogenizing on the palette. The result should shimmer between warm pink, pale gold, and chalky white. This is a daytime passage, and its warmth makes the night glow feel colder by contrast.

Channel Dark

3 Indigo : 1 Neutral Tint

Water that swallows light ten feet from the hull — the darkness that gives bioluminescence its drama.

Indigo
Neutral Tint
Apply as a flat, confident wash. This is the background against which everything glows, so it needs to be even, deep, and non-competitive. Neutral Tint desaturates the Indigo just enough to prevent it from reading as blue — this water is dark, not colorful. Two coats if needed, drying between. Don’t let it get streaky; the strait at midnight is not streaky.

Why These Colors?

The palette splits cleanly between night water and day slope, and the pigment choices follow that divide. Phthalo Blue GS and Phthalo Green BS are the transparency-and-staining workhorses for bioluminescence — they sink into the paper and glow from within, which is the only way to convincingly paint light in water. Indigo and Neutral Tint provide layered darks that stay blue-black without going chalky. The daytime contingent — Hansa Yellow Light, Potter’s Pink, Chinese White — is deliberately soft and granulating, a textural counterpoint to the smooth, staining night passages. Cobalt Teal Blue is the one color that lives in both worlds: too blue-green to be day, too opaque to be glow, it marks the liminal zone where the two realities overlap.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Night Kayak on the Strait

Subject: A kayak cutting through bioluminescent water at midnight — the hull leaves a trail of blue-green fire while headlands sit as featureless dark masses against the sky.

Phthalo Blue GS and Phthalo Green BS are born for this — their transparency and staining power create glow that looks embedded in the paper, not sitting on top. Indigo and Neutral Tint (Channel Dark mix) give you the absorbing background water. Chinese White marks the single brightest spark at the bow.

  • Pre-wash the entire water area with the Channel Dark mix (Indigo 3:Neutral Tint 1) and let it dry completely before adding any glow strokes
  • Use a rigger brush for the Wake Cursive mix (Phthalo Blue GS 2:Phthalo Green BS 1:Chinese White touch) in one continuous gesture — speed matters more than precision
  • Keep all glow passages transparent; the moment you introduce an opaque pigment into the water, the illusion dies
  • Reserve the headland silhouettes for Neutral Tint at full concentration — they should be darker than the water, which is counterintuitive but accurate
Prompt 2

Pearly Everlasting Hillside at Golden Hour

Subject: A dry south-facing slope covered in pearly everlasting flower heads catching the last hour of August sun, with the dark strait visible below and the promise of the night show in the cooling air.

The Silver Slope mix (Hansa Yellow Light, Potter’s Pink, Chinese White at 1:1:1) was designed for exactly this — Potter’s Pink’s heavy granulation mimics the dusty, fuzzy texture of the flower heads without you having to paint each one.

  • Mix loosely on paper, not on the palette — let the three pigments in the Silver Slope mix separate and shimmer between warm pink, pale gold, and chalky white
  • Use Hansa Yellow Light at full strength for the few flower heads catching direct sun, then dilute progressively for those in shadow
  • Raw Umber anchors the drift logs at the base of the slope where it meets the waterline — keep these shapes simple and dark
  • Paint the slope first, then the water below it; the warmth of the daytime passage makes the night-water feel colder by contrast
Prompt 3

Bioluminescent Hand Print

Subject: A close-up study of a hand lifted from the water, trailing blue-green light from every finger — the intimate, small-scale version of bioluminescence that makes people gasp.

Phthalo Blue GS at full concentration is darker than you’d expect, perfect for the dripping water between fingers. Cascade Green softens the glow in the shallow water around the wrist. Cobalt Teal Blue marks where the glow transitions to reflected sky.

  • Paint the hand as negative space — lay down the dark water first (Channel Dark mix), then lift or reserve the hand shape
  • Phthalo Blue GS and Cascade Green applied wet-into-wet around the fingers create the spreading, dissipating glow of disturbed dinoflagellates
  • Chinese White at the fingertips only — maximum disturbance means maximum brightness, but one dot per finger, no more
  • The background water beyond the glow zone should be flat Indigo/Neutral Tint — the drama needs darkness to work
Prompt 4

Wasp on Dry Thistle

Subject: A late-summer wasp on a dried thistle head, backlit by afternoon sun on a hot slope — the buzzy, golden, last-gasp-of-heat moment before the night takes over.

Potter’s Pink granulates into a warm haze perfect for the dry slope and thistle texture. Hansa Yellow Light gives you the wasp’s striped body and the backlighting. Cobalt Teal Blue in the sky behind keeps it distinctly late-August.

  • This is a small-format subject — work no larger than 6x8 inches to keep the detail intimate
  • Potter’s Pink applied dry-brush on rough paper creates thistle-head texture with almost no effort
  • Hansa Yellow Light for the wasp body, with thin lines of Raw Umber for the stripes — use a Size 2 round at most
  • Leave a rim of untouched paper around the wasp and thistle where the backlight catches; that halo of white is what sells the late-afternoon heat
Notes: All pigments confirmed Daniel Smith. For bioluminescence passages, maintain transparency throughout — the glow must feel embedded in the paper, not painted on top. Avoid granulating pigments in active glow zones; Potter’s Pink and Cobalt Teal Blue should stay on the slopes and transition zones respectively. Phthalo Blue GS at full concentration is darker than you expect — test your value before committing to a glow stroke. Chinese White is the only opaque in the glow zone, and it should mark only the absolute brightest points. Discipline with white is the difference between convincing bioluminescence and a mess.
