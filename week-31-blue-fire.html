Week 31 — PNW Watercolor Series

Blue Fire

The sky is trying to burn you. Ninety-five degrees and not a cloud anywhere — the blue overhead is so intense it looks weaponized, pressing down on bleached grass and baked earth like a flat iron. Shadows are razor-cut and black. The air shimmers above every paved surface. Even the trees look thirsty. This is the week when the Pacific Northwest remembers it can be brutal.

Primary Trio

Cerulean Blue

Blazing summer sky at its most opaque and chalky; the blue of a day that won’t let up, granulating into haze at the horizon

Phthalo Blue (Green Shade)

Intense sky depth at the zenith where the atmosphere thins; transparent, staining, the deepest blue the summer sky reaches

New Gamboge

Scorching light itself — the yellow of heat that bleaches everything it touches, warm and relentless

Mixes & Ratios

Brutal Sky

2 Cerulean Blue : 1 Phthalo Blue GS : ½ Cobalt Blue

The sky on the hottest day of the year — blue so intense it feels like pressure on your skin.

Cerulean Blue
Phthalo Blue GS
Cobalt Blue
Three blues, each doing different work. Cerulean Blue is the dominant sky tone — opaque, chalky, it reads as hot-weather sky the way no transparent blue can. Phthalo Blue GS intensifies the zenith where the atmosphere thins and the blue deepens toward violet. Cobalt Blue provides the granulation that breaks up the wash and suggests heat shimmer at the molecular level. Grade this mix across the sky: more Phthalo Blue GS at the top (zenith), more Cerulean Blue in the middle, fading to Cerulean Blue Chromium alone near the horizon where the sky bleaches out. Apply as a single graded wash on wet paper — the blues will mingle on the surface and the Cobalt will granulate into the valleys.

Scorched Earth

2 Raw Sienna : 1 Yellow Ochre : ¼ Burnt Sienna

Baked hillside in late July — every drop of moisture gone, the ground cracked and pale, grass bleached to straw.

Raw Sienna
Yellow Ochre
Burnt Sienna
Raw Sienna is the transparent base — it captures the warm, golden-brown of soil that has baked in the sun for weeks without rain. Yellow Ochre adds opacity and lifts the value, mimicking the chalky, powdery quality of PNW earth in drought. Burnt Sienna at the edges and in cracks adds the darker, iron-rich tone of exposed subsoil where the surface has split. Apply Raw Sienna as a wash first, then drop Yellow Ochre into the sunlit areas and Burnt Sienna into the cracks and shadow zones while damp. Let the yellow and brown pigments argue — the uneven mixing IS the texture of dry, cracked earth.

Heat Bleach

2 New Gamboge : 1 Hansa Yellow Deep : ¼ Cerulean Blue

The searing quality of direct sunlight at 2 p.m. — so bright it burns color out of everything it touches.

New Gamboge
Hansa Yellow Deep
Cerulean Blue
New Gamboge is warm yellow leaning orange — it reads as heat the way cooler yellows cannot. Hansa Yellow Deep reinforces the warmth and adds the staining power needed for sunlit passages that need to glow through subsequent layers. The touch of Cerulean Blue seems counterintuitive, but it knocks the yellow back just enough to suggest the bleaching effect — pure yellow reads as color, but yellow slightly grayed by its complement reads as blinding light. Use this mix at extreme dilution as a glaze over sunlit areas after the underlying forms are painted and dry. The warmth will unify everything the sun touches.

Why These Colors?

Blue Fire is sky so intense it burns. Four blues — Cerulean Blue, Phthalo Blue Green Shade, Cobalt Blue, and Cerulean Blue Chromium — each a different intensity of the brutal summer sky. Cerulean Blue is the workhorse, opaque and chalky, reading as hot-weather sky in a way transparent blues cannot. Phthalo Blue GS pushes the zenith toward that deep, almost violet intensity the summer sky reaches at its apex. Cobalt Blue provides mid-sky granulation — its mineral particles settling on paper create the visual shimmer of overheated air. Cerulean Blue Chromium is the specific, slightly greener blue of the sky near the horizon where heat haze bleaches it. New Gamboge and Hansa Yellow Deep handle the searing, bleaching light — the yellow of heat that strips color from everything it touches. Ivory Black for the razor-sharp shadows that only high summer produces, the kind with edges so crisp they look cut with a blade. Burnt Sienna and Raw Sienna for the baked earth and dried trails. Yellow Ochre for the grass that went from green to straw in a week and won’t come back until October rain.

Painting Prompts

The hottest week demands paintings that feel hot. Every prompt here is about intensity — intense blue, intense yellow, intense shadows. The palette was built for extremes, and these subjects push it there.

Prompt 1

Blazing Sky Over Dry Meadow

Subject: A vast summer sky filling two-thirds of the frame, grading from deep blue at the zenith to chalky blue at the horizon. Below, a bleached grass meadow with razor-sharp shadows from scattered oaks. Heat shimmer blurs the horizon line.

The Brutal Sky mix (Cerulean Blue + Phthalo Blue GS + Cobalt Blue) is the main event. Scorched Earth mix (Raw Sienna + Yellow Ochre + Burnt Sienna) for the meadow below. Ivory Black for the shadows that cut like knives across dry grass.

  • The sky wash is everything — practice the graded wash on scrap first. Phthalo Blue GS at the top edge, transitioning to Cerulean Blue in the middle, adding Cobalt Blue for granulation, fading to Cerulean Blue Chromium at the horizon
  • The meadow is Yellow Ochre and Hansa Yellow Deep applied dry-brush on rough paper — the texture of the paper becomes the texture of dead grass
  • Shadows are Ivory Black at full concentration with ruler-straight edges on the sun side — high summer shadows are geometric, not atmospheric. Use the edge of a flat brush
  • Blur the horizon line by softening the junction between sky and meadow with a damp brush — heat shimmer erases that boundary in reality, and a hard line would look wrong
Prompt 2

Heat Shimmer on Pavement

Subject: A straight PNW road stretching to the horizon through dry farmland, the surface shimmering with heat mirages. The sky presses down. Everything in the distance liquefies where the hot air bends light.

New Gamboge and Hansa Yellow Deep as a Heat Bleach glaze over the entire sunlit area. Ivory Black for the road surface and its hard-edged shadow lines. The four blues handle the enormous sky above.

  • Paint the road as a strong perspective line converging to a vanishing point — Ivory Black at full concentration near the viewer, lightening with Raw Sienna as it recedes into the mirage zone
  • The heat mirage is the absence of detail — where the road meets the horizon, soften everything with clean water and let the road, sky, and fields merge into an undefined shimmer
  • Flanking fields are the Scorched Earth mix with Hansa Yellow Deep for the driest grass patches — apply with quick horizontal strokes suggesting harvested stubble
  • Apply the Heat Bleach mix as a final glaze over the entire middle ground after everything is dry — the yellow-over-everything effect creates the visual sensation of unbearable brightness
Prompt 3

Oak Shadow on Baked Earth

Subject: A single Garry oak casting a hard, complex shadow on cracked earth. The tree is dark against blazing sky, its shadow a precise blueprint of its canopy on the ground. The contrast between shade and sun is absolute.

Ivory Black for the shadow, which on a 95-degree day with no clouds is genuinely black by contrast with the surrounding blaze. Burnt Sienna and Raw Sienna for the baked earth. Cerulean Blue and Cobalt Blue for the sky visible through the canopy’s gaps.

  • Map the shadow shape carefully with pencil first — the shadow is a portrait of the tree projected onto the ground, and its accuracy is what makes this painting work
  • Paint the sunlit earth first with the Scorched Earth mix and let it dry completely. Then paint the shadow as a single shape of Ivory Black at medium concentration — the shadow should be cooler but still translucent enough to show the earth texture beneath
  • The tree itself is a silhouette against sky: Ivory Black trunk and branches, with Cobalt Blue sky visible through the gaps in the canopy as positive shapes
  • Leave a thin halo of New Gamboge around the tree’s edges where the backlighting catches individual leaves — even a dark tree glows at its margins when the sun is behind it
Prompt 4

Swimming Hole in the Cascades

Subject: A mountain swimming hole on the hottest day — cold blue-green water in a rock basin, surrounded by sun-scorched boulders and bleached grass. The tension between the searing landscape and the cold water is the whole story.

Cerulean Blue and Phthalo Blue GS for the water, which reads as cold precisely because everything around it is hot. Burnt Sienna for the sun-baked boulders. Yellow Ochre for the bleached grass on the banks. Ivory Black for the deep pool shadow where the water is over your head.

  • Paint the hot surroundings first — Burnt Sienna boulders, Yellow Ochre grass, Raw Sienna trail. Establish the heat with warm colors before introducing any blue to the water
  • The water is Cerulean Blue in the shallows grading to Phthalo Blue GS where it deepens, with Ivory Black at the very deepest point. Apply on damp paper so the transitions are smooth — water doesn’t have hard edges between depth zones
  • Where rock is submerged, paint Burnt Sienna first then glaze Cerulean Blue over it after dry — the rock color showing through the blue is what makes the water look transparent rather than opaque
  • Leave bright white paper for the sunlit faces of the boulders surrounding the pool — the contrast between white-hot rock and cold blue water is the temperature story of this painting
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Cerulean Blue (PB35) is a genuine cobalt tin oxide — opaque, chalky, granulating, irreplaceable for hot-weather sky. Phthalo Blue Green Shade (PB15:3) is the highest-chroma transparent blue available, perfect for the deep zenith. Cobalt Blue (PB28) granulates beautifully and sits between the other blues in temperature. Cerulean Blue Chromium (PB36) is a slightly greener, more opaque cerulean ideal for the bleached sky near the horizon. New Gamboge (PY153) is a warm, transparent yellow that reads as heat when used as a glaze. Ivory Black (PBk9) produces the hard-edged shadows that define high summer.
