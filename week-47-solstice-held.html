Solstice Held

The light is leaving and we all know it. By four in the afternoon the sky is already turning — that thin red band along the Olympics lasts twenty minutes, maybe, before Indanthrone swallows it whole. You hold the last warmth in your hands like something borrowed. Buff Titanium light on the kitchen wall. The year tilting toward its darkest seam, and everything feels precious because it’s almost gone.

Primary Trio

Indanthrone Blue

The deep approaching dark of late December; a blue so deep it reads as night, relentless and inevitable, swallowing the horizon

Alizarin Crimson

The thin red sunset band that barely survives twenty minutes; cool-leaning red, transparent, fragile against the encroaching blue

Buff Titanium

Held light, pale and precious; the last warm glow on a wall, on skin, on the underside of clouds before dark claims them

Mixes & Ratios

Solstice Dusk

2 Indanthrone Blue : 1 Alizarin Crimson : ½ Carbazole Violet

The sky fifteen minutes after sunset — blue consuming red, violet blooming in between like a bruise the year earned.

Indanthrone Blue
Alizarin Crimson
Carbazole Violet
Indanthrone Blue is one of the deepest blues available in watercolor — a PB60 pigment that reads as near-black at full strength but retains blue identity even in shadow. Alizarin Crimson bleeds into it the way sunset bleeds into approaching night, the cool red finding the cool blue and making something neither warm nor cold but inevitable. Carbazole Violet bridges the gap — it’s the color your eye actually sees in that transition zone where red meets blue at dusk. Apply Indanthrone as a graded wash from the top, then charge Alizarin Crimson into the lower third while still wet. Let Carbazole Violet bloom in the wet boundary where the two meet. Don’t touch it once the colors start moving.

Held Light

3 Buff Titanium : 1 Yellow Ochre : ¼ Burnt Sienna

The last warm glow on a west-facing wall — not sunlight anymore, but the memory of it, clinging to plaster.

Buff Titanium
Yellow Ochre
Burnt Sienna
Buff Titanium is semi-opaque and slightly warm — it captures the quality of exhausted light better than any transparent pigment because it has that chalky, soft-focus quality of light that’s lost its intensity. Yellow Ochre warms it just enough to read as the golden hour’s afterglow rather than mere pallor. Burnt Sienna at the edges gives the warmth somewhere to go — a whisper of earth where the light meets shadow. Apply Buff Titanium generously to damp paper for the broad glow, then touch Yellow Ochre into the center of the lit area and let it drift outward. The Burnt Sienna goes only at the transition to shadow, blending into the Solstice Dusk mix.

Twilight Separation

2 Moonglow : 1 Payne’s Gray : ½ French Ultramarine

The moment objects lose their edges — when trees become shapes and the ground becomes a guess.

Moonglow
Payne’s Gray
French Ultramarine
Moonglow is Daniel Smith’s convenience mix of Ultramarine Blue, Burnt Sienna, and Viridian — it separates on paper into its component pigments, which is exactly what twilight does to color. The granulation creates the visual noise of low-light perception. Payne’s Gray flattens and darkens without going black, maintaining the uncertainty of dusk. French Ultramarine adds a cooler, brighter note where the sky still holds color above the horizon. Wet the paper thoroughly and let Moonglow go down first — give it thirty seconds to start separating before adding Payne’s Gray to the shadow areas. French Ultramarine only at the sky edge, where twilight is still technically blue.

Why These Colors?

This palette is about scarcity of light. Indanthrone Blue dominates because the dark dominates now — it’s the deep, relentless approaching night that starts at three-thirty and doesn’t let go until nine the next morning. Alizarin Crimson is the thin red band of sunset that lasts maybe twenty minutes on a clear day, a cool transparent red that fights the blue and loses beautifully. Buff Titanium holds the last light — pale, precious, semi-opaque like light that’s lost its conviction. Moonglow earns its name this week: its three-pigment separation on paper mimics the way twilight breaks color apart into uncertain components. Carbazole Violet returns as the year’s color cycles back to dusk — it was present in the earliest weeks and here it is again, closing the loop. French Ultramarine for the sky that’s still technically blue before Indanthrone takes over. Payne’s Gray and Lamp Black bracket the darkest values — Payne’s with its blue lean for atmospheric dark, Lamp Black for the absolute. Burnt Sienna grounds the warm earth tones, and Yellow Ochre holds the last golden memory of autumn’s warmth.

Painting Prompts

The shortest days demand attention to what little light remains. Each prompt here works with those twenty precious minutes of color before the dark settles in — paint fast, or paint from memory.

Prompt 1

The Twenty-Minute Sunset

Subject: The Olympic Mountains silhouetted against a thin band of red sunset, the rest of the sky already consumed by deep blue-black. The red is narrow — a ribbon, not a wash. Below, Puget Sound reflects a darker version of what’s above.

The Solstice Dusk mix (Indanthrone Blue + Alizarin Crimson + Carbazole Violet) handles the sky. The Held Light mix (Buff Titanium + Yellow Ochre + Burnt Sienna) catches whatever warmth remains on the horizon.

  • Paint the Alizarin Crimson band first as a narrow horizontal strip at the horizon — keep it thin and precise, because the whole point is scarcity
  • While the red is still wet, bring Indanthrone Blue down from the top of the page in a strong graded wash, letting it touch and consume the edges of the crimson
  • The mountains are Lamp Black silhouettes — wait until the sky is fully dry, then paint them in one confident pass with a flat brush
  • The water below mirrors the sky but darker: repeat the same color sequence at half the height, with more Payne’s Gray mixed into each color to dull the reflections
Prompt 2

Last Light on a Kitchen Wall

Subject: Interior scene: a west-facing kitchen window throwing a rectangle of pale gold light across a wall and counter. Outside the window, twilight is already blue-violet. The contrast between held warmth inside and approaching cold outside.

Buff Titanium and Yellow Ochre for the light rectangle. Moonglow and French Ultramarine through the window. Burnt Sienna for warm wood surfaces inside. The interior/exterior contrast is the painting.

  • The light rectangle on the wall is the Held Light mix at its most dilute — Buff Titanium barely tinted with Yellow Ochre, applied to dry paper so the edges stay crisp
  • Everything outside the window is the Twilight Separation mix — Moonglow separating into its components on wet paper, with French Ultramarine at the top of the visible sky
  • Burnt Sienna for the wooden window frame and any warm interior surfaces — it bridges the warm light inside and the cool dark outside
  • The shadow side of the kitchen (away from the window) gets Payne’s Gray with a touch of Carbazole Violet — interior shadows at dusk lean violet, not neutral
Prompt 3

Solstice Treeline

Subject: A line of Douglas firs seen from below, their tops barely distinguishable against a sky that’s passing from deep blue to black. One star visible. The trees are shapes, not details — the light is too far gone for needles.

Indanthrone Blue grading to Lamp Black for the sky. Payne’s Gray for the tree silhouettes. Buff Titanium for the single star. Carbazole Violet where tree-dark meets sky-dark and the eye can’t quite tell the difference.

  • Grade the sky from French Ultramarine at the horizon through Indanthrone Blue at mid-sky to near-Lamp Black at the zenith — this gradient is the entire light story
  • The treeline goes in while the sky is still slightly damp — use Payne’s Gray so the edges bleed just enough to lose definition, because that’s what happens to edges at dusk
  • The star is a dot of Buff Titanium applied with a fine brush to dry paper — its semi-opacity reads as a point of light against the dark wash
  • Where the trees meet the sky, charge a thin line of Carbazole Violet — it creates the visual ambiguity of dark-on-dark that makes this scene feel like actual failing light
Prompt 4

Solstice Bonfire on the Beach

Subject: A small bonfire on a Puget Sound beach at the solstice. Figures as dark shapes around the fire. The fire is the only warm thing in the frame — everything else is deep blue, violet, and black. Driftwood logs, wet sand reflecting firelight.

Yellow Ochre and Burnt Sienna for the fire and its reflected warmth. Indanthrone Blue and Lamp Black for the surrounding darkness. Moonglow for the middle ground where firelight fades to dark. Alizarin Crimson for the ember glow.

  • Preserve the fire area with masking fluid before painting the entire background in a graded wash of Indanthrone Blue to Lamp Black — the fire needs to be the lightest thing in the painting
  • Once the background is dry, remove the masking and paint the fire: Yellow Ochre at the center, Burnt Sienna radiating outward, Alizarin Crimson at the ember edges
  • The figures around the fire are Lamp Black silhouettes with a thin edge of Burnt Sienna on the fire-facing side — just enough warmth to suggest reflected light on a coat or a face
  • Wet sand reflecting firelight is a dilute wash of Moonglow with Yellow Ochre charged into the zone nearest the fire — the granulation of Moonglow reads as wet sand texture
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Indanthrone Blue (PB60) is one of the deepest, most lightfast blues available — it reads as near-black at full concentration but remains unmistakably blue in dilution. Alizarin Crimson (PR83) is the classic cool transparent red, fugitive in some formulations but essential for its particular quality of light. Buff Titanium (PW6:1) is a warm semi-opaque white that excels at depicting exhausted, low-intensity light. Moonglow is a three-pigment convenience mix that separates on paper into blue, brown, and green components — the granulation is the point. Carbazole Violet (PV23) is a deep, staining violet that bridges blue and red in the dusk sky.
