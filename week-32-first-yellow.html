First Yellow

A single bigleaf maple branch turns gold among ten thousand green ones — the first defector. Bioluminescence still flickers on warm nights but the urgency is fading. Pink salmon muscle upstream, their backs humped and olive. Summer is looking over its shoulder.

Primary Trio

Quinacridone Gold

The lone maple branch surrendering to autumn while every other leaf holds green; semi-transparent, warm, and impossible to ignore against a dark canopy

Hooker’s Green

The canopy still holding summer, the majority vote; dense and reliable, it makes the gold defection feel more dramatic by contrast

Indanthrone Blue

The strait cooling toward fall, deeper and less hospitable each night; granulating and dark, it anchors the bottom of the composition

Mixes & Ratios

Salmon Olive

2 Olive Green : 1 Raw Umber : touch Burnt Sienna Light

Pink salmon back breaking the surface — humped, mottled, exhausted from the push upstream.

Olive Green
Raw Umber
Burnt Sienna Lt
Wet-on-wet for the mottled quality of spawning salmon skin. The Olive Green base should be uneven — salmon backs are not uniform. Touch in Raw Umber for the darker dorsal stripe, and the barest Burnt Sienna Light for the pink beginning to show through. These fish are changing color mid-journey; your paint should look mid-transition too.

Defector Gold

2 Quinacridone Gold : 1 Hooker’s Green

Single leaf turning against the consensus — one branch of gold in a wall of green, the first visible crack in summer’s argument.

Quin. Gold
Hooker's Green
Don’t fully mix. Let Quinacridone Gold dominate the center of the leaf shape with Hooker’s Green at the margins — the leaf is turning from its veins outward. On the paper, the two pigments should create an unstable border zone where gold and green negotiate. This transition is the entire narrative of the week: the margin where summer becomes fall.

Last Glow

1 Phthalo Green BS : 1 Phthalo Blue GS : 2 Indanthrone Blue

Bioluminescence dimming into cold water — still present but no longer commanding, a footnote to the summer that was.

Phthalo Green BS
Phthalo Blue GS
Indanthrone Blue
The ratio tells the story: Indanthrone Blue now dominates what was once a phthalo-led glow. The bioluminescence is being absorbed by the cooling water. Use more water in your mix than you would for peak-glow weeks — dilution is dimming. Apply over a pre-wet dark wash and let the glow drift and dissipate rather than holding its shape. The light is leaving.

Why These Colors?

This is a transitional palette, and every choice reflects the hinge. Quinacridone Gold is present but outnumbered — one branch against a canopy of Hooker’s Green — mirroring the single defector in a sea of holdouts. The bioluminescence pigments remain from previous weeks but are now subordinate to Indanthrone Blue’s increasing dominance, tracking the water’s cooling. Olive Green does double duty as salmon-back and general autumnal shift in the green spectrum. Lavender is a quiet surprise — Daniel Smith’s granulating Lavender perfectly captures the dusty bloom on Oregon grape berries, a detail-level reward for painters working close. Raw Umber grounds the creek scenes where salmon stage, appropriately dark and unremarkable.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

The First Defector Branch

Subject: A single bigleaf maple branch turning gold against a wall of green canopy — the one break in summer’s consensus, lit by early September sun while everything else holds firm.

The Defector Gold mix (Quinacridone Gold 2:Hooker’s Green 1) was built for this exact moment. The unstable border where gold and green negotiate on the paper mirrors the leaf turning from its veins outward.

  • Paint the Hooker’s Green canopy first as a broad, confident wash — this is the majority, and it should feel like a wall
  • While the green is still damp at the edges, lay Quinacridone Gold into the single defector branch, letting the two pigments create that unstable transition zone
  • Don’t fully mix the Defector Gold — let the gold dominate the leaf centers with green at the margins
  • Indanthrone Blue at the bottom anchors the cooling water; its granulation should read as a different texture from the smooth canopy above
Prompt 2

Pink Salmon Pushing Upstream

Subject: A humped pink salmon back breaking the surface of a creek, mottled olive and pink, with creek gravel visible through shallow water around it.

The Salmon Olive mix (Olive Green 2:Raw Umber 1:Burnt Sienna Light touch) captures the mid-transition skin of a spawning pink salmon — not one color but several arguing on the same body.

  • Work wet-on-wet for the mottled salmon back — Olive Green should be uneven, because salmon backs never aren’t
  • Touch Raw Umber into the darker dorsal stripe while the base is still wet, then the barest Burnt Sienna Light where the pink is beginning to show through
  • Raw Umber at full concentration for the creek gravel, applied with a dry-brush drag to get the pebbly texture
  • The water around the fish should be a thin wash of Indanthrone Blue — cold, granulating, making the warm salmon tones pop
Prompt 3

Oregon Grape in Berry

Subject: A close-up of Oregon grape clusters — dusty blue-purple berries on wiry red-brown stems against dark understory shadow, a detail-level study that rewards careful observation.

Lavender is deployed specifically for this — Daniel Smith’s PrimaTek mineral pigment granulates into the exact dusty bloom on Oregon grape berries. Burnt Sienna Light handles the wiry stems.

  • Paint each berry as a small circle of Lavender at medium concentration, leaving a tiny white highlight on each — the dusty bloom catches light
  • Burnt Sienna Light for the stems: thin, wiry lines connecting the berry clusters, using a rigger brush
  • Hooker’s Green darkened with a touch of Indanthrone Blue for the background understory — keep it out of focus
  • This is a small-format, close-observation painting; work at actual size or slightly larger, no bigger than 5x7 inches
Prompt 4

Last Bioluminescence

Subject: A fading bioluminescent glow in dark water — still present but no longer commanding, a ghost of summer dissolving into the cooling strait.

The Last Glow mix (Phthalo Green BS 1:Phthalo Blue GS 1:Indanthrone Blue 2) tells the story in its ratio — Indanthrone Blue now dominates what was once a phthalo-led glow. The light is leaving.

  • Use more water in your glow mix than you would for a peak-summer bioluminescence painting — dilution is dimming
  • Pre-wet a dark wash of Indanthrone Blue, then drop the diluted phthalo glow into it and let it drift and dissipate
  • Chinese White used extremely sparingly — maybe three or four pinpoint sparks, the last few, not the many
  • Let Indanthrone Blue’s granulation encroach on the smooth phthalo territory; that textural shift from smooth glow to granulating dark is the water telling you summer is ending
Notes: All pigments confirmed Daniel Smith. Burnt Sienna Light is distinct from regular Burnt Sienna — it’s more transparent and less red, better for the subtle pink-through-olive of salmon transitional skin. For the remaining bioluminescence passages, keep transparency high in the glow zones but don’t fight the dimming — let Indanthrone Blue’s granulation encroach on the phthalo territory. That textural shift from smooth glow to granulating dark is how the water tells you summer is ending. Lavender is Daniel Smith’s PrimaTek line — genuine mineral, heavy granulation, beautiful in small doses.
