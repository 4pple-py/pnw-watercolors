Week 32 — PNW Watercolor Series

First Yellow

One leaf on the bigleaf maple has turned. Just one — bright as a struck match against a wall of green that still thinks it’s July. Late August in the Pacific Northwest is a held breath: the creeks are low, the sword ferns are curling at their tips, and somewhere in the canopy a single branch has decided summer is over. You notice it the way you notice a wrong note — everything else is still playing green, but this one leaf has changed key. The pivot is always quieter than you expect.

Primary Trio

Hansa Yellow Deep

The first turned leaf, that specific warm yellow that says autumn before anything else does; transparent, staining, holds its chroma even in the thinnest wash

Quinacridone Gold

The deeper golden transition two days behind the first yellow; rich amber undertone with gentle granulation, the color of honey held up to afternoon light

Sap Green

The still-dominant canopy, summer green hanging on; transparent and mixable, the baseline against which that first yellow screams

Mixes & Ratios

Pivot Gold

2 Hansa Yellow Deep : 1 Quinacridone Gold : ¼ Sap Green

The exact moment a leaf stops being green and starts being gold — the hinge color of the whole season.

Hansa Yellow Deep
Quin. Gold
Sap Green
Hansa Yellow Deep (PY65) is the structural color here — a warm, transparent yellow with real body that holds its saturation even in thin washes. Quinacridone Gold deepens the mix toward amber without cooling it, adding that rich undertone that separates a turning leaf from a sunlit green one. The whisper of Sap Green is critical: it keeps the gold from reading as pure yellow, anchoring it to the green it’s departing from. On wet paper, the Quinacridone Gold will granulate slightly, creating the mottled texture of a leaf mid-turn. Apply Hansa Yellow Deep as the base wash, then drop Quinacridone Gold into the still-wet surface and let it bloom — the color will find its own gradients.

Last Green

2 Sap Green : 1 Hooker’s Green : ½ Olive Green

The canopy that still believes in summer — deep, tired green, not quite ready to admit what’s coming.

Sap Green
Hooker’s Green
Olive Green
Sap Green is late-summer green — not the electric spring version, but a mature, slightly dusty green that has been photosynthesizing for three months straight. Hooker’s Green pushes the mix toward the deep shadows under the canopy where the light barely reaches, adding the blue-green depth of Douglas fir and western red cedar. Olive Green is the key: it introduces that yellow-brown undertone of green that’s starting to fatigue, the color of leaves that haven’t turned yet but are thinking about it. Layer Sap Green first as a broad canopy wash, then drop Hooker’s Green into shadow areas while still damp. Touch Olive Green into the edges where green meets sky — that’s where the transition whispers.

Dry Leaf

2 Burnt Sienna : 1 Transparent Brown Oxide : ¼ Quinacridone Gold

The leaf that turned a week ago and is already crisp — curled at the edges, caught in the dry grass below the tree.

Burnt Sienna
Trans. Brown Oxide
Quin. Gold
Burnt Sienna (PBr7) provides the warm, opaque base of a dried leaf — that particular ruddy brown of a bigleaf maple leaf one week past its golden peak. Transparent Brown Oxide adds depth without weight, its full transparency letting lower layers show through the way a real dry leaf is translucent at its thinnest points. Quinacridone Gold at the edges recalls the gold the leaf used to be, the memory of color fading from the margins inward. Work dry-on-dry for the crisp, curled texture: load Burnt Sienna onto a thirsty brush and drag it with broken strokes. While still tacky, touch Transparent Brown Oxide into the center where the veins darken. Let the Quinacridone Gold rim the edges like a fading halo.

Why These Colors?

This palette is about a ratio: mostly green, punctuated by yellow. That’s what the forest actually looks like in late August — not a blaze of fall color, but a vast green canopy with occasional bright interruptions. Hansa Yellow Deep and Quinacridone Gold carry the turning leaves, each at a different stage: Hansa is the fresh turn, all bright optimism, while Quinacridone Gold is two days further along, deeper and more committed. Sap Green and Hooker’s Green still dominate — summer isn’t done, the conifers haven’t noticed, and even the deciduous canopy is mostly holding. Olive Green is the bridge color, the green that’s yellowing from within, and it does more narrative work than anything else on this palette. Lemon Yellow handles the brightest, freshest turned leaves — the ones catching direct sun that almost hurt to look at. New Gamboge warms the middle register between green and gold. Burnt Sienna and Transparent Brown Oxide are here for the early casualties — leaves already curled and dropped, evidence that this change has been underway longer than you thought. French Ultramarine provides the late-summer sky, still warm but deepening, and mixed with Sap Green makes the shadowed evergreen masses that frame everything.

Painting Prompts

Late summer in the PNW is a study in ratios — how much green, how much gold, and where the first yellow appears. These prompts lean into the tension between seasons.

Prompt 1

The First Turned Branch

Subject: A single branch of bigleaf maple, fully turned to gold and yellow, hanging against a wall of deep green canopy. The branch catches afternoon sun while everything around it is still in summer mode. Forest floor below, ferns still green but curling at the tips.

The Pivot Gold mix (Hansa Yellow Deep + Quinacridone Gold + Sap Green) is the star — vary the ratio leaf by leaf across the branch. The Last Green mix provides the massive green backdrop that makes the yellow sing by contrast.

  • Paint the green canopy backdrop first using the Last Green mix on wet paper — this needs to be a unified dark mass so the yellow branch reads against it like a signal flare
  • Each leaf on the turned branch should be slightly different: some pure Hansa Yellow Deep, some leaning toward Quinacridone Gold, a few still holding traces of Sap Green at the stem end
  • Leave crisp white paper edges on the sunlit yellow leaves to suggest backlighting — the contrast between hard-edged yellow and soft-edged green canopy creates depth
  • Drop a few Lemon Yellow highlights on the brightest leaves where direct sun hits — this cooler, more electric yellow pops against the warmer Hansa Yellow Deep base
Prompt 2

Creek Trail in Late August

Subject: A trail along a PNW creek, canopy overhead forming a green tunnel. Scattered yellow leaves on the trail surface and floating in the low creek. Dappled light. The air has that end-of-summer weight — warm but tilting toward something else.

Hooker’s Green and Sap Green for the overhead canopy tunnel, individual fallen leaves painted with Hansa Yellow Deep and New Gamboge on the trail, Burnt Sienna for the packed-earth surface. French Ultramarine reflected in the shallow creek water.

  • Establish the green tunnel with a large wet wash of Sap Green overhead, dropping Hooker’s Green into the deepest shadows where branches overlap — let the pigments mingle on paper
  • The trail is Burnt Sienna with touches of Transparent Brown Oxide, worked dry to suggest packed earth and gravel — scatter Hansa Yellow Deep leaves across it with a small round brush
  • Creek water is French Ultramarine thinned to near-transparency, with reflected Sap Green dropped wet-in-wet — floating yellow leaves are Lemon Yellow with crisp dry edges sitting on the wet blue wash
  • Dappled light means irregular patches of warm New Gamboge on the trail where sun breaks through — paint the shadows first, then lift out the light shapes with a damp brush
Prompt 3

Maple Leaf Progression

Subject: Five bigleaf maple leaves arranged in a row, each at a different stage of turning: full green, green with yellow edges, half-turned, full gold, and dried brown. A botanical study of one leaf’s entire journey through the pivot.

The full palette in sequence — Sap Green and Hooker’s Green for leaf one, Olive Green bridging into Hansa Yellow Deep for leaf two, the Pivot Gold mix for three, pure Quinacridone Gold for four, and the Dry Leaf mix (Burnt Sienna + Transparent Brown Oxide) for five.

  • Paint each leaf the same size and shape to emphasize the color progression — the consistent form makes the color shift the subject, not the structure
  • Leaf two is the hardest: use Olive Green as the base with Hansa Yellow Deep creeping in from the edges inward, the green-to-gold transition happening within a single leaf
  • The full-gold leaf (number four) should glow — apply Quinacridone Gold to damp paper so it granulates slightly, then touch New Gamboge into the veins while wet for luminous depth
  • The final dried leaf gets the Dry Leaf mix applied to dry paper with a nearly dry brush — broken, scratchy strokes that convey the brittle, curled texture of a leaf past its prime
Prompt 4

View Through the Canopy

Subject: Looking straight up through a bigleaf maple canopy. Mostly green leaves with scattered yellow ones, backlit by a deep late-summer sky. The view you get lying on your back in a PNW park in late August, counting defectors.

French Ultramarine for the sky seen through gaps, the Last Green mix for the majority of leaves, Hansa Yellow Deep and Lemon Yellow for the turned ones glowing with backlight. New Gamboge where sun passes through leaves and warms them to translucence.

  • Start with the sky: paint French Ultramarine into the negative spaces between leaves, working carefully around leaf shapes — the sky is the lightest value and sets the backlight for everything
  • Green leaves are Sap Green with Hooker’s Green in the overlapping areas — paint them as flat shapes since you’re looking at their undersides, simplify the forms
  • Backlit yellow leaves are Lemon Yellow at the thinnest parts (where light passes through) shifting to Hansa Yellow Deep at the thicker center — the value change within each leaf suggests translucence
  • Where two leaves overlap, let New Gamboge and Sap Green mix on paper to create that specific green-gold you see when a yellow leaf overlaps a green one against the sky
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Hansa Yellow Deep (PY65) is a warm, transparent, staining yellow with excellent lightfastness — it holds its intensity in thin washes better than most yellows. Quinacridone Gold (PO49) granulates gently and has a rich amber quality that no cadmium yellow can match. Sap Green (PG36/PY150) is a convenience mix but a good one — transparent and versatile. Olive Green (PY150/PG7) bridges green and gold naturally, making it the most important transitional pigment this week. Transparent Brown Oxide (PBr41) is one of Daniel Smith’s most glazeable earth tones, fully transparent and endlessly layerable without going muddy.
