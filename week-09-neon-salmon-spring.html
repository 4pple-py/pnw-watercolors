Mixes & Ratios

Bloom Core

2 Opera Pink : 1 Pyrrol Red : touch Buff Titanium

Full-sun salmonberry, the kind of pink that makes you check if your eyes are working correctly.

Opera Pink
Pyrrol Red
Buff Titanium
Opera Pink gives you the fluorescent punch, but Pyrrol Red does something Quinacridone Rose can’t — it shifts the bloom toward a hot coral-pink at the petal edges where sun hits hardest. Both pigments are transparent, so the mix stays luminous even at full strength. Drop Buff Titanium into the wet center for the stamen ring. Paint each petal as a single stroke radiating outward. The tight buds get Pyrrol Red alone — deeper, more concentrated, the color of heat before it opens.

Canopy Ignition

2 Green Gold : 1 Lemon Yellow : touch Sap Green

Maple flowers dripping chartreuse before the leaves even show up — yellow-green so sharp it almost buzzes.

Green Gold
Lemon Yellow
Sap Green
Green Gold on its own is warm chartreuse. Adding Lemon Yellow sharpens it cooler and brighter — closer to that electric lime-green of actual maple flowers in early sun. All three components are transparent, so the mix glows on white paper. The Sap Green touch darkens the shadow side of each cluster without muddying the hue. Paint the flower chains as continuous downward strokes with a loaded round brush, varying pressure for the individual floret clusters.

Neck Flash

1 Duochrome Cactus Flower : 1 Cobalt Blue : touch Payne’s Gray

Band-tailed pigeon neck catching the light — that brief shimmer where gray becomes iridescent.

Duo. Cactus Flower
Cobalt Blue
Payne’s Gray
This is the fun one. Duochrome Cactus Flower shifts between pink and green depending on how it settles on the paper — on rough paper, the high points catch the pink and the valleys go green. Mixed with Cobalt Blue’s granulation, you get a shimmer that genuinely mimics iridescent feathers. Payne’s Gray anchors it as plumage rather than abstract color play. Apply wet to rough cold-press paper and don’t tilt — let the duochrome sort itself out. The body of the bird is Payne’s Gray alone; save this mix for the neck crescent only.

Why These Colors?

Opera Pink and Green Gold are non-negotiable for this week — no other pigments capture salmonberry neon and maple chartreuse this accurately. Pyrrol Red earns its spot by doing something Opera Pink can’t alone: it shifts the bloom-edge color toward a hot coral where sunlight concentrates, and it carries the tight buds at full intensity before they open. Lemon Yellow is cooler and sharper than the yellows used elsewhere in the series, which pushes the chartreuse mixes toward the specific electric lime of early maple flowers. Duochrome Cactus Flower is a calculated risk — it’s unpredictable on the paper and that’s the point. Band-tailed pigeon iridescence is unpredictable too. The pigment does what brushwork can’t fake. Cobalt Blue gets its first real sky appearance in the series — March earns it.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are built for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and what’s happening outside right now.

Prompt 1

Salmonberry Branch in Full Sun

Subject: A single salmonberry branch arching across the frame, three to five blooms at different stages — tight bud, half-open, full blast — with chartreuse maple flowers in the background.

The Bloom Core mix gives you the open flowers, Pyrrol Red alone handles the tight buds, and Canopy Ignition fills the background with that buzzing chartreuse. The bud-to-bloom progression lets you show the full range of the pink-to-coral shift.

  • Paint blooms on dry white paper — Opera Pink loses its fluorescent quality on damp surfaces
  • Tight buds are pure Pyrrol Red, small and concentrated; open blooms get the full Bloom Core mix
  • Drop Buff Titanium into each open flower center while still damp for the stamen ring
  • Sap Green for calyx and stems, Burnt Umber where branch thickens — keep these quick and structural
Prompt 2

Carbon River Trail After Rain

Subject: Wet gravel trail through moss-covered alders, puddles reflecting a March sky that’s turning blue, salmonberry blooms lining both sides.

Sap Green on damp paper builds the moss without effort. Cobalt Blue in the puddle reflections — and finally, the actual sky — anchors the cool side while Opera Pink blooms pop along the trail edges.

  • Lay the sky first: Cobalt Blue graded wash, stronger at the top, letting some white paper through for clouds
  • Moss on tree trunks is Sap Green applied to damp paper over a dry Burnt Umber trunk base
  • Trail is Burnt Umber and Payne’s Gray mixed loosely — skip the brush for gravel texture
  • Salmonberry along the edges goes on last, dry paper, full strength — the neon hits harder against the muted surroundings
Prompt 3

Band-Tailed Pigeons in the Alders

Subject: A loose flock moving fast through bare alder crowns, half-hidden by the first green haze of leaf buds.

Payne’s Gray for the body, the Neck Flash mix for the iridescent crescent. The Duochrome Cactus Flower will sort itself into pink and green on the paper — which is exactly what pigeon neck feathers do in changing light.

  • Paint the birds with quick strokes — a crescent for the body, a V for the tail, don’t overdefine
  • While the Payne’s Gray body is still damp, touch the Neck Flash mix into the neck area and let it bleed
  • Lift the white tail band with a damp brush while the wash is wet — one clean horizontal line per bird
  • Dot Green Gold and Lemon Yellow along branch tips for the first leaf buds — tiny, sparse
Prompt 4

Nurse Log Close-Up

Subject: A fallen nurse log carpeted in moss, with a single salmonberry bloom resting on it — neon pink on neon green.

Sap Green layered with Green Gold builds the moss. The single Opera Pink bloom on the moss surface is a small focal point that earns most of the attention in the painting.

  • Build moss in two passes: wet Sap Green base, then Green Gold on the dry ridges where sun catches
  • The log itself shows through as Burnt Umber in the gaps between moss
  • Paint the fallen bloom small — three-quarter inch at most — Bloom Core mix on dry paper
  • Payne’s Gray in the shadow under the log gives the greens something dark to push against
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. This is the highest-chroma palette in the series — Opera Pink is fluorescent and may glow under certain lighting. Pyrrol Red (PR254) is a clean, intense red with excellent lightfastness; it mixes toward coral with Opera Pink rather than the berry-mauve you get with quinacridone reds. Duochrome Cactus Flower is a Daniel Smith interference pigment that shifts between pink and green depending on paper texture and viewing angle — apply to rough cold-press for maximum shift. Lemon Yellow (PY175) is cooler than Hansa Yellow Deep, giving sharper chartreuse mixes. Keep white paper aggressive in the design — these saturated colors need space to breathe.
