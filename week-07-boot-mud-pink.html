Week 7 — PNW Watercolor Series

Boot Mud Pink

Mid-February — the ground never dries. Raw Umber everywhere, the color of February’s personality. But look up at 7 AM and the sky’s going pink for the first time since October. And in the mud itself, impossibly, crocus shoots. February is ugly and beautiful at the same time.

Mixes & Ratios

February Floor

2 Raw Umber : 1 Burnt Sienna : touch Transparent Brown Oxide

The mud that takes your boot and considers keeping it — weeks of rain in every footprint, iron and clay and leaf rot.

Raw Umber
Burnt Sienna
Transp. Brown Oxide
Raw Umber and Burnt Sienna both granulate, so this mix separates on the paper into warm and cool particles — exactly what real mud does when it dries at the edges. Apply generously to damp paper and tilt. The Transparent Brown Oxide is the secret: glazed over the dried granulation, it adds depth without opacity, the way standing water darkens mud without hiding it. For boot prints, wait until the wash is damp and lift with a thirsty brush. The lifted areas will be cooler and lighter — standing water in the impressions. Don’t paint boot prints. Excavate them.

First Pink Sky

2 Potter’s Pink : 1 Buff Titanium : trace Rose Madder Permanent

Seven AM, mid-February — the eastern sky goes pink for the first time in months. Muted, mineral, not quite believing itself.

Potter’s Pink
Buff Titanium
Rose Madder Perm.
Potter’s Pink (PR233) is a chromium-tin mineral pigment — it granulates beautifully and has an earthy warmth unlike any other pink on the market. It looks like winter dawn light because it basically is winter dawn light: muted, chalky, not trying to be anything brighter than it is. The Buff Titanium keeps the mix pale and luminous. The trace of Rose Madder Permanent is for the horizon line only, where the pink concentrates just above the treeline — transparent, so it glows rather than sits. Apply as a graded wash, heavier Rose Madder at the horizon, dissolving into Potter’s Pink, then mostly Buff at the zenith.

Crocus Through Gravel

1 Cobalt Violet : 1 Raw Umber (side by side, not premixed)

A violet shoot pushing up through brown earth — the whole argument of February in two pigments touching.

Cobalt Violet
Raw Umber
Don’t premix these. Paint the crocus shoot in Cobalt Violet on dry paper — a thin stroke, maybe two inches tall. While it’s still damp, touch Raw Umber at the base where the shoot meets the earth. Let the brown bleed upward into the violet no more than a third of the way. That boundary — where mud becomes flower — is the painting. Cobalt Violet (PV14) granulates just like Potter’s Pink, giving the crocus a fragile, particulate quality that feels right for something this new and this brave. For the surrounding gravel, use Raw Umber with Payne’s Gray, dry brush, letting the paper texture do the work.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built on a single contrast: mud and beauty. Raw Umber, Burnt Sienna, and Transparent Brown Oxide give you three ways to paint February’s dominant personality — granulating, transparent, layerable, each one a different temperature of wet earth. Against that brown ground, Potter’s Pink and Rose Madder Permanent split the dawn between mineral muting and transparent glow. Potter’s Pink granulates, which means it settles into the paper’s texture the way real dawn light settles into cloud — uneven, particulate, quietly warm. Cobalt Violet is the crocus: granulating too, fragile, the only cool chromatic note that isn’t brown or gray. French Ultramarine handles the cold sky above the pink horizon, and Payne’s Gray anchors every shadow and fence post. Hansa Yellow Light is thin early light catching a puddle edge. Buff Titanium is the pale glow that makes the dawn passages breathe. Every pigment earns its place by being either mud or the thing that defies it.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are built for the tension in this week’s palette — mud below, beauty arriving, and the contrast between them doing all the work.

Prompt 1

Muddy Garden Path

Subject: A garden path in mid-February — churned to mud by weeks of boots and rain, stepping stones half-submerged, a wheelbarrow abandoned in the muck. But the sky above is going pink, and two crocus shoots have appeared at the path’s edge.

The February Floor mix (Raw Umber + Burnt Sienna + Transparent Brown Oxide) handles the entire foreground. Potter’s Pink is the sky. Cobalt Violet is the crocus. The painting’s story is the distance between the ground and what’s growing through it.

  • Paint the path first with the February Floor mix on damp paper — tilt the board and let the granulation do the texture work. The mud should occupy at least two-thirds of the painting
  • Lift boot prints with a thirsty brush while the wash is still damp — the lifted areas read as standing water in the impressions
  • Paint the stepping stones by masking or lifting — Payne’s Gray with a touch of Buff Titanium, flatter and cooler than the mud around them
  • The crocus shoots are tiny. Two strokes of Cobalt Violet at the path edge, Raw Umber bleeding in at the base. Let them be small. They’re supposed to be almost missed
Prompt 2

Dawn from the Porch

Subject: The view from a covered porch at 7 AM, looking east. The sky is going pink above the treeline — the first pink dawn since October. A muddy yard in the foreground, a fence, a pair of boots by the door.

The First Pink Sky mix (Potter’s Pink + Buff Titanium + trace Rose Madder Permanent) is the entire upper half. French Ultramarine is the cold sky above the pink band. The February Floor mix is the yard. This is a painting about looking up after months of looking down at mud.

  • Paint the sky as a graded wash — French Ultramarine at the top, transitioning through Potter’s Pink + Buff Titanium, with Rose Madder Permanent concentrated at the horizon where the pink is strongest
  • The treeline silhouette is Payne’s Gray, painted into the still-damp sky edge so it bleeds slightly — trees at dawn are soft, not crisp
  • Paint the muddy yard with the February Floor mix, keeping it deliberately ugly. The contrast between the beautiful sky and the churned yard is the whole painting
  • The boots by the door are Payne’s Gray + Raw Umber — small, dark shapes that anchor the foreground and tell you someone’s about to step out into it
Prompt 3

Crocus Pushing Through Gravel

Subject: A close study of crocus shoots breaking through gravel and mud — violet against brown, the fragile against the blunt. Three shoots at different stages: one just a point, one an inch tall, one showing the first hint of a bud.

The Crocus Through Gravel technique (Cobalt Violet + Raw Umber, not premixed) is the core of this painting. Both pigments granulate, giving the shoots and the earth a shared particulate texture that makes them look like they belong to the same world.

  • Paint the gravel ground first — Raw Umber + Payne’s Gray with dry brush on rough paper, letting the paper texture stand in for individual stones
  • Each crocus shoot is Cobalt Violet, painted after the ground is fully dry. One stroke per shoot. Touch Raw Umber at the base and let it bleed upward — that boundary is where mud becomes flower
  • For the most developed shoot, add a tiny stroke of Hansa Yellow Light at the tip where the bud is about to open — crocus stigma gold, barely visible
  • Keep the background out of focus with a soft wash of Transparent Brown Oxide — this is a close-up, and everything beyond the crocus should recede
Prompt 4

Rubber Boots on a Fence

Subject: A pair of rubber boots upended on fence posts to dry, silhouetted against a pink-and-gray February sky. Mud-caked, practical, the landscape’s most honest portrait. The sky behind them is doing something beautiful that the boots don’t care about.

Payne’s Gray + Raw Umber for the boots and fence posts — dark, grounded, functional. The First Pink Sky mix fills the background. The painting is about indifference meeting beauty: the boots face down, the sky faces up, and neither notices the other.

  • Paint the sky first with the First Pink Sky mix — Potter’s Pink + Buff Titanium grading to Rose Madder Permanent at the horizon, French Ultramarine at the top
  • Paint the boots and fence posts as strong silhouettes with Payne’s Gray, adding Raw Umber for the mud caked on the soles. Keep the shapes simple and blocky — boots are not graceful objects
  • Add Burnt Sienna for the dried mud on the boot soles — a warm note against the cool gray, the boots carrying evidence of the ground even when they’re off it
  • Leave a thin line of Buff Titanium along the boot edges where the dawn light catches the rubber — the only moment where the boots and the sky acknowledge each other
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Potter’s Pink (PR233) is a chromium-tin sphene — it granulates heavily and has a muted, mineral quality unlike any synthetic pink. It’s the only pink that looks like winter dawn rather than summer. Cobalt Violet (PV14) granulates too, which gives the crocus passages a fragile, particulate quality that flat-washing violets can’t achieve. Transparent Brown Oxide is the sleeper on this palette — used as a glaze over dried mud washes, it adds depth the way standing water darkens earth without hiding it. Temperature runs warm in the mud tones and splits to cool only in French Ultramarine and Payne’s Gray. Three of the ten pigments granulate (Raw Umber, Potter’s Pink, Cobalt Violet), which means the palette does texture work on its own if you let it.
