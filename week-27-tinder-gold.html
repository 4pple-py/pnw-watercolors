Week 27 — PNW Watercolor Series

Tinder Gold

The hillsides have turned to straw. Grass crunches underfoot like something already burning. Fire danger signs ratcheted to red weeks ago and stayed there. The air tastes of dust and dried fennel, and every afternoon the wind comes from the east — hot, bone-dry, carrying the memory of a hundred summers that ended in smoke.

Primary Trio

Yellow Ochre

The color of dry grass at eye level — opaque, earthy, the baseline of every sun-baked hillside from the Cascades east

Raw Sienna

Tinder gold itself, the deeper amber of grass seed heads catching late light; transparent, warm, the essence of desiccation

Quinacridone Gold

Amber heat haze made visible — semi-transparent, granulating slightly, the warmest gold in the Daniel Smith line

Mixes & Ratios

Flashpoint Gold

2 Quinacridone Gold : 1 Transparent Pyrrol Orange : ½ Hansa Yellow Deep

The specific amber of grass that could ignite in an hour — gold pushed toward orange, shimmering with stored heat.

Quinacridone Gold
Trans. Pyrrol Orange
Hansa Yellow Deep
Quinacridone Gold provides the structural amber — semi-transparent, with the slight granulation that reads as heat shimmer on rough paper. Transparent Pyrrol Orange pushes the gold toward that dangerous threshold color where dry grass stops looking pastoral and starts looking combustible. Hansa Yellow Deep brightens the highlights where seed heads catch direct backlight. All three pigments are transparent, so this mix stays luminous even at full concentration. Apply to dry paper with a fan brush for the feathered texture of grass heads, or wet-into-wet for the blurred look of distant hillsides dissolving in heat.

Smoke Ridge

2 Neutral Tint : 1 French Ultramarine : ¼ Yellow Ochre

The color of distant mountains when smoke has been in the valley for a week — neither blue nor gray, but something in between that makes your eyes ache.

Neutral Tint
French Ultramarine
Yellow Ochre
Neutral Tint is the smoke itself — a convenience mix of blue-black pigments that reads as atmospheric rather than colorful. French Ultramarine provides just enough blue to keep the distant ridges from going dead gray, the remnant of actual sky color struggling through particulate. Yellow Ochre is the warmth the smoke carries from fire — smoke is not cool gray, it’s tinted by what burned. Apply in graded horizontal washes, each ridge lighter and warmer than the last. The furthest ridge should be almost entirely Yellow Ochre + Neutral Tint, with the French Ultramarine visible only in the closest range.

Charred Stump

2 Lamp Black : 1 Burnt Sienna : ¼ Olive Green

Last year’s burn scar — blackened wood with the faintest green where moss has begun its slow return.

Lamp Black
Burnt Sienna
Olive Green
Lamp Black is the char — dense, opaque, the pigment of carbon itself. Burnt Sienna warms the black where the char has weathered to expose the underlying wood, giving the stump a history beyond the burn. The touch of Olive Green at the base suggests the beginning of recovery, moss starting its decades-long reclamation of the dead wood. Apply Lamp Black with a dry brush on rough paper for the cracked, fissured texture of burned bark. While still damp, drop Burnt Sienna into the deepest fissures where wood grain shows through. Olive Green goes in last, barely touching the base with a damp brush.

Why These Colors?

Everything is gold and drying to tinder. Yellow Ochre, Raw Sienna, and Quinacridone Gold layer the dry grass from opaque baseline to transparent amber to granulating heat — three golds that are actually three different stories about the same dying hillside. Olive Green is the only green left, and it looks exhausted: drought-stressed, yellowing at the edges, barely holding on in the shade of fence lines and creek banks. Transparent Pyrrol Orange is the alarm color, that amber quality where everything looks like it could ignite — pure chroma warning. Hansa Yellow Deep intensifies the backlit grass to a brightness that makes you squint. Burnt Sienna is the baked soil, the earth that has given up all its moisture. French Ultramarine is the sky you can barely see through the smoke haze, rationed like water. Neutral Tint is the haze itself — not quite blue, not quite gray, the color of an entire valley filling with particulate. Lamp Black is the end of the story: char, dead wood, the burn scars that every PNW summer leaves behind.

Painting Prompts

Everything in the landscape is a value study in gold right now. These prompts lean into the narrow warmth of this palette — the challenge is making ten shades of dry look interesting.

Prompt 1

Golden Hillside — Fire Danger Red

Subject: A rolling grass hillside east of the Cascades, entirely gold and amber, with a fire danger sign reading “EXTREME” at the roadside. Smoke haze on the distant ridgeline. The sky is not blue but a washed-out yellow-white.

The Flashpoint Gold mix (Quinacridone Gold + Transparent Pyrrol Orange + Hansa Yellow Deep) handles the hillside. The Smoke Ridge mix for the distant mountains. Yellow Ochre for the road shoulder. Lamp Black for the sign post.

  • Paint the sky first with heavily diluted Yellow Ochre + Neutral Tint — this is not a blue sky, it’s smoke-filtered light, warm and flat and slightly oppressive
  • The hillside is the Flashpoint Gold mix applied in broad diagonal strokes following the slope — vary the ratio across the hill, more Transparent Pyrrol Orange on the sun-facing slope, more Quinacridone Gold in the hollows
  • Distant ridgelines get progressively lighter and warmer Smoke Ridge washes — each layer of distance adds more Yellow Ochre and less French Ultramarine
  • The fire danger sign is Lamp Black post with a single dot of Transparent Pyrrol Orange for the “red” indicator — keep it small and sharp against all that soft gold
Prompt 2

Fence Line — Last Green

Subject: A barbed-wire fence line where Olive Green survives in a narrow strip of shade — the fence’s shadow is the only thing keeping a thin line of green alive. Gold grass everywhere else. A study in contrast between drought and the barest survival.

Olive Green in its element — drought-stressed, yellowish, clinging to shade. Yellow Ochre and Raw Sienna own everything outside the shadow line. Burnt Sienna for the fence posts. Lamp Black for the wire.

  • Paint the gold field first with Yellow Ochre at full coverage, grading into Raw Sienna in the middle distance — let it dry completely
  • The shadow strip is a narrow band of Olive Green mixed with a touch of Quinacridone Gold to push it toward exhaustion — this green should look like it’s barely surviving, not thriving
  • Fence posts are Burnt Sienna, sun-bleached and dry, with Lamp Black shadows on the shaded side. Barbed wire is Lamp Black applied with a rigger brush in a single continuous stroke
  • The shadow of the fence wire on the ground is Neutral Tint diluted almost to nothing — just enough to read as shade without competing with the main shadow strip
Prompt 3

Smoke Valley — Cascades Obscured

Subject: A wide valley view where the Cascades should be visible but aren’t — smoke has erased them to a series of ghostly ridgeline echoes, each fainter than the last. The foreground is sharp and golden; the background dissolves into haze.

The Smoke Ridge mix (Neutral Tint + French Ultramarine + Yellow Ochre) in progressively lighter layers for the disappearing ridges. Flashpoint Gold for the sharp foreground. The painting is about the contrast between presence and erasure.

  • Work back to front: the farthest ridge is barely visible — the palest wash of Neutral Tint with Yellow Ochre, almost white. Each successive ridge gets slightly darker and bluer
  • The nearest ridge should still not be fully saturated — even the closest mountains are compromised by smoke. French Ultramarine should be visible but muted
  • A sharp transition to the foreground: Flashpoint Gold at full intensity, dry-brushed for grass texture. The contrast between blurred background and crisp foreground is the whole painting
  • Leave the very top of the paper white or barely tinted with Yellow Ochre — the sky in smoke season is not blue, it’s a blank white-gold void
Prompt 4

Burn Scar — Last Season’s Fire

Subject: The edge of a year-old burn scar where blackened stumps meet unburned gold grass. The border is abrupt — fire stops where fire stops. New growth pushing through char at the base of stumps.

The Charred Stump mix (Lamp Black + Burnt Sienna + Olive Green) for the burn zone. Flashpoint Gold for the surviving grass. The painting is split down the middle between two worlds — black and gold.

  • Divide the composition roughly in half — char on one side, gold on the other. The border should be irregular but decisive, painted with a dry edge on the char side
  • Charred stumps are the Charred Stump mix applied with a dry brush on rough paper — the skip of the dry brush across the texture creates the cracked, fissured look of burned bark
  • At the base of each stump, touch in Olive Green with a small round brush for the new shoots pushing through — these should be tiny and hopeful against all that black
  • The gold side is Quinacridone Gold and Yellow Ochre, alive and untouched, a reminder of what the fire took. Hansa Yellow Deep at the boundary where backlit grass catches fire’s-edge light
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Yellow Ochre (PY43) is a natural iron oxide — opaque, granulating, and the most physically accurate dry-grass pigment available. Quinacridone Gold (PO49) is semi-transparent and granulates lightly on rough paper, adding texture that mimics heat shimmer. Raw Sienna (PBr7) is transparent and layers beautifully without muddying. Transparent Pyrrol Orange (PO71) is one of Daniel Smith’s most intense transparent oranges. Neutral Tint is a convenience mix — useful for atmospheric smoke effects where you want desaturation without color bias. Lamp Black (PBk6) is a carbon black, dense and absolute.
