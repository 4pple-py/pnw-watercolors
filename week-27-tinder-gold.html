Week 27 — PNW Watercolor Series

Tinder Gold

Mid-July noon, everything crackles. Salmonberries split on the stem, thimbleberries dissolve on the tongue, and the forest floor hasn’t seen rain in six weeks. Dry easterlies strip moisture from cedar bark until it curls like parchment. Summer is hoarding — abundant and withholding at once.

Primary Trio

Quinacridone Gold

The controlling light here, mid-spectrum warmth that reads as parched noon glare on every dry surface it touches. Semi-transparent, granulates lightly on rough paper.

Raw Sienna

The anchor underfoot, bleached trail dust and hardpack. Transparent and low-staining, it lets Quinacridone Gold boss it around in washes.

Sap Green

The lone holdout, surviving understory clinging to whatever groundwater remains. Mixes down into olive tones fast when it meets the warm dominants.

Mixes & Ratios

Easterly Haze

3 Buff Titanium : 1 Quinacridone Gold

Bleached warmth of a wind-dried ridgeline where the easterlies have scoured every surface pale.

Buff Titanium
Quinacridone Gold
Load the brush with dilute Buff Titanium first, then touch Quinacridone Gold into the wet field and let it drift. The opacity of Buff fights the transparency of QG — use that tension. Don’t overwork it. Let the gold find its own edges in the haze.

Cedar Tinder

2 Burnt Sienna : 1 Raw Sienna : touch Indigo

Parched bark ready to spark — the kind of dry that makes fire crews lose sleep.

Burnt Sienna
Raw Sienna
Indigo
Mix Burnt Sienna and Raw Sienna on the palette first to get the base bark tone, then feather in the barest whisper of Indigo while still wet. The Indigo should read as shadow in the bark fissures, not as blue. If you can see blue, you used too much.

Berry Crush

1 Pyrrol Scarlet : 1 Quinacridone Gold

Salmonberry juice on fingertips — that specific sticky-sweet stain that won’t wash off until tomorrow.

Pyrrol Scarlet
Quinacridone Gold
Wet-on-wet, letting the two pigments mingle without stirring. Pyrrol Scarlet is opaque and pushy; Quinacridone Gold is transparent and yielding. The result should look like crushed fruit — uneven, vivid, a little vulgar. Drop onto damp paper for the best bleed.

Why These Colors?

This palette runs hot and dry by design. Quinacridone Gold and Raw Sienna are both transparent earth-spectrum pigments that layer without going muddy, essential for building up the bleached, repetitive warmth of a rainless July. Sap Green provides the lone biological counterpoint, but it’s outnumbered — mixed with any of the warm dominants, it immediately shifts toward olive and exhaustion. Pyrrol Scarlet punches through as the one saturated hue, the way a split salmonberry shocks against brown duff. Buff Titanium’s opacity serves a specific atmospheric purpose: heat shimmer is not transparent. Indigo and Cerulean Blue Chromium hold the only coolness, rationed like water.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Gravel Road Heat Shimmer

Subject: A forest service road stretching into the distance, heat shimmer blurring the middle ground, dry grass shoulders fading to bleached gold. One of those July roads where the air itself is visible.

The Easterly Haze mix (Buff Titanium + Quinacridone Gold) was built for this — the opacity of Buff Titanium physically creates the density of heat shimmer on paper. Raw Sienna is the hardpack road itself. Cerulean Blue Chromium holds the one patch of sky between smoke wisps.

  • Paint the road with Raw Sienna, grading lighter toward the horizon where the heat bleaches it — the transparency lets you build the effect in layers
  • The shimmer zone is the Easterly Haze mix applied to damp paper so the edges blur. Don’t overwork it — let the Buff Titanium’s opacity and Quinacridone Gold’s transparency fight each other
  • Dry grass shoulders are Quinacridone Gold dry-brushed on rough paper, with Hansa Yellow Deep at the tips catching backlight
  • A single strip of Cerulean Blue Chromium sky at the top, granulating and hard, is all the cool relief the scene gets
Prompt 2

Cedar Bark Tinder Study

Subject: A tight crop on peeling cedar bark — the long curling strips that make fire crews nervous. A botanical-level close-up of texture, color, and dryness.

The Cedar Tinder mix (Burnt Sienna + Raw Sienna + touch of Indigo) is precisely this subject. Transparent Brown Oxide adds the duff layer beneath. The palette was designed around this exact kind of parched, combustible warmth.

  • Base the bark with Burnt Sienna in vertical strokes following the grain, then layer Raw Sienna into the lighter areas where the bark has bleached
  • The Indigo goes into bark fissures only — if you can see blue, you’ve used too much. It should read as shadow, nothing more
  • Peeling strips are painted with a rigger brush: Burnt Sienna at the attached end, grading to Raw Sienna where the curl catches light
  • Transparent Brown Oxide dry-brushed at the base for the accumulated duff — drag it across cold-press texture for instant forest floor
Prompt 3

Salmonberry Split Open

Subject: A cluster of salmonberries on the branch — some intact, some split to reveal juice-bright interiors. The kind of trail-side moment that stops you mid-hike.

The Berry Crush mix (Pyrrol Scarlet + Quinacridone Gold, 1:1 wet-on-wet) is literally salmonberry juice. Sap Green handles the leaves, and the contrast between that lone surviving green and all the warm dominants tells the whole drought story.

  • Drop the Berry Crush mix onto damp paper for each split berry — Pyrrol Scarlet’s opacity and Quinacridone Gold’s transparency create the uneven, vivid look of crushed fruit naturally
  • Intact berries are Pyrrol Scarlet alone, less dilute, with a highlight of white paper reserved on each one
  • Leaves are Sap Green, but mix it with a touch of Quinacridone Gold to push it toward olive — nothing stays pure green this late in a dry July
  • The branch is Raw Sienna with Burnt Sienna shadows, thin and wiry
Prompt 4

Douglas Fir Shadow Pool

Subject: The deep shadow under a lone Douglas fir in an otherwise sun-blasted clearing — the kind of shade that feels ten degrees cooler. Looking from sun into shadow, with the grass color shifting from gold to green at the threshold.

Indigo is rationed in this palette like water, and this is where you spend it. Quinacridone Gold and Raw Sienna own the sunlit clearing. Sap Green survives only in the shade. The painting is really about the boundary between two worlds.

  • Paint the sunlit clearing first: Quinacridone Gold graded into Raw Sienna, warm and dry, covering most of the paper
  • The shadow pool is Indigo at medium dilution — not black, just deeply cool. Lay it in with a single confident wash after the gold is fully dry
  • At the shadow’s edge, Sap Green marks where grass is still alive, mixed with a trace of Quinacridone Gold so it reads as struggling, not thriving
  • Buff Titanium haze along the distant treeline completes the story — even the air is hot
Notes: All pigments confirmed Daniel Smith. Buff Titanium is the most opaque color here — use it for atmospheric effects where you want the paper to disappear. Transparent Brown Oxide is underrated for dry-brush work on textured paper; drag it across cold-press for instant duff. Cerulean Blue Chromium granulates beautifully — let it do so in sky passages rather than fighting the texture.
