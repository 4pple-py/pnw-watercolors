Week 28 — PNW Watercolor Series

Heat Phosphor

After midnight the bay gives back the day’s heat as light. Each paddle stroke detonates blue-green fire in the black water. Dinoflagellates bloom in the warm shallows, and every disturbance — a hull, a jellyfish, a hand trailed over the gunwale — triggers a chain of cold sparks that outlast the gesture. The water remembers your movement in light.

Primary Trio

Phthalo Blue (Green Shade)

The bioluminescent glow itself — transparent, staining, electric; sinks into the paper fiber and glows from within like dinoflagellates glow from within water

Phthalo Green (Blue Shade)

Electric phosphorescence, the green side of the glow spectrum; equally staining, equally impossible to lift — commit like the light does

Indanthrone Blue

Deep night water between the glow trails; granulating, dark, the blackness that makes the phosphorescence legible

Mixes & Ratios

Dinoflagellate Glow

2 Phthalo Blue (Green Shade) : 2 Phthalo Green (Blue Shade) : ¼ Cobalt Teal Blue

The exact color of Noctiluca scintillans in warm water — that electric blue-green that makes you question whether water can actually do this.

Phthalo Blue GS
Phthalo Green BS
Cobalt Teal Blue
Phthalo Blue GS and Phthalo Green BS together create the exact blue-green of dinoflagellate bioluminescence — this is not an approximation, it’s the actual color. Both phthalos are transparent and staining, which means they sink into the paper fiber and glow from within the way real bioluminescence glows from within water. The touch of Cobalt Teal Blue at the brightest points adds opacity — the flash of a dinoflagellate is briefly opaque before it fades. Apply to pre-darkened dry paper. Work fast: single brushstrokes, no reworking. Bioluminescence is instant and irreversible, and your painting of it should be too.

Abyssal Dark

2 Indanthrone Blue : 1 Ivory Black : ½ Lamp Black

The water between glow events — not quite black, not quite blue, the deep that makes the light meaningful.

Indanthrone Blue
Ivory Black
Lamp Black
Indanthrone Blue is the foundation — it granulates into the paper’s valleys, creating a texture that reads as depth rather than flatness. Its inherent blueness keeps the dark from going neutral, preserving the sense that this is water, not void. Ivory Black has a warm, brownish undertone that suggests organic depth — water full of life, not empty. Lamp Black is the true neutral darkness that pushes the deepest zones beyond color entirely. Mix heavily on the palette, apply in a single confident wash over the entire sheet before any glow work begins. This is your ground; everything else sits on top of it.

Phosphor Fade

2 Cascade Green : 1 Indanthrone Blue : ¼ Duochrome Cactus Flower

The afterglow in a paddle stroke’s wake — where the bright flash has subsided to a lingering, shifting luminescence that changes color as it dies.

Cascade Green
Indanthrone Blue
Duochrome Cactus Fl.
Cascade Green is softer than the phthalos — the diffused glow after the initial flash, when the light has spread across a wider area of water and lost its electric edge. Indanthrone Blue pulls the green toward darkness as the glow fades, grading from visible to invisible. Duochrome Cactus Flower is the secret weapon: this interference pigment shifts from green to pink depending on viewing angle, mimicking the way real bioluminescence seems to change color as it dissipates. Apply Cascade Green in loose, spreading strokes radiating outward from the bright glow center, grade into Indanthrone Blue at the edges, and touch Duochrome Cactus Flower into the transitions while wet. Tilt the painting under a lamp to see the color shift.

Why These Colors?

Bioluminescence IS Phthalo territory — that staining, electric blue-green. Phthalo Blue Green Shade and Phthalo Green Blue Shade together create the exact color of dinoflagellate glow, and their transparency means they sink into paper fiber the way real bioluminescence sinks into water: the light comes from within. Duochrome Cactus Flower shifts color with viewing angle — green to pink, mimicking the way bioluminescence seems to change color as it fades and your angle of observation shifts. Cobalt Teal Blue handles the brighter individual flashes, its opacity creating the momentary density of a triggered dinoflagellate before the light disperses. Indanthrone Blue is the deep water and the darkness that makes all the light legible — without enough dark, bioluminescence just looks like blue paint. Two blacks because night water needs both: Lamp Black for true darkness and Ivory Black for the warmer, organic depths. French Ultramarine for the night sky overhead, granulating into stars. Cascade Green for the softer, diffused glow in the shallows. New Gamboge is the only warm note — a distant shore light, the warm night air itself, the reminder that this electric cold light exists in the hottest week of the year.

Painting Prompts

These are night paintings. The palette is built for darkness punctuated by light — let the dark ground do most of the work and save your bright pigments for the moments that matter.

Prompt 1

Paddle Strike — First Light on Dark Water

Subject: A kayak paddle blade entering dark water, the impact detonating blue-green bioluminescence in a radiating burst. The paddle shaft disappears into darkness above. The water surface is black except where it’s alive with light.

The Dinoflagellate Glow mix (Phthalo Blue GS + Phthalo Green BS + Cobalt Teal Blue) is exactly this subject. The Abyssal Dark mix for the surrounding water. A single moment of contact between human gesture and biological response.

  • Paint the entire sheet with the Abyssal Dark mix first — a single, even, very dark wash. Let it dry completely. Everything else is painted on top of this ground
  • The glow radiates outward from the paddle blade’s point of entry — apply the Dinoflagellate Glow mix in a burst pattern, brightest at the blade and fading outward in loose concentric arcs
  • Touch Cobalt Teal Blue into the very brightest point while the phthalos are still wet — its opacity creates the flash that reads as actual light, not just color on dark paper
  • The paddle shaft is Lamp Black applied with a rigger brush — a single dark line descending from above, minimal detail, because your eyes have adapted to the dark and the paddle is felt more than seen
Prompt 2

Bioluminescent Wake — San Juan Channel

Subject: A boat’s wake seen from above and behind, the disturbed water lit up in a long V-shaped trail of blue-green phosphorescence. Dark islands on either side of the channel. Stars above.

The Dinoflagellate Glow mix for the wake trail, the Phosphor Fade mix (Cascade Green + Indanthrone Blue + Duochrome Cactus Flower) for the edges where the glow dissipates. French Ultramarine for the night sky. Lamp Black for the island silhouettes.

  • Compose the V-wake as the central feature — it should fill the lower two-thirds of the page, with the boat implied but not visible at the top edge
  • The brightest glow is at the wake’s center line: Dinoflagellate Glow mix at full intensity. Grade outward through the Phosphor Fade mix to the dark water at the edges
  • Island silhouettes are Lamp Black, hard-edged and featureless — at night, islands are just absences against sky. The sky is French Ultramarine at moderate dilution with white paper dots left for stars
  • Apply Duochrome Cactus Flower into the wake’s outer edge while the Cascade Green is still damp — the interference pigment will shift color depending on the viewing angle, exactly like real bioluminescence
Prompt 3

Hand in Glowing Water

Subject: A hand trailing over the side of a boat, fingers parting the dark water and leaving five parallel trails of blue-green light. The hand itself is silhouetted. An intimate, human-scale encounter with the phenomenon.

Dinoflagellate Glow for the finger trails. Cobalt Teal Blue for the brightest flashes where fingertips break the surface tension. New Gamboge for the warm skin tones of the hand’s top surface catching ambient light.

  • The hand is the compositional center — paint it larger than feels natural, filling a quarter of the sheet. The intimacy of the subject demands close framing
  • Five parallel glow trails radiating from the fingertips, each a single stroke of the Dinoflagellate Glow mix with a round brush — one confident pull per finger, no reworking
  • The hand’s underside is silhouetted in Ivory Black against the glow, but the top surface catches a faint warm light from above — a thin wash of New Gamboge to suggest moonlight or distant shore glow on skin
  • The water between the finger trails is Abyssal Dark — the contrast between the five bright lines and the four dark gaps is what makes the image read
Prompt 4

Shoreline Glow — Breaking Wavelets

Subject: A rocky shoreline where small waves break against barnacle-covered rocks, each wavelet igniting a line of blue-green phosphorescence along the impact zone. The shore curves away into darkness. Warm night air above, cold light below.

Cascade Green for the diffused glow in the shallows, Phthalo Green BS for the bright impact lines, Indanthrone Blue for the deeper water. New Gamboge for the warm night atmosphere above the waterline. Lamp Black for the rocks.

  • The composition divides horizontally: warm dark above the waterline (French Ultramarine sky with a wash of New Gamboge near the horizon for warmth), cold glowing dark below
  • Paint the shore rocks as dark masses in Lamp Black with Indanthrone Blue shadows, leaving the gap between rock and water as a thin bright line for the glow
  • The breaking wavelets are Phthalo Green BS applied as a thin line along each rock’s waterline — the impact zone where water meets stone and the dinoflagellates fire. Apply to dry dark paper with a rigger brush
  • Cascade Green fills the shallow water behind the breaking waves with a softer, diffused glow — this is the afterlight, spreading and fading. Let it grade into Indanthrone Blue as the water deepens away from shore
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Phthalo Blue Green Shade (PB15:3) and Phthalo Green Blue Shade (PG7) are both intensely staining — they cannot be lifted once dry, so every mark is permanent. Indanthrone Blue (PB60) granulates and dries lighter than it appears wet; build value in layers. Duochrome Cactus Flower is an interference pigment that shifts from green to pink-red depending on viewing angle and underlying color — apply over dark grounds for maximum effect. Cobalt Teal Blue (PG50) provides genuine opacity in a palette otherwise dominated by transparent stainers. Cascade Green is a Daniel Smith convenience mix, softer than raw phthalo intensity. New Gamboge (PY97 + PY150) is a warm, transparent yellow-orange.
