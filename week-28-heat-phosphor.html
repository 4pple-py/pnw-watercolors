Week 28 — PNW Watercolor Series

Heat Phosphor

Triple digits pin the valley flat. Blackberry canes sag under fruit and sun alike. But after midnight the bay exhales: Noctiluca stir in warm shallows, first blue-green sparks answering the oar. The tension is thermal — scorched days feeding bioluminescent nights.

Primary Trio

Phthalo Green BS

The Noctiluca flash itself, terrifyingly intense at full strength; transparent, staining, impossible to lift — commit to it

Phthalo Blue GS

The deeper glow trail where light diffuses into dark water; equally staining, equally permanent

Quinacridone Burnt Orange

The furnace sunset that precedes the glow, the daytime half of this palette's thermal equation

Mixes & Ratios

Noctiluca Spark

2 Phthalo Green BS : 1 Phthalo Blue GS : touch Chinese White

First bioluminescent pinprick on a paddle blade — the moment the dark water answers your movement with light.

Phthalo Green BS
Phthalo Blue GS
Chinese White
Work on dry, dark-washed paper. The Chinese White touch creates the opacity of the brightest spark point — without it, the phthalos just glow. With it, they ignite. Apply with a fine round brush, single stroke, no reworking. Bioluminescence is instant; your brushwork should be too.

Bramble Furnace

2 Quinacridone Burnt Orange : 1 Burnt Sienna

Blackberry cane baking at 98 degrees — the thorns almost shimmer in the heat distortion.

Quin. Burnt Orange
Burnt Sienna
Both pigments are transparent, so this mix stays luminous even at medium concentration. Layer it over a dry Hansa Yellow Medium wash for sun-baked cane. The Burnt Sienna grounds the orange, keeps it from floating into neon territory.

Strait Midnight

3 Indanthrone Blue : 1 Phthalo Blue GS

Black water between glow-trails — the darkness that makes the bioluminescence legible.

Indanthrone Blue
Phthalo Blue GS
Indanthrone Blue is the workhorse dark here — granulating, deep, almost black at full concentration. The touch of Phthalo Blue GS keeps it from going completely neutral, preserving just enough chromatic energy to read as water rather than void. Mix on the palette, apply in confident single washes. Lifting is not an option with these stainers.

Why These Colors?

The palette is engineered around a thermal cycle. The daytime colors — Quinacridone Burnt Orange, Burnt Sienna, Hansa Yellow Medium — are warm, dry, and exhausted. The nighttime colors — both phthalos, Cascade Green, Indanthrone Blue — are cool, liquid, and electric. Chinese White bridges the two as the hottest point of light in either world. Carbazole Violet marks the transition hour. The phthalos are chosen specifically for their transparency and staining power: bioluminescence reads as light coming from within the paper, which means you need pigments that sink into the fiber and glow from there. Opaque pigments would kill the effect dead.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Bioluminescent Paddle Stroke

Subject: A kayak paddle entering dark water, the blade detonating blue-green light that trails behind in a glowing arc. The scene is nighttime — dark water, dark sky, and this impossible light answering human movement.

The Noctiluca Spark mix (Phthalo Green BS + Phthalo Blue GS + touch of Chinese White) is this exact subject. Indanthrone Blue provides the dark water. The dual-phthalo approach gives you the full bioluminescent spectrum in a single painting.

  • Start by laying a full dark ground: Indanthrone Blue at near-full concentration, washed over the entire sheet and dried completely. This is your water
  • The glow goes on dry dark paper — Phthalo Green BS for the brightest zone around the paddle blade, graduating into Phthalo Blue GS as the trail disperses
  • Touch Chinese White into the very brightest point while the phthalos are still wet — this creates the opaque spark that reads as actual light, not just color
  • The paddle shaft is just a dark silhouette in Lamp Black or more Indanthrone Blue — don’t detail it. The light is the subject, not the tool
Prompt 2

Blackberry Bramble in the Furnace

Subject: Blackberry canes heavy with fruit in full August heat — thorns shimmering, leaves curling slightly, berries at every stage from green to dead ripe. The heat distortion is almost visible.

The Bramble Furnace mix (Quinacridone Burnt Orange + Burnt Sienna) handles the sun-baked canes. Hansa Yellow Medium as an underlayer gives the heat-blanched effect. Carbazole Violet does double duty as ripe berry shadows and the dusk color approaching.

  • Lay a dilute Hansa Yellow Medium wash over the entire bramble area first and let it dry — this is your heat base, the reason everything looks sun-baked
  • Canes go over that with the Bramble Furnace mix in loose, tangled strokes — blackberry is chaos, so embrace messy brushwork
  • Berries at different stages: Raw Umber for green ones, Carbazole Violet + Indanthrone Blue for the ripe dark ones, and pure Indanthrone Blue for the overripe
  • Leave thorns as tiny slivers of the Hansa Yellow Medium underlayer showing through — don’t paint thorns, let the underpainting reveal them
Prompt 3

Furnace Sunset into Bioluminescent Night (Diptych)

Subject: A two-panel study — left panel is the scorching sunset (Quinacridone Burnt Orange sky, dark silhouette headlands), right panel is the same bay hours later alive with bioluminescence. Same composition, two thermal states.

This palette was engineered around a thermal cycle, and a diptych lets you use the full range: the daytime colors (Quinacridone Burnt Orange, Burnt Sienna, Hansa Yellow Medium) on one side, the nighttime colors (both phthalos, Cascade Green, Indanthrone Blue) on the other. Carbazole Violet bridges the two as the transition hour.

  • Sunset panel: Quinacridone Burnt Orange graded from the horizon upward, meeting Carbazole Violet at the zenith. Headland silhouettes in Indanthrone Blue at full concentration
  • Night panel: Indanthrone Blue sky and water base, dried completely, then bioluminescent trails in Phthalo Green BS with Phthalo Blue GS
  • The water line should be at the same height in both panels — this sells the same-location conceit
  • Cascade Green in the night panel’s shallows for the diffused, softer glow that fills the bay between the bright paddle-stroke trails
Prompt 4

Dried Mud Tideline

Subject: A close-up of cracked mud at the tideline in August heat — the geometric patterns of drought, with a few remnant tide pools reflecting sky. Small and textural, almost abstract.

Raw Umber is exactly dried mud. The cracking pattern is a drawing exercise that turns into a painting when you add Cascade Green tide pool reflections and Quinacridone Burnt Orange for the warm light hitting the raised mud edges.

  • Draw the crack pattern first with a pencil — the geometry is important and hard to get right wet-into-wet
  • Base wash of Raw Umber over the entire mud field, then darken the cracks with more concentrated Raw Umber while damp
  • Raised mud edges catch light: a touch of Quinacridone Burnt Orange on the sunward side of each crack ridge
  • Tide pools are small negative spaces left as white paper, then glazed with dilute Cascade Green or Phthalo Blue GS to reflect the sky. Keep them very still — no brushwork visible
Notes: All pigments confirmed Daniel Smith. For bioluminescence passages, keep transparency high — the phthalos and Cascade Green should be your workhorses, with Chinese White reserved only for the brightest spark points. Avoid granulating pigments in glow zones; granulation reads as particulate matter, which is the opposite of liquid light. Save Indanthrone Blue's granulation for the dark water surrounding the glow. Cascade Green is Daniel Smith's convenience mix — useful here for its softness compared to raw phthalo intensity.
