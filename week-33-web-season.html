Week 33 — PNW Watercolor Series

Web Season

August mornings, every garden fence and hedge is strung with silk. The orb weavers have been busy all night, and now the dew arrives to make their work visible — each strand beaded with droplets that catch the low sun like tiny prisms. For a few minutes the whole yard is a cathedral of geometry and refracted light. Then the sun climbs, the dew burns off, and the webs go invisible again. You have to be early and you have to be looking.

Primary Trio

Buff Titanium

The pale warmth of web silk catching sidelight; semi-opaque, gentle, the quietest warm note in the palette — silk before the dew finds it

Cobalt Blue

Morning dew refracting sky; granulating, luminous, each granule a tiny blue lens on the strand — the color of water remembering the sky

Duochrome Cactus Flower

Prismatic light shifting through dewdrops; color-shifting pigment that changes with viewing angle, mimicking refraction in miniature

Mixes & Ratios

Ghost Silk

3 Buff Titanium : 1 Chinese White : ¼ Lavender

The near-invisible thread of a web in sidelight — barely there, cooler than air, warmer than nothing.

Buff Titanium
Chinese White
Lavender
Buff Titanium is the structural pigment here — its semi-opacity on white paper reads as silk catching just enough light to be seen. Chinese White lifts it further toward the paper tone, keeping the thread from disappearing entirely. The whisper of Lavender shifts the mix cool, the way morning air tints everything slightly violet before the sun fully warms. Apply with a rigger brush in single confident strokes. The line should be barely visible against a dark background — if you can see it easily, you’ve loaded too much pigment. Real spider silk is thinner than thought.

Dewdrop Prism

2 Duochrome Cactus Flower : 1 Cobalt Blue : ½ Transparent Pyrrol Orange

The tiny rainbow inside a single dewdrop — prismatic, shifting, impossible to pin down.

Duochr. Cactus Flower
Cobalt Blue
Trans. Pyrrol Orange
Duochrome Cactus Flower is doing something no conventional pigment can — it literally shifts color depending on viewing angle and layer thickness, which is exactly what refracted light does inside a water droplet. Cobalt Blue anchors the cool side of the spectrum and contributes granulation that reads as individual drops separating on paper. Transparent Pyrrol Orange provides the warm flash — the red-orange spike you see when light passes through water at the right angle. Do not premix these on the palette. Instead, lay Cobalt Blue dots for individual dewdrops, then touch Duochrome Cactus Flower into each while still wet, and finally add the smallest possible touch of Transparent Pyrrol Orange to one edge of each drop. Let the pigments mingle on paper — the result is different every time, which is exactly right.

Garden Dark

2 Neutral Tint : 1 Sap Green : ½ Payne’s Gray

The deep shadow behind the web — hedge and fence in pre-dawn dark, making the silk visible by contrast.

Neutral Tint
Sap Green
Payne’s Gray
The web is only visible because the background is dark. Neutral Tint provides the depth without pulling toward any color bias — it’s the blackboard the silk writes on. Sap Green keeps the dark reading as vegetation rather than void; this is a garden hedge, not outer space. Payne’s Gray adds the blue-gray atmospheric quality of early morning shadow. Apply this mix generously as a background wash first, let it dry completely, then paint the web strands over it with the Ghost Silk mix. The contrast between dark background and pale silk is the entire painting. Get the dark rich enough and the silk will practically glow.

Why These Colors?

Spider webs are invisible until light and water conspire to reveal them. This palette is built around that revelation. Buff Titanium and Chinese White handle the silk itself — barely-there warmth against dark backgrounds, the semi-opacity mimicking how silk catches sidelight without fully reflecting it. Cobalt Blue earns its place through granulation: each granule on paper reads as an individual dewdrop, separating naturally the way real dew beads on silk rather than sheeting. Duochrome Cactus Flower is the secret of this palette — its genuine color-shifting behavior replicates what happens inside a dewdrop acting as a prism, something no conventional pigment can do. The color changes when you tilt the painting, just as prismatic light shifts when you change your angle to the web. Transparent Pyrrol Orange provides the warm spike in the spectrum — that flash of fire you see when sunrise hits a dew-laden web at exactly the right angle. Lavender cools the morning atmosphere. French Ultramarine handles the deeper sky refractions where larger drops act as stronger lenses. Payne’s Gray and Neutral Tint build the essential dark backdrop without which no web is visible. Sap Green keeps the garden present — this is a hedge corner, not a void.

Painting Prompts

These subjects are built around the interplay of silk and light — each one uses the palette’s unique color-shifting and granulating pigments to capture something most people walk right past.

Prompt 1

Orb Web in Morning Dew

Subject: A complete orb web strung between two fence posts, every strand beaded with dew, backlit by low morning sun. The web fills the frame. Behind it, a dark hedge in deep shadow. The geometry is perfect — radial threads and spiral capture silk, each junction marked by a larger drop.

Garden Dark mix (Neutral Tint + Sap Green + Payne’s Gray) for the hedge background, Ghost Silk mix (Buff Titanium + Chinese White + Lavender) for the threads, and the Dewdrop Prism mix for individual dew beads catching prismatic light.

  • Paint the dark background first and let it dry completely — the entire success of this painting depends on having a rich, deep backdrop for the silk to glow against
  • Use a rigger brush loaded with the Ghost Silk mix for the web strands — single strokes, no going back over them. Real silk is one continuous line and your brush stroke should be too
  • Dew drops are tiny dots of clean water placed on the dry Ghost Silk lines, then touched with Cobalt Blue and Duochrome Cactus Flower while wet — the granulation and color-shift happen inside each drop
  • Add a single tiny touch of Transparent Pyrrol Orange to the dewdrops nearest the sun source — this warm prismatic flash against the cool blue drops creates the rainbow effect of refraction
Prompt 2

Single Strand with Droplets

Subject: A macro view of a single web strand stretching diagonally across the frame, five or six dewdrops hanging from it in descending sizes. Each drop acts as a tiny lens, refracting the garden behind it upside-down. Soft-focus green background.

Buff Titanium for the silk strand, Cobalt Blue and Duochrome Cactus Flower for the drops, Sap Green softened with Lavender for the out-of-focus garden behind.

  • The background is a soft wet-into-wet wash of Sap Green and Lavender — keep it unfocused and gentle, the way a macro lens throws everything behind the subject into blur
  • The silk strand is a single rigger-brush line of Buff Titanium, slightly thicker where it sags under the weight of the largest drops
  • Each dewdrop is a small oval of clean water, then Cobalt Blue floated into the bottom half (gravity pulls pigment down, mimicking how light concentrates in the lower hemisphere of a hanging drop)
  • Touch Duochrome Cactus Flower into the highlight edge of each drop — the color-shift effect will vary with each drop, which is exactly right for prismatic refraction
Prompt 3

Web Between Douglas Fir Branches

Subject: A large web suspended between two Douglas fir branches at the edge of a forest trail. Morning fog drifts through the trees behind. The web is slightly damaged — a section torn and re-spun, the repair threads different from the original geometry. Fog catches in the web like the finest fabric.

Payne’s Gray and French Ultramarine for the foggy forest depth, Sap Green for the fir branches, Ghost Silk mix for the web itself, Lavender washed through the entire scene for morning atmosphere.

  • Lay a full-sheet wash of dilute Lavender first — everything in this scene is filtered through cool morning fog, and this undertone unifies the entire painting
  • The forest background is Payne’s Gray and French Ultramarine wet-into-wet, with soft lost edges everywhere — fog erases boundaries between trees
  • Fir branches in the foreground are Sap Green darkened with Neutral Tint, painted with crisp edges to contrast with the foggy background — this depth separation makes the web pop
  • The repaired section of the web should have slightly different thread angles than the original spiral — this imperfection tells a story and makes the painting feel observed rather than invented
Prompt 4

Sunset Web — Warm Refraction

Subject: A web at golden hour, the low sun turning every dewdrop into a point of warm light. The web hangs between tall grass stems at the edge of a PNW meadow. The background is warm-toned for once — amber grasses and a peach-colored sky near the horizon.

Transparent Pyrrol Orange dominates here — each dewdrop catches the warm sun. Buff Titanium for the silk, Cobalt Blue for the cool shadow side of the drops, French Ultramarine for the sky above the warm horizon band.

  • This prompt flips the palette’s temperature — instead of cool morning blue, the light source is warm. Let Transparent Pyrrol Orange and Buff Titanium lead, with blues playing supporting roles
  • The sky is a gradient from French Ultramarine at the top through Lavender to dilute Transparent Pyrrol Orange at the horizon — the full warm-to-cool transition of a PNW summer sunset
  • Grass stems are Sap Green at the base shifting to Buff Titanium at the sun-bleached tips — late summer meadow grass is half-golden by August
  • Each dewdrop gets a dominant warm treatment: Transparent Pyrrol Orange with a tiny crescent of Cobalt Blue on the shadow side. The warm drops against the cool upper sky create the magic-hour glow
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Duochrome Cactus Flower is an interference/duochrome pigment that genuinely shifts between pink-violet and green depending on viewing angle and background — it is irreplaceable for prismatic light effects. Cobalt Blue (PB28) granulates naturally, with each granule reading as a separate point of light or dew. Buff Titanium (PW6:1) is a reduced titanium white with a warm cream tone, semi-opaque, perfect for silk effects. Transparent Pyrrol Orange (PO71) is intensely transparent and staining, providing the warm flash in the prismatic spectrum. Chinese White (PW4) is zinc white, cooler and more transparent than titanium, ideal for web highlights without chalky opacity.
