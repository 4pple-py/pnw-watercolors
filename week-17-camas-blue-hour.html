Week 17 — PNW Watercolor Series

Camas Blue Hour

Blue hour turns camas meadows into extensions of the sky. The flowers and the overhead become the same blue-violet, and you lose the horizon in it. Cobalt Violet flower heads catch the last warm light. Moonglow granulates across everything — its pigments separating into the same blues and violets the landscape is already made of. The meadow breathes purple.

Primary Trio

Ultramarine Blue

The camas flower itself — slightly more violet-leaning than French Ultramarine, transparent, granulating, and ancient-feeling on paper

Cobalt Violet

Individual camas flower heads catching the last warm light; PV14 granulates beautifully, each stroke breaking into violet particles

Indanthrone Blue

Blue hour deep sky, the overhead darkness pressing down; transparent, slow-drying, with a red undertone that makes it glow rather than flatten

Mixes & Ratios

Camas Drift

3 Ultramarine Blue : 1 Cobalt Violet : ½ Quinacridone Rose

A thousand camas flowers seen from twenty feet — not individual blooms but a blue-violet current moving through grass.

Ultramarine Blue
Cobalt Violet
Quin. Rose
Ultramarine Blue is the engine — its violet lean already halfway to camas. Cobalt Violet (PV14) introduces granulation that breaks the wash into what reads as separate flower heads without painting a single one. Quinacridone Rose warms the mix just enough to keep it alive in the last light. Apply wet-in-wet on tilted paper and let gravity pull the pigments into drifts. Cobalt Violet will settle into the valleys of cold-pressed paper while Ultramarine floats — this separation is the whole effect.

Lost Horizon

2 Indanthrone Blue : 1 Moonglow : ½ Lavender

Where the meadow ends and the sky begins — or doesn’t. The line you came to find and couldn’t.

Indanthrone Blue
Moonglow
Lavender
Indanthrone Blue is transparent and deep — it makes the sky feel like it has actual depth behind it. Moonglow is the secret weapon: a multi-pigment convenience mix that separates on paper into blue, violet, and gray particles. It IS blue hour in a tube. Lavender lifts the value just enough for the transition zone where sky meets field. Wet the entire horizon band and float this mix across it in a single pass. The pigment separation will do the rest — you’ll get a horizon that dissolves rather than divides.

Dusk Meadow Floor

2 Sap Green : 1 Payne's Gray : ½ French Ultramarine

Grass at the hour when green gives up and lets blue take over — the meadow floor surrendering to twilight.

Sap Green
Payne's Gray
Fr. Ultramarine
Sap Green at dusk isn’t really green anymore — Payne’s Gray cools and darkens it into the blue-green that grass actually becomes when the sun drops below the horizon. French Ultramarine nudges the whole mix toward violet, matching the reflected sky color on wet grass blades. Use diluted for the middle-distance meadow floor between camas clumps. The granulation from French Ultramarine gives the grass texture without brushwork.

Why These Colors?

This palette is almost monochromatic on purpose — because blue hour is almost monochromatic on purpose. Ultramarine Blue and French Ultramarine occupy nearly the same hue space but behave differently: Ultramarine Blue leans slightly more violet (the camas flower), while French Ultramarine leans slightly warmer (the transitional sky). Cobalt Violet is the only true violet, and its PV14 granulation means every stroke breaks into particles that mimic individual flower heads. Indanthrone Blue handles the deep overhead sky with a transparency that feels like actual depth. Moonglow is the cheat code — its multi-pigment formula separates into the same blues, violets, and grays the landscape is already made of. Lavender provides the atmospheric haze between you and the far meadow. Sap Green and Payne’s Gray anchor the ground plane. Buff Titanium is the only warm holdout, the last ribbon of light at the horizon, and Quinacridone Rose gives the violet flowers their hidden warmth — the pink undertone you sense more than see.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Camas Meadow at Dusk

Subject: A wide Willamette Valley camas meadow at blue hour, the entire field a single blue-violet drift, dark tree line barely visible, sky and flowers merging into the same color.

The Camas Drift mix (Ultramarine Blue + Cobalt Violet + Quinacridone Rose) handles the entire meadow. The Lost Horizon mix dissolves the tree line into sky. This is a painting about losing edges, not finding them.

  • Wet the entire paper and work sky-down: Indanthrone Blue at the zenith, the Lost Horizon mix across the middle band, the Camas Drift mix for the meadow — all in one continuous wet-in-wet pass
  • Let Cobalt Violet’s granulation do the work of suggesting individual flowers — don’t paint them, let the pigment particles settle into the paper valleys
  • The tree line is a single stroke of Payne’s Gray while the wash is still damp, so it bleeds softly rather than cutting the composition in two
  • Leave one small strip of dry paper near the horizon and touch it with Buff Titanium — the last light, the only warm note in the entire painting
Prompt 2

Single Camas Stem Close-Up

Subject: One camas stem in sharp focus, six-petaled flowers open along the raceme, with the rest of the meadow blurred into a blue-violet wash behind it.

Cobalt Violet for the individual petals, Ultramarine Blue for the shadows between them. Quinacridone Rose warms the petal edges where the last light hits. Lavender blurs the background into haze.

  • Paint the background first: a wet-in-wet wash of Lavender and dilute Ultramarine Blue, completely soft and out of focus — let it dry fully
  • The stem is Sap Green darkened with a touch of Payne’s Gray, one confident vertical stroke with a rigger brush
  • Each petal is Cobalt Violet applied to dry paper — PV14’s granulation gives the petals texture that reads as real even at close range
  • Touch the petal tips with Quinacridone Rose while the violet is still damp for the warm glow of translucent petals catching side light
Prompt 3

The Horizon Line Disappearing

Subject: A minimal composition: just the horizon band where camas meadow meets blue-hour sky, the line dissolving as you watch. More feeling than scene.

The Lost Horizon mix (Indanthrone Blue + Moonglow + Lavender) was designed for exactly this moment. Buff Titanium is your only anchor to the physical world — the last warm line before everything goes blue.

  • This is an exercise in wet-in-wet courage: wet the full sheet and commit to a single pass, sky to ground, with no hard edges anywhere
  • Indanthrone Blue at the top, transitioning to the Lost Horizon mix at center, then French Ultramarine and Ultramarine Blue below — the sky and meadow should be nearly indistinguishable
  • While everything is still wet, drag a line of Buff Titanium across the horizon — it’s opaque enough to hold its shape briefly before the blues absorb it
  • Moonglow’s pigment separation will create the entire twilight atmosphere as it dries — resist the urge to add more. The paint knows what blue hour looks like.
Prompt 4

Meadow Path at Blue Hour

Subject: A mown path curving through a camas prairie at dusk, the path itself a darker line of trampled grass, camas rising on both sides, the whole scene sinking into violet.

The Dusk Meadow Floor mix (Sap Green + Payne’s Gray + French Ultramarine) gives you the path and the grass between flowers. Camas Drift handles the flowers themselves. The path is where you are; the flowers are where you want to be.

  • Block in the sky with Indanthrone Blue and Moonglow first, leaving the meadow area as white paper — let the sky dry before proceeding
  • The path is the Dusk Meadow Floor mix applied in a curving shape that leads the eye into the middle distance — darker (more Payne’s Gray) in the foreground, lighter as it recedes
  • Camas flowers flanking the path are the Camas Drift mix touched in while the grass wash is still slightly damp, so they bleed at the base but hold shape at the top
  • Drop concentrated Cobalt Violet into the nearest flower clumps for foreground detail — the granulation will give them presence that the distant flowers lack, creating natural depth
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Moonglow is a multi-pigment convenience mix (PB29, PR177, PBk6) that separates on paper into blue, violet, and gray particles — it is blue hour in a tube. Cobalt Violet (PV14) is a genuine cobalt pigment that granulates heavily; it’s expensive and worth it. Ultramarine Blue vs. French Ultramarine: both are PB29, but Daniel Smith’s Ultramarine Blue formulation leans slightly more violet. Buff Titanium (PW6:1) is opaque by nature — use it sparingly or it will flatten your washes.
