Camas Blue Hour

Camas prairies at dusk, the blue so dense it looks painted. Cottonwood resin sweetens the whole valley. A western tanager lands like a lit match on a dark branch. Spring running hot and cool simultaneously — fire-orange bird, ice-blue flower, and the tension between them is the whole season in one frame.

Mixes & Ratios

Camas Dusk

3 Phthalo Blue GS : 1 Cobalt Blue : ½ Indanthrone Blue

The field as one blue mass at arm's length — individual flowers dissolved into collective color.

Phthalo Blue GS
Cobalt Blue
Indanthrone
Phthalo Blue GS is the engine — staining, transparent, it floods the wash with camas blue. Cobalt Blue introduces granulation, breaking the flatness into something that reads as a thousand separate flowers. Indanthrone Blue deepens the shadows between stems. Apply wet-in-wet at high dilution for the field at distance; concentrate for foreground clusters. This mix stains permanently — commit to it.

Tanager Flash

2 Pyrrol Scarlet : 1 Transparent Pyrrol Orange : ½ Hansa Yellow Deep

Hot enough to see from fifty yards — the bird that evolution made visible on purpose.

Pyrrol Scarlet
Pyrrol Orange
Hansa Yellow
Pyrrol Scarlet is semi-opaque — it sits on the paper with presence, which is exactly what a tanager does on a branch. Transparent Pyrrol Orange adds fire without competing. Hansa Yellow Deep at the edges gives the breast its golden transition toward the belly. Mix on the palette and apply in one confident stroke. This bird doesn't wait for you to adjust.

Cottonwood Resin

2 Quinacridone Gold : 1 Green Gold : ½ Hansa Yellow Light

Sticky amber warmth you smell before you see the trees — the synaesthetic color of spring evening.

Quin. Gold
Green Gold
Hansa Yellow Lt
Quinacridone Gold granulates subtly, adding texture that feels like resin on bark. Green Gold keeps the mix alive — it's a living amber, not a dead one. Hansa Yellow Light lifts the value for the haze effect. Use diluted as a unifying glaze over dried washes to tie a composition together with evening warmth. Quinacridone Gold's slight granulation on cold-pressed paper is the whole trick here.

Why These Colors?

The palette is polarized by design — Phthalo Blue GS and Pyrrol Scarlet are near-complements that vibrate against each other the way camas fields vibrate against tanager plumage. Phthalo Blue GS is dangerously strong and staining; it needs Cobalt Blue and Indanthrone Blue to give it dimension. The warm side (Hansa Yellow Deep, Transparent Pyrrol Orange, Quinacridone Gold) is all transparent or semi-transparent, so the warmth glows from within. Green Gold and Phthalo Green BS bridge the temperature gap — greens that can live next to both the fire-orange bird and the ice-blue meadow without choosing sides.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Camas Prairie at Blue Hour

Subject: A wide camas meadow at dusk, the entire field dissolved into a single blue-purple mass, with a dark tree line and a sky going violet above.

The Camas Dusk mix (Phthalo Blue GS + Cobalt Blue + Indanthrone Blue) handles the whole meadow in one wash. Cobalt Blue’s granulation creates the illusion of individual flowers without painting a single one.

  • Wet the meadow area completely and flood in the Camas Dusk mix at medium dilution — Phthalo Blue GS stains permanently, so this is a one-shot commitment
  • While the wash is still wet, touch in concentrated Indanthrone Blue at the base of the tree line for shadows between stems
  • The tree line silhouette is Indanthrone Blue mixed with a breath of Phthalo Green BS, applied after the meadow dries
  • Leave a thin dry strip between meadow and sky so they don’t merge — the horizon line matters in a flat prairie composition
Prompt 2

Western Tanager on a Dark Branch

Subject: A single western tanager perched on a cottonwood branch, scarlet and gold against deep blue-green background.

The Tanager Flash mix (Pyrrol Scarlet + Transparent Pyrrol Orange + Hansa Yellow Deep) gives you the entire bird in three strokes. Phthalo Green BS behind it provides maximum complementary contrast.

  • Paint the background first: Phthalo Green BS with a touch of Indanthrone Blue, let it dry completely
  • The tanager’s head is pure Pyrrol Scarlet — one stroke, semi-opaque, it will sit on top of the dried background with physical presence
  • Transition from Pyrrol Scarlet to Transparent Pyrrol Orange to Hansa Yellow Deep down the breast while each color is still damp
  • The black wings are Indanthrone Blue, not black — it keeps the bird chromatic and lets the warm body blaze by contrast
Prompt 3

Cottonwood Grove at Golden Hour

Subject: Backlit cottonwood trees with translucent spring leaves glowing amber-gold, the resinous evening air almost visible as color.

The Cottonwood Resin mix (Quinacridone Gold + Green Gold + Hansa Yellow Light) is this exact scene in a puddle of paint. Hansa Yellow Deep does the backlit leaves.

  • Glaze the entire scene with dilute Hansa Yellow Light first to establish the golden-hour color temperature
  • Paint individual backlit leaves as Hansa Yellow Deep on dry paper — transparency lets the underlying gold wash glow through
  • The Cottonwood Resin mix works as a unifying glaze applied over the dried tree trunks to suggest resinous air
  • Quinacridone Gold’s subtle granulation on cold-pressed paper mimics the sticky texture of cottonwood bark — let the pigment do the work
Prompt 4

The Collision (Tanager Over Camas Field)

Subject: The full scene — a camas meadow at dusk with a tanager crossing the frame, fire-orange against ice-blue. The whole season in one painting.

This is why the palette is polarized. Phthalo Blue GS and Pyrrol Scarlet are near-complements that vibrate against each other the way this scene actually vibrates in life.

  • Paint the camas meadow first and let it dry thoroughly — Phthalo Blue GS needs to be fully set before you layer anything over it
  • The tanager can be small — even a centimeter across, the Pyrrol Scarlet will read against the blue because complementary contrast does the work of scale
  • Frame the bird against the darkest part of the meadow (concentrated Camas Dusk mix) for maximum pop
  • Add a Cottonwood Resin glaze along the horizon line to suggest the warm evening air where the blue field meets the golden sky
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Phthalo Blue GS is among the strongest tinting pigments available — a little obliterates a lot, so load your brush with respect. Pyrrol Scarlet is lightfast and semi-opaque; it won't glow from the paper the way Quinacridone Rose does, but it holds its ground. Indanthrone Blue is a slow dryer that rewards patience — let it settle before judging the value.
