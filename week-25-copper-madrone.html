Week 25 — PNW Watercolor Series

Copper Madrone

Madrone trunks glow copper-red in hard July light — the tree that looks like it's on fire and isn't. Ocean-spray cascades cream over roadside banks. Thimbleberries ripen soft as eyelids. Summer peaking with the confidence of something that knows it's temporary. The heat is real now, and the madrone wears it like jewelry.

Primary Trio

Aussie Red Gold

Madrone trunk mid-tone, the copper-red that defines the species; semi-transparent, warm, slightly granulating

Transparent Pyrrol Orange

Madrone bark at its hottest, where direct sun hits peeled wood; transparent, intense, the orange that reads as heat

Mayan Blue Genuine

Strait water afternoon, the turquoise counterweight to all that copper; mineral pigment, granulating, irreplaceable

Mixes & Ratios

Madrone Glow

2 Aussie Red Gold : 1 Transparent Pyrrol Orange : ½ Burnt Sienna Light

The impossible copper-red of peeled bark in direct sun — the color that makes people stop their cars.

Aussie Red Gold
Pyrrol Orange
Burnt Sienna Lt
Aussie Red Gold is the madrone's truth — warm, semi-transparent, with a subtle granulation that mimics bark texture. Transparent Pyrrol Orange adds the fire where the sun hits directly, pushing the copper toward molten. Burnt Sienna Light grounds the shadow-side, keeping the trunk three-dimensional. Apply in vertical strokes following the trunk's curve. Let the Aussie Red Gold granulate on textured paper — it will build bark detail for you.

Ocean-Spray Cream

3 Buff Titanium : ½ Cascade Green : ¼ Aussie Red Gold

Foam-white flower clusters cascading over roadside banks — cream waterfalls of tiny blooms.

Buff Titanium
Cascade Green
Aussie Red Gold
Buff Titanium in quantity — ocean-spray is cream, not white, and Buff Titanium's warm opacity is exactly that color. Cascade Green in the shadows between flower clusters, where leaf shows through. Aussie Red Gold barely there, just enough to warm the sun side. Apply with a dry-brush technique for the cascading effect — ocean-spray has that foamy, broken texture that dry brush captures. Let the paper's white show through as highlights.

Thimbleberry Soft

2 Pyrrol Scarlet : 1 Buff Titanium : ¼ Cobalt Blue

Ripe fruit so soft it falls apart in your fingers — the berry you eat but can never carry home.

Pyrrol Scarlet
Buff Titanium
Cobalt Blue
Pyrrol Scarlet provides the ripe red — not the dark red of a blackberry but the soft, dusty scarlet of a thimbleberry. Buff Titanium adds the dusty bloom on the fruit's surface, the matte quality that separates thimbleberry from raspberry. Cobalt Blue cools the shadow hemisphere and adds granulation that mimics the drupelets' individual roundness. Apply lightly — heavy application would suggest a tough fruit, and the thimbleberry is anything but.

Why These Colors?

This palette invests heavily in the warm spectrum because the madrone demands it. Two orange-family primaries (Aussie Red Gold and Transparent Pyrrol Orange) is unusual, but the madrone's range from copper mid-tone to fire highlight justifies both. Mayan Blue Genuine provides the only blue strong enough to hold its own against that much warmth — its mineral granulation adds substance that a transparent blue would lack. Burnt Umber on the shadow-side keeps the madrone dimensional. Hooker's Green is the toughest green on the palette, appropriate for madrone's leathery evergreen leaves. Cobalt Blue in the atmospheric role means even the sky leans warm this week.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Madrone Portrait — Sun Side and Shadow Side

Subject: A single madrone trunk filling most of the frame, peeling bark revealing smooth copper underneath. One side catches full July sun; the other falls into cool shadow.

The Madrone Glow mix (Aussie Red Gold + Transparent Pyrrol Orange + Burnt Sienna Light) handles the sun-struck side, while Burnt Umber takes the shadow. Two primaries dedicated to the same tree is unusual, and this subject justifies every bit of it.

  • Paint the sunlit bark with the Madrone Glow mix using vertical strokes that follow the trunk’s curve — the Aussie Red Gold will granulate on textured paper and build bark detail without you trying
  • Burnt Umber on the shadow side, applied while the sunlit side is still slightly damp at the edge so you get a soft transition — madrone trunks are round, not flat
  • Peeling bark curls are Transparent Pyrrol Orange at the edges where they catch light, Burnt Sienna Light where they curl away into shadow
  • Leave slivers of white paper where the freshly exposed wood is palest — the smoothest bark is almost white before it oxidizes to copper
Prompt 2

Ocean-Spray Cascading Over a Road Cut

Subject: A roadside bank covered in ocean-spray (holodiscus), its cream-white flower clusters pouring downhill like foam. Dark evergreen behind, dusty gravel below.

Buff Titanium is the exact cream of ocean-spray — not white, warmer and more opaque. The Ocean-Spray Cream mix (Buff Titanium + Cascade Green + Aussie Red Gold) was designed for exactly this. Hooker’s Green handles the dark backdrop.

  • Use dry-brush technique for the flower clusters — load a flat brush with the Ocean-Spray Cream mix and drag it across rough paper so the texture breaks up the stroke into the foamy, cascading quality of the flowers
  • Hooker’s Green behind the flowers, applied as a flat dark wash first, lets the cream flowers pop by contrast
  • Burnt Sienna Light at the bottom for the gravel road cut — dry brush again, lighter touch
  • Let the paper’s white show through the dry-brush flowers as your brightest highlights — don’t paint highlights, preserve them
Prompt 3

Thimbleberry Close-Up

Subject: Two or three thimbleberries on the branch, one ripe red, one still green, with the large maple-shaped leaves behind them. The intimate scale of a trail-side discovery.

The Thimbleberry Soft mix (Pyrrol Scarlet + Buff Titanium + Cobalt Blue) captures the dusty, matte quality that makes thimbleberries look different from every other red berry. Cascade Green and Hooker’s Green split the leaf duties.

  • The ripe berry is Pyrrol Scarlet with Buff Titanium mixed in — this desaturates the red just enough to get the dusty bloom. Apply lightly; heavy paint would make the fruit look tough, and thimbleberries fall apart at a touch
  • Cobalt Blue on the shadow side of each drupelet adds granulation that mimics the individual bumps on the fruit surface
  • The unripe berry is Cascade Green with a whisper of Cobalt Blue — cool and hard where the ripe one is warm and soft
  • Big thimbleberry leaves are Hooker’s Green with Cascade Green in the veins — paint the leaf as a single wash, then lift the veins with a damp brush while wet
Prompt 4

Island Shoreline at Low Tide

Subject: A madrone-lined shore at low tide — copper trunks leaning over exposed rocks and tidal pools, with the strait turquoise beyond. The classic San Juan composition in July heat.

Madrone Glow for the trees, Mayan Blue Genuine for the water, Cobalt Blue for the hazy sky — the full warm-to-cool range of this palette in one scene. Burnt Sienna Light handles the exposed tidal rocks.

  • Start with the sky: a graded wash of Cobalt Blue, heavier at the top, nearly nothing at the horizon where marine haze bleaches it
  • The strait is Mayan Blue Genuine applied to wet paper — let the mineral granulation settle and create the texture of moving water on its own
  • Madrone trunks are the Madrone Glow mix, but push the Transparent Pyrrol Orange harder on the trunks that face the water, where reflected light intensifies the copper
  • Tidal rocks are Burnt Sienna Light base with Burnt Umber shadows and touches of Cascade Green where seaweed clings
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Aussie Red Gold is a PrimaTek pigment with unique granulating properties — it settles into paper texture in a way that reads as bark naturally. Burnt Sienna Light is lighter and warmer than regular Burnt Sienna, better for July trail dust. Mayan Blue Genuine continues from Week 24 — if you have it on your palette, it's still relevant. Pyrrol Scarlet applied thinly reads very differently than applied thickly; test your dilution for thimbleberry.
