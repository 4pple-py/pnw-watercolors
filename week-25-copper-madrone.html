Week 25 — PNW Watercolor Series

Copper Madrone

Madrone bark peels in sheets, revealing copper underneath that feels more like skin than wood. Late June, the trees shed their papery outer layer and stand exposed — smooth, warm, catching light like hammered metal. The bark curls hang like scrolls nobody asked to read. Heat presses down through the canopy and the whole hillside smells like sun on bare wood.

Primary Trio

Transparent Red Oxide

The deep copper of peeling bark mid-strip, where the outer layer lifts away; fully transparent, warm, glazes like stained glass

Burnt Sienna Light

Warm copper tones of freshly exposed inner bark; lighter and warmer than regular Burnt Sienna, sun-struck and glowing

Quinacridone Burnt Orange

Intense copper-orange where bark catches direct sun; synthetic quinacridone, transparent, staining, the hottest note on the trunk

Mixes & Ratios

Living Copper

2 Transparent Red Oxide : 1 Quinacridone Burnt Orange : ½ Burnt Sienna Light

The impossible color of madrone inner bark in direct sun — copper so warm it looks alive.

Trans. Red Oxide
Quin. Burnt Orange
Burnt Sienna Lt
Transparent Red Oxide is the structural glaze — fully transparent, it lets paper light bounce back through the pigment the way real bark lets inner glow show through. Quinacridone Burnt Orange intensifies the copper toward orange where sun hits at a low angle, pushing the warmth without losing transparency. Burnt Sienna Light softens the transition into half-shadow, adding body without going opaque. Layer wet-on-dry: Transparent Red Oxide first as a full glaze, then float the Quinacridone Burnt Orange into the sunlit zones while the first layer is still barely damp. The transparency of all three pigments means each layer deepens rather than covers.

Mineral Bark

2 Green Apatite Genuine : 1 Raw Sienna : ¼ Burnt Sienna

Where lichen and mineral deposits pattern the bark — the green-gray zones between copper strips.

Green Apatite Gen.
Raw Sienna
Burnt Sienna
Green Apatite Genuine is ground mineral — actual apatite stone — and it granulates on paper the same way mineral deposits pattern real bark. You cannot fake this with synthetic greens. Raw Sienna warms the mineral green toward the bark’s natural color, bridging the gap between copper zones and lichen zones. Burnt Sienna at the edges anchors the mix to the trunk’s overall warmth. Apply to damp paper and tilt the board slightly — let the mineral particles settle into the paper’s valleys. The granulation will build bark texture without brushwork.

Strait Heat

2 Cerulean Blue : 1 Cobalt Blue : ¼ Hansa Yellow Deep

San Juan water on the hottest day of the week — turquoise shading to deep blue, heat haze over everything.

Cerulean Blue
Cobalt Blue
Hansa Yellow Deep
Cerulean Blue has the chalky, opaque quality of hot-weather sky reflected in water — it reads as summer the way no transparent blue can. Cobalt Blue deepens the midwater, where the strait drops off and the color turns serious. Hansa Yellow Deep is the secret ingredient: a whisper of it shifts the blues toward the green-turquoise you actually see in the San Juans, and its warmth adds the heat-haze shimmer that makes summer water look different from winter water. Grade the mix across the page — more Cerulean near shore, more Cobalt in the distance.

Why These Colors?

Three copper-family primaries is unusual, and this is the week that demands it. Transparent Red Oxide glazes the bark’s deep undertone. Burnt Sienna Light catches the warmer, lighter exposed wood. Quinacridone Burnt Orange pushes the hottest highlights toward molten. Each is a different temperature of the same subject — the madrone trunk requires all three to read as round and alive. Green Apatite Genuine earns its place through behavior: its mineral granulation mimics the way deposits and lichen pattern real bark, a trick no synthetic green can replicate. Cerulean Blue overhead gives you the chalky, wide-open summer sky that makes the copper bark pop by contrast. Hooker’s Green handles the leathery evergreen leaves without competing with the trunk. Cobalt Blue in the distance keeps the strait present but secondary — this week, the tree is the subject. Raw Sienna and Burnt Sienna round out the bark’s full value range from papery outer layer to deepest shadow. Hansa Yellow Deep warms the air itself.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Madrone Trunk Close-Up — Peeling Bark

Subject: A madrone trunk filling the frame, bark peeling in long curling strips to reveal smooth copper inner wood. Sun hits the exposed surface from the left. Papery outer bark hangs in scrolls.

The Living Copper mix (Transparent Red Oxide + Quinacridone Burnt Orange + Burnt Sienna Light) was built for exactly this — three layers of copper, each a different temperature. Raw Sienna handles the papery outer bark still clinging to the trunk.

  • Glaze the entire trunk area with dilute Transparent Red Oxide first and let it dry — this establishes the underlying glow that makes madrone bark look lit from within
  • Float Quinacridone Burnt Orange into the sun-struck zones wet-on-dry, letting the edges feather naturally — the transparency of both layers means the light builds instead of muddying
  • Peeling bark curls are Raw Sienna on the outer face (the papery side) and Burnt Sienna Light where they curl inward to reveal the copper underneath
  • Leave slivers of white paper where the freshest exposed wood catches maximum light — the smoothest inner bark is nearly white before it oxidizes to copper
Prompt 2

Madrone Against San Juan Water

Subject: A single madrone leaning over a rocky shoreline, copper trunk against turquoise strait water. Hot June sky above, dark evergreen leaves framing the composition. The classic island tree in its best light.

Living Copper for the trunk, the Strait Heat mix (Cerulean Blue + Cobalt Blue + Hansa Yellow Deep) for the water, Hooker’s Green for the canopy. The full warm-to-cool range of this palette in one scene.

  • Paint the sky first with Cerulean Blue in a graded wash — full strength at the zenith, thinning to almost nothing at the horizon where heat haze bleaches it out
  • The strait is the Strait Heat mix applied to wet paper — grade from Cerulean-dominant near shore to Cobalt-dominant at the horizon for natural depth
  • The madrone trunk goes in after the water is dry. Build the Living Copper mix in layers, pushing Quinacridone Burnt Orange harder on the water-facing side where reflected light intensifies the copper
  • Hooker’s Green for the leaf canopy, applied as a single dark mass — madrone leaves are leathery and dense, not delicate. Let the trunk be the star
Prompt 3

Bark Curl Detail

Subject: An intimate study of a single bark curl — the papery outer layer lifting away from the smooth inner surface, backlit so the curl is translucent at its thinnest edge. Botanical-scale observation.

Raw Sienna for the translucent outer bark, Burnt Sienna for the curl’s shadowed interior, and the Mineral Bark mix (Green Apatite Genuine + Raw Sienna + Burnt Sienna) where lichen deposits pattern the peeling edge.

  • The backlit curl edge is Raw Sienna at its most dilute — thin enough to be translucent on the paper, mimicking the way real bark curls glow when sun passes through them
  • Where the curl thickens and rolls, switch to Burnt Sienna for the shadow side — the temperature shift from warm Raw Sienna to cooler Burnt Sienna describes the curl’s three-dimensional form
  • Drop the Mineral Bark mix into damp areas where lichen or mineral staining patterns the bark — let Green Apatite Genuine’s granulation do the detail work
  • The smooth inner bark revealed beneath the curl is Burnt Sienna Light at mid-value — keep it even and unblemished to contrast with the textured peeling layer above
Prompt 4

Madrone Grove in Summer Heat

Subject: Three or four madrone trunks in a grove, each at a different stage of bark-shedding. Dappled light through the canopy. Dry grass and exposed rock at the base. The shimmer of a hot afternoon.

All three copper primaries get a workout here — different trunks at different stages let you vary the Living Copper mix from tree to tree. Hansa Yellow Deep for the dry grass and heat shimmer, Green Apatite Genuine for mineral texture on the oldest bark.

  • Vary the copper mix for each trunk: the most freshly peeled tree gets the most Quinacridone Burnt Orange, the tree with intact bark gets more Raw Sienna, and the middle tree gets the full Living Copper blend
  • Hansa Yellow Deep for the dry grass at the base — apply with quick, upward flicks of a rigger brush to suggest individual blades catching the heat
  • Dappled light means leaving irregular patches of white paper on the trunks and ground. Map these out first with masking fluid or careful negative painting
  • The canopy overhead is Hooker’s Green with touches of Green Apatite Genuine where the light filters through — the mineral granulation suggests the play of light through leaves
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Transparent Red Oxide (PBr101) is one of Daniel Smith’s most transparent earth tones — it glazes beautifully and layers without muddying. Burnt Sienna Light is lighter and warmer than regular Burnt Sienna, specifically designed for sun-warmed subjects. Quinacridone Burnt Orange (PO48) is a synthetic organic with intense chroma and full transparency. Green Apatite Genuine is a PrimaTek pigment — ground mineral stone — and its granulation is unique and irreplaceable by mixing.
