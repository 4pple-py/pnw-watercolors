Week 1 — PNW Watercolor Series

Pewter Eagle Light

Early January on the Skagit flats. The sky is a single sheet of pewter pressed down on silver-gray water, and bald eagles are everywhere — hunched on driftwood, wheeling over sandbars, dark shapes against a world drained to metal. The only real color is on the birds themselves: chocolate brown, white head, a beak like a dropped coin of cadmium yellow.

Primary Trio

Payne’s Gray

The pewter sky itself — that heavy, unbroken overcast pressing flat on the river corridor.

French Ultramarine

The cold blue undertone hiding inside every gray sky, visible only where the cloud layer thins.

Buff Titanium

Silver-white light skidding across flat water — the brightest thing in a world with no sun.

Mixes & Ratios

Raptor Mantle

2 Burnt Sienna : 1 Payne’s Gray : touch Ivory Black

The dark chocolate-brown of an eagle’s back feathers catching no light at all.

Burnt Sienna
Payne’s Gray
Ivory Black
Burnt Sienna is transparent and staining — it locks into the paper and won’t lift. Payne’s Gray cools it into that ashy brown that eagle plumage actually is (not the warm brown your brain assumes). The touch of Ivory Black deepens without shifting the hue. Mix on the palette first, then apply in single directional strokes following the feather lay. For lighter body areas, dilute more and skip the Ivory Black. For the dark mantle across the shoulders, load heavy and work wet on dry paper. The Burnt Sienna will granulate slightly against the Payne’s Gray, giving feather texture without fussing.

River Tin

1 Cerulean Blue : 2 Buff Titanium : 1 Chinese White

The silver surface of flat water under a sky that refuses to commit to any color.

Cerulean Blue
Buff Titanium
Chinese White
Cerulean Blue granulates beautifully — it’s a particulate pigment that settles into paper texture in a way that reads as light catching water. Buff Titanium is semi-opaque and warm, which keeps the mix from going dead. Chinese White adds body and that milky, foggy quality river surfaces get when the light is flat. Lay this as a broad wash on slightly damp paper and tilt — the Cerulean will separate from the opaques and settle in the valleys, creating the illusion of rippled metallic water with zero brushwork. For more pewter, add a whisper of Payne’s Gray.

Cottonwood Straw

2 Yellow Ochre : 1 Raw Umber : trace Neutral Tint

Dead grass and bare cottonwood bark — the warm frequency the eye grabs onto when everything else is gray.

Yellow Ochre
Raw Umber
Neutral Tint
Yellow Ochre is naturally opaque and granulating — an iron oxide pigment that settles with real mineral weight. Raw Umber pulls it down into earth without killing the warmth. The trace of Neutral Tint is the key: it adds just enough cool shadow to keep the mix from looking too sunny for a January scene. Use this for dry grass clumps along the riverbank, cottonwood bark, and the driftwood logs eagles perch on. Apply dry-brush for grass texture, or as a thin wash for distant fields. The granulation in both Yellow Ochre and Raw Umber will create natural irregularity.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built around a narrow value range on purpose. Payne’s Gray, French Ultramarine, Neutral Tint, and Ivory Black all live in the cool-dark neighborhood, but they do different jobs: Payne’s Gray is the atmospheric workhorse, French Ultramarine granulates for sky texture, Neutral Tint goes darker without shifting temperature, and Ivory Black provides the hard graphic punch an eagle silhouette needs. Buff Titanium and Chinese White handle the lights — Buff warm and semi-opaque for that tarnished-silver water glare, Chinese White cooler and chalky for fog and mist. The warm accents are deliberately scarce: Yellow Ochre and Burnt Sienna carry the entire burden of color, the way an eagle’s beak and body carry all the warmth in a pewter landscape. Cerulean Blue sits between the cools and the lights, granulating into pale winter sky breaks that feel genuinely cold. Raw Umber grounds everything in mud and gravel. Ten pigments, most of them gray, because January on the Skagit is mostly gray — and the colors that aren’t gray hit harder for it.

Painting Prompts

Four subjects from the first week of January on the Skagit — each one built to use these specific pigments and the cold, flat light that defines this week.

Prompt 1

Eagle on Driftwood

Subject: A single bald eagle hunched on a bleached driftwood log at the river’s edge. Pewter sky, flat silver water behind it, dead grass in the foreground. The bird is the darkest thing in the scene.

The Raptor Mantle mix (Burnt Sienna + Payne’s Gray + Ivory Black) gives you the eagle’s body in two or three loaded strokes. Buff Titanium and Chinese White handle the white head, and Yellow Ochre is the beak — the only spot of real warmth.

  • Paint the sky first: a wet-in-wet wash of Payne’s Gray with a little French Ultramarine dropped in at the top for granulation texture
  • Reserve the eagle and driftwood as dry paper, masking if needed — the hard edges matter against that soft sky
  • The driftwood log is Raw Umber dry-brushed over Buff Titanium, keeping it lighter than the bird so the eagle reads as the focal point
  • Paint the white head last with Chinese White mixed into very dilute Cerulean Blue — eagle heads are never pure white, they carry reflected sky
Prompt 2

Skagit Flats, Dozens of Eagles

Subject: The wide river flats from a distance. Bare cottonwood trees line the bank, and in every tree, dark shapes — eagles, ten or twenty of them, waiting. The river is a flat silver band; the sky is indistinguishable from the water at the horizon.

This is a painting about value, not detail. Payne’s Gray and Cerulean Blue build sky and water as nearly the same tone. The Cottonwood Straw mix (Yellow Ochre + Raw Umber + Neutral Tint) gives the bare trees warmth against the cold field. Eagles are just Ivory Black dots.

  • Keep the entire painting within three values: light (Buff Titanium water glare), mid (Payne’s Gray sky), and dark (Ivory Black eagle shapes)
  • Use the River Tin mix for the water, laid as one continuous horizontal wash — no brushstrokes, just tilt and let it settle
  • The cottonwoods are calligraphic: Raw Umber trunks painted with a rigger brush, branching upward in single strokes
  • Add the eagles last as confident Ivory Black marks with a small round brush — no detail, just dark comma shapes in the branches
Prompt 3

Fog Lifting off the River

Subject: Early morning, the Skagit still shrouded. Fog sits on the water like gauze. A single eagle is airborne above it, dark wings spread against the white. The far bank is barely visible — cottonwoods as gray ghosts.

Chinese White and Buff Titanium dominate — this is their painting. Payne’s Gray, heavily diluted, suggests the far bank. The eagle overhead is Neutral Tint and Ivory Black, hard-edged and sharp against all that softness.

  • Wet the entire sheet and lay Chinese White mixed with a breath of Cerulean Blue as a full wash — this is the fog
  • While still damp, suggest the far bank cottonwoods with very dilute Payne’s Gray, letting the edges bleed and disappear into the fog
  • Let everything dry completely before painting the eagle — the bird must have hard edges to read against the soft background
  • The water below the fog is the River Tin mix, but even paler; the point is that water and air are nearly the same thing at this hour
Prompt 4

Chum Salmon Run, Eagle Waiting

Subject: Looking down at shallow water over a gravel bar. Dark shapes of chum salmon are visible beneath the surface. On the bank above, an eagle stands in the grass, watching. Mudflat, river stones, winter stubble.

Raw Umber is the gravel bar — granulating over rough paper, it becomes river stones without any detail work. The salmon beneath the surface are Neutral Tint softened into a Payne’s Gray wash. Yellow Ochre and the Cottonwood Straw mix handle the dry grass around the eagle.

  • Paint the shallow water as a wash of Payne’s Gray over Buff Titanium, keeping it transparent enough to see the gravel bar beneath
  • Suggest salmon with Neutral Tint shapes dropped wet-into-wet — keep them soft-edged and submerged, not illustrated
  • Dry-brush Raw Umber for the gravel bar in the foreground, letting the paper texture do the work of individual stones
  • The standing eagle is Raptor Mantle mix for the body, Chinese White with a touch of Cerulean for the head, and one sharp stroke of Yellow Ochre for the beak
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Pigment numbers: Payne’s Gray (PBk6, PB29, PV19), French Ultramarine (PB29), Buff Titanium (PW6:1), Raw Umber (PBr7), Neutral Tint (PBk6, PV19, PB29), Chinese White (PW4), Yellow Ochre (PY43), Burnt Sienna (PBr7), Ivory Black (PBk9), Cerulean Blue (PB35). This is a deliberately low-chroma palette — most of the ten pigments live in the gray-brown range, which means your warm accents (Yellow Ochre, Burnt Sienna) carry disproportionate visual weight. Use them sparingly. Cerulean Blue and French Ultramarine both granulate, which makes them ideal for skies on textured paper — they settle into the valleys and create atmospheric texture without brushwork. Buff Titanium is semi-opaque; use it for body in light washes rather than mixing. Chinese White is fully opaque and best used for fog effects and lightening, not for correcting mistakes. The palette rewards a limited approach: two or three pigments per passage, tilted paper, and patience with the granulation.
