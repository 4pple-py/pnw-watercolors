Week 1 — PNW Watercolor Series

Pewter Eagle Light

Early January on the Skagit — bald eagles hunched in bare cottonwoods over chum-dark water, 3:30 PM already dimming. Sword ferns stay defiantly green beneath a sky that hasn’t committed to rain or clearing. Winter here doesn’t storm; it settles. The tension: stillness that is actually predation.

Primary Trio

Payne’s Gray

The structural overcast, pressed low and unmoving; mixes cool without going lifeless

Raw Umber

Gravel bar silt and riverbank earth; granulating, transparent, the ground truth of every Skagit scene

Perylene Green

Sword fern blades in zero direct light; deep, staining, refuses to gray out even under heavy wash

Mixes & Ratios

Chum River Pewter

3 Payne’s Gray : 1 Raw Umber : trace Potter’s Pink

Silty current carrying salmon tannins past gravel bars — the river’s color when it’s doing the work of decay.

Payne’s Gray
Raw Umber
Potter’s Pink
Lay Payne’s Gray wet, drop Raw Umber in from the side to let granulation do the silt work. Add Potter’s Pink while still damp — just a breath of it — for the organic warmth that distinguishes living river from plain gray. Tilt the paper to let the mix drift. More Pink shifts it toward spent-salmon water; less keeps it mineral.

Snag Bark Dark

2 Burnt Umber : 1 Neutral Tint

Wet deadwood hosting eagle weight — the perch branch that’s been dead longer than the bird has been alive.

Burnt Umber
Neutral Tint
Mix on the palette, not the paper. Burnt Umber gives the bark its organic warmth; Neutral Tint sinks it into shadow without turning it blue. Apply in a single confident stroke — this mix doesn’t reward overworking. For the darkest crevices, load heavier on Neutral Tint. The granulation in Burnt Umber will do the texture for you.

Fern Under Overcast

2 Perylene Green : 1 Raw Umber

Sword fern blade in zero direct light — still impossibly green, but earthed, grounded, refusing the spotlight it doesn’t have.

Perylene Green
Raw Umber
Perylene Green stains hard, so commit. Premix, then apply to dry paper for crisp frond edges. Raw Umber granulates against the smooth staining green — that texture difference is the fern’s leathery surface. For deeper shadow between fronds, increase Raw Umber. For the newest growth at the crown, decrease it. This mix will not lift easily; plan your lights first.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built on transparency and granulation serving a single purpose: pewter light over living water. Payne’s Gray and Raw Umber both granulate, which means every wash carries sediment texture without brushwork — the river paints itself. Perylene Green’s staining power is the point: sword ferns hold their color against the prevailing gray the way the actual pigment holds against lifting. Potter’s Pink and Buff Titanium are both opaque-leaning, which gives the sky and the salmon flesh a chalky materiality distinct from the transparent darks. Indanthrone Blue and French Ultramarine split the cool-accent duties — Indanthrone for the deep dusk, Ultramarine for the granulating mountain distance. Every pigment earns its place through behavior, not just hue.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Eagle Perch on the Skagit

Subject: A bald eagle hunched on a bare cottonwood snag over the chum-dark river, the pewter sky pressing down behind it. The bird is a dark shape; the scene is all atmosphere.

The Snag Bark Dark mix (Burnt Umber + Neutral Tint) gives you the perch in one confident stroke, while Payne’s Gray and Raw Umber build the river and sky with natural granulation. Buff Titanium handles the flat glare off the water.

  • Paint the sky first as a wet-in-wet wash of Payne’s Gray with a trace of Potter’s Pink — tilt the paper and let gravity do the grading
  • Reserve the eagle as white paper, then paint the bird last with Neutral Tint and Burnt Umber on dry paper for hard edges against the soft sky
  • Use Raw Umber dropped wet-into-wet into the Payne’s Gray sky wash for the bare cottonwood branches — the granulation will make them look like real wood
  • Keep the value range narrow everywhere except the eagle itself; the bird should be the darkest shape in the painting
Prompt 2

Sword Fern Bank

Subject: A hillside of sword ferns under deep overcast, each frond impossibly green against the gray-brown forest floor. No direct light — just the ambient glow of a January afternoon at 3 PM.

Perylene Green is built for this moment — it holds its saturation against any amount of gray. The Fern Under Overcast mix (Perylene Green + Raw Umber) gives you the leathery frond color, and Payne’s Gray recedes behind it for the forest atmosphere.

  • Lay the background forest in Payne’s Gray + Raw Umber first and let it dry completely before painting any ferns
  • Paint each frond with Perylene Green on dry paper for crisp pinnae edges — start from the tip and pull toward the rachis
  • Vary the Raw Umber proportion: more for older, lower fronds; less for the bright new growth at the crown
  • Leave thin slivers of unpainted paper between fronds where light catches the edges — Perylene Green stains, so plan these gaps before you start
Prompt 3

Chum Salmon Gravel Bar

Subject: Spawned-out chum salmon visible through silty current over a gravel bar — ghostly pink-gray shapes in pewter water, cottonwood leaves caught on the rocks.

The Chum River Pewter mix (Payne’s Gray + Raw Umber + trace Potter’s Pink) IS this water. Potter’s Pink gives the salmon their spent, ghostly flesh tone. Quinacridone Gold provides the lone cottonwood leaf accent.

  • Wet the entire paper and lay the Chum River Pewter mix as a full-sheet wash, tilting to let the granulation settle into current patterns
  • While still damp, suggest the salmon shapes with Potter’s Pink — keep them vague and half-dissolved, because that’s what they are
  • Once dry, add the gravel bar in the foreground with Raw Umber dry-brushed on rough paper
  • Drop one or two Quinacridone Gold marks for cottonwood leaves caught on rocks — the warm accent hits harder for being rare
Prompt 4

Dusk on the River Corridor

Subject: The Skagit at 4 PM, the last light a cold blue line along the Cascade silhouette to the east, the river already in shadow, bare alders lining the bank.

Indanthrone Blue and French Ultramarine split the dusk perfectly — Indanthrone for the deep creeping shadow, Ultramarine for the granulating mountain distance. Buff Titanium is the last pale light at the horizon.

  • Work top to bottom: Indanthrone Blue at zenith grading to French Ultramarine at the mountain line, then a thin strip of Buff Titanium at the horizon
  • Let the sky dry, then paint the Cascade silhouette in French Ultramarine — its granulation will read as atmospheric haze at distance
  • Use Payne’s Gray + Raw Umber for the river surface, reflecting the dark sky above
  • The bare alders along the bank are Burnt Umber + Neutral Tint, painted as single calligraphic strokes with a rigger — don’t overwork the branches
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. This is a low-chroma palette by design — the only saturated note is Perylene Green, and it hits harder for being alone. Temperature range runs cool-neutral with Potter’s Pink and Quinacridone Gold as the sole warm interruptions. Payne’s Gray and Raw Umber are natural mixing partners; they won’t mud out because they’re already in mud’s neighborhood. Buff Titanium is semi-opaque — use it for sky glare and reserved lights, not for mixing. The palette rewards restraint: fewer colors per passage, more granulation time on tilted paper.
