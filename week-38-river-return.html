River Return

Atmospheric rivers reclaim the sky. King tides push salt miles upstream. Coho in force — silver flanks flashing in brown water. Witch hazel blooms yellow on bare branches, the only flower still opening. Autumn dusk drops violet.

Mixes & Ratios

King Tide Silt

2 Transparent Brown Oxide : 1 Raw Umber : touch French Ultramarine

Salt-and-silt water pushing past its normal boundary — the river remembering it was once the sea.

Trans. Brown Oxide
Raw Umber
Fr. Ultramarine
Both earth pigments are transparent, so the mix stays luminous even at concentration. The French Ultramarine touch introduces granulation and a cool note — the salt in the silt. Sweeping horizontal strokes, letting the brush skip for surface texture.

Witch Bloom

2 Hansa Yellow Deep : 1 Cobalt Violet

Yellow petals against violet dusk — complementaries that vibrate when placed side by side but gray out where they overlap.

Hansa Yellow Deep
Cobalt Violet
Side by side, not blended. Where they overlap they gray out — and that’s information, not a mistake. Paint sky first with Cobalt Violet, leave petal-shaped gaps, fill with Hansa Yellow Deep while the violet is still damp. The complementary vibration is the point.

Atmospheric River

2 Indigo : 1 Neutral Tint : touch Cobalt Violet

Visible moisture column hitting the coast — the darkest mix in the palette, the firehose from the sky.

Indigo
Neutral Tint
Cobalt Violet
The darkest mix in the palette. Indigo base provides the depth, Neutral Tint kills chroma to read as pure weight, Cobalt Violet adds the purple cast that distinguishes a storm sky from mere darkness. Graded wash from dark top to lighter base — the bottom edge should bleed and feather.

Why These Colors?

This palette is organized around the tension between horizontal and vertical forces — water pushing laterally while rain falls straight down. Cobalt Violet is a genuine cobalt pigment with weight and granulation that no synthetic substitute provides. Transparent Brown Oxide is the river itself — iron-based, luminous, honest about what it carries. Hansa Yellow Deep is the week’s emotional anchor: witch hazel’s insistence on blooming when every other flower has surrendered. French Ultramarine appears only in rain-breaks, brief and blue. Chinese White handles the coho’s silver flash: opaque, instantaneous, gone.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

King Tide Flooding the Lowlands

Subject: Brown silt-water pushing past its normal boundary, flooding lowland fields — horizontal force, the river remembering it was the sea.

The King Tide Silt mix (Transparent Brown Oxide 2:Raw Umber 1:French Ultramarine touch) stays luminous even at full concentration — sweeping horizontal strokes for the water’s lateral push.

  • Sweeping horizontal strokes for the water — let the brush skip for surface texture
  • Cobalt Violet sky pressing down from above, heavy and low
  • Neutral Tint for the overcast ceiling — uniformly flat, no drama in the clouds
  • Leave thin strips of Hansa Yellow Deep for warm notes where light breaks through
Prompt 2

Witch Hazel Against Violet Dusk

Subject: Yellow petals on bare branches against a violet dusk sky — the only flower still opening, complementary colors vibrating against each other.

The Witch Bloom approach (Hansa Yellow Deep 2:Cobalt Violet 1) is about adjacency, not blending — complementaries placed side by side, graying where they touch.

  • Paint Cobalt Violet sky first, leaving petal-shaped gaps in the wash
  • Fill gaps with Hansa Yellow Deep while the violet is still damp — complementary vibration is the point
  • Bare branches in Burnt Sienna — wiry, angular, leading the eye to the blooms
  • Where yellow meets violet, let them gray out — that overlap is information, not a mistake
Prompt 3

Coho Flash in Brown Water

Subject: A silver coho flank flashing in silt-heavy river water — one fish, one flash, blink and it’s gone.

Chinese White is deployed for exactly this moment — opaque, instantaneous, a single stroke against the King Tide Silt river.

  • Establish the river first with King Tide Silt mix — let it dry completely
  • Coho flash is a single stroke of Chinese White, applied with confidence and not reworked
  • One fish, one flash — restraint is the composition
  • Indigo for the deeper water beyond, where the river drops off
Prompt 4

Atmospheric River Hitting the Coast

Subject: The visible moisture column — an atmospheric river making landfall, the firehose from the sky, dark and heavy and relentless.

The Atmospheric River mix (Indigo 2:Neutral Tint 1:Cobalt Violet touch) is the darkest value in the palette — graded from impenetrable top to feathered base.

  • Graded wash from dark top to lighter base — work fast, let the bottom edge bleed
  • French Ultramarine for the brief rain-break blue where the column hasn’t reached yet
  • Quinacridone Gold for last maple holdouts catching light below the storm
  • Burnt Sienna for alder leaves plastered to the wet trail in the foreground
Notes: All pigments confirmed Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. Cobalt Violet is genuine cobalt — expensive, granulating, irreplaceable by any synthetic substitute. Neutral Tint leans cool in the Daniel Smith formulation, which pairs well with the warm earth tones. Transparent Brown Oxide dries significantly darker than it appears wet — test your values before committing to a wash.
