River Return

The salmon are back. You smell the river before you see it — that mineral, animal edge to the air that means fish are dying in the water they were born in. The creek runs shallow over gravel and the shapes move underneath like dark thoughts: red-sided, battered, single-minded. Cottonwoods drop yellow leaves onto the surface and they drift downstream past bodies that are swimming up. The whole forest corridor is watching.

Mixes & Ratios

Spawning Red

2 Pyrrol Red : 1 Transparent Red Oxide : ½ Quinacridone Burnt Orange

The color of a salmon mid-spawn — no longer ocean-bright, not yet spent. The red of a body burning through its last reserves.

Pyrrol Red
Trans. Red Oxide
Quin. Burnt Orange
Pyrrol Red is the vivid base — the color of a salmon that still has fight in it, sides blazing red as it pushes upstream over gravel. Transparent Red Oxide darkens and earthens the red, adding the bruised, oxide quality that develops as the fish’s skin breaks down during spawning. Quinacridone Burnt Orange warms the belly and gill plates, where the color shifts from red toward copper-orange. For a single fish, lay down Pyrrol Red wet along the lateral line, then feed Transparent Red Oxide into the dorsal edge while damp. Touch Quinacridone Burnt Orange along the belly. The three reds will bleed into each other the way real salmon color transitions — never a hard edge, always a gradient.

River Corridor

2 Hooker’s Green : 1 Olive Green : ¼ Raw Umber

The forest pressing in on both sides of the salmon stream — dark conifers above, fall-faded alders and willows below, everything leaning toward the water.

Hooker’s Green
Olive Green
Raw Umber
Hooker’s Green is the structural dark — the Douglas firs and western red cedars that form the permanent canopy over salmon streams. It’s dark enough to frame the scene without needing black. Olive Green warms and lightens the understory: the alders going yellow-green, the sword ferns beginning to brown, the willows at water’s edge fading toward fall. Raw Umber anchors both greens to the earth, adding the brown undertone of a riparian forest in October when everything is beginning to decompose. Apply Hooker’s Green in the upper canopy, transition to Olive Green as you move down toward the riverbank, and let Raw Umber creep in at the waterline where mud meets vegetation.

Deep Pool

2 Cobalt Blue : 1 Ivory Black : ¼ Hooker’s Green

The dark water where salmon hold before their final push upstream — cold, deep, the color of patience and inevitability.

Cobalt Blue
Ivory Black
Hooker’s Green
Cobalt Blue granulates beautifully, and in this mix that granulation reads as the rocky bottom visible through clear but dark water. Ivory Black deepens the pool without shifting the hue — it’s the simple darkness of deep water over dark stone. Hooker’s Green adds the reflected-forest tint that makes river pools look green-black rather than blue-black; it’s the canopy overhead showing in the water’s surface. Apply the mix to wet paper and tilt the board — let the Cobalt Blue granulate naturally into the paper’s texture. The granulation will suggest the rocky riverbed beneath the surface without any detail brushwork. Leave a few flecks of white paper for surface reflections where the current breaks.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built around one of the most primal color events in the PNW: the salmon return. Pyrrol Red is the vivid, arterial red of fresh-run fish — strong enough to read underwater through moving current. Transparent Red Oxide is the same red after the river has had its way with the fish: darker, earthier, the oxide-red of spawning bodies breaking down. Quinacridone Burnt Orange catches the transitional stage between bright and spent, the copper-orange of gill plates and belly. Three reds for three stages of the same animal. Hooker’s Green and Olive Green build the river corridor — the dark conifer canopy and the fading deciduous understory that frame every salmon stream. Cobalt Blue earns its place through behavior: its granulation on wet paper mimics the look of clear water over a rocky riverbed, a texture you cannot achieve with non-granulating blues. Burnt Sienna and Raw Umber are the earth colors of gravel bars and muddy banks — the substrate the salmon dig their redds in. Ivory Black handles the deep pools where salmon hold, the darkest darks in the scene. Hansa Yellow Deep provides the fall cottonwoods along the corridor — their golden leaves floating downstream are the visual counterpoint to the fish fighting upstream.

Painting Prompts

The salmon run is brief, violent, and beautiful. These prompts capture the river at the moment when life and death are happening in the same frame — paint fast and don’t be precious about it.

Prompt 1

Salmon in the Shallows

Subject: Two or three salmon visible through shallow, clear water over a gravel bar. Their red sides distorted by current, dark backs breaking the surface. Fallen cottonwood leaves drifting past. The view from a bridge or high bank looking straight down.

The Spawning Red mix (Pyrrol Red + Transparent Red Oxide + Quinacridone Burnt Orange) for the fish bodies, Burnt Sienna for the gravel, Cobalt Blue for the water itself. Hansa Yellow Deep for the floating cottonwood leaves.

  • Paint the gravel bar first with a variegated wash of Burnt Sienna and Raw Umber — the substrate needs to be in place before the water goes over it, and it establishes the warm ground that the cool water will modify
  • The water layer is Cobalt Blue at high dilution applied over the dry gravel wash — the granulation of Cobalt Blue over the textured gravel wash will create a convincing sense of clear water over stone
  • The salmon shapes are the Spawning Red mix applied after the water layer is dry. Distort them slightly — salmon seen through moving water are never perfectly shaped, their edges waver and shift
  • Drop Hansa Yellow Deep leaves onto the surface while the final layer is still slightly damp — they should have soft edges, floating on the surface rather than sitting hard-edged on top of the painting
Prompt 2

The River Corridor — Looking Upstream

Subject: A salmon stream seen from water level, looking upstream into the forest corridor. Dark conifers leaning in from both sides, golden cottonwoods punctuating the green, the river narrowing to a vanishing point. Late afternoon light catching the water’s surface.

The River Corridor mix (Hooker’s Green + Olive Green + Raw Umber) for the forest walls, Deep Pool mix for the river center, Hansa Yellow Deep for cottonwood accents. The forest is the subject here; the fish are implied.

  • The river is your central axis — lay it in first with the Deep Pool mix, darker toward the center where the channel runs deep, lighter at the edges where gravel shows through as Burnt Sienna
  • Build the forest corridor with the River Corridor mix, darkest (more Hooker’s Green) at the top of the canopy, transitioning to more Olive Green and Raw Umber at the waterline where deciduous trees and mud take over
  • Cottonwood accents in Hansa Yellow Deep should be specific shapes within the green mass — individual trees that catch the eye and break up the dark corridor with warm gold. Don’t overdo them; three or four is enough
  • Late afternoon light on the water surface: leave horizontal white paper streaks where the low sun catches ripples. These highlights are what make the river feel like it’s moving
Prompt 3

Single Salmon Portrait

Subject: A single spawning salmon in profile, head to tail, close enough to see the hooked jaw and the color changes along its body. Out of water or half-submerged. The portrait of an animal at the end of its journey.

All three fish reds come into play: Pyrrol Red for the brightest flanks, Transparent Red Oxide for the darkening dorsal surface, Quinacridone Burnt Orange for the gill plates and pectoral zone. Ivory Black for the eye and jaw detail.

  • Sketch the profile carefully first — the hooked jaw (kype) is the defining feature of a spawning salmon and getting its curve right matters more than any color work
  • Lay Pyrrol Red along the lateral line as a wet stroke, then immediately feed Transparent Red Oxide along the back while the red is still wet. The two reds will merge with a soft gradient that mimics how real salmon color transitions from dark back to bright flank
  • Quinacridone Burnt Orange on the gill plate and lower jaw — this is where the fish shows the most copper-orange, and the warmth here contrasts with the cooler reds elsewhere on the body
  • The eye is Ivory Black with a tiny white paper highlight left unpainted — salmon eyes are dark and round, and getting the eye right is what makes the portrait feel alive rather than like a fish-shaped color study
Prompt 4

Gravel Redd at Dawn

Subject: A salmon redd (nest) in the river gravel at first light — the cleared oval of clean gravel where eggs have been laid, the female salmon still holding position above it, mist rising from the water surface. The forest corridor dark behind, the sky barely light.

Burnt Sienna and Raw Umber for the gravel, the Spawning Red mix for the female salmon, Cobalt Blue for the cold dawn water. Hooker’s Green as near-silhouette for the dark forest. Ivory Black deepens the pre-dawn shadows.

  • The dawn sky is Cobalt Blue at high dilution with a warm strip of Quinacridone Burnt Orange along the horizon — just enough warmth to suggest first light. Keep it simple; the sky is not the subject
  • The forest corridor is Hooker’s Green mixed with Ivory Black, applied as a near-silhouette. At dawn, the trees are shapes, not details. One confident wash, no fussing
  • The redd is the compositional center — an oval of clean Burnt Sienna gravel, lighter than the surrounding riverbed (which gets more Raw Umber). The contrast between the cleaned redd and the undisturbed gravel tells the story
  • Mist rising from the water: lift color from the water surface with a damp brush while the Cobalt Blue wash is still slightly wet. The lifted areas will read as mist without needing to paint white over blue
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Pyrrol Red (PR254) is a vivid, semi-transparent red with excellent lightfastness — one of the most intense modern reds available. Transparent Red Oxide (PBr101) is a deeply transparent earth red ideal for glazing and layering. Quinacridone Burnt Orange (PO48) bridges the gap between red and orange with full transparency. Cobalt Blue (PB28) is prized for its granulation on textured paper, which mimics natural textures like stone and water. Ivory Black (PBk9) is a warm, brownish black from bone char — softer than Mars Black, better for organic subjects.
