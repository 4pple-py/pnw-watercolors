Week 35 — PNW Watercolor Series

Equinox Tilt

The light has gone amber and doesn’t know it yet. September’s sun still feels like summer but lands at a different angle — slanting through the cedars, catching spider silk, turning ordinary meadows into something gilded and temporary. Shadows stretch earlier each afternoon. The green hasn’t broken yet but you can feel it thinking about it, the way a held breath changes the shape of a room.

Primary Trio

Quinacridone Gold

The amber cast of equinox light itself; transparent, staining, warm gold that glazes everything it touches with late-season warmth

French Ultramarine

The sky is deeper now, bluer and more serious than summer’s pale wash; granulating, warm-leaning blue with real weight

Burnt Sienna

Warm earth underfoot, the ground catching up to what the light already knows; transparent, reliable, the backbone of every autumn shadow

Mixes & Ratios

Amber Hour

2 Quinacridone Gold : 1 Hansa Yellow Deep : ¼ Burnt Sienna

The color of four o’clock light in September — everything it touches turns gold, and the gold is already half-shadow.

Quin. Gold
Hansa Yellow Deep
Burnt Sienna
Quinacridone Gold anchors this mix with its deep, transparent amber — it’s the light itself, not the thing the light hits. Hansa Yellow Deep brightens the gold toward that specific late-afternoon warmth that makes you squint. Burnt Sienna grounds it just enough to keep it from reading as artificial. Use this as a unifying glaze over an entire composition: lay it down first on dry paper, then paint the scene on top. Everything built over Amber Hour will inherit the equinox’s color temperature. For the most luminous results, keep the Quinacridone Gold layer thin — its staining transparency means even a whisper changes the mood.

Turning Canopy

2 Olive Green : 1 Sap Green : ¼ Quinacridone Gold

The forest is still green but the green has gone warm at the edges — olive bleeding into gold like a secret slowly told.

Olive Green
Sap Green
Quin. Gold
Olive Green does the heavy lifting here — it’s already a warm, yellowish green that reads as “summer thinking about quitting.” Sap Green adds just enough cool, vivid chlorophyll to remind you the canopy hasn’t fully turned. The touch of Quinacridone Gold is the tell: it pushes the mix toward amber at the edges, the way equinox light does to real foliage. Apply to damp paper and let the Quinacridone Gold drift toward the edges of each leaf mass — the staining pigment will settle into the paper’s texture while the Olive Green sits on top, creating a natural warm-to-cool gradient within each brushstroke.

Long Shadow

2 French Ultramarine : 1 Payne’s Gray : ¼ Burnt Sienna

The shadows that stretch across the meadow at five, blue-gray and cool against all that amber — the day’s counterargument.

French Ultramarine
Payne’s Gray
Burnt Sienna
French Ultramarine gives the shadow its color identity — these are blue shadows, the complement of all that golden light. Payne’s Gray cools and darkens without adding new hue, pushing the shadow toward the neutral it needs to be at its core. Burnt Sienna is the bridge: a whisper of it keeps the shadow connected to the warm ground it falls across, preventing the mix from going cold and disconnected. French Ultramarine’s granulation is the secret weapon here — on rough paper, the pigment separates into grainy, textured passages that read as dappled shadow on uneven ground. Don’t fight the granulation; let it happen.

Why These Colors?

This is the mirror of Week 11’s spring equinox, but the tilt runs the other way — toward dark instead of light. Quinacridone Gold dominates because the light itself has changed color; it’s amber now, not the fresh yellow of spring. French Ultramarine and Cobalt Blue hold the same structural roles they did in March, but they’re deeper, more serious, the sky having thickened with autumn’s lower angle. Olive Green replaces spring’s cool Cascade Green because the forest’s green has gone warm — chlorophyll fading, xanthophyll emerging, the same trees wearing different chemistry. Sap Green hangs on as the last cool green, the holdout. Alizarin Crimson barely appears — a single vine maple leaf turning early, a hint of the full spectrum coming in two weeks. It’s a promise, not a statement. Payne’s Gray handles the longer shadows that are September’s most visible change: the same meadow, the same trees, but the shadows reach twice as far by five o’clock. Raw Sienna and Burnt Sienna ground the palette in earth that’s been drying all summer. Hansa Yellow Deep catches the last slant light that turns everything it touches to gold.

Painting Prompts

The equinox is a threshold you walk through without noticing. These subjects catch the moment mid-step — still summer in the shade, already autumn in the light.

Prompt 1

Meadow at Golden Hour — Long Shadows

Subject: A Cascade foothill meadow at five in the afternoon, dried grass catching amber light. Shadows of scattered Douglas firs stretch across the field at extreme angles. The sky overhead is deep blue fading to gold at the horizon.

The Amber Hour mix (Quinacridone Gold + Hansa Yellow Deep + Burnt Sienna) glazed across the entire meadow, with the Long Shadow mix (French Ultramarine + Payne’s Gray + Burnt Sienna) for those dramatic stretched shadows cutting across the gold.

  • Lay the Amber Hour mix as a full-sheet glaze first and let it dry completely — this establishes the light’s color temperature before any detail goes in
  • Paint the long shadows with the Long Shadow mix while tilting your board so the French Ultramarine granulates along the shadow’s length — this creates the texture of uneven ground
  • The sky grades from French Ultramarine at zenith to Cobalt Blue mid-sky to Quinacridone Gold at the horizon — wet-in-wet, letting the colors bleed into each other at the transitions
  • Dried grass in the foreground is Raw Sienna with quick vertical strokes of Hansa Yellow Deep where individual stems catch the light at their tips
Prompt 2

First Red Leaf on Green

Subject: A single vine maple branch where one leaf has turned crimson against a background of still-green forest. Close-up composition, the red leaf sharp and the green background soft. The tension between seasons in a single frame.

Alizarin Crimson finally gets its moment — that one red leaf is the entire reason it’s in this palette. The Turning Canopy mix (Olive Green + Sap Green + Quinacridone Gold) for the soft green background, slightly out of focus.

  • Paint the green background first with the Turning Canopy mix applied to very wet paper — let everything blur and bleed. This is bokeh, not botanical illustration
  • Once dry, paint the single vine maple leaf with Alizarin Crimson at full strength, keeping edges sharp — the contrast between the soft background and crisp leaf creates the visual punch
  • Add Quinacridone Gold to the leaf’s veins and stem where light passes through — vine maple leaves are translucent enough to glow when backlit
  • The branch itself is Raw Sienna with a touch of Burnt Sienna on the shadow side — keep it thin and subordinate to the leaf
Prompt 3

Spider Silk in Slant Light

Subject: An orb weaver’s web stretched between two fence posts, catching September’s low-angle light. Each strand glows gold. Dewy. Behind it, a dark forest edge in deep shadow. The web is the brightest thing in the frame.

This is a negative-painting challenge — the web is white paper and Hansa Yellow Deep. The dark background is Payne’s Gray and French Ultramarine. Quinacridone Gold touches each strand where dew catches light.

  • Mask the web strands with masking fluid or a wax resist before painting the dark background — the web must stay clean white paper to read as luminous
  • The dark forest behind is Payne’s Gray mixed with French Ultramarine, applied in a single bold wash — the darker the background, the more the web glows
  • After removing the mask, tint the web strands with extremely dilute Hansa Yellow Deep and tiny dots of Quinacridone Gold where dew catches the light
  • The fence posts are Burnt Sienna and Raw Sienna, weathered and secondary — they frame the web but don’t compete with it
Prompt 4

Mixed Forest at the Tipping Point

Subject: A hillside of mixed forest — dark evergreen conifers interspersed with deciduous trees just beginning to shift. Some still fully green, some going gold at the edges, one or two with a flush of red. The whole spectrum of “almost but not yet.”

Every green in the palette gets used: Sap Green for the holdouts, Olive Green for the ones starting to turn, Quinacridone Gold for the trees that have gone amber. Alizarin Crimson for the one early turner. French Ultramarine mixed with Sap Green for the conifers’ dark mass.

  • Map out the hillside as a mosaic of color zones — Sap Green clusters, Olive Green clusters, Quinacridone Gold individuals, one Alizarin Crimson accent. Vary the proportions: mostly green, some gold, almost no red
  • Evergreen conifers are French Ultramarine mixed with Sap Green for a dark, cool mass that anchors the warm deciduous trees by contrast
  • Apply the Turning Canopy mix to the transitional trees — let Quinacridone Gold bleed into the Olive Green edges to show the change happening in real time
  • Payne’s Gray for the shadowed faces of the hillside — the equinox’s longer shadows mean more of the forest is in shade than it was a month ago
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Quinacridone Gold (PO49) is a transparent, staining pigment prized for its deep amber warmth — it glazes beautifully without lifting. French Ultramarine (PB29) granulates on rough paper, adding texture to skies and shadows. Olive Green (PY129/PG7) reads as “autumn green” straight from the tube. Alizarin Crimson here is Daniel Smith’s Permanent Alizarin Crimson (PR177), which is lightfast unlike the traditional formulation. Payne’s Gray (PBk6/PV19/PB15) is a mixing workhorse for shadows without the deadness of pure black.
