Week 12 — PNW Watercolor Series

Fiddlehead Spawn

Fiddleheads uncoil like fists opening. Four different greens and none of them the same — electric at the tip, yellow-green where the frond uncurls, structural on the mature blade, shadow beneath. In the creek below, a steelhead holds in the current, scarlet flashing along its lateral line. Everything is either growing or spawning. March doesn’t do subtlety.

Mixes & Ratios

Fiddlehead Spiral

1 Green Gold : 1 Sap Green : touch Hooker’s Green

The unfurling crozier from tip to base — acid yellow-green where the coil opens, deepening to structural green on the stipe, shadow-dark where the spiral overlaps itself.

Green Gold
Sap Green
HG
This mix is designed to be used as a gradient, not a flat wash. Load the brush with Green Gold first and begin at the unfurling tip of the crozier, where the coil opens and light hits new growth. As you spiral inward, pick up Sap Green — the mid-tone of the maturing frond. Where the spiral overlaps itself and shadow pools, touch in Hooker’s Green. Paint the fiddlehead in one continuous stroke from outside in, tightening toward the center. The color should shift from warm yellow-green to cool dark green without lifting the brush. Green Gold is transparent and staining; Sap Green is transparent and smooth; Hooker’s Green is the pre-mixed convenience dark that grounds the shadows. Together they map the fiddlehead’s growth gradient onto the paper in a single gesture.

Creek Over Gravel

2 French Ultramarine : 1 Raw Umber : touch Phthalo Green (BS)

Cold March creek running fast over round stones — blue depth shot through with earth, a whisper of green where moss stains the current.

Fr. Ultramarine
Raw Umber
PG
French Ultramarine granulates beautifully, and when mixed with Raw Umber it produces the specific blue-gray of cold creek water over a stony bottom — the Ultramarine settling into the paper valleys while the Raw Umber separates into the ridges. That granulation is the gravel. The touch of Phthalo Green is critical but dangerous: too much and the creek becomes tropical. A brush-tip’s worth stirred into the mix gives the green tinge of moss and algae on submerged stones. Apply as a wet wash and tilt the paper slightly to create flow direction. Drop in pure Raw Umber while damp for individual stones breaking the surface. For the deepest pools where steelhead hold, increase the Ultramarine and add a second layer after the first dries.

Spawning Flash

3 Pyrrol Scarlet : 1 New Gamboge

The lateral stripe of a spawning steelhead rolling in the shallows — hot scarlet flushed with orange, opaque and sudden against dark water.

Pyrrol Scarlet
New Gamboge
Pyrrol Scarlet (PR255) is intensely opaque — it sits on the paper surface rather than sinking in, which is exactly how a spawning steelhead’s color reads against dark water. New Gamboge warms it toward orange without muddying it, shifting from scarlet toward the salmon-orange of the gill plate and adipose fin. Paint the fish over a fully dried Creek Over Gravel wash. The opacity of Pyrrol Scarlet means it covers the dark water underneath, creating the visual punch of a bright fish in a dark stream. One stroke for the body, tapered at both ends. A second, thinner stroke for the lateral line. Don’t blend — the hard edge of opaque pigment over transparent wash is what makes the fish look like it’s in the water rather than printed on it.

Why These Colors?

Four greens in one palette sounds redundant until you stand in a PNW forest in late March and realize no two surfaces are the same green. Phthalo Green (Blue Shade) is the electric, almost unnatural intensity of a new fern tip catching sidelight — PG7, one of the most powerful staining pigments in watercolor, used in tiny amounts. Green Gold occupies the warm yellow-green zone where croziers uncurl, transparent and luminous. Sap Green is the reliable structural mid-tone of the mature frond. Hooker’s Green goes darker and cooler, into the shadow beneath the canopy where ferns overlap. Each green does a job the others cannot. Pyrrol Scarlet breaks the green dominance with the violence of a spawning steelhead — opaque, hot, unapologetic. New Gamboge pushes spring light through the canopy. French Ultramarine and Raw Umber build the creek: granulating blue over granulating earth, separating on the paper into the visual texture of water over stone. Nickel Azo Yellow (PY150) has a natural green bias that makes it perfect for the acid yellow-green of the newest fiddlehead tips, bridging the gap between New Gamboge’s warmth and Green Gold’s leaf tones. Burnt Umber anchors the banks and roots. This is the greenest palette in the series, because this is the greenest week.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Fiddlehead Close-Up

Subject: Three sword fern fiddleheads at different stages of unfurling — one tight crozier still wearing brown scales, one half-open with the first pinnae visible, one almost fully extended — backlit by spring sun on a mossy nurse log.

The Fiddlehead Spiral mix (Green Gold, Sap Green, touch of Hooker’s Green) was designed for exactly this gradient from new growth to mature frond. Nickel Azo Yellow picks out the acid-bright tips. Burnt Umber handles the brown scale casings on the tightest coil. New Gamboge glazed behind the fronds creates the backlit spring glow.

  • Paint each fiddlehead as a single continuous spiral stroke from outside in — load Green Gold at the tip and pick up Sap Green as you tighten the coil
  • The tightest crozier gets a Burnt Umber dry-brush pass for the papery brown scales still clinging to the spiral
  • Touch Nickel Azo Yellow onto the newest unfurling tips while the green is still damp — it blooms into a sharp acid highlight
  • Glaze New Gamboge behind all three fiddleheads after the greens have fully dried to create backlit warmth without muddying the fronds
Prompt 2

Steelhead Holding in the Creek

Subject: A single spawning steelhead holding in a fast riffle, seen from the bank above — dark water, pale gravel bottom visible through the current, the fish’s scarlet lateral line the brightest thing in the frame, sword ferns hanging over the creek from both banks.

The Creek Over Gravel mix (French Ultramarine, Raw Umber, touch Phthalo Green) builds the water and streambed in one granulating wash. The Spawning Flash mix (Pyrrol Scarlet, New Gamboge) paints the fish on top — opaque over transparent, hot over cold. Hooker’s Green and Sap Green frame the scene from the overhanging fern banks.

  • Lay in the creek first as a wet wash of the Creek Over Gravel mix, tilting the paper to create flow direction — drop in Raw Umber stones while damp
  • Let the creek dry completely, then paint the steelhead with the Spawning Flash mix — one tapered stroke for the body, a thinner line for the lateral stripe
  • The opacity of Pyrrol Scarlet over the dark transparent water is the whole painting — don’t overwork the fish, two strokes maximum
  • Frame the creek with overhanging ferns: Sap Green fronds on the sunlit side, Hooker’s Green where the canopy creates shadow
Prompt 3

Fern Canopy from Below

Subject: Looking straight up through a canopy of sword ferns and lady ferns, spring light filtering through layered fronds, each layer a different green depending on age and light, the dark trunks of red alders cutting vertical lines through the horizontal fronds.

This is why the palette has four greens. Phthalo Green (BS) for the electric tips catching direct sun. Green Gold for the translucent fronds backlit overhead. Sap Green for the mid-canopy. Hooker’s Green for the oldest fronds in deep shadow. New Gamboge punches through as shafts of light between the layers.

  • Work from light to dark: start with dilute New Gamboge and Green Gold for the sky gaps and backlit fronds, then layer Sap Green fronds on top
  • Phthalo Green (BS) goes on last and sparingly — just the tips of the lowest, closest fronds catching sidelight, intensely saturated
  • The alder trunks are Raw Umber with Burnt Umber shadow sides, painted as clean verticals after the fern layers have dried
  • Hooker’s Green in the deepest overlapping zones creates the shadow depth that makes the bright greens read as canopy rather than flat pattern
Prompt 4

Creek Bank Where Green Meets Water

Subject: The transition zone where a fern-covered bank meets a spawning creek — exposed roots and wet earth at the waterline, fiddleheads emerging from the bank above, the creek rushing past below with a dark pool where a steelhead redd dimples the gravel.

Every pigment on the palette earns its place here. The greens stack up the bank from Hooker’s Green at the waterline to Sap Green to Green Gold to Nickel Azo Yellow at the newest growth. Burnt Umber and Raw Umber handle the exposed earth and roots. The creek is the Creek Over Gravel mix. A small flash of Pyrrol Scarlet marks the redd.

  • Divide the painting into three horizontal bands: green bank (top), earth transition (middle), creek (bottom) — paint each band separately with drying time between
  • The earth band is the most interesting: Burnt Umber roots, Raw Umber wet soil, with tendrils of Hooker’s Green moss creeping down toward the water
  • In the creek, the steelhead redd is suggested by a patch of lighter gravel (less Ultramarine in the mix) with the faintest Pyrrol Scarlet hint at the upstream edge
  • Nickel Azo Yellow on the fiddlehead tips at the top of the bank creates a bright acid-green crown that draws the eye upward from the dark water
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Phthalo Green (Blue Shade) is PG7 — one of the most staining, powerful pigments in watercolor. Use it in trace amounts; it will dominate any mix it enters. Green Gold (PY150 + PG36) is transparent and warm, a genuine yellow-green that doesn’t need mixing. Pyrrol Scarlet (PR255) is opaque and intense — its covering power over dark washes is the key to painting bright fish in dark water. Hooker’s Green is a convenience mix already balanced between blue and yellow; it’s the workhorse shadow green that doesn’t need babysitting. Nickel Azo Yellow (PY150) has a green bias that distinguishes it from New Gamboge — it’s a yellow that already wants to be green, perfect for the youngest growth. The four greens on this palette occupy genuinely different positions: electric (Phthalo), warm (Green Gold), structural (Sap), and shadow (Hooker’s). Don’t treat them as interchangeable. This is the greenest week in the series. Lean into it.
