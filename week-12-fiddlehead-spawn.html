Mixes & Ratios

Spawn Shallows

2 Jadeite Genuine : 1 Buff Titanium

Milky turquoise where herring breed — the water column made opaque by a billion eggs and milt, jade-green and glowing.

Jadeite Genuine
Buff Titanium
Jadeite Genuine is a PrimaTek pigment — actual ground stone. It granulates dramatically, with heavy particles settling into paper valleys to create a mineral, oceanic texture no synthetic pigment matches. Buff Titanium adds the milky opacity of spawn water — the biological cloud that turns Puget Sound shallows turquoise every spring. Mix loosely and apply to wet paper, letting the Jadeite particles drift and settle. Tilt the paper gently. For the milkiest zones near shore, add more Buff Titanium. For the edge where spawn water meets clear, let the mix fade into pure Cerulean Blue. The transition is the spawn boundary — paint it as a soft bleed, not a hard line.

Fiddlehead Curl

2 Hooker's Green : 1 Burnt Umber

Coiled frond still wearing brown scales — the fiddlehead in its first week above ground, green spiral inside a brown casing.

Hooker's Green
Burnt Umber
The fiddlehead is a spiral, and the painting technique follows the form. Load a round brush with the premixed color and paint the coil from outside in, one continuous curving stroke, tightening the spiral toward the center. Hooker's Green is the living green visible through gaps in the brown scale casing; Burnt Umber is the casing itself. For the fuzzy scales, use the Burnt Umber side of the mix with a dry-brush touch along the outer curve. For the interior of the coil where new green shows, use purer Hooker's Green. The fiddlehead gets greener as it unfurls. The tightest coils are brownest.

Osprey Return

1 Transparent Pyrrol Orange : 2 Raw Sienna

Raptor breast against stick nest — the osprey's rust-orange chest visible above the platform rim, surveying the spawn below.

Tr. Pyrrol Orange
Raw Sienna
Transparent Pyrrol Orange is vivid — it needs Raw Sienna to ground it into something that reads as feather rather than paint. Both pigments are transparent, so the mix glows with a warm, backlit quality appropriate for a sunlit raptor. Paint the nest first: pure Raw Sienna for the bleached sticks, angular strokes, letting gaps show sky beneath. Then paint the bird's breast above the rim — the mix of both pigments, applied to dry paper, one smooth passage. The dark eye and head markings are pure Burnt Umber. The wings, if extended, catch Cerulean Blue as a glaze. But the osprey is standing, not flying. It just arrived. Paint it still.

Why These Colors?

The palette is built around three parallel emergences — herring spawn, fern unfurling, and osprey return — each assigned its own chromatic zone. Jadeite Genuine is a PrimaTek mineral pigment that granulates like nothing else in the Daniel Smith line; its stone-origin texture is the milky, mineral quality of spawn water made real. Hooker's Green serves the fiddleheads as a pre-balanced convenience mix, saving the painter from mixing blue and yellow under field conditions. Quinacridone Gold provides the backlit warmth that spring sun finally delivers — transparent enough to glow from within when glazed over dried passages. Cobalt Teal Blue extends the water palette past the spawn zone into deeper, clearer water. The earths (Raw Sienna, Burnt Umber) handle the structural elements: nest sticks, fern scales, bark. This palette has more greens than any previous week — Jadeite Genuine, Hooker's Green, Sap Green — because the landscape has finally committed to the color.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Fiddlehead Parade

Subject: Three to five fiddleheads at different stages of unfurling — from tight brown coil to half-open spiral showing green — lined up along a mossy nurse log, backlit by spring sun.

The Fiddlehead Curl mix (2 Hooker’s Green : 1 Burnt Umber) gives you the full range from tightly coiled to unfurling. Quinacridone Gold glazed behind the fronds creates that backlit spring glow. Sap Green picks up as the fronds open wider and catch more light.

  • Paint each fiddlehead as a single continuous spiral stroke from outside in, tightening toward the center — load a round brush with the Fiddlehead Curl mix
  • The tightest coils get more Burnt Umber; the most open fronds shift toward pure Hooker’s Green
  • Dry-brush the fuzzy brown scales along the outer curve of each coil with Burnt Umber on a nearly dry round brush
  • Glaze Quinacridone Gold behind the fronds after the green has fully dried — the transparency creates genuine backlit glow
Prompt 2

Herring Spawn Shallows from Above

Subject: An aerial-perspective view of Fidalgo Island shallows where herring spawn has turned the water milky turquoise, fading to clear blue further out, dark eelgrass beds visible beneath the cloud, a few gulls floating on the surface.

Jadeite Genuine is literally ground stone — its granulation creates the mineral, oceanic texture of spawn water that no synthetic pigment can fake. The Spawn Shallows mix (2:1 Jadeite Genuine to Buff Titanium) handles the milky zone, bleeding into Cerulean Blue and then Cobalt Teal Blue as the water clears.

  • Wet the entire water area first, then drop in the Spawn Shallows mix near shore and let the Jadeite particles drift and settle naturally
  • Tilt the paper gently toward deeper water — the heavy mineral particles will migrate, creating a natural gradient
  • The boundary between spawn water and clear water is a soft bleed, not a hard line — let Cerulean Blue creep in at the edges
  • Dark eelgrass beds beneath the milky water: Hooker’s Green applied very dilute under the Jadeite wash while still damp
Prompt 3

Osprey on the Platform

Subject: A single osprey standing on its stick-nest platform, seen from below, rust-orange breast catching sun, the milky turquoise spawn water visible in the distance behind and below the nest.

The Osprey Return mix (1 Transparent Pyrrol Orange : 2 Raw Sienna) glows with transparent warmth for the breast. Raw Sienna handles the bleached nest sticks. Burnt Umber sharpens the head markings. And the turquoise spawn water in the background connects this predator to its food source with the Spawn Shallows mix.

  • Paint the nest first — Raw Sienna in angular, overlapping strokes, leaving gaps where sky shows through the sticks
  • The osprey’s breast is one smooth passage of the Osprey Return mix on dry paper — don’t overwork it
  • Eye and head stripe are pure Burnt Umber, applied with a small round brush after the breast is completely dry
  • Background spawn water should be pale and soft — dilute Jadeite Genuine with lots of water, keeping it atmospheric, not competing with the bird
Prompt 4

Creek-Side Fern Bank with Salmon Fry

Subject: A shaded creek bank thick with sword ferns and unfurling lady ferns, dappled spring sun breaking through the canopy, and in the shallow water below, a cloud of salmon fry hovering in the current.

Three greens for three roles: Sap Green for the sword ferns in light, Hooker’s Green for the darker lady ferns, and Jadeite Genuine for the shallow creek water where the fry school. Quinacridone Gold dapples through as spring sunlight hitting the fern bank.

  • Layer the fern bank in two passes: a wet-into-wet base of Sap Green and Hooker’s Green, then sharper individual frond shapes on top once dry
  • Dappled sunlight is Quinacridone Gold dropped into still-damp green passages — it blooms warmly without going muddy because both pigments are transparent
  • The salmon fry in the shallows are tiny flicks of Raw Sienna and Buff Titanium over a Jadeite Genuine wash — suggest the school, don’t paint individual fish
  • Use Burnt Umber for the creek rocks and the fern bank’s exposed root structure
Prompt 5

Rosario Strait on a Clear Day

Subject: A wide view across Rosario Strait from the Fidalgo shore — milky spawn water in the foreground shallows, clear blue water in the middle distance, forested islands beyond, and an osprey circling high above.

This painting uses the full water gradient: Spawn Shallows mix near shore, transitioning through Cerulean Blue to Cobalt Teal Blue in the deep channel. The forested islands layer Hooker’s Green over Sap Green. One small osprey silhouette ties the water to the sky.

  • Paint the water in one continuous wet-into-wet pass, working from milky Jadeite foreground through Cerulean Blue middle to Cobalt Teal Blue channel — no hard edges
  • The island tree line is Hooker’s Green applied to dry paper with a flat brush — keep the silhouette simple and slightly varied in height
  • Cerulean Blue is opaque, so use it for the sky as well — it creates a natural atmospheric haze over the distant islands
  • The osprey is a tiny silhouette: Burnt Umber, two strokes, angled wings — place it in the upper third and let it be small
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Jadeite Genuine is a PrimaTek pigment — ground mineral, genuinely granulating, heavy particles that settle unpredictably. Give it rough paper and drying time. Temperature runs warm overall: Quinacridone Gold, Transparent Pyrrol Orange, and Raw Sienna push the palette toward spring sun. Cerulean Blue is opaque — use it for the sky and the clear-water edge, not for glazing. Hooker's Green is a convenience mix; it behaves well and doesn't need adjustment for most fiddlehead work. The three greens on the palette (Jadeite, Hooker's, Sap) occupy different corners of the green space — mineral, balanced, and yellow-leaning — so don't treat them as interchangeable. This is the week everything opens. The palette should feel generous.
