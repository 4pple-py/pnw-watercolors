Week 11 — PNW Watercolor Series

Equinox Tilt

Twelve hours of light, twelve of dark, and you can feel the year tipping. Morning sun hits the south side of everything with golden warmth while the north faces stay cold blue. Daffodils are out. Puddles reflect both warm sky and cool shadow. The equinox doesn’t split the year — it tilts it.

Primary Trio

New Gamboge

Warm equinox sun on the south-facing wall; transparent, luminous, the color of morning light that finally has weight to it

French Ultramarine

Cool equinox sky on the north-facing side; granulating, deep, the shadow that still bites even as the sun gains ground

Quinacridone Gold

Golden balanced light of the tipping point; transparent, amber-warm, deeper than morning — this is afternoon settling in

Mixes & Ratios

South Wall Gold

2 New Gamboge : 1 Burnt Sienna

The south-facing side of a house at 10 AM on the equinox — warm, saturated, the first sun that feels like it means something.

New Gamboge
Burnt Sienna
New Gamboge is fully transparent (PY97), so it glows on white paper the way actual sunlight glows on white siding. Burnt Sienna (PBr7) adds warmth without killing that transparency — both pigments let light bounce off the paper and back through. At 2:1, the mix reads as warm golden light, not orange. Push toward 3:1 for morning sun, pull toward 1:1 for late afternoon when the warmth deepens. Apply to dry paper for architectural edges — the south wall has a hard line where sun meets shade. That line is the whole painting. Everything warm-dominant on one side, everything cool on the other.

North Shadow

2 French Ultramarine : 1 Payne’s Gray

The north-facing side of everything — blue shadow that still holds winter in it, even as the year tips toward warmth.

Fr. Ultramarine
Payne’s Gray
French Ultramarine (PB29) granulates beautifully and pushes warm for a blue — which sounds contradictory for a cool shadow mix, but that granulation is what makes the shadow feel like atmosphere rather than absence. Payne’s Gray (PBk6/PB15/PBk7) deepens it without muddying it, adding a steely neutrality that reads as cold air. At 2:1, this is a shadow that knows it’s losing ground to the sun but hasn’t given up yet. Use it on the north face of buildings, under eaves, on the shaded side of tree trunks. Pair it directly against the South Wall Gold mix and the warm-cool split tells the whole equinox story.

Tipping Point

1 Quinacridone Gold : 1 Sap Green : touch Cobalt Blue

The exact color of a daffodil stem catching both sun and shadow — warm and cool held in genuine balance.

Quin. Gold
Sap Green
CB
This is the equinox in a puddle on the palette. Quinacridone Gold (PO49) brings amber transparency. Sap Green (PG36/PY150) brings yellow-leaning plant life. The touch of Cobalt Blue (PB28) tips the whole mix just slightly cool — enough that it reads as balanced rather than warm. This is the color of new growth that’s getting real sun for the first time but hasn’t baked in it yet. Use it for daffodil stems, new grass in half-shade, the warm-cool edge where a puddle reflects both sky and bank. The 1:1 ratio is the point — neither pigment dominates, and the Cobalt Blue touch is what keeps it from tipping warm.

Why These Colors?

The palette is built on a literal warm-cool split because the equinox is a literal warm-cool split. New Gamboge and Hansa Yellow Deep handle the sun side — both transparent yellows, but New Gamboge is lighter and more luminous (morning) while Hansa Yellow Deep is richer and more saturated (the daffodil itself). French Ultramarine and Cobalt Blue handle the shadow side — Ultramarine granulates and warms, Cobalt stays smooth and cool, so you get texture variation in your blues. Quinacridone Gold sits right on the fulcrum, amber enough to read warm but deep enough to anchor. Burnt Sienna and Raw Sienna warm the earth tones: Burnt for sun-baked surfaces, Raw for the paths and ground just starting to thaw. Sap Green and Cascade Green split the new growth between sun-side (Sap, transparent, yellow-leaning) and shade-side (Cascade, cooler, muted). Payne’s Gray holds the lingering shadow — the winter that hasn’t quite let go. Count the warms and cools and they’re even. That’s the equinox.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Daffodil Bed, Half Sun, Half Shade

Subject: A bed of daffodils along a south-facing fence line — the left half blazing in direct equinox sun, the right half in the blue shadow of a house. The same flowers, two entirely different color temperatures.

Hansa Yellow Deep in full sun, Quinacridone Gold where the light deepens, and then the North Shadow mix (French Ultramarine + Payne’s Gray) cooling everything on the shade side. The daffodils in shadow shift from yellow to a muted gold-green — the Tipping Point mix handles that transition.

  • Paint the sunny daffodils first with Hansa Yellow Deep on dry paper — bright, saturated, unapologetic
  • For shaded daffodils, mix Hansa Yellow Deep with a touch of Cobalt Blue to cool them — same flower, different light
  • The fence line is Raw Sienna in sun, Payne’s Gray in shade — the same warm-cool split as the flowers
  • The hard shadow edge cutting across the bed is the painting’s spine — make it crisp and diagonal
Prompt 2

Equinox Shadow Study

Subject: A single object — a fence post, a mailbox, a parked bicycle — casting a shadow at exactly noon on the equinox. The shadow and the object the same length. Everything described in warm light and cool shadow only.

The South Wall Gold mix (New Gamboge + Burnt Sienna) for every sunlit surface. The North Shadow mix (French Ultramarine + Payne’s Gray) for every shadow. No other colors needed. The constraint is the point — two mixes, the warm-cool binary of equinox noon.

  • Choose a simple object with clear geometry — the shadow is the co-subject, so keep the object readable
  • The shadow should be the same length as the object is tall — that’s the equinox angle at our latitude
  • Apply the South Wall Gold mix first, let it dry completely, then paint the North Shadow mix right up against it
  • Where shadow meets sunlit ground, the edge should be sharp — equinox noon light is direct and definite
Prompt 3

Puddle Reflecting Both

Subject: A parking-lot puddle or sidewalk puddle reflecting warm sky on one side and cool building shadow on the other. The puddle holds both temperatures simultaneously — the equinox in a square foot of water.

New Gamboge and Quinacridone Gold for the warm reflection. Cobalt Blue and Cascade Green for the cool reflection. Raw Sienna for the wet pavement surrounding the puddle. Payne’s Gray for the puddle’s own depth. The whole palette in one small subject.

  • Wet the puddle shape and drop in New Gamboge from the warm side, Cobalt Blue from the cool side — let them meet in the middle
  • Where warm and cool reflections merge, you’ll get a subtle green — that’s correct, don’t fight it
  • The pavement around the puddle is Raw Sienna with Payne’s Gray cracks — dry brush for texture
  • Add a single Sap Green reflection of a nearby tree to anchor the puddle in a real place
Prompt 4

The Walk Home Without a Headlamp

Subject: A residential street at 6:30 PM in mid-March — still light. The first evening you walk home from work and don’t reach for the headlamp. Street trees casting long shadows. The sky still holds color. You notice it because yesterday you didn’t have this.

Quinacridone Gold and New Gamboge for the low western sky. French Ultramarine overhead, deepening toward the east. Burnt Sienna for warm-lit house fronts. Cascade Green for the street trees still in shadow. Payne’s Gray for the long shadows stretching east across the street.

  • The sky is the story — graduate from Quinacridone Gold at the horizon through New Gamboge to French Ultramarine at the top
  • Street trees are Cascade Green with Sap Green where the last sun catches the west-facing leaves
  • Shadows stretch long and east across the street in Payne’s Gray — they’re the clock that says it’s later than it looks
  • One lit window in warm Raw Sienna says someone’s home already, but you’re still outside, and that’s the point
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. The palette splits evenly warm and cool — five on each side of the temperature line, which is the equinox expressed in pigment. New Gamboge (PY97) is fully transparent and staining; it glows on white paper like no opaque yellow can. French Ultramarine (PB29) granulates, which gives shadow texture and atmosphere. Quinacridone Gold (PO49) is the fulcrum pigment — warm enough to anchor the sun side, deep enough to bridge into the earth tones. Cobalt Blue (PB28) stays smooth where Ultramarine granulates, giving you two different blue personalities. Cascade Green and Sap Green split the same way — Cascade cooler and more muted for shade, Sap warmer and more transparent for sun. Payne’s Gray is the last holdout of winter on this palette. By next week it’ll be gone.
