Week 11 — PNW Watercolor Series

Equinox Tilt

Equinox. Twelve hours sharp. Tree swallows arrive over Fir Island, banking silver at 6 PM — the light finally bilateral, morning and evening equal weight. Skagit tulip fields show ruler-straight green rows in black mud. The tension: perfect balance that lasts exactly one day before tipping toward excess.

Mixes & Ratios

Swallow Bank

2 Cobalt Teal Blue : 1 Buff Titanium

Tree swallow dorsal in equinox light — the iridescent back that flashes between teal and silver as the bird tilts.

Cobalt Teal Blue
Buff Titanium
Cobalt Teal Blue granulates with a metallic quality that no other pigment matches — it's the closest watercolor gets to iridescence. Buff Titanium adds the silver flash, the overexposed edge of the bird in direct equinox light. Paint the swallow as a simple forked shape in motion — crescent body, split tail, one stroke each. Apply the mix to dry paper for a crisp silhouette. For the white underside, leave paper bare. For a banking bird showing dorsal, go heavier on the Cobalt Teal Blue. For one catching full sun, increase the Buff Titanium. Paint a flock, not one bird. The equinox belongs to the plural.

Tulip Furrow

1 Sap Green : 2 Raw Umber : trace Ivory Black

Green row emerging from black tilth — the geometry of agriculture imposed on the Skagit floodplain.

Sap Green
Raw Umber
IB
The ratio tells the story: more mud than green, because at two inches, the tulip rows are barely visible against the field. Paint the furrows first — Raw Umber and Ivory Black applied in parallel horizontal lines on dry paper, ruler-straight. Use a flat brush. While the furrows dry, mix the Sap Green separately and paint the emerging rows between the furrows as thin parallel lines — a rigger brush or the edge of a flat. The green should sit on top of the dark furrows, not blend into them. The geometry is the point. If the rows aren't straight, the painting isn't right. Use a straightedge if you need to. Agriculture is not organic.

Field Dusk

2 Lavender : 1 Yellow Ochre

Skagit flats at the equinox hour — the sky and the barn and the field all holding the same amount of gold and violet.

Lavender
Yellow Ochre
Lavender is a convenience mix that granulates and separates; Yellow Ochre is opaque and warm. Together they produce the specific peach-lavender of equinox dusk over flat agricultural land — the hour when warm and cool light are in genuine balance. Apply as a sky wash, graduated from Lavender-heavy at zenith to Yellow Ochre-heavy at the horizon. Let the Lavender's internal pigments separate on their own. For the barn, use Yellow Ochre with a touch of Raw Umber, and let the sky wash's warmth be the light source. The equinox is the one day this balance exists — paint it like you believe in equilibrium, even if it's temporary.

Why These Colors?

The equinox palette is built on bilateral symmetry: warm and cool in equal measure, neither dominating. Cobalt Teal Blue is the visual anchor — a pigment that reads as both warm and cool depending on its surround, which is exactly what equinox light does. Sap Green and Cascade Green split the greens between cultivated (Sap, transparent, in rows) and wild (Cascade, granulating, at the ditch bank). Raw Umber and Yellow Ochre handle the earth tones with different opacities — Raw Umber transparent for mud, Yellow Ochre opaque for sunlit surfaces. Ivory Black is reserved for the furrow shadows, the only true absolute on the palette, because agricultural geometry demands hard edges. Lavender provides the sky's instability, appropriate for an equinox that lasts one day before the balance tips. French Ultramarine appears in reflections only — the sky seen in water, not directly.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Skagit Tulip Field at First Green

Subject: A wide-format view of the Skagit flats — ruler-straight tulip rows barely two inches tall emerging from black tilled mud, a red barn small in the middle distance, and the flat horizon bisecting the painting exactly in half.

The Tulip Furrow mix (1 Sap Green : 2 Raw Umber : trace Ivory Black) was designed for exactly this moment. Yellow Ochre warms the barn. Lavender handles the enormous equinox sky. The geometry of this palette — its most disciplined week — matches the geometry of the subject.

  • Use a flat brush and a straightedge for the furrows — Raw Umber and Ivory Black in parallel horizontal lines on dry paper
  • The tulip rows are thin Sap Green lines painted with a rigger brush between the dried furrows — don’t let them bleed into the mud
  • Place the horizon at exact center to echo the equinox balance of equal day and night
  • The barn is Yellow Ochre with Raw Umber shadow — keep it small, let the field geometry dominate
Prompt 2

Tree Swallows Over Fir Island

Subject: A flock of eight to twelve tree swallows banking over an irrigation ditch at golden hour, their iridescent backs catching equinox light, the ditch below reflecting both sky and birds.

Cobalt Teal Blue is the only watercolor pigment that approximates tree swallow iridescence. The Swallow Bank mix (2:1 Cobalt Teal Blue to Buff Titanium) gives you the dorsal flash. French Ultramarine in the ditch reflection and Lavender in the sky create a cool envelope the warm-teal birds pop against.

  • Paint each swallow as two strokes max: a crescent for the body, a forked V for the tail — resist adding detail
  • Vary the Cobalt Teal Blue to Buff Titanium ratio bird by bird — more silver on birds catching direct sun, more teal on those in shadow
  • The ditch reflection is French Ultramarine with broken bits of Cobalt Teal Blue — paint it looser than the birds themselves
  • Leave paper bare for the white undersides of banking swallows — that contrast is what makes them read as swallows
Prompt 3

Equinox Dusk Over the Flats

Subject: The Skagit Valley at exactly 6 PM on the equinox — equal light on both horizons, a lavender-gold sky graduated from zenith to horizon, flat fields dissolving into atmospheric haze.

The Field Dusk mix (2 Lavender : 1 Yellow Ochre) produces the specific peach-violet of equinox dusk over flat agricultural land. Buff Titanium brightens the horizon band. The palette’s deliberate warm-cool balance mirrors the one day a year when neither side wins.

  • Wet the entire sky area and lay in Lavender from the top, graduating to Yellow Ochre at the horizon — let the Lavender’s internal pigments separate naturally
  • Add a thin band of Buff Titanium at the horizon line while the sky wash is still damp
  • The fields below are Raw Umber washed flat with Sap Green hints — keep them simple, the sky is the subject
  • Resist the urge to add drama — the equinox is about balance, not spectacle
Prompt 4

Irrigation Ditch and Ditch Bank

Subject: A close-up composition — an irrigation ditch cutting diagonally across the frame, still water reflecting sky, wild grass on the bank vivid green against the dark water, a single dandelion at the edge.

French Ultramarine in the ditch water, Cascade Green for the wild bank grass (its granulation gives you texture for free), and Hansa Yellow Medium for that first dandelion — a tiny warm accent that pulls the whole composition together.

  • Paint the ditch water as a flat wash of French Ultramarine with a touch of Ivory Black for depth — keep it still and mirror-like
  • The bank grass is Cascade Green applied with upward flicking strokes on dry paper, overlapping with Sap Green for the newer growth
  • Drop a single Hansa Yellow Medium dandelion at the ditch edge — small, specific, the only pure warm spot in the painting
  • Use Raw Umber for the exposed dirt of the ditch bank where grass hasn’t filled in yet
Prompt 5

Barn and Daffodil Row

Subject: A weathered Skagit barn with a row of daffodils along its foundation, late afternoon equinox light hitting the west-facing wall, the first swallows circling above the roofline.

Yellow Ochre is the barn wall in warm light. Hansa Yellow Medium handles the daffodils — brighter, more saturated than the barn, so they read as flowers against wood. The Swallow Bank mix dots the sky above. Ivory Black defines the barn’s shadow side with hard, architectural edges.

  • Lay in the barn’s sunlit wall as a flat Yellow Ochre wash, then add Raw Umber for the wood grain texture with a dry-brush pass
  • The shadow side is Ivory Black mixed with a touch of French Ultramarine — hard-edged, geometric, no soft gradients
  • Daffodils are Hansa Yellow Medium with Sap Green stems — paint them as simple cup shapes, five or six in a casual row
  • The swallows above the roofline are tiny Cobalt Teal Blue crescents — three or four, no more, suggesting the season’s arrival
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Temperature is deliberately balanced — count the warms and cools and they're even, which is the equinox in pigment form. Cobalt Teal Blue is the palette's signature; no substitute captures the metallic, shifting quality of tree swallow plumage. Ivory Black is fully opaque — use it only for furrow darks and graphic line work, not for mixing. Cascade Green and Sap Green are close in hue but different in personality: Cascade granulates and cools, Sap stays smooth and warm. The palette rewards precision: straight furrows, clean swallow shapes, clear horizon lines. This is the most geometric week in the series. Let the Skagit's flatness and the tulip rows' discipline dictate the composition.
