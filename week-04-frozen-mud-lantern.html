Week 4 — PNW Watercolor Series

Frozen Mud Lantern

The ground has been frozen solid for three mornings straight. Ice on the puddles thick enough to step on, cracking in clean lines under your boot. The porch light throws amber across frozen mud and you can see your breath in the cone of it — everything outside that glow is Indanthrone Blue darkness.

Primary Trio

Yellow Ochre

The lantern glow pooling on frozen ground — warm, opaque, the only hospitable color in the scene.

Raw Umber

Frozen mud itself — granulating, earthy, locked solid and holding the shapes of last week’s boot prints.

Indanthrone Blue

The deep cold darkness past the reach of any light — granulating, staining, absolute.

Mixes & Ratios

Porch Light Halo

2 New Gamboge : 2 Yellow Ochre : 1 Burnt Sienna

The amber corona where lantern light meets frozen air — warm enough to melt the dark at its edges but not to warm your hands.

New Gamboge
Yellow Ochre
Burnt Sienna
New Gamboge is transparent and high-chroma — it provides the luminous core of the light source where the glow is brightest. Yellow Ochre is semi-opaque and granulating, which grounds the light as it spreads across the frozen surface, giving it a dusty warmth that reads as real lamplight rather than sunlight. Burnt Sienna enters at the halo’s edge where the warmth starts to redden and die. Apply New Gamboge at the center on dry paper, then immediately blend outward into premixed Yellow Ochre + Burnt Sienna while the edge is still wet. The transition should be seamless. Don’t go back in — one pass.

Black Ice Glaze

1 French Ultramarine : 1 Payne’s Gray : trace Buff Titanium

The skin of ice on a frozen puddle — not white, not blue, but something between glass and sky that you only see when you crouch down.

French Ultramarine
Payne’s Gray
Buff Ti.
French Ultramarine granulates beautifully — its particles settle into the paper’s valleys, creating a crystalline texture that mimics the mineral quality of ice. Payne’s Gray cools and neutralizes the blue without killing it, pulling the mix toward that ambiguous gray-blue of actual ice rather than storybook blue. The trace of Buff Titanium adds the faintest opacity — ice isn’t transparent when it’s frozen hard, it’s translucent, and that milky quality is what Buff provides. Apply as a single thin wash over dry paper. Let the granulation do the work. Where the ice is thickest, add a second pass of pure Buff Titanium while the first layer is still damp.

Rut Water at Midnight

2 Transparent Brown Oxide : 1 Lamp Black : 1 Indanthrone Blue

The color of the tire rut puddle you almost step in on a dark rural road — not water anymore, not mud, just cold ground with depth.

Transp. Brown Oxide
Lamp Black
Indanthrone Blue
Transparent Brown Oxide is the key here — it’s genuinely transparent, which means you can glaze it in layers that build depth the way actual muddy water has depth. Lamp Black is dense and opaque, so use it sparingly; it anchors the darkest areas where the rut is deepest. Indanthrone Blue granulates and stains, giving the mix a cold undertone that prevents it from reading as warm chocolate. Lay Transparent Brown Oxide first as a glaze. While still wet, drop Indanthrone Blue into the deepest areas and watch it bloom and granulate. Once dry, touch Lamp Black into only the very darkest points — the center of the rut, the shadow under a frozen lip of mud.

Why These Colors?

The palette is built on a thermal divide. Three pigments carry warmth — Yellow Ochre, New Gamboge, Burnt Sienna — and they’re outnumbered by cold: Indanthrone Blue, French Ultramarine, Payne’s Gray, Lamp Black. That’s the ratio of a late January night. The warm pigments cluster near the lantern; the cold ones own everything else. Raw Umber and Transparent Brown Oxide hold the middle ground as frozen earth, neither warm nor cold, just present. Raw Umber granulates for surface texture; Transparent Brown Oxide is glassy and glazeable for depth. Buff Titanium is the ice — its opacity is structural, not decorative. It sits on top of whatever you put it over, the way ice sits on a puddle. Lamp Black provides a darkness that Indanthrone Blue can’t: total, neutral, without any blue romance. The space between Lamp Black and New Gamboge is the full range of this week — from the flame in the lantern to the nothing past the tree line.

Painting Prompts

Late January in the PNW lowlands. The freeze has settled in. These four subjects live entirely within this palette’s warm-cold tension — each one asks you to decide where the light ends and the dark begins.

Prompt 1

Porch Light on Frozen Mud

Subject: A single porch light throwing an amber cone across a frozen mud yard. Boot prints frozen into the ground. Your breath visible in the light. Beyond the cone’s edge, darkness.

The Porch Light Halo mix (New Gamboge + Yellow Ochre + Burnt Sienna) gives you the gradient from bright core to warm edge. Indanthrone Blue and Lamp Black split the darkness — Indanthrone for the sky and middle distance, Lamp Black for the true void at the painting’s edges.

  • Start with the light — paint the amber cone first on dry paper using the Porch Light Halo mix, brightest at top center and fading as it spreads downward and outward
  • Let the warm wash dry completely before painting the frozen mud beneath it with Raw Umber dry-brushed for granular texture, leaving the boot print shapes as negative space
  • Build the darkness around the cone with Indanthrone Blue first, then deepen the corners with Lamp Black — the transition from blue-dark to black-dark is what makes the night feel real
  • The breath is reserved white paper — just leave it unpainted within the amber cone, and it reads immediately
Prompt 2

Frozen Puddle Close-Up

Subject: A frozen puddle in a gravel road, thick enough to be opaque at center, thin enough at the edges to see mud through the ice. Air bubbles trapped in layers. Late afternoon light catching the surface.

The Black Ice Glaze mix (French Ultramarine + Payne’s Gray + trace Buff Titanium) handles the ice itself. Transparent Brown Oxide beneath it gives the mud visible through thinner ice at the edges. Yellow Ochre provides the low-angle afternoon light catching the surface.

  • Paint the mud beneath the ice first with Transparent Brown Oxide as a light glaze — this is what shows through at the puddle’s thin edges
  • Once dry, apply the Black Ice Glaze mix over the center, leaving the edges lighter where the ice is thin and the brown shows through
  • Scratch out air bubbles with a palette knife while the ice wash is still damp — they should be irregular, not perfectly round
  • Touch Yellow Ochre along the surface where afternoon light catches it — a thin line of warmth on a cold surface
Prompt 3

Headlights on a Dark Rural Road

Subject: A dark rural road at night, headlights approaching from a distance. The light catches frozen ruts and ice patches in the road surface. Trees line both sides as dark masses.

New Gamboge at the headlight core, Yellow Ochre where the beams hit frozen ground. The Rut Water at Midnight mix (Transparent Brown Oxide + Lamp Black + Indanthrone Blue) handles the road surface. Payne’s Gray builds the tree silhouettes.

  • Reserve two small areas of white paper for the headlights, then surround them with New Gamboge fading to Yellow Ochre as the beams spread across the road
  • Paint the road surface with the Rut Water at Midnight mix, working around the light areas — the Transparent Brown Oxide should be visible where headlights illuminate the frozen ruts
  • Build tree masses on both sides with Payne’s Gray layered with Indanthrone Blue — don’t try to paint individual trees, just the shape of the dark pressing in from both sides
  • Use Buff Titanium for ice patches catching headlight glare — a few opaque strokes on the road surface, flat and reflective
Prompt 4

Lantern in a Barn Doorway

Subject: An open barn door with a lantern hung just inside. Warm light spills out across frozen mud and straw. The barn interior is dark behind the lantern. The sky outside is Indanthrone Blue, starless.

This prompt uses almost every pigment. The lantern core is New Gamboge, the light-on-mud is Yellow Ochre over Raw Umber, the barn interior behind the lantern is Lamp Black. Burnt Sienna warms the doorframe. Indanthrone Blue holds the sky.

  • Paint the lantern as a small shape of pure New Gamboge — transparent and glowing, the brightest point in the painting
  • Build the warm light spilling outward with Yellow Ochre, grading into Burnt Sienna at the edges where warmth meets cold — the Porch Light Halo mix works here
  • The frozen mud in the light should be Raw Umber with Yellow Ochre glazed over it — two separate layers, not premixed, so the warmth sits on top of the earth
  • Paint the barn interior behind the lantern in Lamp Black — dense, opaque, no detail. The sky is Indanthrone Blue, granulating and deep, and it should wrap around the barn and meet the Lamp Black at the roofline
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Yellow Ochre (PY43) is natural iron oxide — semi-opaque and granulating. Raw Umber (PBr7) granulates heavily on cold-pressed paper, which gives frozen mud its texture for free. Indanthrone Blue (PB60) is a staining, granulating blue with extraordinary depth — it goes darker than you expect and stays blue doing it. New Gamboge (PY97) is fully transparent and non-granulating, which is why it reads as light rather than substance. French Ultramarine (PB29) granulates with a coarser grain than Indanthrone Blue, making it better for ice surfaces. Transparent Brown Oxide (PR101) is the glazing earth — build depth with layers rather than concentration. Buff Titanium (PW6:1) is the palette’s only true opaque light, and here it plays ice: a surface that sits on top of things. Lamp Black (PBk6) is carbon-based, dense, and neutral — no blue bias, no warm bias, just dark. The warm-cold contrast in this palette works because it’s numerically lopsided: three warm pigments against seven cool and neutral ones, which is the actual ratio of warmth to cold on a January night.
