Mixes & Ratios

Spathe Glow

3 Hansa Yellow Deep : 1 Buff Titanium

Skunk cabbage catching weak sun through fog — a lantern with no flame, just chlorophyll and will.

Hansa Yellow Deep
Buff Titanium
Hansa Yellow Deep is transparent and clean; Buff Titanium adds just enough body to suggest the waxy opacity of the spathe itself. Mix on the palette — don’t let them mingle on paper or you’ll lose the glow. Apply to dry paper in a single stroke shaped like the spathe’s hood. Leave a hard edge on the light side, soften the shadow side with a damp brush. The Buff Titanium keeps the yellow from going shrill. For the spadix inside, use pure Hansa Yellow Deep, darker and more saturated.

Bottomland Freeze

2 Burnt Umber : 1 Payne’s Gray : trace Buff Titanium

Cracked mud with ice — the surface of a bottomland field that froze mid-heave, each polygon rimmed in white.

Burnt Umber
Payne’s Gray
Buff Ti.
Apply Burnt Umber and Payne’s Gray premixed for the mud polygons, using dry-brush technique on rough paper to catch the texture of frost-cracked earth. While still slightly damp, touch Buff Titanium into the cracks between polygons — it will bloom outward just enough to suggest ice crystal growth. Don’t smooth it. The granulation in Burnt Umber handles the grit. For deeper cracks, increase the Payne’s Gray. The Buff Titanium trace is structural — it’s the ice, not decoration.

Heron Field

2 Payne’s Gray : 1 Raw Umber

Standing water reflecting nothing — flooded pasture under fog, a great blue heron knee-deep and motionless.

Payne’s Gray
Raw Umber
This is the color of water that has no sky to reflect — all information comes from the sediment in it. Both pigments granulate, producing a soft, silty wash ideal for flooded field water. Apply wet-in-wet for the water surface, letting the pigments settle and separate. For the heron, wait until the wash is bone dry, then paint the bird in pure Payne’s Gray with a firm hand. The heron should be sharp against the soft field. One bird. One stroke for the neck. Don’t fuss.

Why These Colors?

This palette is organized around a single thermal event: warmth erupting through cold. Hansa Yellow Deep is the eruption — transparent, stain-free, the cleanest warm yellow available, isolated against a field of granulating earth tones and atmospheric grays. Burnt Umber and Raw Umber both granulate, which gives every mud passage the particulate texture of frost-heaved ground without brushwork. Potter’s Pink is the fog note — it’s opaque and chalky, which is exactly what fog is. Payne’s Gray and Neutral Tint split the cool darks between atmosphere (Payne’s) and depth (Neutral Tint). Buff Titanium’s opacity is structural here: ice is opaque. The palette places one transparent warm against nine pigments that range from cool to neutral, making the skunk cabbage impossible to ignore — which is the plant’s entire reproductive strategy.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Skunk Cabbage Lantern

Subject: A single skunk cabbage spathe pushing through ice-skinned mud, glowing yellow against the frozen brown bottomland. Fog behind it. Nothing else in the scene is warm.

The Spathe Glow mix (Hansa Yellow Deep + Buff Titanium) gives you the waxy, luminous hood. The Bottomland Freeze mix (Burnt Umber + Payne’s Gray + trace Buff Titanium) handles the cracked mud around it. One warm shape against nine cool pigments — the palette is engineered for this exact drama.

  • Paint the spathe first on dry paper — a single stroke shaped like the hood, using the Spathe Glow mix. Leave a hard edge on the light side, soften the shadow side with a damp brush
  • Build the frozen mud around the spathe with the Bottomland Freeze mix using dry-brush technique on rough paper — the granulation handles the frost-crack texture
  • Touch Buff Titanium into the mud cracks while still slightly damp for ice crystal blooms
  • The fog behind everything is Potter’s Pink + Buff Titanium, barely there — keep it pale and chalky
Prompt 2

Great Blue Heron in Flooded Pasture

Subject: A great blue heron standing motionless in a flooded Snoqualmie bottomland field, knee-deep in silty water that reflects nothing. Fog barely lifting at 10 AM.

The Heron Field mix (Payne’s Gray + Raw Umber) is the water — granulating, sediment-heavy, no sky to reflect. Pure Payne’s Gray gives you the heron’s sharp silhouette against the soft field. Cobalt Blue appears only in the wing flash if the bird lifts off.

  • Paint the flooded field first as a wet-in-wet wash of Payne’s Gray + Raw Umber — tilt the paper and let the pigments settle and separate for five minutes minimum
  • Wait until the wash is bone dry before painting the heron — the bird must be sharp against the soft water
  • Paint the heron in pure Payne’s Gray with a firm hand: one stroke for the neck, a few strokes for the body. Don’t fuss
  • Add Cobalt Blue as a tiny flash at the wing bend — this is the only cool-bright note in the painting and it reads as potential movement
Prompt 3

Frost-Heaved Mud Close-Up

Subject: A close study of frozen bottomland mud — polygonal cracks rimmed in ice, dead grass flattened across the surface, everything mineral and geometric.

Burnt Umber granulates for grit, Payne’s Gray cools the shadows between polygons, and Buff Titanium — opaque and matte — IS the ice in the cracks. Olive Green handles the winter-flattened dead grass.

  • Sketch the crack pattern lightly in pencil first — these polygons are the painting’s composition
  • Paint each mud polygon individually with the Bottomland Freeze mix, using dry-brush on rough paper and leaving thin white lines between them
  • Once dry, fill the crack lines with Buff Titanium — apply it opaquely so it sits on top, reading as ice rather than space
  • Lay dead grass across the mud with Olive Green in thin, flat strokes — the grass is winter-pressed, horizontal, defeated
Prompt 4

Fog Lifting Over the Bottomland

Subject: The moment the fog begins to lift at 10 AM — the distant treeline barely visible through Potter’s Pink haze, the field still flooded, the sky a non-color.

Potter’s Pink is opaque and chalky, which is exactly what fog is. Indanthrone Blue dissolves the treeline into the distance — deep enough to register behind the fog without going black. Neutral Tint handles the standing water’s depth.

  • Wet the top half of the paper and lay Potter’s Pink + Buff Titanium as the fog — keep it milky and even
  • While the fog wash is still damp, touch Indanthrone Blue along the bottom edge for the treeline dissolving into mist — let it bleed upward
  • Paint the flooded field below with Payne’s Gray + Raw Umber on dry paper for a hard horizon line where land meets fog
  • Use Neutral Tint in the deepest water foreground — it’s transparent and cool, giving the standing water depth without reflection
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Temperature runs cold except for the Hansa Yellow Deep and its immediate neighbors — Olive Green, Potter’s Pink. The two Umbers are close in hue but different in behavior: Burnt Umber is darker and more opaque, Raw Umber is lighter and more transparent. Use Burnt for the mud surface, Raw for the water over it. Cobalt Blue appears only in the heron’s wing flash — a tiny note of pure cool that reads as movement against the stillness. Indanthrone Blue handles the distant treeline because it’s deep enough to sit behind fog without going black. Reserve your whites aggressively — ice and spathe highlights are the architecture of this painting.
