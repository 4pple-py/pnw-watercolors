Week 15 — PNW Watercolor Series

Tulip Field Burn

The Skagit tulip fields don’t do subtlety. Acres of Pyrrol Red, rows of Cadmium Orange, blocks of Hansa Yellow — flat, saturated, geometric. The Cascades behind in Cobalt Blue. Mud between the rows. It looks fake and it’s completely real.

Primary Trio

Pyrrol Red

The red tulip blocks that stop traffic on Memorial Highway; opaque, lightfast, and unapologetically saturated

Cadmium Orange Hue

Orange tulip fields in flat agricultural rows; dense, opaque warmth that reads as color blocks from a quarter mile away

Hansa Yellow Deep

Yellow tulip rows glowing in mid-April sun; warmer and denser than Hansa Yellow Light, matching the deep gold of peak-season blooms

Mixes & Ratios

Tulip Furnace

2 Pyrrol Red : 1 Alizarin Crimson : ½ Cadmium Red Medium Hue

The deepest red block in the field — the row that makes everything else look pastel by comparison.

Pyrrol Red
Alizarin Crimson
Cad. Red Med.
Pyrrol Red (PR254) lays down the opaque body — it’s lightfast and hits hard. Alizarin Crimson deepens the shadow side with transparent glazing, pulling the red toward burgundy without killing chroma. Cadmium Red Medium Hue adds sheer density, the pigment equivalent of turning the volume knob past ten. Apply in flat, confident washes to mimic the geometric blocks of the actual fields. Don’t blend at the edges — tulip rows have hard boundaries.

Furrow Mud

2 Burnt Sienna : 1 Hooker’s Green : ½ French Ultramarine

The reality beneath the spectacle — Skagit Valley alluvial mud, wet and dark between perfect rows of impossible color.

Burnt Sienna
Hooker's Green
Fr. Ultramarine
Burnt Sienna is the warm, iron-rich base — Skagit Valley soil is genuinely this color. Hooker’s Green cools it into the mossy darkness where boots sink. French Ultramarine in small doses pushes the mix toward near-black for the wettest furrows without going dead. The beauty of this mix is that it granulates gently, giving you soil texture for free. Drag it dry-brush for foreground paths and let it pool wet for background furrows.

Cascade Wall

2 Cobalt Blue : 1 French Ultramarine : ½ Burnt Sienna

The mountain backdrop behind every tulip photo — snowcapped Cascades going blue-violet in spring humidity.

Cobalt Blue
Fr. Ultramarine
Burnt Sienna
Cobalt Blue gives you the cool, slightly granulating base that reads as atmospheric distance. French Ultramarine deepens the shadowed valleys between peaks, its warmer blue-violet creating depth. The touch of Burnt Sienna is the secret — it grays the mix just enough to push the mountains back behind the screaming tulip foreground. Without it, the blues compete with the reds for attention. Lay this in wet and leave it alone. The mountains are background; they should look effortless.

Why These Colors?

This palette is deliberately loaded with opaque warm primaries because the Skagit tulip fields are, themselves, opaque warm primaries. Pyrrol Red, Cadmium Orange Hue, and Cadmium Red Medium Hue all sit on the paper as flat, dense blocks — exactly how tulip rows read from the road. Hansa Yellow Deep brings the warm gold that Hansa Yellow Light can’t reach. Alizarin Crimson is the lone transparent red, doing the shadow work that opaques can’t. Sap Green and Hooker’s Green split the foliage duties — bright stems versus dark leaf mass. French Ultramarine and Cobalt Blue handle the sky and mountains, with Cobalt Blue’s granulation providing atmospheric texture. Burnt Sienna grounds everything in the alluvial mud that makes the whole spectacle possible.

Painting Prompts

Four subjects built for this week’s palette — each one leans into the saturated geometry of peak tulip season in the Skagit Valley.

Prompt 1

Tulip Rows at Peak

Subject: A straight-on view down converging tulip rows at absolute peak bloom — red, orange, yellow bands receding toward the vanishing point, Cascade foothills soft behind.

Pyrrol Red, Cadmium Orange Hue, and Hansa Yellow Deep give you three distinct hot rows. The Cascade Wall mix (Cobalt Blue + French Ultramarine + Burnt Sienna) dissolves the mountains into the background where they belong.

  • Paint the Cascade Wall mix first, wet-in-wet, and let it dry completely before any tulip color touches the paper
  • Use a flat brush loaded with Pyrrol Red for single horizontal strokes per row — the opacity does the work, no layering needed
  • Drop Sap Green between rows while the warm colors are still slightly damp for natural stem-to-petal bleeding
  • Increase saturation from background to foreground — dilute washes for distant rows, full-strength pigment in front
Prompt 2

Single Tulip Close-Up

Subject: One red tulip filling the frame, petals backlit by afternoon sun, the interior glowing with transmitted light, out-of-focus color blocks behind.

Pyrrol Red for the lit petals, Alizarin Crimson for the translucent shadow folds, Cadmium Red Medium Hue for the densest petal bases. Hansa Yellow Deep glazed over Pyrrol Red creates the backlit glow.

  • Start with a pale Hansa Yellow Deep wash for the backlit center, then build Pyrrol Red around it while the yellow is still damp
  • Alizarin Crimson’s transparency is essential for the petal fold shadows — glaze it over dry Pyrrol Red for luminous depth
  • The blurred background is soft wet-in-wet blocks of Cadmium Orange Hue and Sap Green, unfocused and suggestive
  • Leave a sliver of white paper along one petal edge for the sharpest highlight — no masking fluid needed, just steady hands
Prompt 3

Cascade Backdrop Behind the Fields

Subject: The classic Skagit postcard — snowcapped Cascades rising behind striped tulip fields, the scale absurd, the color contrast almost offensive.

Cobalt Blue and French Ultramarine own the mountains. The entire warm half of the palette fires in horizontal bands below. Hooker’s Green separates the color rows like mortar between bricks.

  • Give the mountains a full third of the composition — they’re the cool counterweight to all that warm saturation below
  • Use Cobalt Blue for the peaks and French Ultramarine for the shadowed valleys, letting them bleed together on wet paper
  • Burnt Sienna dry-brushed at the tree line creates the dark conifer band that separates mountains from fields
  • Resist softening the tulip rows — the geometric hard edges against the soft mountains create the tension that makes this view work
Prompt 4

Mud Path Between Color Blocks

Subject: A muddy walking path cutting between red and yellow tulip blocks, boot prints in wet soil, the humble infrastructure of a festival field.

The Furrow Mud mix (Burnt Sienna + Hooker’s Green + French Ultramarine) is the star. Pyrrol Red and Hansa Yellow Deep flank it on either side. The contrast between careful agriculture and trampled earth tells the story.

  • Paint the tulip blocks first as flat, saturated panels on either side, leaving the path as reserved white paper
  • Lay the Furrow Mud mix wet into the path area, tilting the paper to let pigment pool in the boot-print depressions
  • Sap Green at the tulip-row edges softens the transition from flower to stem to mud
  • A few calligraphic strokes of concentrated Hooker’s Green suggest trampled leaves where the path meets the rows
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. All warm primaries are opaque — this is deliberate. Tulip fields ARE opaque blocks of color. Pyrrol Red (PR254) is lightfast and clean, one of the best modern reds. Cadmium Red Medium Hue adds density where Pyrrol Red needs reinforcement. Alizarin Crimson (PR177 in DS formulation) provides the transparent red glazing that opaques cannot. Cobalt Blue granulates gently on rough paper. French Ultramarine is the warmer, deeper blue for mountain shadows. ~April 9–15.
