Week 15 — PNW Watercolor Series

Tulip Field Burn

Skagit tulip fields at 2 PM, fourteen hours of daylight and the sun finally means it. Madrone bark peels to lime-green skin while barn swallows carve the air above irrigation ditches. Spring showing off — gorgeous and slightly too much. The valley is a saturated postcard that dares you to paint it without flinching.

Primary Trio

Hansa Yellow Light

Tulip field yellow rows blazing in flat valley light; transparent, cool-leaning yellow that stays clean in mixes

Opera Pink

Electric tulip pink, the color that shouldn't exist in nature but does; fluorescent, fugitive in sunlight but unapologetic on paper

Cerulean Blue Chromium

Granulating spring sky over the flats; opaque and chalky, it sits on the paper like real atmosphere

Mixes & Ratios

Tulip Blaze

2 Opera Pink : 1 Transparent Pyrrol Orange : ½ Hansa Yellow Light

The searing red-orange row that stops traffic and makes everyone pull over on Best Road.

Opera Pink
Pyrrol Orange
Hansa Yellow
Mix bold — this isn't a moment for subtlety. Opera Pink and Transparent Pyrrol Orange together make a red-orange so vivid it looks backlit. The Hansa Yellow Light lifts the edges toward flame. Apply in confident single strokes; Opera Pink muddies if reworked. Accept the fugitivity — this color, like the tulips, isn't meant to last forever.

Madrone Peel

3 Green Gold : 1 Raw Sienna : ½ Hooker's Green

Lime-to-tan of bark curling off living wood, the tree undressing in slow motion.

Green Gold
Raw Sienna
Hooker's Green
Green Gold dominates — it's the raw, almost acidic lime of fresh madrone bark. Raw Sienna warms the curl where bark dries and lifts. Hooker's Green in the shadow where bark still clings. Let the mix separate on textured paper; the color transitions should feel organic, not blended.

Skagit Haze

2 Cerulean Blue Chromium : 1 Lavender : ½ Buff Titanium

Cascade foothills dissolving into spring humidity, mountains that are more suggestion than fact.

Cerulean Blue Chr.
Lavender
Buff Titanium
Cerulean Blue Chromium granulates aggressively — that's the point. Lavender shifts the mix violet, mimicking the way mountains go purple-blue in humid air. Buff Titanium softens the whole thing into milk. Lay it down wet and don't touch it again. The granulation will do the work of distance for you.

Why These Colors?

This palette leans into maximum chroma because the Skagit Valley in tulip season demands it. Opera Pink is technically fugitive — it will fade in direct sunlight over years — but its fluorescent intensity is irreplaceable for capturing tulip pink at peak saturation. Cerulean Blue Chromium's heavy granulation provides the textural counterweight the palette needs; without it, everything would feel slick and synthetic. Green Gold bridges the warm and cool sides, its yellow-green equally useful for madrone bark and spring foliage. Hooker's Green and Raw Sienna do the unglamorous work of grounding the composition in earth and leaf mass.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Tulip Rows Converging at Infinity

Subject: A low-angle view down endless tulip rows in the Skagit Valley, color bands converging toward the Cascade foothills in spring haze.

Opera Pink and Hansa Yellow Light give you alternating hot rows, while the Skagit Haze mix (Cerulean Blue Chromium + Lavender + Buff Titanium) dissolves the mountains behind them. Transparent Pyrrol Orange handles the flame-colored rows between.

  • Lay the Skagit Haze mix wet-in-wet for the distant Cascades first and let it dry completely before painting any tulip rows over it
  • Use a flat brush loaded with Opera Pink for single confident strokes per row — reworking kills the fluorescent punch
  • Drop Hooker’s Green between rows while the pinks are still damp for leaf mass that bleeds naturally into the flower color
  • Raw Sienna dragged dry-brush between foreground rows gives you the muddy paths without overworking
Prompt 2

Barn Swallows Over the Irrigation Ditch

Subject: A sun-bleached barn and irrigation ditch in flat valley light, barn swallows banking low over the water.

Buff Titanium is the sun-bleached wood. Cobalt Teal Blue is the metallic ditch water. The swallows are tiny gestural marks of Cerulean Blue Chromium and Raw Sienna — dark enough to read as birds, small enough to stay calligraphic.

  • Paint the barn with Buff Titanium and a touch of Raw Sienna on dry paper for crisp, weathered edges
  • The ditch reflection is Cobalt Teal Blue laid in a single horizontal stroke — its opacity gives it that flat, metallic sharpness
  • Swallows are two-stroke marks: one curved stroke of Cerulean Blue Chromium for the back, a flick of Buff Titanium for the belly
  • Keep the sky simple — Cerulean Blue Chromium diluted, one wash, let the granulation be the only texture
Prompt 3

Madrone Peeling Above the Field Edge

Subject: A madrone tree at the edge of a tulip field, its bark curling to reveal lime-green underbark, with a strip of yellow tulips below.

The Madrone Peel mix (Green Gold + Raw Sienna + Hooker’s Green) was built for this exact tree. Hansa Yellow Light at the base gives you the tulip row that frames it.

  • Paint the lime-green underbark with concentrated Green Gold first, then drop Raw Sienna into the curling edges while wet
  • Use dry brush with Hooker’s Green for the shadow side of the trunk where bark still clings
  • Let the Green Gold and the Hansa Yellow Light row nearly touch without merging — the narrow gap of white paper between tree and flowers creates visual snap
  • The bark texture comes from working on rough paper and letting pigment skip across the tooth
Prompt 4

The Saturated Postcard (Full Valley Panorama)

Subject: The classic Skagit view — striped tulip fields stretching to hazy foothills, the whole scene so vivid it looks retouched.

This is why you have a maximum-chroma palette. Every primary fires at once: Hansa Yellow Light rows, Opera Pink rows, Transparent Pyrrol Orange rows, with Lavender foothills and Cerulean Blue Chromium sky.

  • Work the flower rows in horizontal bands from background to foreground, increasing saturation as you come forward — dilute washes in the distance, full-strength in the front
  • The Tulip Blaze mix (Opera Pink + Transparent Pyrrol Orange + Hansa Yellow Light) is your hottest row — save it for one foreground band to create a focal point
  • Hooker’s Green between every row keeps the stripes from floating — it’s the anonymous green that holds the whole composition together
  • Resist the urge to soften. This landscape is genuinely this saturated in mid-April. Trust the pigments.
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Opera Pink (PV1 / PR122) is brilliant but fugitive — protect finished work from UV exposure. Cerulean Blue Chromium is a true chromium-based cerulean and significantly more granulating than Cerulean Blue. Cobalt Teal Blue is opaque; use sparingly for reflections and accents. Green Gold is being reformulated periodically — check your tube batch for consistency.
