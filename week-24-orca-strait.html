Week 24 — PNW Watercolor Series

Orca Strait

A dorsal fin breaks the surface in Haro Strait and the scale of it stops you. Lamp Black body, Chinese White eye patch, cutting through Phthalo Blue water so deep it’s almost black. Behind the pod: Cobalt Teal Blue shallows, Cascade Green kelp beds, Cerulean Blue sky. The orcas are the only thing in the composition that’s not blue.

Mixes & Ratios

Orca Water

3 Phthalo Blue GS : 1 Indigo : ½ Cobalt Teal Blue

Haro Strait at depth — the water the pod moves through, so blue it reads as black until the light catches it.

Phthalo Blue GS
Indigo
Cobalt Teal
Phthalo Blue GS stains the paper like deep ocean stains memory — it doesn’t lift, doesn’t forgive, and that’s the point. Indigo pushes the mix toward the near-black of deep channel water. Cobalt Teal Blue at half-part rescues the mix from pure darkness, adding the mineral turquoise that makes Haro Strait look different from open ocean. Apply to wet paper in a single confident wash. The Phthalo will creep and bloom on its own — fight the urge to correct it. That irregular spread is what strait water actually looks like from a ferry deck.

Orca Flank

3 Lamp Black : 1 Neutral Tint : touch Phthalo Blue GS

The body of a resident orca — not flat black but a living, light-absorbing surface with blue undertones where wet skin catches sky.

Lamp Black
Neutral Tint
Phthalo Blue
Lamp Black is carbon pigment — PBk6 — dense and cool, with a velvety matte quality that dries darker than it looks wet. Neutral Tint adds chromatic depth to the shadow side of the body, preventing the flat-poster look you get from black alone. The touch of Phthalo Blue GS is the secret: orca skin in sunlight has a blue sheen, and this tiny addition makes the black read as alive rather than printed. Apply at full concentration. Diluted black looks like dirty water. Orca black should be committed.

Kelp Drift

2 Cascade Green : 1 Cobalt Teal Blue : ½ French Ultramarine

Bull kelp beds off the San Juan shoreline — green-brown fronds tinting the turquoise water darker where the canopy floats.

Cascade Green
Cobalt Teal
Fr. Ultramarine
Cascade Green is the kelp itself — a muted, organic green that reads as plant life in water rather than landscape foliage. Cobalt Teal Blue integrates the kelp color into the surrounding strait water so the transition doesn’t look cut-and-pasted. French Ultramarine at half-part adds the depth you see when looking through kelp canopy to the water beneath — it granulates slightly, which helps suggest the layered, translucent quality of floating fronds. Wet-on-wet application is essential. Kelp beds don’t have hard edges.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built on a stark fact: orcas are black and white animals in blue water. Lamp Black — PBk6, carbon-based, cooler than Ivory Black — gives the dense, velvety opacity that orca skin demands. Chinese White provides the eye patch and saddle without the chalkiness of Titanium White; it’s semi-opaque, which is exactly how those markings read against a wet black body. Phthalo Blue GS is the strait itself, a staining pigment so permanent it feels like the water’s commitment to being blue. Cobalt Teal Blue handles the turquoise shallows — PG50 is the only pigment that honestly captures that mineral-bright San Juan color where the bottom comes up. Indigo takes the water to its darkest honest depth. French Ultramarine softens the far islands into haze. Cascade Green is the kelp beds, Cerulean Blue is the sky, Neutral Tint is the shadow the pod casts beneath itself, and Buff Titanium catches the foam where a fluke just was. Every pigment serves the scene. Nothing decorates.

Painting Prompts

Four subjects built for this palette — each one uses the colors you already have on your palette and the mixes described above. The strait is right outside. The orcas may or may not cooperate.

Prompt 1

Dorsal Fin Breaking Surface

Subject: A single tall dorsal fin — bull male, six feet of black blade — slicing through Haro Strait water. Just the fin, the spray, and the water. No context needed. The fin is the context.

The Orca Flank mix (Lamp Black + Neutral Tint + Phthalo Blue GS) for the fin itself, the Orca Water mix for the strait, and preserved white paper for the spray catch at the base. Buff Titanium for the secondary spray that’s already falling back.

  • Paint the water first with the Orca Water mix on wet paper — let Phthalo Blue GS bloom and stain before touching anything else
  • The dorsal fin goes in after the water is bone-dry. Use the Orca Flank mix at full concentration; the fin should be the darkest value in the painting by a wide margin
  • Preserve white paper at the waterline where the fin meets the surface — this is the spray catch, and painting it white will never look as good as leaving it
  • Buff Titanium for the falling spray droplets on either side — small, scattered, irregular. Spray is not symmetrical
Prompt 2

Orca Spy-Hop

Subject: An orca rising vertically out of the water, eye above the surface, looking directly at you. The eye patch is the focal point — Chinese White against Lamp Black, with Cobalt Teal Blue water streaming off the body.

Lamp Black for the body at full saturation, Chinese White for the eye patch applied thick enough to hold against the black, Cobalt Teal Blue for the shallow water cascading off the whale’s skin. Cerulean Blue sky in the upper third.

  • Sketch the eye patch shape first and mask it or leave it — the white must be clean, not painted over black. Chinese White is semi-opaque but it can’t fully cover Lamp Black
  • The body is Orca Flank mix applied in one pass, wet and confident. Orca skin is smooth; brush marks that suggest texture are lies
  • Water running off the body is Cobalt Teal Blue pulled downward in streaks while the black is still damp — the two will mingle at the edges exactly the way water sheets off a whale
  • Cerulean Blue sky should be a simple graded wash — the drama is in the animal, not the atmosphere
Prompt 3

Pod Through Kelp Water

Subject: Three orcas traveling in formation through a kelp bed — black dorsal fins breaking green-tinged water, bull kelp fronds visible at the surface between them. San Juan island shoreline distant.

The Kelp Drift mix (Cascade Green + Cobalt Teal Blue + French Ultramarine) for the water, transitioning to the Orca Water mix in the deeper channel. Lamp Black fins, French Ultramarine for the distant island.

  • Lay the entire water area wet-on-wet: Kelp Drift mix in the foreground kelp beds, blending into Orca Water mix in the deeper channel behind the pod
  • While the water is still damp, drop concentrated Cascade Green where individual kelp fronds float — they’ll soften naturally into the wet wash
  • The three dorsal fins go in after the water dries. Vary their sizes — the lead fin tallest, one smaller, one medium — and stagger their positions to avoid a picket-fence look
  • French Ultramarine for the distant San Juan shoreline, very dilute, almost a suggestion. The orcas should dominate the composition, not the geography
Prompt 4

Whale from the Ferry

Subject: The view from a Washington State Ferry railing — white-painted steel in the foreground, Haro Strait stretching out, and an orca surfacing mid-distance. The composition every San Juan visitor has photographed badly and never painted well.

Buff Titanium and Chinese White for the ferry railing, the full Orca Water mix for the strait, Lamp Black for the distant orca, Cerulean Blue overhead. Neutral Tint for the railing’s shadow on the deck.

  • The ferry railing is the foreground frame: Buff Titanium base with Chinese White highlights on the sun-facing surfaces. Neutral Tint for the shadow it throws on the deck
  • The strait beyond the railing is the Orca Water mix, covering most of the painting. Let it dry with all its Phthalo Blue irregularities intact
  • The orca is small — mid-distance, maybe two inches of dorsal fin at most. Lamp Black, single stroke, decisive. This is about scale, not detail
  • Resist the urge to add other passengers, cameras, or the ferry structure. The power of this composition is the railing, the water, and one fin. That’s enough
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Lamp Black (PBk6) is carbon-based and cooler than Ivory Black (PBk9) — for orca bodies, the cooler temperature is more accurate. Phthalo Blue GS (PB15:3) stains permanently; test your lifting strategy before committing. Cobalt Teal Blue (PG50) is a genuine cobalt pigment — semi-transparent, with a turquoise no mixing can replicate. Chinese White (PW4) is zinc-based and semi-opaque, deliberately less chalky than Titanium White. Indigo is a convenience mix in Daniel Smith’s line, but its deep blue-black is useful enough to earn its spot.
