Week 18 — PNW Watercolor Series

Rhododendron Shade

Pacific rhododendron blooms in filtered forest light — pink against dark. Madrone flowers hang cream-quiet above. Rufous hummingbirds stitch between them, furious with purpose. Spring has gone interior, working the understory while the canopy thickens overhead. Everything happens in shade now, and the shade is where the color lives.

Primary Trio

Quinacridone Rose

Rhododendron cluster, the pink that owns the forest in May; transparent, staining, intense even in shade

Nickel Azo Yellow

Madrone flower cluster, cream-gold and quiet; semi-transparent, muted, content to be secondary

Cobalt Blue

Sky through canopy gap, blue broken into shards; granulating, luminous, seen only in fragments

Mixes & Ratios

Rhodo Pink

3 Quinacridone Rose : 1 Buff Titanium : ¼ Cobalt Blue

Cool pink of native Pacific rhododendron — not garden pink, not hot pink, but the specific shade that means second-growth forest in May.

Quin. Rose
Buff Titanium
Cobalt
Quinacridone Rose straight is too hot for native rhododendron. Buff Titanium cools and softens it — adds the dusty quality of petals in shade. The touch of Cobalt Blue leans the mix cool, away from tropical and toward temperate. Apply at moderate dilution; too concentrated and it reads like garden azalea, too dilute and you lose the rhododendron's presence in the dark forest.

Nurse Log

2 Raw Umber : 1 Perylene Green : ½ Hooker's Green

Dark rotting wood alive with seedlings — the dead feeding the living, the forest's central deal.

Raw Umber
Perylene Green
Hooker's Green
Raw Umber gives you decomposing wood — granular, earthy, honest about its origins. Perylene Green darkens without muddying, adding the near-black of wet rot. Hooker's Green introduces the living element — moss and hemlock seedlings rooted in the decay. Layer dry over dry to build the textural complexity of a nurse log. Let the granulation of Raw Umber do the heavy lifting.

Gorget Spark

2 Burnt Sienna : 1 Quinacridone Rose : ½ Nickel Azo Yellow

Rufous hummingbird throat catching one ray of sun that made it through the canopy — copper flashing to gold.

Burnt Sienna
Quin. Rose
Nickel Azo Yellow
Burnt Sienna is the gorget's copper base — warm, semi-transparent, it glows. Quinacridone Rose pushes it toward the iridescent magenta that rufous hummingbirds flash at certain angles. Nickel Azo Yellow adds the gold shift. Apply this mix small and precise — the gorget is tiny, and the power comes from contrast against the dark forest, not from size. One charged brushstroke against a Perylene Green background.

Why These Colors?

This palette is calibrated for low light. Quinacridone Rose and Cobalt Blue are both luminous at moderate dilution — they glow rather than recede, which is essential for painting color that exists in shade. Perylene Green and Hooker's Green give the palette two registers of forest darkness: Perylene for the floor, Hooker's for the foliage. Moonglow handles the diffused overhead light with its built-in granulation and violet undertone. Burnt Sienna is the only warm accent, and it's deployed for one purpose: the hummingbird. The restraint is intentional. In the understory, less warmth means each warm note detonates.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Rhododendron Cluster in Deep Shade

Subject: A single Pacific rhododendron flower cluster glowing pink against the dark understory, waxy leaves framing it, barely enough light to read by.

The Rhodo Pink mix (Quinacridone Rose + Buff Titanium + Cobalt Blue) is this exact flower in this exact light. Perylene Green and Hooker’s Green behind it create the darkness that makes the pink detonate.

  • Paint the dark background first — Perylene Green for the deepest shadows, Hooker’s Green for the waxy leaves — and let it dry completely
  • Mix Rhodo Pink at moderate concentration and apply in petal-shaped strokes on top of the dried dark ground; the opacity of Buff Titanium helps the pink sit on the surface
  • Use pure Quinacridone Rose at higher concentration for the deepest folds between petals, where the pink intensifies
  • Leave small flecks of untouched Buff Titanium at petal edges for the dusty quality of rhododendron in shade
Prompt 2

Rufous Hummingbird at the Flower

Subject: A rufous hummingbird hovering at a rhododendron bloom, gorget catching one shaft of filtered light, wings blurred with motion.

The Gorget Spark mix (Burnt Sienna + Quinacridone Rose + Nickel Azo Yellow) is the only warm accent in an otherwise cool palette. One tiny mark of copper-gold against a Perylene Green background, and the whole painting ignites.

  • The hummingbird body is Burnt Sienna — one or two strokes maximum at the scale of a real hummingbird relative to a rhododendron cluster
  • The gorget flash is the Gorget Spark mix applied while the body is still damp, concentrated at the throat, bleeding slightly into the copper
  • Wings are a single stroke of very dilute Moonglow dragged quickly — the blur is the point, and Moonglow’s violet undertone reads as iridescence
  • Place the bird off-center against the darkest part of your background; the contrast ratio does the work of detail
Prompt 3

Nurse Log With Seedlings

Subject: A decomposing nurse log colonized by hemlock seedlings and moss, the forest’s cycle of death-feeding-life in one horizontal composition.

The Nurse Log mix (Raw Umber + Perylene Green + Hooker’s Green) was built for rotting wood. Hooker’s Green and Cascade Green handle the moss and seedlings growing from it.

  • Lay down Raw Umber first for the log body, working on dry paper with a textured brush to catch the tooth — the granulation mimics bark grain
  • Drop Perylene Green into the damp Raw Umber on the shadow side for the wet-rot darkness — let them mingle without mixing
  • Hemlock seedlings are tiny upward strokes of Hooker’s Green, spaced irregularly along the top of the log
  • Moss is Cascade Green dabbed with a nearly dry brush — the cool green reads differently from the Hooker’s Green seedlings, giving you two textures of life
Prompt 4

Light Through the Canopy Gap

Subject: A looking-up view through the forest canopy, one gap of sky letting Cobalt Blue through, Moonglow filtering down, a rhododendron bloom catching the light.

Cobalt Blue is your sky-shard. Moonglow is the diffused light reaching the understory. The Rhodo Pink cluster is the payoff — the bloom that caught the one available ray.

  • Start with the canopy: Hooker’s Green and Perylene Green layered wet-on-dry, leaving one irregular gap of white paper for the sky
  • Fill the sky gap with Cobalt Blue — its granulation at the edges will soften naturally into the leaf shapes surrounding it
  • Wash dilute Moonglow over the mid-ground areas where filtered light reaches — its violet-blue shift reads as the specific color of forest shade
  • The rhododendron bloom is your focal point: Rhodo Pink mix, placed where the Moonglow light is brightest, so it reads as the flower catching the best available light
Prompt 5

The Interior Forest (Everything in Shade)

Subject: A wide view down a trail through old second-growth, rhododendrons blooming on both sides, the path disappearing into green darkness.

This is a value painting disguised as a color painting. Perylene Green, Hooker’s Green, Cascade Green, and Raw Umber handle four registers of darkness, with Rhodo Pink clusters as the only high-chroma notes.

  • Establish the darkest darks first with Perylene Green at the vanishing point — the trail disappears into near-black, and that depth pulls the eye in
  • Build the forest walls with Hooker’s Green layered over dried Cascade Green for canopy depth
  • Rhododendron clusters on each side of the path are Rhodo Pink applied at decreasing saturation toward the background — atmospheric perspective in pink
  • The trail surface is Raw Umber with a touch of Moonglow — duff-colored, soft, the kind of path you walk quietly on
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Perylene Green is extremely dark in mass tone — squeeze out a small amount and test before committing. Moonglow is a three-pigment convenience mix that granulates unpredictably and shifts between violet and blue depending on dilution. Buff Titanium is semi-opaque; it physically lightens mixes rather than just diluting them, which is why it works for the madrone flower cream.
