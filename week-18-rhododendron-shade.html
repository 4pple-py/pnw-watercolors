Week 18 — PNW Watercolor Series

Rhododendron Shade

Rhododendrons bloom where it’s dark. That’s what makes them extraordinary — Quinacridone Rose and Opera Pink explosions in Perylene Green shadow, like someone left neon signs in the forest. The blooms catch whatever light exists and throw it back amplified. Indanthrone Blue darkness pressing in from every side. One shaft of Hansa Yellow Medium light. April 30 through May 6.

Primary Trio

Quinacridone Rose

The rhododendron itself — hot magenta that refuses to behave in shade; transparent, staining, PV19 at its most defiant

Perylene Green

Deep forest shade so dark it swallows brushstrokes; PBk31, transparent, the near-black that makes every pink detonate

Alizarin Crimson

Deep pink-red of the oldest blooms — cool-leaning PR177 that makes clean violets when it meets blue, no mud

Mixes & Ratios

Neon Understory

2 Opera Pink : 1 Quinacridone Rose : ¼ Hansa Yellow Medium

The youngest rhododendron petals catching a shaft of light — so bright they look radioactive against the forest dark.

Opera Pink
Quin. Rose
Hansa Yellow Med.
Opera Pink (BV10:1) is fugitive and fluorescent — it literally glows, which is exactly the point for the freshest petals in a light shaft. Quinacridone Rose anchors the mix with PV19’s permanence and transparency, pulling the fluorescence toward a believable magenta. The whisper of Hansa Yellow Medium warms the mix just enough to suggest sunlight hitting the petal. Apply at moderate dilution over dry white paper; the glow depends on paper reflectance. This mix will fade over months — like the real petals. Use it knowing that.

Rhody Midnight

2 Indanthrone Blue : 1 Perylene Green : ½ Alizarin Crimson

The deepest shade beneath a mature rhododendron canopy — not black, but a darkness with violet memory.

Indanthrone Blue
Perylene Green
Alizarin Crimson
Indanthrone Blue (PB60) is already one of the darkest blues in the Daniel Smith line — transparent, staining, nearly black at full concentration. Perylene Green deepens it further and shifts the temperature toward the cold of actual forest shade. The Alizarin Crimson is the secret: it introduces a violet undertone that reads as reflected bloom-light even in the darkest passages. This is your shadow color for everything under the rhododendron canopy. Layer wet-on-dry for depth that stays luminous instead of going dead.

Forest Floor Dark

2 Burnt Umber : 1 Transparent Red Oxide : ½ Hooker’s Green

Decomposing needles and bark under the rhododendron thicket — the ground nobody sees because they’re looking up at the flowers.

Burnt Umber
Trans. Red Oxide
Hooker’s Green
Burnt Umber (PBr7) gives you the dark, cool base of decomposing organic matter — it granulates on rough paper, which is exactly the texture of forest duff. Transparent Red Oxide warms it, adding the iron-rich color of Douglas fir bark and hemlock needles breaking down. Hooker’s Green introduces the green of moss and liverwort colonizing the litter layer. Apply loosely on dry paper and let the pigments separate in their own time. The granulation is the texture. Don’t overwork it.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built around one idea: light trapped in darkness. Seven of the ten pigments are dark — Perylene Green, Hooker’s Green, Burnt Umber, Indanthrone Blue, Transparent Red Oxide, Payne’s Gray, and Alizarin Crimson all live in the lower registers. That’s intentional. Rhododendron shade is mostly shade. The three light-carriers — Opera Pink, Quinacridone Rose, and Hansa Yellow Medium — detonate precisely because they’re outnumbered. Opera Pink is fugitive and fluorescent, which mirrors the actual optical strangeness of rhododendron petals catching stray photons in deep forest. Quinacridone Rose is its permanent anchor. Hansa Yellow Medium is the single warm note of actual sunlight, used sparingly because in this forest, sunlight is rare. Indanthrone Blue and Perylene Green together create darkness with depth instead of mud. Payne’s Gray structures the shadows without competing with the color darks. The whole palette asks one question: how bright can pink burn when everything around it is nearly black?

Painting Prompts

Four subjects built for this palette’s drama of light against dark. Every prompt leans into the contrast — hot pink detonating in near-black forest.

Prompt 1

Rhody Cluster in Shade

Subject: A full Pacific rhododendron flower cluster glowing magenta in deep understory shadow. Waxy leaves frame it. The light is almost gone, but the bloom doesn’t care.

The Neon Understory mix (Opera Pink + Quinacridone Rose + Hansa Yellow Medium) is the bloom itself. Rhody Midnight (Indanthrone Blue + Perylene Green + Alizarin Crimson) is everything around it. The painting lives or dies on the contrast between them.

  • Paint the Rhody Midnight dark background first and let it dry completely — Perylene Green deepest at center, Indanthrone Blue at edges
  • Apply Neon Understory mix for the flower cluster on the dried dark ground; the fluorescence of Opera Pink will physically glow against the dark
  • Use pure Quinacridone Rose at full concentration for the petal folds and centers where color intensifies in shadow
  • Hooker’s Green for the waxy leaves, applied around the bloom to frame it — they’re the intermediary between the magenta blast and the Perylene dark
Prompt 2

Single Bloom Catching a Light Shaft

Subject: One rhododendron flower isolated by a single shaft of light breaking through the canopy. Everything else is dark. The bloom is incandescent.

Hansa Yellow Medium is the light shaft itself. Where it hits the flower, the Neon Understory mix goes warm and electric. Where the shaft misses, Rhody Midnight closes in.

  • Mask or preserve a diagonal strip of white paper for the light shaft — this is the structural decision that makes the painting work
  • Wash dilute Hansa Yellow Medium through the shaft area to establish the warm light, bleeding slightly at edges into the surrounding dark
  • The bloom where it catches the shaft gets the full Neon Understory treatment — Opera Pink over the Hansa wash creates an almost unbearable warmth
  • Surround everything with Rhody Midnight, letting the Alizarin Crimson component creep closest to the bloom for that violet reflected-light transition
Prompt 3

Rhody Tunnel

Subject: A trail overhung by mature rhododendrons forming a living tunnel — blooms overhead, waxy leaves pressing in from both sides, the path disappearing into green-black darkness ahead.

This is a value painting. Perylene Green and Indanthrone Blue build the tunnel’s depth. Hooker’s Green is the leaf canopy overhead. Bloom clusters in Quinacridone Rose and Opera Pink hang above like lanterns in a cave.

  • Establish the vanishing point with concentrated Perylene Green — the trail disappears into near-black, and that depth is the painting’s engine
  • Build the tunnel walls with Hooker’s Green over dried Perylene Green, layering wet-on-dry for canopy depth
  • Bloom clusters overhead get smaller and less saturated toward the background — atmospheric perspective in magenta, from Opera Pink in the foreground to dilute Quinacridone Rose in the distance
  • The trail surface is Forest Floor Dark mix (Burnt Umber + Transparent Red Oxide + Hooker’s Green) — soft, dark, duff-colored, pulling the eye inward
Prompt 4

Forest Floor Under Rhododendrons

Subject: Looking down instead of up. The ground beneath a rhododendron thicket — fallen petals on dark duff, decomposing leaves, Payne’s Gray shadows of the canopy above.

Forest Floor Dark mix is the ground itself. Fallen petals are small marks of Quinacridone Rose and Alizarin Crimson scattered across the duff. Payne’s Gray handles the canopy shadow pattern cast on the floor.

  • Lay down the Forest Floor Dark mix first — Burnt Umber and Transparent Red Oxide for the duff, letting the pigments granulate naturally on rough paper
  • While damp, drop in Payne’s Gray for the overhead canopy shadow pattern — irregular, organic shapes of leaves blocking whatever light reaches down
  • Once dry, scatter small petal-shaped marks of Quinacridone Rose and dilute Alizarin Crimson across the surface — fallen flowers, still vivid against the brown
  • A few tiny dots of Hansa Yellow Medium where light penetrates the canopy and hits the forest floor — each one a small event in an otherwise dark composition
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Opera Pink (BV10:1) is a fluorescent pigment — it will fade with prolonged UV exposure. Use it for studies and working pieces where the glow matters more than archival permanence. Perylene Green (PBk31) is extremely dark in mass tone; test dilutions before committing. Indanthrone Blue (PB60) is equally dark and staining — together with Perylene Green it creates a two-pigment darkness with more depth than any pre-mixed black. Alizarin Crimson (PR177) is the synthetic, lightfast version — not the fugitive PR83 of student paints. Hansa Yellow Medium (PY97) is semi-transparent and non-staining; it lifts cleanly, which matters when you need to reclaim light in a mostly-dark painting.
