Week 21 — PNW Watercolor Series

Foxglove Column

Foxglove lines clearcuts in purple verticals, deliberate as architecture. Songbirds go quiet on nests. Snowmelt rivers run high and cold enough to ache. Spring turning formal — the wildness organized into spires, the silence organized around eggs. Structure where you expected chaos, and cold water reminding you the mountains haven't finished with winter yet.

Primary Trio

Quinacridone Rose

Foxglove bell exterior, the pink-purple that claims clearcuts; transparent, staining, builds intensity in layers

New Gamboge

Clearcut stumps in sun, the raw gold of exposed wood; transparent, warm, non-granulating

French Ultramarine

Snowmelt river deep channel, blue with weight; transparent, granulating, warm-leaning

Mixes & Ratios

Foxglove Bell

2 Quinacridone Rose : 1 Chinese White : ¼ Transparent Red Oxide

Spotted interior of each hanging bell — pink throat freckled with dark marks that guide bumblebees inward.

Quin. Rose
Chinese White
Red Oxide
The bell exterior is pure Quinacridone Rose. The interior needs Chinese White to create the paler throat, and Transparent Red Oxide dabbed in while still damp for the freckles. Work each bell individually — paint the pink-white base, then touch Transparent Red Oxide with a fine brush for the spots before it dries. The white is structural here, not atmospheric.

Snowmelt Rush

2 French Ultramarine : 1 Cobalt Teal Blue : ½ Indanthrone Blue

River water too cold for skin — glacial blue running fast, carrying silt and snowfield memories.

Fr. Ultramarine
Cobalt Teal
Indanthrone
French Ultramarine granulates into the current's texture. Cobalt Teal Blue adds the turquoise note that says glacial origin — no lowland river looks this color. Indanthrone Blue handles the depth, the pools where the current slows and the blue goes black. Lay the mix in flowing horizontal strokes; let the granulation of French Ultramarine do the work of moving water.

Clearcut Gold

2 New Gamboge : 1 Burnt Sienna : ½ Hooker's Green

Sun-blasted stump field where foxglove colonizes — gold wreckage being slowly claimed by pink spires.

New Gamboge
Burnt Sienna
Hooker's Green
New Gamboge is the fresh-cut wood catching sun — still golden, not yet gray. Burnt Sienna ages it, adding the oxidation of exposed heartwood. Hooker's Green is the moss and foxglove rosette beginning to reclaim the stump. Apply Hooker's Green last, wet-on-dry, to keep the green colonization distinct from the wood beneath. The palette tells a succession story: cut, exposed, reclaimed.

Why These Colors?

This palette pairs construction with destruction. Quinacridone Rose is a colonizer pigment — transparent, staining, it claims the paper permanently, just as foxglove claims clearcuts. French Ultramarine's granulation gives the snowmelt river geological texture. Cobalt Teal Blue is the only pigment that accurately captures glacial meltwater's specific turquoise. Chinese White provides the literal whiteness of foxglove throats — opacity with purpose. Indanthrone Blue and Moonglow handle depth and distance respectively, both granulating in different registers.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Foxglove Spires in the Clearcut

Subject: Three or four foxglove stalks rising from a stump field, pink vertical columns against golden wreckage and a Moonglow sky.

Quinacridone Rose builds intensity in layers for the bell exteriors. The Clearcut Gold mix (New Gamboge + Burnt Sienna + Hooker’s Green) handles the stump field. The vertical forms practically paint themselves.

  • Paint the stumps and exposed earth first with the Clearcut Gold mix, varying the ratio — more New Gamboge for sun-blasted tops, more Burnt Sienna for oxidized sides
  • Let it dry, then paint each foxglove stalk as a vertical column of Quinacridone Rose bells from bottom to top, largest at the base, diminishing upward
  • Each bell is two strokes: Quinacridone Rose for the exterior, then the Foxglove Bell mix (with Chinese White) touched into the opening while still damp
  • Dab Transparent Red Oxide into the pale throat with a fine rigger for the freckle spots — work fast, before the Chinese White dries
Prompt 2

Snowmelt River With Glacial Color

Subject: A river running high and fast with snowmelt, turquoise water churning over boulders, dark pools where the current pauses.

The Snowmelt Rush mix (French Ultramarine + Cobalt Teal Blue + Indanthrone Blue) gives you three registers of cold water. Cobalt Teal Blue’s opacity is the glacial turquoise no other pigment can fake.

  • Paint the fast water with French Ultramarine in horizontal flowing strokes on dry paper — let the granulation provide the texture of current
  • While still damp, touch Cobalt Teal Blue into the areas where the turquoise glacial color shows strongest — shallow riffles and where light hits the surface
  • Deep pools are Indanthrone Blue at full concentration, applied after the surrounding water dries so the edges stay hard and the pools read as depth
  • Boulders are Burnt Sienna with Moonglow shadow sides, reserved as dry paper shapes in the initial water wash
Prompt 3

Foxglove Bell Close-Up

Subject: A single foxglove stalk at close range — hanging bells big enough to see inside, freckles visible, a bumblebee entering one.

The Foxglove Bell mix was designed for this intimate scale. Quinacridone Rose exterior, Chinese White throat, Transparent Red Oxide freckles — every pigment has a specific job at this zoom level.

  • Work each bell as its own small painting: wet the bell shape, lay in Quinacridone Rose at the top edge, Chinese White at the throat, and let them meet in the middle
  • Before the throat dries, touch Transparent Red Oxide spots in with a size 0 or 1 round — these are the bee guides, and they should blur just slightly
  • The bumblebee is a small dark shape (Indanthrone Blue) half-inside a bell, with a stripe of New Gamboge — suggestion, not illustration
  • Hooker’s Green leaf rosettes at the base of the stalk anchor the composition and give scale to the bells above
Prompt 4

Clearcut Succession (The Stump Field Story)

Subject: A wide view of a clearcut being reclaimed — stumps, exposed soil, foxglove colonizing, young conifers starting, fog on the distant ocean.

This is a narrative painting. New Gamboge and Burnt Sienna for the raw earth, Hooker’s Green for the return of life, Quinacridone Rose foxglove as the pioneer, Moonglow for the distant fog bank.

  • Start with the Moonglow fog bank on the horizon — wet paper, single wash, its granulation and violet shift will handle the distance
  • The stump field is Clearcut Gold mix applied in rough horizontal patches, leaving some white paper for the raw mineral soil
  • Burnt Sienna dragged through wet New Gamboge creates the exposed earth color of disturbed ground
  • Foxglove spires are Quinacridone Rose vertical strokes scattered across the mid-ground, decreasing in size toward the background — they’re the protagonists of the succession story
Prompt 5

River and Foxglove Together

Subject: Foxglove growing on the bank above a snowmelt river — pink spires reflected in turquoise water, the two signature colors of early June in one frame.

Quinacridone Rose and the Snowmelt Rush blues are the complementary tension that makes this palette sing. Put them together and you have the whole season.

  • Paint the river first with the Snowmelt Rush mix, leaving the bank area as dry paper
  • The foxglove on the bank is Quinacridone Rose applied after the river is completely dry — you need the hard edge between pink and blue for maximum vibration
  • Reflections of the foxglove in the water are dilute Quinacridone Rose stroked vertically into a rewet strip of the river area — they should be paler and longer than the actual flowers
  • Hooker’s Green on the bank ties the two zones together — the green that can live next to both pink and blue without choosing sides
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Transparent Red Oxide is an iron oxide pigment — lightfast and permanent, it dries darker than it looks wet. Chinese White lifts easily when rewet, so protect your foxglove throats from subsequent washes. Indanthrone Blue is a slow-drying pigment; give it time before layering over it. Cobalt Teal Blue is strongly opaque — a single stroke over dried blue creates the glacial melt effect.
