Week 21 — PNW Watercolor Series

Foxglove Column

Foxgloves are vertical. Five feet of spotted bells stacking up like a tower — Rose Madder Permanent at the top fading to Imperial Purple at the base. Deep Scarlet spots inside each bell like a warning. They grow in clearings where the darkness presses in on all sides. The whole composition is vertical, and you paint it that way or not at all.

Primary Trio

Rose Madder Permanent

Foxglove pink, the upper bells catching light; NR9 pigment, semi-transparent, granulates beautifully like petal texture

Imperial Purple

Deep foxglove purple, the lower bells in shadow; rich, dark, first appearance in the series — earned it

Hooker’s Green

Forest backdrop pressing in from every side; dark, cool, the green wall that makes the pink spires read

Mixes & Ratios

Bell Gradient

2 Rose Madder Permanent : 1 Imperial Purple : ¼ French Ultramarine

The color that runs down a foxglove spire — pink at the top darkening to purple at the base, each bell a step deeper than the one above it.

Rose Madder
Imperial Purple
Fr. Ultra.
This is a gradient, not a flat mix. Start with pure Rose Madder Permanent at the top of the spire and add Imperial Purple incrementally as you paint each bell moving downward. The French Ultramarine touch deepens the shadow fold between bells — its granulation mimics the texture where one bell overlaps the next. Rose Madder Permanent's own granulation (NR9) is the key: it settles into the paper grain like petal veination. Don't overmix on the palette. Let the pigments meet on the paper.

Spotted Throat

1 Buff Titanium : ½ Deep Scarlet : ¼ Rose Madder Permanent

The inside of a foxglove bell — pale cream speckled with dark crimson spots that tell bumblebees where to land.

Buff Titanium
Deep Scarlet
Rose Madder
Two-stage application. First, lay down Buff Titanium with a whisper of Rose Madder Permanent for the pale pink-cream throat. While it's still damp, touch Deep Scarlet spots with a size 0 round — they should bleed just slightly at the edges, which is accurate. Real foxglove spots are not hard-edged; they feather into the surrounding tissue. The spots are arranged in rows pointing inward. Deep Scarlet is genuinely dark enough to read as warning markings against the Buff Titanium ground.

Forest Press

2 Perylene Green : 1 Hooker’s Green : ½ Burnt Sienna

The dark forest wall that surrounds every foxglove clearing — green so deep it's almost black, pressing inward.

Perylene Green
Hooker’s Green
Burnt Sienna
Perylene Green is near-black and cool — the deep forest where light doesn't reach. Hooker's Green brings it into the mid-value range so the wall has dimension, not just darkness. Burnt Sienna warms the lower portion where trunks and duff enter the mix. Apply the forest backdrop first and let it dry completely before painting any foxglove spires against it. The pink-on-dark-green contrast is the entire composition. Don't soften the forest; the foxgloves need something to push against.

Why These Colors?

Foxgloves demand a vertical palette — colors that grade from light to dark the way the spire grades from bud to spent bell. Rose Madder Permanent at the top granulates like petal texture, its NR9 pigment settling into paper grain the way foxglove pink settles into a clearing. Imperial Purple handles the dark lower bells where no other purple goes deep enough without going dead. Deep Scarlet is the throat-spot pigment, dark and warm enough to read as warning against Buff Titanium's cream. Three greens — Sap Green for stems, Hooker's Green for the mid-forest, Perylene Green for the blackest depths — build the wall of forest that makes every foxglove spire look like a lit match in a dark room. French Ultramarine granulates into the shadow folds between bells. Quinacridone Rose is the hotter pink for bells in direct sun, brighter than Rose Madder and staining where Rose Madder lifts. Burnt Sienna grounds the whole scene in forest-floor duff.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Foxglove Spire, Full Length

Subject: A single foxglove stalk from rosette to tip — the full vertical, three feet of bells grading from Imperial Purple at the base to Rose Madder Permanent at the top, tight buds still closed at the apex.

The Bell Gradient mix was built for exactly this. One spire, one gradient, the whole pink-to-purple story told in a vertical stroke. Buff Titanium buds at the tip and Sap Green stem tying it to the earth.

  • Paint the Sap Green stem first as a single confident vertical stroke — foxglove stems are straight and structural, not wispy
  • Starting at the bottom, paint each bell with the Bell Gradient mix, using more Imperial Purple for the lowest bells and shifting to pure Rose Madder Permanent as you move upward
  • The uppermost buds are Buff Titanium with a touch of Rose Madder — they haven’t opened yet, so they’re paler and tighter
  • Touch French Ultramarine into the shadow fold where each bell meets the stem — its granulation reads as the crumpled texture of the bell’s attachment point
Prompt 2

Single Bell Close-Up With Spots

Subject: One foxglove bell at intimate scale — looking up into the hanging tube, seeing the spotted throat, the hairy interior, the way light comes through the pink walls.

The Spotted Throat mix (Buff Titanium + Deep Scarlet + Rose Madder Permanent) handles the interior. The bell’s exterior wall is Quinacridone Rose where the sun hits, Rose Madder Permanent in shadow.

  • Paint the bell exterior first with Quinacridone Rose on the sunlit side grading to Rose Madder Permanent on the shadow side — the difference between staining and granulating pink is visible at this scale
  • The interior throat is Buff Titanium applied while the bell is still slightly damp at the opening, creating a soft edge where inside meets outside
  • Deep Scarlet spots are the critical detail — touch them in with a fine round while the Buff Titanium is still wet, letting them bleed just slightly at the edges
  • French Ultramarine in a thin line at the bell’s rim creates the shadow of the curled-back lip, granulating into the fold
Prompt 3

Clearing With Multiple Spires

Subject: A forest clearing filled with foxglove spires — five or six stalks at varying heights, the Forest Press dark green wall behind them, late May light angling in from above.

The Forest Press mix (Perylene Green + Hooker’s Green + Burnt Sienna) goes down first as the dark backdrop. Then the spires rise against it — pink on near-black-green, the contrast that defines foxglove season.

  • Paint the entire forest backdrop first with the Forest Press mix — darker at the edges (more Perylene Green), lighter in the center where the clearing opens up (more Hooker’s Green)
  • Let it dry completely. The foxglove spires are painted on dry forest, and the hard edges between pink and green are essential to the composition
  • Vary the spire heights and bell-gradient ratios — the tallest stalk gets the full Rose Madder to Imperial Purple range; shorter ones in back may be mostly Imperial Purple in the dimmer light
  • Burnt Sienna forest floor between the stalks, with Sap Green foxglove leaf rosettes at each base, anchors the spires to the ground
Prompt 4

Foxglove Against Fern Backdrop

Subject: A single foxglove spire rising through sword ferns — the vertical pink column against the horizontal green frond layers, two geometries competing.

Sap Green fern fronds against Hooker’s Green deeper shade, with Rose Madder Permanent and Quinacridone Rose foxglove bisecting them vertically. The geometry tells the story — horizontal green, vertical pink.

  • Paint the fern layer as overlapping horizontal arcs of Sap Green, lighter fronds in front and Hooker’s Green fronds receding behind — work from back to front
  • The foxglove spire cuts vertically through the fern mass — paint it after the ferns dry, using the Bell Gradient mix from Imperial Purple at the fern line to Rose Madder Permanent above
  • Where the foxglove stem passes behind fern fronds, leave gaps — the interruption of the vertical by horizontal fronds creates depth without any tricks
  • Perylene Green in the darkest background behind both fern and foxglove pushes everything forward and makes the Buff Titanium upper buds glow
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Imperial Purple makes its series debut here — a deep, rich purple that stays chromatic even at full concentration. Rose Madder Permanent (NR9) granulates distinctively; the pigment particles settle into paper grain like petal texture, so choose a rough or cold-press surface to maximize the effect. Deep Scarlet dries darker than it looks wet, which is perfect for the warning spots inside each bell. Perylene Green is close to black at full strength — thin it slightly so the green still reads. Buff Titanium is semi-opaque; it can sit on top of a dried dark wash for the lit upper buds.
