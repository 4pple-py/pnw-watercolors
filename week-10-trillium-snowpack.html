Trillium Snowpack

Trillium carpets a second-growth floor near Deception Pass, 9 AM, cool and shadowless. Red-breasted sapsuckers drill overhead. Peak snowpack gleams on Baker. Maximum white — flowers at sea level, snow at treeline — while the forest between is all dark vertical.

Mixes & Ratios

Trillium Floor

2 Chinese White : 1 Sap Green : trace Neutral Tint

White petals scattered across green — the forest floor as a field of small white flags, each one three-petaled and deliberate.

Chinese White
Sap Green
NT
Chinese White is opaque — that's the point. Trillium petals sit on top of the forest floor, not inside it. Paint the green leaf-and-shadow layer first with Sap Green and Neutral Tint, let it dry completely, then apply the petals in Chinese White with a small brush. Three petals per flower, each a single stroke from edge to center. The carpet effect comes from repetition: dozens of small three-stroke flowers, not one carefully rendered specimen.

Sapsucker Bark

2 Burnt Sienna : 1 Neutral Tint

Drilled trunk, sap-stained — the neat rows of wells that a sapsucker maintains like an orchard no one else can see.

Burnt Sienna
Neutral Tint
Burnt Sienna granulates; Neutral Tint stays smooth. The contrast gives bark its dual texture — rough surface, smooth fissures. Apply the mix to dry paper for the trunk, using vertical dry-brush strokes. For the sapsucker wells, wait until the bark wash is bone dry, then paint each well as a tiny dark dot — pure Neutral Tint, evenly spaced in horizontal rows. The bird itself is small — save the Pyrrol Scarlet for the red head patch, one precise mark.

Peak Snowfield

1 French Ultramarine : 3 Chinese White

Baker snowpack catching morning — the mountain wearing its maximum accumulation, white so bright it has to be painted as blue.

Fr. Ultramarine
Chinese White
Snow in painting is never white — it's blue-white, and the ratio matters. Three parts Chinese White to one part French Ultramarine gives you the pale, granulating blue of snowpack in morning sun. The Ultramarine's granulation provides the surface texture of wind-scoured snow. Apply in broad strokes for the snowfield, heavier on the Ultramarine for shadowed crevasses, nearly pure white for the ridgeline catching direct sun. For the glacier below, increase the Ultramarine and add a touch of Cobalt Blue — glacial ice is a different blue than snow.

Why These Colors?

This palette is organized around a value problem: how to paint two kinds of white (flowers, snow) separated by a wall of dark green. Perylene Green's staining depth provides that wall — it goes dark enough to create the contrast both whites need. French Ultramarine and Cobalt Blue split the snow duties: Ultramarine for the granulating, warm snowpack, Cobalt for the cooler, cleaner glacial ice. Chinese White is the structural pigment, opaque by nature, used to build the trillium petals that sit on top of the transparent green forest floor. Neutral Tint and Lunar Black handle the shadows — Neutral Tint for the forest floor (transparent, layerable), Lunar Black for the oldest stumps (granulating, textured). Pyrrol Scarlet and Burnt Sienna are the only warm notes, and both belong to the sapsucker — the red head patch and the bark it drills. The palette keeps warmth scarce because the scene is cool and the light is still morning-angled.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Trillium Carpet from Above

Subject: A downward-looking view of the forest floor — dozens of white trillium scattered across moss and leaf litter, with dark trunk bases cutting vertical lines through the scene.

This is what the Trillium Floor mix (Chinese White + Sap Green + trace Neutral Tint) was born for. The whole painting is basically two layers: a green-and-shadow wash for the forest floor, then opaque white petals stamped on top.

  • Paint the entire ground plane first — wet-into-wet Sap Green and Neutral Tint, letting them mingle. Let it dry completely.
  • Add trillium with a small round brush: three strokes per flower, edge to center. Don’t render — repeat. Twenty loose flowers beats one fussy one.
  • Use Perylene Green for the darkest shadow pockets between roots. Its staining power gives you real depth under the white.
  • Leave some flowers as pure Chinese White, shadow others with a Neutral Tint glaze. The value variation makes the carpet feel three-dimensional.
Prompt 2

Mt. Baker from the Lowlands

Subject: Baker’s snowpack seen across a foreground of dark conifer canopy — the classic PNW composition of white peak floating above a green-black tree wall.

The three blues each have a job: French Ultramarine for the mountain’s bulk, Cobalt Blue for the distant snowfield catching sun, Cerulean Blue for the thin strip of sky. Perylene Green handles the entire tree wall in one dark wash.

  • Work back to front. Lay in the sky (Cerulean Blue, very dilute) and the mountain (French Ultramarine + Chinese White) first.
  • The snowpack isn’t flat white — use the Peak Snowfield mix and vary the Ultramarine ratio. More blue in the crevasses, nearly pure white on the ridgeline.
  • Paint the conifer wall as one continuous Perylene Green wash with vertical dry-brush texture at the top edge for individual tree crowns. Don’t try to paint every tree.
  • The drama is in the value contrast: the tree wall should be the darkest thing in the painting, making both the snow and sky glow by comparison.
Prompt 3

Red-Breasted Sapsucker on a Drilled Trunk

Subject: A close-up vignette — a single trunk with neat horizontal rows of sapsucker wells, sap stains running down, and the small bird clinging to the bark with its red head blazing.

The Sapsucker Bark mix (Burnt Sienna + Neutral Tint) gives you the trunk. Pyrrol Scarlet — which barely appears anywhere else in this cool palette — gets its starring moment as the bird’s head patch.

  • Use dry paper and dry-brush vertical strokes for bark texture. Let the Burnt Sienna granulate against the smooth Neutral Tint.
  • The sapsucker wells are tiny dots of pure Neutral Tint in precise horizontal rows. Four to six across, evenly spaced. This geometric pattern is the composition — make it deliberate.
  • For sap stains, load a wet Burnt Sienna and let it bleed downward from the wells. Gravity does the work.
  • The bird is small — resist the urge to overwork it. A Neutral Tint body, a single confident stroke of Pyrrol Scarlet for the head. That red will pop against all the cool tones like a lit match.
Prompt 4

Deception Pass Through the Canopy

Subject: A forest scene looking outward — dark trunks framing a gap where you can see water, distant headlands, and a slice of sky. Trillium dots the foreground floor.

This uses nearly every color on the palette in its natural role. Perylene Green and Lunar Black for the dark frame of trees, Cerulean Blue for the sky gap, Cobalt Blue for the distant water, and the trillium mix for the foreground flowers.

  • Compose around the gap — the light area should be off-center and irregularly shaped, framed by dark trunks. The eye goes to the brightest spot, so make it interesting.
  • Paint the distant view through the gap first (sky, water, headland), then frame it with the dark tree trunks. Perylene Green stains, so plan your gaps before you commit.
  • Lunar Black on the old-growth stumps gives you a different dark than Neutral Tint — granulating and textured vs. smooth and transparent. Use both.
  • Scatter trillium in the foreground for depth. The white flowers in the near ground echo the light in the far gap, pulling the viewer’s eye through the painting.
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Chinese White is the only truly opaque pigment on the palette — use it structurally for trillium petals and snowfield highlights, not as a mixing white. Temperature runs definitively cool; Burnt Sienna and Pyrrol Scarlet are the only warms, and they occupy maybe five percent of the painting surface. Three blues (French Ultramarine, Cerulean, Cobalt) each serve a different atmospheric distance — Ultramarine for the mountain, Cerulean for the sky gap, Cobalt for the far snowfield. Don't interchange them. Perylene Green stains permanently; plan your trunk spaces before committing to the canopy wash.
