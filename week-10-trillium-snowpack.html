Week 10 — PNW Watercolor Series

Trillium Snowpack

Snow still lingers in north-facing hollows but the trillium don’t care. Three white petals against dark duff, clean as paper on a desk. Meltwater threads between roots. The forest is hushed — Perylene Green darkness overhead, cold blue trickling through, and these impossible white flowers catching whatever light filters down.

Mixes & Ratios

Petal Shadow

3 Chinese White : 1 Lavender : touch Cobalt Blue

The shadowed underside of a trillium petal — white that remembers it’s standing in a forest, not a studio.

Chinese White
Lavender
CB
Chinese White (PW4) is semi-opaque zinc white — it sits on a dark wash without fully obliterating it, which is exactly how trillium petals behave in dim forest light. Lavender’s cool purple-gray shifts the white toward the violet end, the way shadows on white flowers actually read in nature. The touch of Cobalt Blue adds granulation to the shadow, giving it a mineral texture rather than a flat tint. Paint the lit side of each petal in pure Chinese White, then drop this mix into the shadow side while still damp. The semi-opacity means the dark forest floor ghosts through just enough to anchor the petal to its environment.

Forest Floor Dark

2 Hooker’s Green : 1 Transparent Brown Oxide : touch Perylene Green

Wet duff and decomposing needles — the dark ground that makes every trillium look like it’s carrying its own spotlight.

Hooker’s Green
Tr. Brown Oxide
PG
Hooker’s Green (PG36+PY150) brings the blue-green darkness of conifer shade. Transparent Brown Oxide (PR101) warms it toward the actual color of decomposing organic matter — that rich, reddish-brown undertone you see in old-growth duff when you kick it apart. Both pigments are transparent, so the mix stays luminous even at full concentration. Perylene Green (PBk31) in the deepest pockets pushes the value down to near-black without going opaque. Lay this mix wet-into-wet across the forest floor, varying the ratio — more brown where leaves dominate, more green where moss takes over. The trillium petals, applied later in opaque Chinese White, will pop against this dark ground like lanterns.

Meltwater Thread

2 Cobalt Blue : 1 Buff Titanium : touch Lavender

Last snowmelt trickling between roots — water so cold it looks blue even in the shade.

Cobalt Blue
Buff Titanium
Lav
Cobalt Blue (PB28) granulates beautifully — on rough paper, the pigment settles into the valleys and leaves the peaks bare, which mimics how light scatters across shallow running water. Buff Titanium (PW6:1) adds a slight milky warmth, the way snowmelt carries fine sediment that catches the light. Lavender cools the edges and gives the water a sense of atmospheric depth where it disappears into shadow. Apply in thin, sinuous lines with a rigger brush, following the contour of roots and stones. Let the granulation do the work — don’t overbrush. Where the stream widens into a small pool, dilute heavily and let the Cobalt granules settle on their own.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built around contrast: opaque white against transparent dark. Chinese White is the structural pigment — semi-opaque zinc white that sits on top of dark washes the way trillium petals sit on top of the forest floor. It’s not a mixing white; it’s a painting white. The three greens each serve a different depth: Sap Green for the living moss and leaf layer, Hooker’s Green for the deeper shade between trunks, and Perylene Green for the near-black of the farthest canopy shadow. The two earth tones split the forest floor — Raw Umber for dry duff and bark, Transparent Brown Oxide for the richer, wetter layer of decomposing leaves underneath. Cobalt Blue carries the cold water and the clean light; its granulation reads as both. Lavender handles atmospheric distance — that cool purple-gray haze you see looking through trees on a still morning. Buff Titanium tints the lingering snow patches with just enough warmth to separate them from the pure white of the flowers. Hansa Yellow Light is the one bright accent, used sparingly for the rare shaft of spring sunlight that reaches the floor. The palette runs cool and quiet because the scene is cool and quiet.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are built for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the early-March moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Trillium Close-Up

Subject: A single white trillium seen from slightly above — three petals, three sepals, the dark rosette of leaves beneath, forest floor falling away into shadow on all sides.

This is what the Petal Shadow mix was made for. Chinese White builds the lit petals, the Lavender-tinted mix handles the shadow side, and the Forest Floor Dark mix creates the ground that makes the whole thing glow.

  • Paint the forest floor first — Forest Floor Dark mix applied wet-into-wet, letting Hooker’s Green and Transparent Brown Oxide mingle freely
  • Let the ground dry completely before touching Chinese White to the petals. The opacity depends on the underlayer being set.
  • Each petal is a single confident stroke from tip to center. Three strokes, three petals. Don’t go back in.
  • Drop the Petal Shadow mix into the base of each petal while the white is still damp — it should bloom softly upward, not cover the petal
Prompt 2

Snowmelt Stream Through Roots

Subject: A thin stream of meltwater winding between exposed tree roots and mossy stones, with a last wedge of grainy snow in a north-facing hollow. No flowers — just water, wood, and cold.

The Meltwater Thread mix carries the stream. Cobalt Blue’s granulation does the surface texture. Buff Titanium and Chinese White share the snow patch, while Raw Umber and Perylene Green build the dark banks.

  • Map the stream’s path with masking fluid or careful negative painting before laying the dark bank washes
  • Use a rigger brush for the stream — thin, sinuous, following the terrain. Vary pressure for width changes around roots.
  • The snow patch is Buff Titanium with Cobalt Blue shadows — not pure white. Save pure white paper for the brightest water highlights.
  • Raw Umber dry-brushed over the root surfaces gives bark texture. Perylene Green in the crevices between roots for maximum depth.
Prompt 3

Forest Floor Still Life

Subject: A square foot of forest floor as a still life — decaying leaves, a patch of moss, one fallen twig, a scatter of fir needles, and a single trillium emerging from the center. Everything at actual scale.

Transparent Brown Oxide and Raw Umber split the leaf litter duties — brown oxide for wet, recently fallen leaves, raw umber for the dry, crumbling ones underneath. Sap Green for the moss. One white flower anchors the composition.

  • Lay individual leaves with a flat brush — one stroke per leaf, Transparent Brown Oxide wet, Raw Umber dry. Overlap them.
  • Moss is Sap Green with touches of Hansa Yellow Light where it catches indirect sun. Apply to damp paper for soft edges.
  • The fir needles are Perylene Green, painted with a fine liner after everything else dries — dark, precise, scattered naturally
  • The single trillium goes on last. Chinese White, three strokes. It should feel like the painting’s only point of light.
Prompt 4

Trail Through Old Growth with Snow Patches

Subject: A narrow trail disappearing into old-growth forest, patchy snow on either side, trillium scattered along the edges, the canopy closing overhead into Perylene Green darkness.

This uses almost every color at its natural scale. Perylene Green and Hooker’s Green build the canopy. Raw Umber and Transparent Brown Oxide handle the trail. Lavender pushes the deep forest into atmospheric haze. Buff Titanium and Cobalt Blue tint the snow. Trillium dots the margins in Chinese White.

  • Establish the trail as negative space — paint the dark forest on either side and let the trail emerge as lighter ground between
  • The canopy overhead should grade from Hooker’s Green at the edges to pure Perylene Green at the top. Let the darks close in.
  • Lavender glazed over the far trees creates atmospheric depth — the forest recedes into cool purple-gray where the trail bends out of sight
  • Snow patches are Buff Titanium base with Cobalt Blue shadow. Trillium along the trail edges should be small, loose, and scattered — three-stroke flowers, not botanical illustrations
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. Chinese White (PW4) is zinc white, semi-opaque — it won’t fully cover a dark wash, which is the point. Trillium petals in forest shade aren’t opaque white; they glow with whatever’s behind them. Perylene Green (PBk31) is technically a black pigment that reads as an extremely dark blue-green — it’s one of the darkest non-black pigments available and it stains permanently. Plan your lights before committing. Cobalt Blue (PB28) granulates heavily on rough paper; use this to your advantage for water textures. Lavender is a convenience mix from Daniel Smith with a cool purple-gray character — it creates atmospheric distance without the weight of a true violet. Temperature runs decisively cool; the only warmth comes from Hansa Yellow Light, Transparent Brown Oxide, and Raw Umber, and all three belong to the forest floor.
