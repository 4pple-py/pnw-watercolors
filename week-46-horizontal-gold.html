Week 46 — PNW Watercolor Series

Horizontal Gold

Under 8.5 hours of daylight and the sun, when it appears, throws flat gold across the water at noon like an apology. Harbor seals haul out on log booms, silver-brown lumps barely distinguishable from bark. Light so rare it becomes architectural — every beam has edges, every shadow has weight.

Primary Trio

Quinacridone Burnt Orange

Noon sun hitting water sideways; highly staining and transparent, it glazes beautifully over gray washes

Payne’s Gray

Log boom, seal, and shadow merged; the week is fundamentally gray and this pigment anchors everything

Cobalt Blue

Sky for forty minutes at midday; granulating and honest, a brief window of blue in an otherwise gray week

Mixes & Ratios

Seal Haul

2 Payne’s Gray : 1 Raw Sienna

Harbor seal indistinguishable from log — mottled pelt and weathered wood sharing a color.

Payne's Gray
Raw Sienna
Raw Sienna warms Payne’s Gray into mottled pelt tone. Apply on damp paper so pigments partially separate — mottling is the point. Seal skin and weathered wood share a color.

Noon Stripe

1 QBO : 1 Hansa Yellow Deep : 1 Chinese White

Horizontal gold on flat water — one stroke across the composition, narrow and confident.

QBO
Hansa Yellow Deep
Chinese White
Three-part mix produces opaque warm gold. Apply in single horizontal stroke across the water, narrow and confident. Chinese White gives it body. One stroke. Do not go back.

Boom Shadow

2 Indigo : 1 Burnt Umber

Creosote and deep water merged — a warm near-black that reads as depth.

Indigo
Burnt Umber
Indigo stains deep; Burnt Umber granulates into it like sediment. Warm near-black that reads as depth. Apply under boom line, hard top edge, lost bottom edge.

Why These Colors?

This palette is organized around scarcity. Only two warm colors because light is rationed in late November. Payne’s Gray anchors everything because this week is fundamentally gray. Quinacridone Burnt Orange is a staining transparent that glazes beautifully over gray washes. Chinese White is essential for gull highlights and the noon stripe.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Harbor Seals on Log Boom

Subject: Seals indistinguishable from weathered logs — gray everything, silver-brown lumps on silver-brown wood.

The Seal Haul mix (Payne’s Gray 2:Raw Sienna 1) was built for this exact ambiguity — seal and log sharing a color on damp paper.

  • Paint water first as flat Payne’s Gray wash
  • Apply Seal Haul mix to entire boom-and-seal shape on damp paper
  • Darken underside with Boom Shadow mix
  • Distinguish seal from log only through shape
Prompt 2

Noon Stripe Across the Harbor

Subject: Wide harbor at winter solstice noon, golden road of reflection — flat gold thrown across the water like an apology.

The Noon Stripe mix (QBO 1:Hansa Yellow Deep 1:Chinese White 1) produces opaque warm gold. One stroke. Do not go back.

  • Paint entire scene in gray first
  • Mix Noon Stripe and apply in SINGLE horizontal stroke
  • Add Hansa Yellow Deep to wave crests while damp
  • Cobalt Blue in narrow sky patch
Prompt 3

Creosote Pilings at Low Tide

Subject: Old harbor pilings, barnacle-crusted, gull on top — tide-stained wood and crusty marine life.

Burnt Umber handles the creosote. Yellow Ochre is opaque, granulating, perfect for barnacle crust. Boom Shadow mix for the deep water at the base.

  • Burnt Umber pilings progressively lighter as they recede
  • Yellow Ochre horizontal band for barnacle zone
  • Water at base is Boom Shadow mix
  • Glaze Lavender over farthest pilings for fog
Prompt 4

Backlit Cloud Edge at Midday

Subject: Pure sky study — gray cloud bank with one backlit edge, the only drama in a monochrome day.

Lavender granulates along the lit cloud edge. Quinacridone Burnt Orange glazes thin along the brightest part. Cobalt Blue fills the sky gap with hard edges.

  • Wet paper, Payne’s Gray for cloud mass
  • Apply Lavender along lit cloud edge
  • Let dry, glaze thin Quinacridone Burnt Orange along brightest part
  • Paint sky gap with Cobalt Blue, hard edges
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. Quinacridone Burnt Orange is highly staining and transparent. Lavender granulates into soft violets. Yellow Ochre is opaque, granulating, perfect for barnacle crust.
