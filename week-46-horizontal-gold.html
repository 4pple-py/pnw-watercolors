Week 46 — PNW Watercolor Series

Horizontal Gold

In November the sun never climbs. It slides along the horizon like a coin rolling on its edge, throwing light sideways through everything. Trees cast shadows longer than streets. Every surface facing south turns gold. The light is horizontal and it turns the ordinary into the extraordinary — a fence post, a wet road, a dog’s ear — all briefly gilded by a sun that barely showed up and is already leaving.

Primary Trio

Quinacridone Gold

The horizontal light itself — warm, transparent, the color that turns ordinary surfaces into gilded ones; the defining pigment of November’s low sun

New Gamboge

Bright slant sun at its most intense — where the light hits directly and the gold becomes almost white-hot, pure warm yellow without any earth

French Ultramarine

The long shadows — blue-violet and granulating, stretching across the ground in the opposite direction of all that gold; the cold counterweight

Mixes & Ratios

Slant Gold

2 Quinacridone Gold : 1 New Gamboge : ½ Aussie Red Gold

November sun hitting a south-facing wall at three in the afternoon — pure horizontal gold, warm enough to feel on your skin through the painting.

Quin. Gold
New Gamboge
Aussie Red Gold
Quinacridone Gold is the structural base — transparent, warm, it glazes like liquid amber and lets the paper glow through. New Gamboge intensifies the yellow toward that white-hot center where the sun actually hits, pushing the gold from amber toward pure light. Aussie Red Gold is the key to November specifically: it adds a red-gold quality that separates low-angle November sun from the cleaner yellows of summer. At 15 degrees above the horizon, sunlight passes through more atmosphere and picks up this red shift — Aussie Red Gold captures it exactly. Apply as a graded glaze from full strength where the sun hits to dilute where the light rakes across the surface. The transparency of all three pigments means you can layer this mix over dried washes to build the light up gradually.

Long Shadow

2 French Ultramarine : 1 Payne’s Gray : ¼ Burnt Sienna

The shadows that stretch longer than the objects casting them — blue-violet and cold, the inverse of everything the gold is doing.

French Ultramarine
Payne's Gray
Burnt Sienna
French Ultramarine makes the shadows blue, which is what outdoor shadows actually are — they’re lit by the blue sky, not the golden sun. Its granulation adds texture to flat ground shadows without any brushwork. Payne’s Gray darkens and grays the blue toward the realistic value of a November shadow, which is deeper and flatter than summer shadows because the sun is weaker. Burnt Sienna at a whisper keeps the shadow from going dead cold — even the coldest shadow has a touch of warmth where it meets the sunlit ground, and this tiny amount of Burnt Sienna creates that warm edge. Paint shadows with the board tilted slightly toward you, pulling the mix downward to create the elongated, stretched quality of low-sun shadows. They should be at least three times longer than the object casting them.

Sunset Ember

2 Aussie Red Gold : 1 Alizarin Crimson : ½ Quinacridone Gold

The last five minutes before the sun drops below the horizon — gold going red, the sky on fire at the edges, November’s brief daily fever.

Aussie Red Gold
Alizarin Crimson
Quin. Gold
Aussie Red Gold is already positioned between gold and red — it’s the hinge point of the sunset, the moment the light shifts from golden to ember. Alizarin Crimson pushes the red deeper without going opaque or heavy; its transparency means the sky still glows rather than going solid. Quinacridone Gold at the edges transitions the ember back into the broader golden wash of the sky, creating a seamless gradient from gold through red-gold to crimson. Apply this mix to the sky in a single pass on damp paper, working fast — sunset colors lose their fire if you overwork them. Grade from Quinacridone Gold at the top through the full mix at the horizon line, and let the Alizarin Crimson concentrate in a narrow band right where the sun touches the land.

Why These Colors?

Four golds is not excessive when the subject is horizontal light. Quinacridone Gold, New Gamboge, Hansa Yellow Deep, and Raw Sienna form a gradient from cool gold to warm gold to bright gold to earth gold — the full spectrum of what November sun does to surfaces. Each reads differently: Quinacridone Gold is the transparent glaze of ambient golden light, New Gamboge is the direct hit where the sun strikes, Hansa Yellow Deep is the intense narrow-angle beam, and Raw Sienna is the gold already absorbed into the earth. Aussie Red Gold captures the specific red-shifted quality of sun at 15 degrees above the horizon — light that has traveled through so much atmosphere it picks up a warmth that summer sun never has. French Ultramarine and Payne’s Gray handle the long, long shadows — the necessary cold counterweight that makes the gold read as gold. Without blue shadows, the gold has nothing to push against. Alizarin Crimson warms the sunset edge. Cobalt Blue overhead keeps the zenith cold and honest — November sky directly above is still blue, even when the horizon is on fire. Burnt Sienna anchors the warm darks where shadow meets sunlit earth.

Painting Prompts

Every prompt here is about the angle of light — the way November sun enters a scene sideways and gilds everything it touches. Chase the gold.

Prompt 1

Fence Line at Golden Hour

Subject: A wooden fence running diagonally across a field, each post catching the low sun on its south face while casting a shadow three times its height to the north. Dry grass. Clear cold air. The fence posts are gilded on one side and blue-shadowed on the other.

Slant Gold (Quinacridone Gold + New Gamboge + Aussie Red Gold) for the sun-struck faces of the posts. Long Shadow mix (French Ultramarine + Payne’s Gray + Burnt Sienna) for the stretched shadows on the ground. Raw Sienna for the dry grass.

  • Paint the shadows first with the Long Shadow mix — establish their length and angle before anything else. They should stretch diagonally across the paper, at least three times the height of each fence post
  • The sun-struck face of each post is Slant Gold at full concentration — a small, bright, saturated rectangle on the south side of each post, the warmest notes in the painting
  • Dry grass is Raw Sienna with flicks of Hansa Yellow Deep where individual blades catch the light — use a rigger brush for these, quick upward strokes, letting some catch the gold and some fall into shadow
  • The shadow side of each post is Payne’s Gray, not black — the contrast between Slant Gold and Payne’s Gray on the same object is what makes the low light read as low
Prompt 2

November Road — Wet Asphalt in Gold Light

Subject: A wet road after rain, late afternoon November sun breaking through clouds and hitting the wet surface at a low angle. The road becomes a mirror — reflecting gold sky, flanked by long blue shadows from roadside trees. Puddles catch the light like scattered coins.

The wet road reflects the Slant Gold mix in its puddles and wet surface. French Ultramarine tree shadows cross the road in parallel bars. Payne’s Gray for the dry asphalt between reflections. Cobalt Blue for the cold sky above the golden horizon.

  • The wet road is the painting — glaze the entire road surface with dilute Quinacridone Gold first, then drop in concentrated New Gamboge for the puddles that catch the most light. Wet asphalt reflects sky color, and in November that sky is gold
  • Tree shadows crossing the road are French Ultramarine bars painted over the dried gold glaze — the blue over gold creates a visual vibration that reads as cold shadow on warm reflected light
  • Puddles need white paper reserved in their centers for the brightest reflection, surrounded by Hansa Yellow Deep for the secondary glow — water reflects more light than road surface
  • Grade the sky from Cobalt Blue at the top through Quinacridone Gold in the middle to the full Sunset Ember mix at the horizon — November sky compresses the entire warm-to-cool transition into a narrow band
Prompt 3

Farmhouse South Wall

Subject: The south-facing wall of a white farmhouse, catching full horizontal light. The wall glows gold. A bare tree casts a shadow pattern across it like a projected drawing. Window glass reflects the low sun as pure fire. The north-facing wall in the background is blue-gray and cold.

New Gamboge and Quinacridone Gold for the sun-struck south wall. Long Shadow mix for the tree shadow pattern on the wall. Alizarin Crimson for the window reflections. Cobalt Blue and Payne’s Gray for the cold north wall in shadow.

  • The south wall is not white — it’s gold. Wash the entire wall with dilute New Gamboge, letting it thin at the edges. A white wall in horizontal light is the color of the light itself
  • The bare tree shadow on the wall is the compositional centerpiece — paint it with the Long Shadow mix using a fine round brush, tracing the branch pattern as a projected silhouette. The shadow is sharper near the tree and softer as it stretches
  • Window glass reflecting low sun is Aussie Red Gold at full strength with a dot of Alizarin Crimson at the center — small, intense, the hottest points in the painting
  • The north wall visible in the background is Cobalt Blue with Payne’s Gray — the temperature contrast between the gold south wall and the blue north wall tells the whole story of November light
Prompt 4

Skagit Flats — Last Light

Subject: The Skagit Valley flats at the last moment before sunset. Completely flat land stretching to the Cascades. The sky is gold and red at the horizon, cold blue above. Every drainage ditch and flooded field reflects the sky like a network of golden veins. Long shadows of distant barns stretch toward the viewer.

The full gold gradient — Quinacridone Gold, New Gamboge, Hansa Yellow Deep, Raw Sienna — across the sky and reflections. Sunset Ember mix at the horizon. French Ultramarine and Cobalt Blue for the upper sky. Payne’s Gray for the barn silhouettes and their impossibly long shadows.

  • This is a painting about horizontal lines — the flat land, the stretched shadows, the horizon, the drainage ditches. Keep everything horizontal except the barn silhouettes, which provide the only vertical accents
  • Grade the sky in a single uninterrupted wash: Cobalt Blue at the zenith, transitioning through Quinacridone Gold to the full Sunset Ember mix at the horizon. Work fast on wet paper, the entire sky in one pass
  • Drainage ditches and flooded fields are thin lines and small rectangles of reserved paper that you fill with concentrated New Gamboge after the surrounding flat earth (Raw Sienna) is dry — they should glow against the darker ground
  • Barn silhouettes are small, dark Payne’s Gray rectangles near the horizon, with shadows that stretch all the way to the bottom of the painting in French Ultramarine — their extreme length is what tells the viewer how low the sun is
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Quinacridone Gold (PO49) is the defining pigment of this palette — a transparent, warm gold that glazes beautifully and is irreplaceable for painting golden light. New Gamboge (PY97) is a clean, bright warm yellow without the earth tones of the natural pigment it replaces. Aussie Red Gold (PY150, PR101) sits between gold and red, capturing the specific red-shifted quality of low-angle sunlight. Alizarin Crimson (PR83) is transparent and cool-leaning red, perfect for sunset edges where gold turns to fire.
