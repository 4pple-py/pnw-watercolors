Week 22 — PNW Watercolor Series

Solstice Ridge

Nearly sixteen hours of light. Beargrass torches the high ridges white. Fireweed climbs from the bottom. Duochrome Cactus Flower week — the sky at 10 PM shifts pink-to-gold like the pigment itself. Summer arriving not as heat but as light that refuses to leave. The mountain ridges are still readable at hours that should be night.

High-altitude sky, deeper and harder than lowland blue; opaque, granulating, mineral-honest

Mixes & Ratios

Beargrass Torch

3 Buff Titanium : 1 Hansa Yellow Light : ¼ Raw Sienna

Cream-white plumes catching horizontal solstice light on a ridgeline — botanical torches marking the elevation where trees give up.

Buff Titanium
Hansa Yellow Lt
Raw Sienna
Buff Titanium gives the plume its physical presence — beargrass flowers are dense, opaque, cream-colored structures. Hansa Yellow Light warms the light side where the 10 PM sun catches them horizontally. Raw Sienna at the base where the plume meets the stalk. Apply with an upward brushstroke, dry brush at the tip for the feathered texture. These are vertical forms; paint them vertically.

Cactus Flower Sky

2 Duochrome Cactus Flower : 1 Deep Scarlet : ½ Hansa Yellow Light

The pink-to-gold shift of a sky that won't go dark — the solstice refusing to end.

Cactus Flower
Deep Scarlet
Hansa Yellow
Duochrome Cactus Flower is the star — it shifts between pink and gold depending on viewing angle and paper texture, which is exactly what a solstice sky does. Deep Scarlet intensifies the pink register. Hansa Yellow Light lifts the gold register. Apply to wet paper in a single sweeping wash and do not touch again. The duochrome shift needs undisturbed pigment settling to work its trick. Tilt the board to let gravity pull the color gradient.

Ridgeline Dusk

2 Cerulean Blue Chromium : 1 Perylene Green : ½ Sap Green

Forested ridge silhouette at 10 PM, still readable — dark enough to be night anywhere else, but here the light holds.

Cerulean Blue Chr.
Perylene Green
Sap Green
This isn't black — it's the specific dark blue-green of conifers silhouetted against a sky that still has light in it. Cerulean Blue Chromium's opacity creates the solid mass of the ridgeline. Perylene Green deepens toward black without actually arriving there. Sap Green adds just enough life to prevent the mix from reading as dead silhouette. Apply with the board flat; the opacity of this mix means drips show.

Why These Colors?

Duochrome Cactus Flower is the palette's reason for existing — a Daniel Smith interference pigment that physically shifts between pink and gold, mimicking the solstice sky's behavior with actual optical physics rather than mixing tricks. The rest of the palette supports it: Hansa Yellow Light and Deep Scarlet extend the duochrome's range in both directions. Cerulean Blue Chromium handles the sky overhead while Cactus Flower handles the horizon. Perylene Green is the only dark capable of rendering 10 PM darkness that still lets you read tree shapes. Buff Titanium earns its place through the beargrass — these are opaque flowers demanding opaque paint.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Beargrass on the Ridge at 10 PM

Subject: Beargrass plumes torching a high ridge in horizontal solstice light, the sky behind them shifting from pink to gold, mountains still readable in the endless dusk.

The Beargrass Torch mix (Buff Titanium + Hansa Yellow Light + Raw Sienna) handles the plumes. The Cactus Flower Sky mix behind them is the reason this palette exists — one pigment doing what the sky actually does.

  • Paint the sky first: wet the paper and sweep the Cactus Flower Sky mix (Duochrome Cactus Flower + Deep Scarlet + Hansa Yellow Light) in a single pass, tilting the board to let gravity create the gradient
  • Do not touch the sky wash again — Duochrome Cactus Flower needs undisturbed settling to achieve its pink-to-gold shift
  • Once dry, paint the beargrass plumes with upward strokes of the Beargrass Torch mix, using dry brush at the tips for the feathered texture
  • Aussie Red Gold on the stalks below each plume gives the amber color of the drying stem catching sideways light
Prompt 2

Subalpine Meadow With Fireweed

Subject: An alpine meadow at treeline, fireweed stalks opening from the bottom up, Sap Green grass between them, a Cerulean Blue Chromium sky overhead.

Deep Scarlet is the fireweed — transparent and deep, it builds intensity in layers the way the flowers build up the stalk. Sap Green meadow and Cerulean Blue Chromium sky are the classic alpine color trio.

  • The sky is Cerulean Blue Chromium applied at full concentration to dry paper — at altitude, the blue is harder and deeper than lowland sky, and the granulation adds mineral honesty
  • The meadow is Sap Green with touches of Hansa Yellow Light where the sun hits, applied in loose horizontal strokes
  • Fireweed stalks are vertical lines of Deep Scarlet, darkest at the bottom where the flowers opened first, fading to buds at the top
  • Cascade Green in a narrow strip marks the snowmelt creek cutting through the meadow — the cool accent that says water is near
Prompt 3

The 10 PM Ridgeline Silhouette

Subject: A forested ridge against a sky that refuses to go dark — the serrated line of conifers still readable at an hour that should be night, Duochrome Cactus Flower sky behind.

The Ridgeline Dusk mix (Cerulean Blue Chromium + Perylene Green + Sap Green) is the specific dark blue-green of conifers silhouetted against lingering light. It’s not black. That’s the whole point.

  • Paint the sky in two zones: Cerulean Blue Chromium at the zenith grading down to the Cactus Flower Sky mix at the horizon. Let it dry completely.
  • The ridgeline is one continuous stroke of Ridgeline Dusk mix applied with a flat brush — jagged top edge for the conifer silhouette, flat bottom
  • Apply with the board flat so the opaque mix doesn’t drip — Cerulean Blue Chromium’s opacity means gravity works against you on tilted surfaces
  • Leave the horizon strip between ridgeline and sky the brightest part of the Duochrome Cactus Flower wash — that’s where the light lives longest
Prompt 4

Alpine Trail at the Solstice

Subject: A boot-packed ridge trail winding through beargrass and heather, the longest day of the year stretching shadows to impossible lengths.

Raw Sienna is the sun-baked trail. Beargrass Torch plumes line both sides. The long shadows are Perylene Green on the ground and Cerulean Blue Chromium in the air.

  • The trail is Raw Sienna with a touch of Buff Titanium for the sun-baked, compacted surface — paint it first as the compositional spine
  • Beargrass plumes along the trail edges are the Beargrass Torch mix, varying the Hansa Yellow Light proportion to show which plumes catch the low sun
  • Shadows stretching across the trail are dilute Perylene Green — laid over the dried Raw Sienna trail, the complementary pairing creates the specific color of shadow on warm earth
  • Sap Green heather patches between the beargrass fill the alpine meadow without competing for attention
Prompt 5

Solstice Panorama (The Whole Ridge)

Subject: The full alpine panorama — beargrass ridgeline in foreground, layered mountain ranges behind, the sky cycling through every color this palette offers as the solstice sun circles but never quite sets.

Every pigment in the palette has a job in this painting. Duochrome Cactus Flower sky, Cerulean Blue Chromium overhead, Ridgeline Dusk silhouettes, Beargrass Torch foreground, Deep Scarlet fireweed, Sap Green meadow. This is the painting the palette was designed for.

  • Work back to front: sky first (Cactus Flower Sky mix wet-in-wet), then distant ridges (Ridgeline Dusk mix at high dilution), then mid-ground ridge (Ridgeline Dusk at full strength), then foreground meadow
  • Distant ranges should be progressively lighter and cooler — add more Cerulean Blue Chromium and dilute more for each receding layer
  • The foreground meadow is the most detailed zone: individual beargrass plumes, fireweed stalks, Cascade Green creek, Raw Sienna trail
  • Duochrome Cactus Flower works best applied generously over white paper — thin washes lose the color shift, so don’t skimp on the sky
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Duochrome Cactus Flower is an interference pigment — it works best over white or very light paper and shifts color depending on viewing angle. Apply it generously; thin washes lose the duochrome effect. Deep Scarlet is a newer Daniel Smith pigment with excellent lightfastness and a blue-leaning red that partners well with the duochrome. Perylene Green in mass tone is nearly black — dilute test swatches before committing.
