Week 22 — PNW Watercolor Series

Solstice Ridge

Sixteen hours of light on a Cascade ridge. The sky is Cerulean Blue so saturated it hurts to look at directly. Glacial tarns hold that impossible Cobalt Teal Blue. New Gamboge meadow light pours over everything. Snow patches still cling to the north faces like they didn’t get the memo. The full spectrum hitting at once because the sun has all day to work the whole sky.

Primary Trio

Cerulean Blue

High summer sky at alpine elevation; opaque, granulating, the blue that means altitude and full-spectrum daylight

New Gamboge

Full sun golden light on everything the ridgeline catches; transparent, warm, relentlessly luminous

Cobalt Teal Blue

Glacial tarns and alpine ice; PG50’s turquoise hits a frequency no other pigment reaches — the color of snowmelt holding sky

Mixes & Ratios

Glacial Tarn

2 Cobalt Teal Blue : 1 Cerulean Blue : ¼ Titanium White

The impossible turquoise of snowmelt pooled in granite — a color that makes people ask if the photo is edited.

Cobalt Teal Blue
Cerulean Blue
Ti White
Cobalt Teal Blue PG50 is doing the heavy lifting — that turquoise frequency is unique in watercolor and nothing else substitutes. Cerulean Blue shifts the mix slightly toward sky-reflected blue, which is what you actually see in a tarn: turquoise water reflecting blue sky. The touch of Titanium White adds the milky opacity that glacial flour creates — these aren’t clear pools, they’re mineral suspensions. Apply wet and let the granulation happen. PG50 and Cerulean both granulate, and together they create the stippled texture of light on glacial water.

Alpine Meadow Gold

2 New Gamboge : 1 Sap Green : ½ Hansa Yellow Deep

Subalpine meadow at peak bloom — that green-gold of grass and sedge that only happens when the snow left eight weeks ago and the sun hasn’t stopped since.

New Gamboge
Sap Green
Hansa Yellow Deep
New Gamboge brings transparent golden warmth — the light itself, not just the surface. Sap Green cools the mix toward vegetation reality without killing the luminosity. Hansa Yellow Deep deepens the gold register and gives it staying power in the warm zone. This mix reads as sunlit meadow from across a valley. Vary the Sap Green proportion: more for shaded grass, less for the sunstruck ridgetop where the meadow is more gold than green. Let these pigments mingle on the paper rather than pre-mixing — the variation is the meadow.

Ridge Rock Warm

1 Burnt Sienna : 1 Transparent Red Oxide : ½ French Ultramarine

Cascade volcanic rock baking in solstice sun — andesite and basalt warming all day until the stone radiates back.

Burnt Sienna
Trans. Red Oxide
Fr. Ultramarine
Burnt Sienna and Transparent Red Oxide together create a richer, more complex warm than either alone — Burnt Sienna leans orange while Transparent Red Oxide leans brick, and alpine rock sits exactly between them. French Ultramarine in the shadow side creates granulating darks that read as mineral texture rather than flat shadow. This is the classic watercolor complementary pairing: iron oxide plus ultramarine, but split across two different iron oxides for depth. Wet the shadow areas first and drop the Ultramarine in to let it granulate against the warm base.

Why These Colors?

The solstice ridge is full-spectrum light hitting everything at once, so this palette spreads wide. Cerulean Blue and Phthalo Blue GS split sky duties — Cerulean for the opaque, granulating, obviously-alpine overhead blue; Phthalo for the zenith where blue goes deeper than a single pigment can reach. Cobalt Teal Blue PG50 is irreplaceable: no other pigment hits the glacial tarn frequency, that turquoise-in-the-mountains color you can’t mix from a standard palette. New Gamboge and Hansa Yellow Deep handle the golden light at two registers, warm-transparent and warm-deep. Burnt Sienna and Transparent Red Oxide are the ridge itself — volcanic rock in sun and shadow. Sap Green is the alpine meadow growing at high speed between snowfields. French Ultramarine pushes distant ranges back into atmosphere. And Titanium White earns its rare watercolor appearance because snow patches on north faces demand actual opacity — you cannot lift white out of a Cerulean Blue wash and have it read as snow.

Painting Prompts

Four subjects built for this palette — each one uses the full-spectrum solstice light and leans into the colors you’ve already got mixed.

Prompt 1

Ridge Panorama at Golden Hour

Subject: A Cascade ridge at 8 PM, sun still high enough to pour New Gamboge light across the whole scene. Alpine meadow foreground, rocky ridgeline mid-ground, layered ranges disappearing into French Ultramarine distance. Snow patches on north faces. The sky that particular Cerulean Blue that only happens above 6,000 feet.

Every pigment has a job. Cerulean Blue sky, Phthalo Blue GS zenith, New Gamboge and Hansa Yellow Deep on the meadow, Burnt Sienna and Transparent Red Oxide on the rock, French Ultramarine distance, Titanium White snow, Sap Green between the wildflowers.

  • Work back to front: sky is Cerulean Blue grading to Phthalo Blue GS at the zenith — wet paper, single wash, tilt the board to let the heavier Cerulean settle toward the horizon
  • Distant ranges are French Ultramarine at high dilution — each successive range gets slightly more pigment and slightly warmer, adding a breath of Cerulean Blue to the nearest
  • The Ridge Rock Warm mix (Burnt Sienna + Transparent Red Oxide + French Ultramarine) for the mid-ground ridgeline, with French Ultramarine dropped into wet shadow sides for granulating texture
  • Snow patches are Titanium White mixed thick, applied after everything is bone dry — they need to sit on top of the paper, opaque and definite
Prompt 2

Glacial Tarn at Treeline

Subject: A glacial tarn sitting in a granite bowl below a snowfield. That absurd turquoise — Cobalt Teal Blue made real by rock flour suspension. Reflected sky and ridge in the water. Snow at the upper edge, meadow at the lower.

The Glacial Tarn mix (Cobalt Teal Blue + Cerulean Blue + Titanium White) is the painting’s center. PG50’s unique turquoise does what no mix of blue and green can replicate — it already is the color.

  • The tarn is the Glacial Tarn mix applied to wet paper in one confident wash — let Cobalt Teal Blue and Cerulean Blue granulate together for the stippled texture of glacial water
  • Reflections of the Ridge Rock Warm mix float on top of the still-wet tarn wash, blurred by the water’s surface tension
  • The snowfield above the tarn is Titanium White mixed with a whisper of Cerulean Blue — snow isn’t white, it’s sky-colored in shadow
  • Sap Green and Hansa Yellow Deep in the meadow below the tarn outlet, where snowmelt waters the most intense alpine green you’ll see all year
Prompt 3

Alpine Meadow With Snow Patches

Subject: A subalpine meadow at peak early-summer bloom, snow patches still lingering in the hollows. The meadow is alpine gold-green, the snow is shelf-edged and retreating, and the contrast between the two says everything about the season’s momentum.

The Alpine Meadow Gold mix (New Gamboge + Sap Green + Hansa Yellow Deep) handles the meadow. Titanium White for the snow. The boundary between them is the painting — where gold-green meets white.

  • Paint the snow patches first as reserved white paper with a thin Titanium White glaze — keep the edges hard and shelf-like on the uphill side, soft and melting on the downhill
  • The Alpine Meadow Gold mix surrounds the snow: more Sap Green in the shaded areas near the snowfield edges, more New Gamboge on the sun-facing slopes
  • Meltwater channels between snow and meadow are thin lines of Cobalt Teal Blue, barely a brushstroke wide, carrying glacial turquoise through the green
  • Burnt Sienna for exposed rocks emerging from the snowpack — warm sentinels marking where the snow was a week ago
Prompt 4

Solstice Light at 9:30 PM

Subject: The sunset that won’t end. A Cascade ridge silhouette against a sky still lit at 9:30 PM, New Gamboge light going horizontal across everything, the blue deepening above while the gold refuses to leave below. The longest day’s last act, which lasts an hour.

New Gamboge and Hansa Yellow Deep own the horizon. Phthalo Blue GS and French Ultramarine take the upper sky. The ridge silhouette is Burnt Sienna and Transparent Red Oxide backlit to near-black. Cobalt Teal Blue lingers in the transition zone where gold meets blue — that teal moment in the solstice sky.

  • The sky is a single graded wash, top to bottom: Phthalo Blue GS at the zenith, grading through French Ultramarine, into Cerulean Blue, touching Cobalt Teal Blue at the transition, then New Gamboge and Hansa Yellow Deep blazing along the horizon — tilt the board and work fast
  • Let the sky dry completely. The ridge silhouette is a concentrated mix of Transparent Red Oxide and Burnt Sienna with a touch of Phthalo Blue GS — dark enough to silhouette but warm enough to show the day’s heat still radiating
  • The gold-to-blue transition is where this painting lives or dies: if the Cobalt Teal Blue bleeds correctly into the New Gamboge zone, you get that exact solstice teal-gold moment
  • Titanium White for the last snow patches catching the final horizontal light — bright flares on an otherwise dark ridgeline, proof the mountain still holds winter
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Cobalt Teal Blue (PG50) is a unique pigment — no combination of Phthalo Blue and Phthalo Green replicates its specific turquoise frequency. It granulates beautifully and is semi-opaque. Cerulean Blue (PB35) is also opaque and granulating; when combined with Cobalt Teal Blue, both granulate into each other for a stippled mineral texture that reads as natural. Titanium White is unusual in watercolor but necessary here — snow patches on dark rock cannot be achieved by lifting or reserving paper in a complex painting. Use it thick. French Ultramarine granulates with Burnt Sienna for that classic watercolor texture trick; it works even better with Transparent Red Oxide.
