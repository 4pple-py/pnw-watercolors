Week 44 — PNW Watercolor Series

Eagle Gray

November flattens the world. The sky is the same gray as the river, the river the same gray as the gravel bars. And then the eagles come back — dark shapes in bare cottonwoods, white heads catching what little light there is. They sit in the skeletal trees like punctuation marks on a page nobody’s reading. The Skagit fills, the salmon run, and the eagles descend into a landscape that has already surrendered its color.

Primary Trio

Payne’s Gray

The November sky itself — blue-gray, heavy, low-hanging; the dominant note of the entire month, neither warm nor cold, just present

Ivory Black

Eagle wing feathers at full spread; the deepest dark in a monochrome landscape, warm-leaning black with a subtle brown undertone

French Ultramarine

The cold blue-gray buried inside the overcast — granulating, deep, the color that makes November sky feel heavy rather than empty

Mixes & Ratios

Lead Sky

3 Payne’s Gray : 1 French Ultramarine : ½ Neutral Tint

November overcast so heavy it looks solid — a sky you could press your hand against and leave a print.

Payne's Gray
French Ultramarine
Neutral Tint
Payne’s Gray carries the structural weight — it’s already a complex blue-gray, and at full strength it reads as genuinely oppressive sky. French Ultramarine deepens the blue undertone just enough to keep the gray from going dead neutral — November overcast has a cold blue memory buried in it. Neutral Tint flattens any remaining color temperature, pushing the mix toward that affectless, even gray that makes November feel like the sky has no opinion. Apply as a graded wash from top to bottom, letting the mix thin naturally toward the horizon where Buff Titanium takes over. Tilt the board slightly and let gravity do the grading — don’t over-brush.

Wing Dark

2 Ivory Black : 1 Burnt Sienna : ¼ French Ultramarine

Bald eagle wing feathers in overcast light — not a flat black but a dark with warmth underneath, the way real feathers absorb and shift.

Ivory Black
Burnt Sienna
French Ultramarine
Ivory Black alone reads dead on paper — no life, no light interaction. Burnt Sienna warms the black toward the actual color of eagle feathers, which have a brown undertone visible when the bird shifts on a branch. French Ultramarine at the edges adds a cold blue sheen that mimics the iridescence visible on fresh plumage in angled light. Apply in a single confident stroke for each wing — eagle feathers are sleek and defined, not fuzzy. The Burnt Sienna should be more visible in the body feathers, less visible in the primary flight feathers where the blue sheen dominates.

River Steel

2 Cobalt Blue : 1 Payne’s Gray : ½ Raw Umber

The Skagit in November — cold, steel-colored, reflecting a sky that gives it nothing to work with.

Cobalt Blue
Payne's Gray
Raw Umber
Cobalt Blue gives the river its cold clarity — even under overcast, the Skagit retains a steely blue that’s distinct from the gray sky above. Payne’s Gray grays down the blue to match November lighting, pulling it away from anything cheerful. Raw Umber muddies the mix exactly the right amount — the river is carrying sediment from weeks of rain, and its clarity has a brown depth to it. Wet the paper first and apply in horizontal strokes, leaving thin dry lines of white paper between passes to suggest the river’s surface movement. The Cobalt Blue granulates slightly against the Payne’s Gray, giving the water a subtle texture without brushwork.

Why These Colors?

November gray is monochrome plus eagle. This palette goes deeper into gray than the silver-leaning early autumn — less shimmer, more lead. Payne’s Gray dominates because it IS the November sky: a complex blue-gray that sits over everything like a lid. Ivory Black handles the eagle’s wing darks with a warmth that pure lamp black can’t match — real eagle feathers have brown in them. French Ultramarine adds cold depth to the overcast and granulates beautifully against the grays, building atmospheric texture. Chinese White earns its place for the eagle’s head and tail — you need body color for white feathers against a gray sky, not just reserved paper, because the white needs to be slightly warm and opaque the way real feathers are. Yellow Ochre is the only real warmth in this palette, and it’s confined to the beak and talons — a small, bright accent that draws the eye exactly where it should go. Neutral Tint flattens everything into November’s affectless light. Raw Umber handles the bare cottonwoods and gravel bars. Cobalt Blue in the river keeps water distinct from sky. Buff Titanium at the horizon is the only hint that the sun exists somewhere behind all this gray.

Painting Prompts

November subjects for a November palette — eagles, bare trees, and rivers that have forgotten what blue sky looks like. Every prompt leans into the gray.

Prompt 1

Eagle in Bare Cottonwood

Subject: A bald eagle perched high in a leafless cottonwood, white head turned to profile. Overcast sky filling the background. The tree’s bare branches radiate outward like cracks in glass. The eagle is the only sharp shape in a soft gray world.

The Wing Dark mix (Ivory Black + Burnt Sienna + French Ultramarine) builds the body. Chinese White for the head. Raw Umber for the cottonwood’s skeletal branches. The Lead Sky mix (Payne’s Gray + French Ultramarine + Neutral Tint) washes the entire background.

  • Paint the Lead Sky wash first and let it dry completely — the eagle needs to sit ON the sky, not bleed into it; a crisp silhouette against soft gray is what makes this composition work
  • The eagle’s white head is Chinese White mixed with a breath of Buff Titanium — pure white paper would be too stark, and the slight warmth of Chinese White reads as feather rather than snow
  • Yellow Ochre for the beak is your focal point anchor — keep it small and saturated, the brightest note in the entire painting, drawing the eye to the eagle’s face
  • Raw Umber branches should be painted as continuous calligraphic strokes, thin to thick — bare cottonwoods have a rhythm to their branching that your brush can follow if you don’t overthink it
Prompt 2

Skagit River Gravel Bar

Subject: A wide gravel bar on the Skagit River, steel-gray water curving around smooth stones. Two or three eagles on the far bank, small in the frame. Low overcast pressing down. The river dominates the composition.

River Steel (Cobalt Blue + Payne’s Gray + Raw Umber) for the water. Neutral Tint and Raw Umber for the gravel. The eagles are small dark accents — Wing Dark for the bodies, tiny dots of Chinese White for the heads.

  • The river is the subject, not the eagles — give it two-thirds of the paper. Apply the River Steel mix to wet paper in long horizontal passes, leaving thread-thin dry lines to suggest current
  • Gravel bars are Neutral Tint with scattered Raw Umber — spatter both from a loaded brush onto damp paper to suggest individual stones without painting each one
  • The eagles on the far bank should be no larger than your thumbnail — a stroke of Ivory Black, a dot of Chinese White, a speck of Yellow Ochre. Their smallness against the river is the whole point
  • Leave a thin band of Buff Titanium at the horizon where sky meets the far treeline — this sliver of pale light is the only depth cue in an otherwise flat gray scene
Prompt 3

Eagle Head Portrait

Subject: Close-up study of a bald eagle’s head and upper body. The white head feathers, the fierce yellow eye, the hooked beak, the transition zone where white neck meets dark brown-black body. Tight crop, no background.

Chinese White built up in layers for the head feathers. Yellow Ochre for the beak and eye. The Wing Dark mix for the body, with Burnt Sienna more visible in the throat transition. Payne’s Gray for the soft shadow shapes on the white head.

  • Start with a very dilute Payne’s Gray wash to map the shadow shapes on the white head — eagle head feathers are not flat white but sculpted by subtle gray shadows that give the head its rounded form
  • Layer Chinese White over the dried shadow wash to build the feather texture — work in short, directional strokes following the feather growth pattern from beak backward toward the nape
  • The transition from white neck to dark body is not a hard line — use Burnt Sienna as a middle value bridge, letting it bleed into the Ivory Black body below for a natural gradation
  • The eye is the anchor: Yellow Ochre iris with a pure Ivory Black pupil, a tiny dot of reserved white paper for the catchlight. Get the eye right and the rest of the portrait follows
Prompt 4

November Treeline at Dusk

Subject: A line of bare deciduous trees and dark conifers along a riverbank, early dusk in November. The sky is marginally lighter than the land. Silhouetted shapes. An eagle circling above, wings spread, dark against the last gray light.

Neutral Tint and Payne’s Gray for the dusk sky. Raw Umber for deciduous tree silhouettes, Ivory Black for conifer silhouettes. French Ultramarine deepens the sky near the zenith. The soaring eagle is a calligraphic W-shape in Ivory Black.

  • Grade the sky from French Ultramarine at top through Payne’s Gray in the middle to Buff Titanium at the horizon — November dusk compresses the entire value range into a narrow band of grays
  • The treeline goes in while the sky is still damp — use Raw Umber for the bare deciduous shapes and Ivory Black for the conifers, letting them bleed slightly upward into the sky for a soft, atmospheric edge
  • The circling eagle is just two curved strokes of Ivory Black forming a shallow W — at this distance, detail kills the poetry. Keep it gestural
  • The river in the foreground mirrors the sky but darker — mix Payne’s Gray with a touch of Cobalt Blue and apply it in a flat, still wash to contrast with the textured treeline above
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Payne’s Gray (PBk6, PV19, PB15) is a convenience mix that reads as a complex blue-gray — it granulates slightly and mixes cleanly with every color here. Ivory Black (PBk9) is warmer than Lamp Black, with a subtle brown cast that matches natural darks better than a blue-black would. French Ultramarine (PB29) granulates heavily, adding atmospheric texture to sky washes. Neutral Tint (PBk6, PV19) flattens color temperature — useful for dead-light November scenes where the world loses its opinion about warm vs. cool.
