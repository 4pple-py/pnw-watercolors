Week 36 — PNW Watercolor Series

Chanterelle Gold

You smell them before you see them — apricot and pepper rising from the duff. Then gold, impossible gold, glowing against the dark floor of second-growth Doug fir. The forest is nearly black around them and the chanterelles don’t care. They hold their own light the way a candle does in a cathedral, small and absolute. Your knees are already wet. You’re already reaching.

Primary Trio

Quinacridone Gold

The deep amber of a mature chanterelle cap; transparent, staining, the gold that glows from inside out against dark forest floor

New Gamboge

Bright mushroom cap where light hits directly; cleaner and more vivid than Quinacridone Gold, the golden highlight on the cap’s crown

Perylene Green

Near-black green of the forest floor in deep shade; transparent, cool, the darkness that makes the gold sing

Mixes & Ratios

Cap Glow

2 Quinacridone Gold : 1 New Gamboge : ¼ Mayan Orange

The chanterelle cap itself — luminous, warm, catching whatever light the canopy lets through like a golden bowl turned upward.

Quin. Gold
New Gamboge
Mayan Orange
Quinacridone Gold provides the deep, transparent amber that is the chanterelle’s core identity — it glows rather than reflects. New Gamboge lifts the highlights where light hits the cap’s crown, brighter and cleaner than the Quin Gold base. Mayan Orange is the textural ingredient: a Primatek pigment ground from genuine mineral, it granulates on paper in a way that mimics the subtle pebbling of a chanterelle’s skin. Apply the Quinacridone Gold base first as a shaped wash for the full cap. While still damp, drop New Gamboge into the crown area and Mayan Orange along the cap’s curling edges. Let the Mayan Orange settle and granulate on its own — its mineral particles will find the paper’s valleys and build organic texture without you trying.

Cathedral Dark

2 Perylene Green : 1 Hooker’s Green : ½ Lamp Black

The near-black forest that surrounds the chanterelle — so dark it’s almost absence, but green enough to still be alive.

Perylene Green
Hooker’s Green
Lamp Black
Perylene Green is already nearly black but crucially still green — it reads as forest shadow rather than void. Hooker’s Green adds just enough chromatic identity to keep the darkness vegetative: you need to feel the canopy, not just the absence of light. Lamp Black pushes the deepest pockets to true dark, the places between the trunks where light genuinely doesn’t reach. This mix is the foil for everything golden in the palette. Apply it as a single confident wash around the mushroom subject, working quickly to avoid hard edges. The contrast between this near-black surround and the Cap Glow mix is the entire painting. Get the values right and the gold will sing without you having to force it.

Rotting Log

2 Burnt Umber : 1 Transparent Brown Oxide : ¼ Raw Umber

The nurse log where chanterelles love to fruit nearby — soft, punky wood returning to earth, warm browns layered with decay.

Burnt Umber
Trans. Brown Oxide
Raw Umber
Burnt Umber is the structural dark of decomposing wood — cooler and more neutral than Burnt Sienna, it reads as decay rather than warmth. Transparent Brown Oxide adds a glazing layer: its full transparency lets you build depth by layering, each coat darkening the log’s interior without losing the sense of wood grain. Raw Umber at the outermost surface captures the dry, crumbling exterior where the log meets air. Dry-brush the Burnt Umber with a flat brush dragged along the log’s length for the grain pattern. Then glaze Transparent Brown Oxide over the shadow side. The Raw Umber goes on last, barely touching the highlights, suggesting bark fragments still clinging to the surface.

Why These Colors?

Chanterelles glow gold in dark forest, and the entire palette is engineered around that single phenomenon: bright warm subjects surrounded by near-black cool environment. Quinacridone Gold and New Gamboge give you two temperatures of chanterelle gold — the deep amber interior and the bright highlighted crown. Mayan Orange’s Primatek granulation adds the organic, slightly rough texture that smooth synthetic pigments can’t replicate — chanterelles have a subtle pebbled skin that demands a mineral pigment. Cadmium Orange Hue pushes the warmest notes toward the ruffled gill edges where the mushroom catches light at extreme angles. Against all this gold, Perylene Green and Hooker’s Green build the near-black forest that makes the contrast work — Perylene for the deepest shadows, Hooker’s for the canopy overhead where some green identity survives. Lamp Black handles the places where even Perylene Green isn’t dark enough: the gaps between trunks, the interior of hollow logs. The three earth tones — Transparent Brown Oxide, Burnt Umber, Raw Umber — are the forest floor itself: duff, decaying wood, and wet soil, the substrate from which the gold emerges.

Painting Prompts

October forest floors are treasure maps if you know how to read them. These subjects find the gold in the dark and let the palette do what it was built for.

Prompt 1

Chanterelle Cluster in Duff

Subject: Three or four chanterelles pushing up through fir needle duff, caps at different stages — one still tightly cupped, one flared open showing gill ridges, one halfway. The forest floor is dark around them and the mushrooms glow.

The Cap Glow mix (Quinacridone Gold + New Gamboge + Mayan Orange) for the mushrooms, surrounded by the Rotting Log mix and Cathedral Dark mix. The gold against the dark is the whole painting.

  • Paint the dark forest floor and background first with the Cathedral Dark mix — leave the mushroom shapes as unpainted white paper, then build the Cap Glow mix into those reserved shapes
  • Vary each mushroom: the cupped one gets more Quinacridone Gold in the shadows of the cup interior; the flared one gets more New Gamboge on its open surface and Cadmium Orange Hue along the gill ridges
  • Mayan Orange applied to damp paper at the cap edges will granulate and build the chanterelle’s characteristic pebbled texture without brushwork
  • Transparent Brown Oxide for the fir needles scattered across the duff — quick strokes radiating outward from the mushroom cluster, some overlapping the caps where debris has fallen on them
Prompt 2

Dark Forest Interior — Light Shaft

Subject: A single shaft of light penetrating the canopy of old-growth Douglas fir, illuminating a small circle of forest floor. Chanterelles just visible in the lit zone. Everything outside the shaft is nearly black.

Cathedral Dark mix dominates — this painting is ninety percent darkness with one blazing circle of New Gamboge and Quinacridone Gold. Hooker’s Green for the canopy where the shaft originates.

  • Start by painting the light shaft as a negative shape: mask or reserve the illuminated circle and the shaft itself, then surround with Cathedral Dark at full concentration
  • The light shaft is Hooker’s Green diluted with water at the top where it passes through the canopy, transitioning to New Gamboge where it hits the forest floor
  • Chanterelles in the lit zone are tiny accents of Quinacridone Gold — keep them small and subordinate to the drama of the light itself
  • Lamp Black in the deepest corners where the Cathedral Dark mix isn’t dark enough — the contrast must be extreme for the light shaft to read as luminous
Prompt 3

Chanterelle in Hand

Subject: A forager’s muddy hand holding a single perfect chanterelle up to the light. The mushroom is backlit, its ruffled edges translucent gold. Dirt on the knuckles. The forest blurred behind.

The Cap Glow mix at its most luminous for the backlit mushroom. Raw Umber and Burnt Umber for the muddy hand. The out-of-focus background is a soft wash of Hooker’s Green and Perylene Green.

  • The backlit edges of the chanterelle are where Cadmium Orange Hue earns its place — at the thinnest ruffled margins, the mushroom goes from gold to orange as light passes through
  • The hand is Raw Umber for the skin, Burnt Umber worked into the creases and under the nails where forest soil has lodged. Keep the hand secondary — it’s the frame, not the subject
  • The blurred forest behind is Hooker’s Green and Perylene Green applied to very wet paper with no hard edges — everything behind the mushroom should be soft bokeh
  • Leave a rim of white paper around the mushroom’s backlit edge where the light is strongest — this halo of bare paper is what makes it glow
Prompt 4

Nurse Log Ecosystem

Subject: A moss-covered nurse log in cross-section, showing the layers of decay: bark fragments, punky wood, root networks, moss, and chanterelles fruiting from the decaying wood. A vertical slice of forest time.

The Rotting Log mix (Burnt Umber + Transparent Brown Oxide + Raw Umber) for the wood structure. Hooker’s Green for the moss. Quinacridone Gold for the chanterelles emerging from the decay. Every earth tone in the palette gets a role.

  • Build the log in layers: Burnt Umber dry-brushed for wood grain first, then Transparent Brown Oxide glazed over the interior for depth, then Raw Umber for the crumbling outer surface
  • Hooker’s Green for the moss blanket on top of the log — apply with a stippling motion for texture, heavier where the moss is thickest
  • Chanterelles emerging from the log are small bursts of New Gamboge and Quinacridone Gold — they should read as the reward for all that patient decay
  • Perylene Green underneath the log where the deepest shade lives, transitioning to Lamp Black in the hollow interior where the wood has completely given way
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Quinacridone Gold (PO49) is transparent and staining — it will not lift once dry, making it ideal for glazing. New Gamboge (PY153) is a modern, lightfast replacement for traditional Gamboge. Perylene Green (PBk31) is one of the darkest greens available, nearly black at full concentration but unmistakably green in dilution. Mayan Orange is a Primatek pigment from the Daniel Smith line — ground from genuine mineral, it granulates heavily and adds organic texture impossible to achieve with synthetic pigments. Lamp Black (PBk6) is a carbon-based black with a cool, slightly blue undertone, distinct from the warmer Ivory Black.
