Week 45 — PNW Watercolor Series

Rain Shadow Ache

On the wet side of the Cascades, November rain is constant and the light is violet-gray and the trees drip and the moss glows and everything is saturated and close. And somewhere east of the passes, the rain shadow begins — dry, golden, open. You can’t see it from here but you can feel it pulling. The ache is the distance between where you are and where the light is.

Primary Trio

Moonglow

Melancholy made visible — a granulating triad that separates into blue, violet, and gray as it dries, the painting literally aching apart on the paper

Indanthrone Blue

The weight of wet-side winter — deep, serious blue with a red undertone that makes it feel heavy and close, the color of saturated air

Shadow Violet

The ache itself — a muted, granulating violet-gray that sits between sadness and beauty, the exact color of rain-light through bare branches

Mixes & Ratios

Wet Longing

2 Moonglow : 1 Shadow Violet : ½ Indanthrone Blue

The color of staring out a rain-streaked window toward mountains you can’t see — violet-gray with blue weight, separating as it dries like memory coming apart.

Moonglow
Shadow Violet
Indanthrone Blue
Moonglow is a three-pigment convenience mix that literally separates on paper — blues drift one way, violets another, grays settle into the valleys. This is not a defect; it’s the entire point. As the wash dries, the painting develops an internal complexity that reads as emotional depth. Shadow Violet reinforces the violet note and adds its own granulation, creating a double-separation effect that’s impossible with synthetic pigments. Indanthrone Blue anchors the bottom of the mix with serious weight — it’s the gravity that keeps the ache from floating away into mere prettiness. Apply to very wet paper and tilt the board in multiple directions — let the pigments find their own resting places. Do not touch it while it dries.

Dry-Side Dream

2 Sedona Genuine : 1 Burnt Sienna : ¼ Buff Titanium

The warm, mineral gold of the dry side you’re imagining from the wet side — sandstone and sage and open sky, granulating like memory of sun on rock.

Sedona Genuine
Burnt Sienna
Buff Titanium
Sedona Genuine is ground mineral — actual sedimentary stone from the American Southwest — and its granulation on paper looks like sandstone under magnification. In a palette dominated by cool violet-grays, this warm mineral accent is the emotional counterweight: the dry side, the open side, the place where rain doesn’t reach. Burnt Sienna deepens and warms the mineral tone, adding the richness of baked earth. Buff Titanium lifts the mix slightly, adding the dusty, pale quality of dry-side light. Use this mix sparingly — a touch of warmth in the lower corner, a suggestion of the landscape beyond the mountains. Its rarity in the composition is what gives it power.

Evergreen Witness

2 Hooker’s Green : 1 Indanthrone Blue : ½ Payne’s Gray

The dark, rain-soaked conifers that stand through everything — not cheerful green but endurance green, the trees that don’t leave.

Hooker's Green
Indanthrone Blue
Payne's Gray
Hooker’s Green is already a dark, serious green — no cheerfulness, no spring. Adding Indanthrone Blue pushes it toward the blue-green of rain-soaked Douglas fir, where water darkens the needles and the whole tree looks like it’s holding its breath. Payne’s Gray grays down the mix just enough to place the trees in the flat November light rather than in some imagined sunny version of the forest. These are the trees that stay — the constant in every season, the witnesses to the rain shadow’s divide. Apply as silhouette shapes on the horizon, layering darker passes for nearer trees. Let the bottom edges bleed into wet Raw Umber ground to root them.

Why These Colors?

This palette is emotional before it’s descriptive. Moonglow is the key — a three-pigment mix that literally separates on paper into blues, violets, and grays as it dries. The painting aches as it develops. You can’t mix this effect from component pigments because the magic is in the differential settling: each pigment finds its own place in the paper’s texture, creating complexity that reads as feeling. Shadow Violet deepens the melancholy with its own granulation, a muted violet-gray that sits exactly at the intersection of sadness and beauty. Indanthrone Blue carries the weight of the wet side — it’s the heaviest, most serious blue in the Daniel Smith line, with a red undertone that makes it feel close and pressing rather than distant. Payne’s Gray structures the overcast. French Ultramarine pushes the distant Cascades into cold blue recession. Hooker’s Green holds the evergreen constant — the trees that never leave, that stand through every season. And then Sedona Genuine: a warm mineral pigment made from actual stone, the counterpoint to all this wet-side violet-gray. It’s the dry side. It’s the longing. Used sparingly, it’s the warmth you can feel pulling from beyond the mountains.

Painting Prompts

Rain-soaked subjects for a rain-soaked palette — every prompt lives on the wet side and looks east. Paint the ache, not just the rain.

Prompt 1

Rain Window — Cascade View

Subject: View through a rain-streaked window toward the Cascade foothills. Water runs down the glass in rivulets. The mountains are violet-gray shapes, barely there. Inside is warm and close; outside is infinite and wet.

The Wet Longing mix (Moonglow + Shadow Violet + Indanthrone Blue) for the mountains seen through rain. Payne’s Gray for the window frame. Burnt Sienna for the warm interior edge. Buff Titanium where thin light breaks through the overcast.

  • Paint the mountain shapes with the Wet Longing mix on very wet paper — the soft, bleeding edges ARE the rain-blurred view through glass; crisp edges would destroy the illusion
  • Rain rivulets on the glass are negative painting — lay the Moonglow wash and then lift thin vertical lines with a damp brush before it dries, creating clear paths through the pigment
  • The window frame in Payne’s Gray should be the crispest element in the painting, establishing the boundary between sharp interior and soft exterior
  • A sliver of Burnt Sienna at the interior edge — the warm side — creates the emotional contrast between shelter and exposure that drives this composition
Prompt 2

Wet Forest Floor

Subject: Looking down at the forest floor after hours of rain. Fallen leaves in browns and umbers, dark wet earth, moss glowing an impossible green. Water pools in footprints and low spots. Everything saturated, everything close.

Raw Umber and Burnt Sienna for the wet earth and fallen leaves. Hooker’s Green for the moss. Shadow Violet in the deepest shadows. Moonglow in the standing water reflections, where the sky’s violet-gray mirrors back at you.

  • Saturate the paper before you begin — this is a wet subject and needs to be painted wet. Let pigments merge and bleed the way water actually behaves on a forest floor
  • Raw Umber for the fallen leaves, applied with the side of a flat brush in quick stamps — each stamp is a leaf. Vary the concentration from dilute (oldest, most decayed) to full strength (freshly fallen)
  • Hooker’s Green moss should glow — use it at its most saturated against the dark Raw Umber earth for maximum contrast. Moss in rain-light is genuinely luminous
  • Standing water pools are Moonglow reflections — let the separation happen naturally, and the pools will develop their own complexity as the paint dries
Prompt 3

Cascade Divide — Wet Side / Dry Side

Subject: A split composition: the left half is wet-side PNW (rain, dark conifers, violet-gray overcast), the right half is the imagined dry side (warm mineral ground, open sky, golden light). The Cascade ridge divides them down the center.

Full Wet Longing mix and Evergreen Witness mix on the left. Dry-Side Dream mix (Sedona Genuine + Burnt Sienna + Buff Titanium) on the right. French Ultramarine for the ridge itself. The two halves should feel like different paintings that share a spine.

  • Paint each half separately, letting the first side dry completely before starting the second — the hard edge where they meet at the mountain ridge IS the rain shadow boundary
  • The wet side (left) gets the full Moonglow + Shadow Violet treatment, applied to soaked paper with maximum granulation. The dry side (right) gets Sedona Genuine and Buff Titanium on dry paper for a crisp, mineral texture
  • The Cascade ridge in French Ultramarine should be the one element that bridges both sides, painted as a continuous line across the center
  • Push the temperature contrast hard — the wet side should feel cold and violet, the dry side warm and golden. The gap between them is the ache
Prompt 4

November Walk — Wet Trail

Subject: A trail through second-growth forest in the rain. The path is dark mud between evergreen walls. Fog obscures everything beyond fifty feet. A single figure in a rain jacket walks away from the viewer, small against the trees.

Evergreen Witness mix for the forest walls. Payne’s Gray for the fog, thinning to Buff Titanium where it swallows the trail. Raw Umber for the muddy path. Shadow Violet in the atmospheric distance. The figure is a small shape of Indanthrone Blue.

  • The fog is the composition — paint the near trees in full Evergreen Witness strength, then progressively dilute the mix with water and Buff Titanium as the trees recede, until the farthest trees are ghostly shapes in Shadow Violet mist
  • The muddy trail is Raw Umber at full strength in the foreground, lightening and narrowing as it recedes — perspective through value, not just line
  • The walking figure should be no more than an inch tall: a quick stroke of Indanthrone Blue for the jacket, a dot of Payne’s Gray for the head. Human presence without human detail
  • Fog eats edges — nothing in the middle distance should have a crisp boundary. Soften every edge beyond the foreground trees with a clean damp brush immediately after painting
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Moonglow (PB29, PR177, PBk7) is a proprietary three-pigment mix that granulates and separates on paper — its behavior is the conceptual engine of this palette. Indanthrone Blue (PB60) is one of the deepest, most serious blues available, with a red undertone that distinguishes it from Phthalo Blue’s green lean. Shadow Violet (PBr25) is a natural iron oxide that granulates heavily in a muted violet-gray — unique and irreplaceable by mixing. Sedona Genuine is a PrimaTek pigment made from actual sedimentary stone, granulating with a warm mineral character that no synthetic pigment can match.
