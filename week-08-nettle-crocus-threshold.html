Week 8 — PNW Watercolor Series

Nettle Crocus Threshold

Nettles don’t ask permission. They shove through the leaf litter like they own the place — Sap Green so vivid it looks wrong against the brown ground. Crocus open in every south-facing bed, purple and rose. The dead leaves are still everywhere but they’ve lost. Green is winning. You can feel the year turning.

Primary Trio

Sap Green

Nettle shoot, first inch above the litter; transparent, yellow-leaning, aggressively alive against dead brown

Carbazole Violet

Crocus petal at full saturation; deeply staining, permanent the instant it touches paper — like the flower itself

Hansa Yellow Medium

Late February light finding its nerve; transparent, warm, the color of a season deciding to commit

Mixes & Ratios

Nettle Patch

2 Sap Green : 1 Nickel Azo Yellow : touch Hooker’s Green

The full depth of a nettle colony — lit tips, mid-leaf body, and deep shadow in a single layered mix.

Sap Green
Nickel Azo Yellow
Hooker’s Green
Sap Green is the workhorse here — transparent and yellow-leaning, it reads as new growth without any help. Nickel Azo Yellow (PY150) has a natural green bias that Hansa yellows lack, so it brightens the mix toward that electric chartreuse of the youngest leaves at the tip without shifting it toward lemon. Drop the Hooker’s Green into the base of the clump while the Sap Green is still damp — it sinks and darkens the shadows between stems without muddying anything. Paint from the top down: Nickel Azo at the lit tips, Sap Green for the body, Hooker’s Green pooling at the roots. Three greens, one continuous stroke per stem.

Crocus Glory

2 Carbazole Violet : 1 Quinacridone Rose

Every crocus in the south-facing bed, purple bleeding into rose — the color of a flower that bloomed before the last frost.

Carbazole Violet
Quin. Rose
Carbazole Violet (PV23) is one of the most staining pigments Daniel Smith makes — once it’s on the paper, it’s permanent. Which is exactly right for crocus: they bloom with that same determination, arriving before anything else dares. Quinacridone Rose warms the violet toward the pinkish tones visible in crocus petals catching sidelight. Both pigments are transparent, so the mix glows on white paper. Paint each petal as a single loaded stroke from tip to center, letting the two colors mingle on the paper rather than premixing fully. The natural variation is the point — some petals land more violet, some more rose, exactly like a real crocus bed.

Threshold Ground

2 Raw Umber : 1 Burnt Sienna : touch Sap Green

Winter’s last stand — dead leaves and wet soil cracking open as green pushes through from below.

Raw Umber
Burnt Sienna
Sap Green
Raw Umber granulates — it settles into the paper’s valleys and creates a pebbly texture that reads as compacted ground without any brushwork tricks. Burnt Sienna warms the mix toward the specific orange-brown of decomposing maple and alder leaves. The touch of Sap Green is the whole story: drop it into the wet brown while the earth tones are still damp, and it will bloom outward like something growing. That’s the threshold — winter brown with green forcing its way through. Don’t control the green blooms. Let them push where they want. Nettles don’t ask permission and neither should your paint.

Why These Colors?

Two greens for one plant, because nettles need it. Sap Green catches the lit side of the leaf — transparent, yellow-leaning, the color of chlorophyll in overdrive. Hooker’s Green handles the shadow side and the dense understory where stems overlap. They’re doing different jobs and no single green covers both. Carbazole Violet is the crocus, intensely staining, and it earns its spot by being as permanent on paper as crocus are in the garden — first to arrive, impossible to remove. Quinacridone Rose gives the crocus bed its range, warming the violet toward the pink tones visible in sidelight. The two earth tones — Raw Umber and Burnt Sienna — are the winter ground that hasn’t left yet: Raw Umber granulating into wet soil texture, Burnt Sienna carrying the specific warmth of dead leaves. Nickel Azo Yellow earns its place over a second Hansa by having a natural green bias (PY150) that makes new-shoot mixes more convincing. French Ultramarine is the last of winter overhead, and Buff Titanium is the overcast bright that passes for sunlight in late February. Hansa Yellow Medium ties the whole thing together — it warms the greens, lights up the crocus stamens, and gives the sky some hope.

Painting Prompts

Four subjects built for this palette and this week in the PNW — each one puts the threshold between winter and spring right on the paper.

Prompt 1

Nettle Patch by the Creek

Subject: A dense patch of stinging nettles pushing up through matted leaves at a creek bank. The nettles are six inches tall, electric green, serrated leaves catching diffused February light. Dead alder leaves everywhere, wet and flattened. The creek is a dark ribbon behind.

The Nettle Patch mix gives you the full colony in one layered application. The Threshold Ground mix is the leaf litter being punctured. French Ultramarine darkened with Hooker’s Green makes the creek water — cold, dark, indifferent to the drama on the bank.

  • Build the leaf litter floor first with the Threshold Ground mix — Raw Umber and Burnt Sienna layered wet, letting the granulation do the texture work
  • Once dry, paint nettles with the Nettle Patch mix on dry paper for crisp serrated edges — each leaf is two or three quick strokes from midrib outward
  • Youngest leaves at the tip get more Nickel Azo Yellow; older leaves below get more Hooker’s Green. Growth gets warmer as it gets newer
  • The creek behind is French Ultramarine glazed over Hooker’s Green — one horizontal wash, no detail, just cold dark water as a backdrop
Prompt 2

Crocus Bed, South-Facing

Subject: A south-facing garden bed with a dozen crocus blooming — purples and pinks, yellow stamens visible in the open ones, a few still closed. Bark mulch ground. A garden edge or low stone wall behind.

The Crocus Glory mix gives you the full purple-to-rose range across the bed. Hansa Yellow Medium drops into the open centers as stamens. Burnt Sienna is the mulch, and Buff Titanium handles the stone wall in flat winter light.

  • Paint the mulch ground first with Burnt Sienna, using dry brush on rough paper for chunky bark texture — let it dry completely
  • Each crocus is one to three strokes of the Crocus Glory mix, varying the Carbazole Violet to Quinacridone Rose ratio per flower for natural variety
  • Drop pure Hansa Yellow Medium into the center of each open bloom while the violet is still wet — the yellow arrests against the drying violet for crisp stamens
  • Closed buds are pure Carbazole Violet, darker and more concentrated — the color of determination before it opens
Prompt 3

The Threshold Line

Subject: The exact edge where dead leaves meet new growth — a horizontal composition. Brown leaf mat on the left, green nettle shoots on the right, and the ragged line between them where the season is changing. No sky, no distance. Just the ground turning over.

This is the Threshold Ground mix becoming the Nettle Patch mix across the page. Raw Umber and Burnt Sienna dominate the left half. Sap Green and Nickel Azo Yellow take the right. The middle is the war zone where both are present.

  • Work wet-in-wet across the full sheet — lay the Threshold Ground mix from the left side, the Nettle Patch mix from the right, and let them collide in the center
  • While still damp, drop individual nettle shoots into the brown side with pure Sap Green — scouts pushing ahead of the main advance
  • Scatter a few dead leaves into the green side with Burnt Sienna on dry paper — stragglers left behind
  • The threshold line itself should be ragged and organic, not straight — nature doesn’t change seasons in a clean line
Prompt 4

Early Garden Cleanup

Subject: A garden in late February — someone has started clearing winter debris. A pile of dead leaves and cut stems in the foreground, a cleared bed with crocus emerging behind, garden tools leaning against a fence. The work of meeting spring halfway.

Every color in the palette has a job here. The debris pile is Raw Umber, Burnt Sienna, and Hooker’s Green for dead stems. The cleared bed behind shows crocus in the Crocus Glory mix. Buff Titanium is the overcast sky, French Ultramarine shadows the fence.

  • Paint the debris pile loosely with Raw Umber and Burnt Sienna, dropping Hooker’s Green into wet areas for the dead stems — don’t define individual leaves, it’s a mass
  • The cleared bed behind is Buff Titanium soil with crocus in the Crocus Glory mix — small and vivid against the neutral ground
  • Garden tools are Burnt Sienna handles and French Ultramarine for metal — a few simple lines leaning at angles
  • The fence is Raw Umber dry-brushed, with French Ultramarine shadows on the north side — winter hasn’t let go of everything yet
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. Nickel Azo Yellow (PY150) is a distinctive pigment — warmer and greener than the Hansa yellows, with a natural olive bias that makes it ideal for new-growth mixes without the artificial chartreuse that cooler yellows produce. It’s semi-transparent and mixes cleanly with both greens. The two-green strategy (Sap Green for lit surfaces, Hooker’s Green for shadow) is worth practicing on a scrap sheet first — the key is loading the brush with Sap Green and touching Hooker’s Green into the base while still wet, so they blend on the paper into a single stem with natural value gradation. Carbazole Violet (PV23) is intensely staining: test it on your paper before committing, because it does not lift. Once it’s down, it’s down. This is a feature, not a bug — crocus petals have that same quality of absolute commitment. French Ultramarine granulates and separates beautifully from the earth tones in wet washes, creating natural texture in sky and shadow passages.
