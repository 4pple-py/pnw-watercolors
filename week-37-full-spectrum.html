Full Spectrum

Gold, scarlet, lemon layered on evergreen — the forest is a paint chart arguing with itself. Porcini and lobster mushrooms fruit in the understory. Elk bugling echoes off Olympic valley walls at dawn. This is the week the PNW stops being subtle. Every color at once, no apology.

Mixes & Ratios

Elk Meadow Dawn

2 Cadmium Orange Hue : 1 Payne’s Gray : touch Nickel Azo Yellow

Olympic valley at first light, fog-gold — the meadow glowing before the sun clears the ridge.

Cad. Orange Hue
Payne's Gray
Nickel Azo
Apply as a graded wash: Payne’s Gray dominant at the base, Cadmium Orange Hue increasing through the middle, Nickel Azo Yellow at the crest. The fog-gold effect comes from letting the warm and cool pigments overlap in the middle zone without premixing. Work fast on wet paper.

Lobster Flesh

1 Pyrrol Red : 1 Cadmium Orange Hue : touch Burnt Umber

Lobster mushroom cross-section — the outer rind where the color lives, thick enough to read as opaque crust.

Pyrrol Red
Cad. Orange Hue
Burnt Umber
The outer rind is where the color lives — Pyrrol Red and Cadmium Orange Hue mixed directly, applied thick enough to read as opaque crust. Leave the center as bare paper. The Burnt Umber touch darkens the outermost edge where forest dirt clings to the fungal surface.

Canopy Stack

2 Sap Green : 1 Quinacridone Gold : touch Pyrrol Red

Layered foliage, the vertical spectrum — three stories of color stacked in a single view.

Sap Green
Quin. Gold
Pyrrol Red
This is a layering exercise, not a mixing exercise. Paint the Sap Green canopy base first and let it dry completely. Glaze Quinacridone Gold over portions where light hits the mid-story. Then touch Pyrrol Red into the uppermost zone where vine maple crowns the stack. Each layer should be readable through the layers above.

Why These Colors?

Full Spectrum is the widest chromatic range in the autumn arc. Cadmium Orange Hue and Pyrrol Red provide the opaque, high-chroma punch that owns the canopy and understory respectively. Sap Green grounds the composition — the evergreen constant against which everything else is measured. Lemon Yellow and Nickel Azo Yellow handle the lightest warm notes: cottonwood and alder going pale. Burnt Umber and Transparent Red Oxide serve the mushroom passages — lobster mushroom crust and porcini cap. Indigo and Payne’s Gray provide the only cool notes, outnumbered but essential as foils. This palette is warm by a 7-to-3 margin. That’s October.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Elk Meadow at Dawn

Subject: Olympic valley at first light — fog-gold meadow backed by dark timber, elk as suggestion rather than portrait, the whole scene pressing warm against a Payne’s Gray overcast.

The Elk Meadow Dawn mix (Cadmium Orange Hue 2:Payne’s Gray 1:Nickel Azo Yellow touch) was built for this exact light — graded from cool base to warm crest, the fog burning off in paint.

  • Graded wash: Payne’s Gray at base, transitioning to Cadmium Orange Hue through the middle, Nickel Azo Yellow at the crest
  • Indigo for the dark timber wall behind the meadow — a single confident band
  • Keep elk as suggestion, not portrait: Burnt Umber silhouette, no detail
  • Payne’s Gray overcast pressing the warm light brighter by contrast
Prompt 2

Lobster Mushroom Cross-Section

Subject: A lobster mushroom sliced open — bright orange-red exterior crust giving way to white interior, forest dirt clinging to the outer surface.

The Lobster Flesh mix (Pyrrol Red 1:Cadmium Orange Hue 1:Burnt Umber touch) captures the rind at full concentration — opaque, dense, where the color lives.

  • Outer rind: Pyrrol Red and Cadmium Orange Hue at full concentration, applied thick
  • Burnt Umber where forest dirt clings to the fungal surface
  • Leave center as bare paper — the white interior needs no paint
  • Small format, botanical framing; Indigo background to push the warm crust forward
Prompt 3

Peak Foliage Canopy Stack

Subject: Vertical cross-section of mixed forest at peak color — evergreen base, gold mid-story, red crown, three layers readable as distinct but connected.

The Canopy Stack approach (Sap Green base, Quinacridone Gold mid-layer, Pyrrol Red top) is a glazing exercise — each layer readable through the layers above it.

  • Glazing exercise, not mixing — each layer must dry before the next
  • Sap Green base, Quinacridone Gold mid-layer, Pyrrol Red top
  • Each layer should be readable through the layers above
  • Lemon Yellow and Nickel Azo Yellow for the lightest notes where light penetrates the canopy
Prompt 4

Porcini in Morning Dew

Subject: A porcini cap wet with dew — mahogany dome catching early light, thick pale stem, surrounded by moss and fallen leaves.

Transparent Red Oxide is the porcini pigment — a single convex wash for the cap, with Burnt Umber for the thick stem and Sap Green for the surrounding moss.

  • Transparent Red Oxide as a single convex wash for the cap — one stroke, one dome
  • Leave small white highlights for dew drops catching light
  • Burnt Umber for the thick stem, dry-brushed for texture
  • Sap Green for surrounding moss, Quinacridone Gold for fallen leaves nearby
Notes: All pigments confirmed Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. Cadmium Orange Hue is cadmium-free — same chroma, lower toxicity, no compromise in performance. Pyrrol Red is among the most lightfast reds available — it won’t fade. Nickel Azo Yellow is unique in the palette: a greenish-yellow, transparent, excellent for the specific yellow of alder and cottonwood leaves that no other pigment captures. Payne’s Gray in Daniel Smith leans blue, which makes it a better complement to the warm-dominant palette than a neutral gray would be.
