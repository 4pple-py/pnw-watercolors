Week 37 — PNW Watercolor Series

Full Spectrum

Peak fall in the Pacific Northwest doesn’t ease in — it detonates. Every deciduous tree fires at once: vine maples go electric orange, bigleaf maples throw gold the size of dinner plates, and the cottonwoods along the rivers turn butter-yellow overnight. The evergreens stand behind all of it like a dark curtain, making every warm color louder. You can’t pick a focal point. You shouldn’t try.

Primary Trio

Pyrrol Scarlet

Blazing maple red at full chroma — the loudest note in the forest, the color you see first from a mile away through the canopy

Hansa Yellow Deep

Birch and cottonwood gold, warm and saturated; the color of whole hillsides turning at once in late October light

Transparent Pyrrol Orange

Vine maple’s signature transparent glow — backlit orange that looks like stained glass held up to the sun

Mixes & Ratios

Vine Maple Fire

2 Transparent Pyrrol Orange : 1 Pyrrol Scarlet : ¼ Alizarin Crimson

The exact color of a vine maple branch backlit by afternoon sun — orange so hot it vibrates against the evergreen dark behind it.

Trans. Pyrrol Orange
Pyrrol Scarlet
Alizarin Crimson
Transparent Pyrrol Orange is the structural color here — fully transparent, it lets the paper light pass through and bounce back, creating the backlit glow that makes vine maples look like they’re producing their own light. Pyrrol Scarlet pushes the mix toward red where leaves are turning further along, adding the scarlet bite that separates vine maple from simple orange. The touch of Alizarin Crimson at the edges deepens the color toward burgundy in the shadows without killing the transparency. Apply the orange first as a wet glaze, then touch Pyrrol Scarlet into the base of each leaf cluster while still damp. The Alizarin goes in last, only in the deepest overlap zones where leaves layer on leaves.

Cottonwood Gold

2 Hansa Yellow Deep : 1 Quinacridone Gold : ¼ Burnt Sienna

The heavy, warm gold of cottonwood leaves just before they drop — not bright yellow anymore, but rich amber-gold, backlit and trembling.

Hansa Yellow Deep
Quinacridone Gold
Burnt Sienna
Hansa Yellow Deep brings the bright, saturated gold — it’s the color cottonwoods are at their peak, before the edges start to brown. Quinacridone Gold warms and deepens that yellow toward amber, adding the richness that separates “golden” from “just yellow.” Burnt Sienna at the edges grounds the mix, adding the brown notes of leaves already beginning to dry and curl. Wet the paper first and drop Hansa Yellow Deep into the center of the canopy mass, then feed Quinacridone Gold around the edges while still wet. The two yellows will bloom into each other with soft transitions. Touch Burnt Sienna into the lowest leaves and the ground below where fallen leaves are browning.

Deep Forest Shadow

2 Hooker’s Green : 1 Carbazole Violet : ½ French Ultramarine

The near-black shadow under the evergreen canopy — the dark stage that makes every fall color louder by contrast.

Hooker’s Green
Carbazole Violet
French Ultramarine
Hooker’s Green is the workhorse evergreen — dark, cool, and opaque enough to hold its value against bright fall colors. Carbazole Violet deepens it toward true dark without the deadness of black; it adds the violet undertone you actually see in deep forest shadow on overcast days. French Ultramarine cools the mix and adds a granulating texture that reads as atmospheric depth. Mix all three on the palette to a near-black, then apply in a single confident wash behind and between the deciduous trees. The fall colors will sing against this dark. Don’t overwork it — the shadow needs to stay quiet so the spectrum in front of it can be loud.

Why These Colors?

Ten pigments for peak fall is not excess — it’s accuracy. The PNW in late October is genuinely this chromatic. Pyrrol Scarlet is the loudest red you can put on paper, and you need it because real vine maples in direct sun are louder than any pigment can match. Transparent Pyrrol Orange handles the backlit glow that makes vine maple orange look like stained glass — no opaque pigment can do this. Hansa Yellow Deep is the gold of birch and cottonwood at peak, saturated and warm. Alizarin Crimson adds the burgundy depth where red leaves overlap and shadow each other, pushing the reds cooler without losing intensity. Quinacridone Gold fills the amber zone between yellow and orange that so many fall palettes miss entirely. Hooker’s Green is the evergreen foil — the dark curtain that makes every warm color pop by simultaneous contrast. French Ultramarine gives you the clear October sky and cools any mix it touches. Burnt Sienna and Raw Umber handle the earth — leaf litter, wet bark, forest floor. Carbazole Violet is the secret weapon: mixed into greens it creates shadows so deep they read as near-black but stay colorful, and glazed over reds it pushes them toward the deep wine tones of fully turned foliage.

Painting Prompts

Peak color waits for nobody. These prompts are designed for the week when everything fires at once — grab the biggest brush you own and commit to chaos.

Prompt 1

Vine Maple Canopy — Looking Up

Subject: Standing underneath a vine maple and looking straight up through the canopy. Backlit orange and red leaves against fragments of blue October sky. Dark fir branches crossing behind. The view that makes you stop on every trail in late October.

The Vine Maple Fire mix (Transparent Pyrrol Orange + Pyrrol Scarlet + Alizarin Crimson) was built for this exact view. French Ultramarine for the sky fragments, Hooker’s Green for the fir branches crossing behind.

  • Paint the sky holes first with French Ultramarine — these are the negative shapes between leaves, irregular and scattered. Let them dry completely before painting any foliage
  • The backlit leaves are Transparent Pyrrol Orange at its most dilute in the center of each leaf, strengthening toward Pyrrol Scarlet at the edges where the leaf is thicker and less light passes through
  • Overlap leaves freely — where two orange leaves cross, the color deepens naturally because Transparent Pyrrol Orange is fully transparent. This is the whole point of choosing this pigment
  • Dark fir branches behind the vine maple are the Deep Forest Shadow mix applied with a rigger brush — they should read as near-black silhouettes, making the orange leaves glow by contrast
Prompt 2

Full Spectrum Hillside

Subject: A PNW hillside in peak fall color seen from across a valley. Bands of color: dark evergreen fir and hemlock, blazing red vine maple, golden bigleaf maple, yellow cottonwood along the creek at the bottom. The full spectrum stacked vertically.

Every primary pigment earns its place here. Pyrrol Scarlet for the maple red band, Hansa Yellow Deep for the cottonwood, Transparent Pyrrol Orange for vine maple zones, and Hooker’s Green holding the evergreen matrix together.

  • Work from the top down: Hooker’s Green for the ridgeline conifers first, then band the warm colors below while the green edge is still slightly damp so the transitions bleed naturally
  • The vine maple zone gets Transparent Pyrrol Orange dropped into damp paper — let it spread and bloom rather than trying to paint individual trees at this distance
  • Pyrrol Scarlet accents go in while the orange is still wet, creating hot spots of red that bleed into the orange exactly the way real vine maples cluster on a hillside
  • Cottonwood Gold mix along the creek bottom — Hansa Yellow Deep is bright enough to read as a distinct band even at this scale. Add Raw Umber to the creek itself for dark water contrast
Prompt 3

Fallen Leaf Mosaic — Forest Floor

Subject: A close-up of the forest floor covered in fallen leaves — bigleaf maple leaves the size of plates in every stage of color, scattered on dark wet earth. Red, orange, gold, brown, all overlapping. Rain-wet and glossy.

Quinacridone Gold for the freshest yellow leaves, Burnt Sienna for browning edges, Alizarin Crimson for the deep burgundy leaves that fell first. Raw Umber for the wet earth showing between them.

  • Lay down Raw Umber as a wet wash for the forest floor first — this is the dark ground that shows between and beneath the leaves, and it needs to be in place before any leaf shapes go down
  • Paint individual leaves as simple flat shapes: Quinacridone Gold for the brightest, Transparent Pyrrol Orange for mid-turn, Alizarin Crimson for the darkest. Vary the value by varying the water ratio
  • Wet leaves are glossy — leave a thin white highlight line along one edge of each leaf to suggest the wet sheen catching light from above
  • Where leaves overlap, the underneath leaf should be darker (more Burnt Sienna) and the top leaf brighter — this layering creates the mosaic depth that makes forest-floor studies compelling
Prompt 4

Country Road Through Fall Color

Subject: A single-lane road disappearing into a tunnel of fall color — vine maples arching overhead in orange and red, bigleaf maples dropping gold, dark firs visible through gaps. Wet pavement reflecting the colors above. A classic PNW back-road scene.

The full palette fires here: Vine Maple Fire mix overhead, Cottonwood Gold at the margins, Deep Forest Shadow behind the deciduous trees. Carbazole Violet mixed into Raw Umber for the wet road surface reflecting warm leaf color.

  • The road is the compositional spine — sketch it first as a simple perspective wedge narrowing to a vanishing point. Use Carbazole Violet + Raw Umber for the wet asphalt, keeping it darker than you think
  • The canopy overhead is the Vine Maple Fire mix applied loosely and wet — let Transparent Pyrrol Orange and Pyrrol Scarlet intermingle on the paper without overcontrolling the edges
  • Reflections on wet pavement: while the road wash is still damp, drop dilute versions of the overhead colors into the road surface. The reflections should be muted and broken, not mirror-sharp
  • Hansa Yellow Deep for scattered fallen leaves on the road surface — small bright marks that lead the eye down the road toward the vanishing point
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Pyrrol Scarlet (PR255) is among the most intense reds available — semi-transparent with extraordinary chroma. Transparent Pyrrol Orange (PO71) is fully transparent and staining, ideal for glazing backlit subjects. Hansa Yellow Deep (PY65) is a warm, strong yellow with good lightfastness. Alizarin Crimson (PR83) provides deep cool red with moderate transparency. Quinacridone Gold (PO49) is Daniel Smith’s beloved amber-gold, now reformulated. Carbazole Violet (PV23) is an intensely dark violet useful for mixing near-blacks that stay chromatic.
