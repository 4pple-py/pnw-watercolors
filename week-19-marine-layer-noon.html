Week 19 — PNW Watercolor Series

Marine Layer Noon

At eight the fog is a wall — Buff Titanium from beach to sky, the ocean erased. By ten, Cerulean Blue cracks through like a bruise healing in reverse. At noon the curtain pulls offshore and suddenly everything is Phthalo Blue water and New Gamboge sun. Two hours from colorless to saturated. May on the PNW coast turns muted into bright while you’re still holding your coffee.

Mixes & Ratios

Fog Wall

3 Buff Titanium : 2 Cerulean Blue : 1 Titanium White

Eight a.m. fog — opaque, featureless, thick enough to lose the ocean in. Not atmospheric haze. A wall.

Buff Titanium
Cerulean Blue
Titanium White
Three opaque pigments doing what opaque pigments do best: blocking light. Buff Titanium dominates because early marine layer fog isn’t blue — it’s the color of nothing, warm and flat. Cerulean Blue provides the faintest cool shift that keeps it from reading as beige. Titanium White (not Chinese White — you need real opacity here) thickens the mix to the point where it sits on the paper like fog sits on the coast: heavy, immovable, with zero transparency. Apply in a single confident wash. Fog doesn’t have brushstrokes.

Ocean Reveal

2 Phthalo Blue GS : 1 Cobalt Blue : ½ Cascade Green

The ocean that was there all along — suddenly visible, staining blue-green, deeper than you remembered.

Phthalo Blue GS
Cobalt Blue
Cascade Green
Phthalo Blue GS is a stainer — once it touches paper, it lives there. That permanence is the point. The ocean doesn’t politely appear; it announces itself. Cobalt Blue warms and granulates the mix slightly, keeping it from going clinical. Cascade Green is the half-part that makes this read as Pacific Northwest water rather than Caribbean — cold, kelp-fed, serious. Lay it into dry paper beside the Fog Wall mix with a hard edge between them. That hard edge is the fog line retreating.

Burn-Off Gold

2 New Gamboge : 1 Yellow Ochre : ½ Raw Sienna

The first warm light after the fog retreats — everything gilded, sand drying, grass going gold.

New Gamboge
Yellow Ochre
Raw Sienna
New Gamboge is transparent, so this mix glows rather than covers — the opposite of the Fog Wall in every way. Yellow Ochre grounds the gold in earth, the sand and dry grass that the sun hits first. Raw Sienna adds depth and a red-lean that keeps the mix from reading as lemon. Glaze this over the dried Fog Wall mix to create the transition moment: warm transparent light arriving on top of cool opaque fog. The layering sequence is the whole narrative of this palette.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built around a phase change. The fog half — Cerulean Blue, Buff Titanium, Titanium White, Payne’s Gray — is opaque, granulating, cool. Pigments that sit on the paper and block light, the way a marine layer sits on the coast and blocks the sun. The reveal half — New Gamboge, Phthalo Blue GS, Cobalt Blue — is transparent and staining, pigments that glow through layers and announce themselves permanently. Raw Sienna, Yellow Ochre, and Cascade Green are the constants: the bluff, the grass, the shore pine that exist in both conditions but change temperature when the light shifts. The whole story of a May morning on the Oregon coast — muted to saturated in two hours — is encoded in the opacity and transparency of ten pigments.

Painting Prompts

Four subjects built for this week’s transition palette — each one asks you to paint the shift from hidden to revealed, muted to saturated, fog to sun.

Prompt 1

Fog Wall From the Beach

Subject: Standing on wet sand at eight a.m., facing a fog wall so thick the ocean is gone. Driftwood at your feet. The world ends at fifty feet.

The Fog Wall mix (Buff Titanium + Cerulean Blue + Titanium White) is this painting — three opaque pigments making a wall of nothing. Payne’s Gray for the driftwood. Raw Sienna for the sand underfoot.

  • Wet the top two-thirds of the paper and apply the Fog Wall mix in a single horizontal wash — no second passes, no fussing. Fog is featureless; your brush should be too
  • While the fog wash is still damp, touch concentrated Payne’s Gray at the bottom edge for driftwood shapes dissolving into the mist — lost edges only, nothing sharp
  • The sand is dry-brushed Raw Sienna and Yellow Ochre on the lower third, the only textured area in the painting
  • Leave the fog wash completely alone while it dries. Cerulean Blue’s granulation will separate on its own, creating subtle fog texture without your help
Prompt 2

The Moment the Fog Lifts

Subject: Ten a.m., the marine layer cracking open. A band of Cobalt Blue sky visible for the first time. Below it, the fog is still thick. The horizon line appears like a secret.

This is the transition painting. Cobalt Blue sky above, the Fog Wall mix below, and a sliver of Phthalo Blue GS ocean between them where the horizon reappears.

  • Paint the sky crack first: a strip of Cobalt Blue at the top, grading down to Cerulean Blue where the fog begins — the shift from transparent to granulating blue tells the story
  • Leave a thin line of white paper for the horizon, then paint a narrow band of Phthalo Blue GS below it — the ocean appearing for the first time
  • The remaining lower half is the Fog Wall mix, still thick, still opaque, but now with a visible bottom edge for the first time
  • A single stroke of New Gamboge at the top edge of the sky crack suggests the sun that’s doing the work of burning this fog off
Prompt 3

Coastal Bluff at Noon

Subject: The fog is gone. Full sun on a coastal bluff — dry grass, shore pine, sandstone edge, the Pacific spread out below in deep blue. Everything saturated.

The reveal half of the palette takes over. New Gamboge and the Burn-Off Gold mix for sun-baked grass. Phthalo Blue GS ocean. Cascade Green shore pine. Raw Sienna sandstone. No fog pigments at all — the gray morning is a memory.

  • Start with the ocean: a flat wash of the Ocean Reveal mix (Phthalo Blue GS + Cobalt Blue + Cascade Green) across the lower half, darkening toward the horizon
  • The bluff edge is Raw Sienna and Yellow Ochre dry-brushed onto dry paper — rough, crumbling sandstone catching full sun
  • Shore pine silhouettes are Cascade Green with a touch of Payne’s Gray, wind-shaped and leaning inland
  • Glaze the Burn-Off Gold mix over the dry grass areas — the transparency of New Gamboge makes the grass glow rather than just sit there
Prompt 4

Tide Pools Revealed by Clearing

Subject: Rocky intertidal zone emerging from the fog — tide pools suddenly visible, dark wet basalt, anemones and sea stars in cold water. The last wisps of fog trailing over the rocks.

Payne’s Gray for wet basalt. Phthalo Blue GS and Cascade Green for tide pool water. The last Fog Wall mix wisps trailing across the top. New Gamboge for an ochre sea star catching the first sun.

  • Paint the rocks first with Payne’s Gray at full concentration — wet basalt is nearly black. Leave small pools of white paper between rock shapes for the tide pools
  • Fill the tide pools with dilute Phthalo Blue GS tinted with Cascade Green — the staining quality makes them look deep even at small scale
  • A sea star is a small shape of New Gamboge with a touch of Raw Sienna — the first warm color in the painting, catching the arriving sun
  • Drag dilute Buff Titanium and Titanium White across the top of the dried composition for the last fog wisps retreating — opaque pigment over dark rocks reads as fog over landscape
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Cerulean Blue (PB35) granulates heavily — it settles into paper texture in a way that perfectly mimics breaking fog. Use Titanium White (PW6) rather than Chinese White (PW4) for fog passages; Chinese White is too transparent to create genuine opacity. Phthalo Blue GS (PB15:3) is a powerful stainer — once it touches paper, it’s permanent. That staining quality is exactly what you want for the ocean reveal: the sea doesn’t arrive tentatively. Layer sequence matters in this palette: opaque fog pigments first, transparent sun pigments glazed on top. Reversing the order defeats the physics.
