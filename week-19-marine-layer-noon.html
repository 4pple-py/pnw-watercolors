Marine Layer Noon

Morning fog sits like a lid. Swainson's thrushes spiral their song upward through salal. By noon the marine layer burns off and everything underneath is suddenly, violently green. The tension between concealment and reveal — fog as curtain, sun as stagehand. The same landscape twice in one day.

Primary Trio

Hansa Yellow Medium

Noon light hitting wet leaves, the shock of color after gray; transparent, clean, medium-warm

Alizarin Crimson

Salal flower interior, urn-shaped and secret; transparent, cool-leaning red that mixes violet easily

Cerulean Blue Chromium

Marine layer breaking apart, chalky and granulating; opaque, it sits on the paper like fog sits on the land

Mixes & Ratios

Fog Lift

3 Cerulean Blue Chromium : 2 Buff Titanium : 1 Payne's Gray

Marine layer thinning, light pushing through — the moment you see blue for the first time since yesterday.

Cerulean Blue Chr.
Buff Titanium
Payne's Gray
This is fog becoming sky. Cerulean Blue Chromium's granulation is the texture of breaking cloud — heavy particles settling into paper valleys while light areas read as openings. Buff Titanium is the milky diffusion that isn't quite cloud and isn't quite clear. Payne's Gray holds the underside of what's left. Apply to wet paper and tilt the board; let gravity separate the pigments the way wind separates fog.

Salal Thicket

2 Sap Green : 1 Neutral Tint : ½ Alizarin Crimson

Dense shrub mass with urn-shaped flowers barely visible — the green wall that lines every PNW trail.

Sap Green
Neutral Tint
Alizarin Crimson
Sap Green alone is too cheerful for salal. Neutral Tint darkens it without adding any hue — the salal interior is genuinely lightless. Alizarin Crimson is the surprise: half a part shifts the green subtly warm and accounts for the tiny urn-flowers hiding inside the hedge. Build in layers. The first wash is the surface; the second is the depth; the third is where the thrushes live.

Noon Scald

2 Hansa Yellow Medium : 1 Raw Sienna : ½ Aussie Red Gold

The shock of full sun after a week of gray — sudden warmth on everything, almost aggressive.

Hansa Yellow Med
Raw Sienna
Aussie Red Gold
This mix is about the temperature change. Hansa Yellow Medium is the light itself — transparent and clean. Raw Sienna grounds it in earth, the trail drying in real time. Aussie Red Gold adds the madrone bark warmth, the first tree to glow when the fog lifts. Glaze this over dried cool washes (Payne's Gray, Cerulean) to create the sensation of sun arriving. The layering tells the story.

Why These Colors?

Two palettes in one: the fog half and the sun half. Cerulean Blue Chromium, Payne's Gray, and Buff Titanium handle the marine layer — all opaque or semi-opaque, all cool, all built to sit on the paper like weather. Hansa Yellow Medium, Raw Sienna, and Aussie Red Gold handle the reveal — all transparent or semi-transparent, all warm, all designed to glow through and over the fog-half's washes. Sap Green and Neutral Tint are the constants, the salal that exists in both conditions. Alizarin Crimson and Cobalt Teal Blue are the hidden details you only notice when the fog is gone.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

The Same View, Two Hours Apart

Subject: A diptych (or a single painting split by a soft edge): the same coastal scene in morning fog, then in noon sun. Everything changes except the salal.

This palette was literally designed as two palettes in one — fog half and sun half. Cerulean Blue Chromium, Payne’s Gray, and Buff Titanium for the left panel; Hansa Yellow Medium, Raw Sienna, and Aussie Red Gold for the right.

  • Paint the fog version first: wet the left half and drop in the Fog Lift mix (Cerulean Blue Chromium + Buff Titanium + Payne’s Gray), letting it granulate undisturbed
  • The salal hedge runs across both halves — paint it continuously with the Salal Thicket mix so it connects the two moments
  • For the sun version on the right, let the fog half dry completely, then glaze the Noon Scald mix (Hansa Yellow Medium + Raw Sienna + Aussie Red Gold) over the right side
  • The transition between the two halves can be a soft wet-into-wet bleed — fog doesn’t end at a hard line
Prompt 2

Salal Thicket With Hidden Flowers

Subject: A dense wall of salal along a trail, dark and impenetrable, with tiny urn-shaped flowers barely visible if you look carefully.

The Salal Thicket mix (Sap Green + Neutral Tint + Alizarin Crimson) handles the whole shrub mass. The Alizarin Crimson does double duty as the flowers hiding inside.

  • Build the salal in three layers, drying between each: first wash of Sap Green, second wash of Sap Green + Neutral Tint in the interior, third layer of concentrated Neutral Tint in the deepest recesses
  • The urn flowers are tiny touches of Alizarin Crimson applied with a rigger brush into the still-damp second layer — they should blur slightly, like something you’re not quite sure you saw
  • Leave the top edge of the salal hedge crisp against the sky or fog to give the dense mass a readable silhouette
  • A few leaves at the foreground edge can be individual Sap Green strokes on dry paper, giving scale to the mass behind
Prompt 3

Tide Pool Revealed

Subject: A rocky tide pool emerging as the fog burns off — Cobalt Teal Blue water, dark wet rocks, maybe an anemone or sea star catching the first sun.

Cobalt Teal Blue is the metallic, startling color of a tide pool that’s been hidden under fog all morning. Neutral Tint handles the wet basalt. Aussie Red Gold gives you the ochre sea star.

  • Paint the rocks first with Neutral Tint at varying concentrations — wet rocks are darker than dry rocks, so keep the ones closest to the water at full strength
  • The tide pool water is Cobalt Teal Blue applied in a single flat stroke to dry paper — its opacity gives the water that opaque, mineral-rich look
  • A sea star is a small star shape of Aussie Red Gold — its slight granulation on cold-pressed paper mimics the bumpy texture of the animal
  • Add wisps of the Fog Lift mix at the top of the composition for the last marine layer burning off above
Prompt 4

Madrone Trunk in New Sun

Subject: A madrone tree trunk catching the first noon sun after fog lifts — warm bark suddenly glowing against the cool green of everything else.

Aussie Red Gold is this exact tree in this exact moment. Glazing the Noon Scald mix over a cool Payne’s Gray underpainting creates the sensation of sun arriving on bark.

  • Underpaint the trunk with dilute Payne’s Gray and let it dry — this is the fog-morning version of the bark
  • Glaze Aussie Red Gold over the dried Payne’s Gray on the sun-facing side; the cool underpainting glowing through the warm glaze is the whole effect
  • The background salal stays in the Salal Thicket mix, still cool, still in shade — the madrone is the only warm thing in the frame
  • Use dry brush with Raw Sienna for the peeling bark edges where the outer layer curls away from the smooth trunk beneath
Prompt 5

Swainson’s Thrush (Heard But Not Seen)

Subject: A dense salal and fern understory in fog, the spiral song of a Swainson’s thrush implied by the composition’s upward energy but the bird completely invisible.

This is a painting about absence and atmosphere. Payne’s Gray fog, Sap Green salal, Neutral Tint shadows — the cool half of the palette doing quiet, patient work.

  • Wet the entire paper and lay in Payne’s Gray at the top grading to Buff Titanium at mid-height for the fog
  • While still damp, touch in Sap Green salal shapes at the bottom third, letting the edges blur upward into the fog
  • Add Neutral Tint vertically for tree trunks dissolving into the mist — lost edges everywhere, nothing fully resolved
  • The thrush is absent from the painting. That’s the point. The fog and the composition imply the upward spiral of its song without showing the bird. Sometimes the best subject is the one you leave out.
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Neutral Tint is a convenience mix — it's designed to darken without shifting hue, making it ideal for value adjustments in green mixes. Cerulean Blue Chromium is genuinely opaque; it will cover underlying washes rather than glazing over them, so sequence your layers accordingly. Aussie Red Gold is a uniquely warm, slightly granulating pigment that feels like it was made for madrone bark.
