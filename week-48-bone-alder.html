Week 48 — PNW Watercolor Series

Bone Alder

The alders have dropped everything. What’s left is bone — white branches against flat gray, the whole tree reduced to its architecture. December strips away the pretense. These trees were green once, then gold, then bare, and now they’re beautiful in the way that skeletons are beautiful: honest, structural, done pretending. Lichen on the north side. Wet bark going dark on the rain side. The palette this week is the palette of restraint.

Primary Trio

Titanium White

Bone-white alder bark in flat December light; opaque, chalky, the color of trees that have given up everything but structure

Raw Umber

Minimal palette, bark shadow; the muted brown of alder bark’s shadow side, dry and understated, no warmth to spare

Payne’s Gray

Winter sky, branch shadow; the blue-gray of December air, flat and still, neither threatening nor inviting

Mixes & Ratios

Bone Bark

3 Titanium White : 1 Buff Titanium : ¼ Raw Umber

The color of alder bark in December — not white, not cream, but bone. The color of something that was alive and is now architecture.

Titanium White
Buff Titanium
Raw Umber
Titanium White provides the opaque, chalky base that reads as actual bark rather than paper-white. Pure white paper is too clean for alder — it needs the slight density of pigment to feel like a surface. Buff Titanium warms it just enough to cross from sterile to organic, the difference between plaster and bone. Raw Umber at the edges and in the shadow side gives the bone-color somewhere to transition into shadow without an abrupt value shift. Mix on the palette, not on paper — this needs to be consistent across the trunk. Apply with a flat brush in confident vertical strokes, leaving deliberate dry-brush gaps where the paper shows through to suggest bark texture.

December Flat

2 Neutral Tint : 1 Payne’s Gray : ¼ French Ultramarine

The sky that doesn’t commit to anything — not rain, not clearing, just flat gray with a cold blue undertow.

Neutral Tint
Payne’s Gray
French Ultramarine
Neutral Tint is the definition of non-committal gray — it has no temperature lean, no personality, which is exactly what a December overcast sky looks like. Payne’s Gray pushes a subtle blue-gray undercurrent into the mix, because even the flattest PNW sky has a blue thread running through it. French Ultramarine is the secret: a touch of it in the upper sky gives just enough color to prevent the wash from looking dead on paper. Apply as a single graded wash on wet paper — more Neutral Tint near the horizon where the sky is lightest, more Payne’s Gray overhead. The French Ultramarine goes only into the top quarter. One pass. Don’t rework it.

Wet Side

2 Lunar Black : 1 Burnt Umber : ½ Raw Umber

The rain-soaked side of an alder trunk — dark, granular, alive with texture where the dry side is smooth bone.

Lunar Black
Burnt Umber
Raw Umber
Lunar Black is a PrimaTek pigment — ground mineral stone that granulates dramatically on paper, separating into dark particles that settle into the paper’s valleys. This granulation is the entire point: it mimics the way wet bark looks rough and textured compared to the smooth dry side. Burnt Umber warms the dark and adds depth — wet alder bark isn’t cold black but a deep warm brown-black. Raw Umber transitions the wet side toward the Bone Bark mix on the dry side. Apply Lunar Black to thoroughly wet paper on the shadow/rain side of the trunk, immediately charge Burnt Umber into the darkest zones, and let Raw Umber bridge to the Bone Bark mix on the lit side. Tilt the board and let gravity pull the granulating particles downward.

Why These Colors?

Restraint is the point. This palette has three whites and near-whites (Titanium White, Chinese White, Buff Titanium) because alder bark in December is a study in subtle variation within a narrow range — the difference between cool white and warm white, between opaque bone and translucent lichen, is the entire subject. Raw Umber and Burnt Umber handle the earth tones with minimal drama: dry shadow and wet ground, nothing more. Payne’s Gray and Neutral Tint flatten the sky the way December flattens everything — no storms, no clearing, just gray. French Ultramarine is used sparingly, a cold blue accent that reminds you the sky is still technically up there behind the overcast. Potter’s Pink is the most restrained accent in the series: a whisper of pink in alder bark that you might not even notice unless you’re looking. Lunar Black earns its place through behavior — its dramatic granulation on wet paper creates bark texture that no amount of brushwork can replicate. This is a minimal palette for a minimal subject. Ten colors, but the painting might use five.

Painting Prompts

Less is the assignment. These subjects ask you to do more with less — to find the painting in value shifts and texture rather than color. The alder is already beautiful. Your job is to not get in the way.

Prompt 1

Single Alder Against Gray Sky

Subject: One bare alder tree, full height, white bark against flat overcast sky. Every branch visible, the architecture of the tree laid bare. No leaves, no distractions. The trunk slightly off-center, branches reaching asymmetrically.

The Bone Bark mix (Titanium White + Buff Titanium + Raw Umber) for the trunk and main branches. December Flat mix (Neutral Tint + Payne’s Gray + French Ultramarine) for the sky. Lunar Black for the rain-facing side of the trunk.

  • Paint the sky first as a flat wash of the December Flat mix, leaving the trunk and major branches as negative shapes — the white of the tree is defined by what surrounds it
  • Once the sky is dry, apply the Bone Bark mix to the trunk with a flat brush, using dry-brush technique to leave texture gaps that suggest bark roughness
  • The rain side of the trunk gets the Wet Side mix (Lunar Black + Burnt Umber + Raw Umber) while the Bone Bark is still damp — let the two mixes bleed at the boundary
  • Smallest branches are Payne’s Gray applied with a rigger brush — they should be barely darker than the sky, almost disappearing at the tips
Prompt 2

Alder Grove — Branch Tangle

Subject: Looking up through a stand of alders, bare branches crossing and overlapping against the flat sky. A tangle of white lines on gray. The composition is the pattern of branches, not any single tree.

Payne’s Gray for the sky showing through. Titanium White and Buff Titanium for the branches (painted opaque on top of the sky wash). Potter’s Pink as a barely-visible tint on the youngest branches.

  • Paint the entire page as a flat December Flat sky wash first — no gaps, no negative painting. The branches will go on top, opaque
  • The Bone Bark mix applied opaquely over the dry sky wash is what makes this work — Titanium White has the covering power to read as white branches against gray
  • Vary the branch thickness dramatically: main trunks at the edges of the frame with a flat brush, smallest twigs with a rigger. The range of line weight creates the sense of depth
  • Touch Potter’s Pink into the youngest, thinnest branches while still wet — young alder bark has a subtle pink that disappears as it ages to bone-white
Prompt 3

Alder Bark Close-Up — Lichen Study

Subject: Tight crop on a section of alder trunk, filling the frame. The north side has lichen patches — pale green-white against bone-white bark. Rain stains run vertically. A minimal, almost abstract composition.

Bone Bark mix for the main bark surface. Chinese White for the lichen — its slight translucency against the opaque Titanium White base creates a visible difference. Lunar Black for rain stains. Raw Umber for shadow.

  • The bark surface is the Bone Bark mix applied evenly as a base, with dry-brush texture — this is the canvas on which everything else happens
  • Lichen patches are Chinese White with the barest touch of Buff Titanium, dabbed on with a sponge or rough brush — the slight green-gray of Chinese White against the warmer Titanium White base creates the color difference
  • Rain stains are vertical streaks of very dilute Lunar Black, applied to damp paper so they soften at the edges — let the granulation create the stain texture
  • The shadow side of the trunk is Raw Umber in a thin glaze, graded from the edge — this is a cylinder, and the value shift from light to shadow describes its roundness
Prompt 4

Alder-Lined Creek in December

Subject: A narrow PNW creek with bare alders lining both banks, their white trunks reflected in dark water. Burnt umber mud banks, fallen leaves in the water. The whole scene in a tight value range — white, gray, brown, dark.

The full range of this palette in one scene: Bone Bark for the trunks, December Flat for the sky, Wet Side mix for the dark water reflections, Burnt Umber for the mud banks. Potter’s Pink tinting the reflected trunks.

  • The water is the darkest element — a mix of Payne’s Gray and Burnt Umber, painted first as a dark horizontal band through the center of the composition
  • Reflected trunks in the water are a dilute Bone Bark mix painted into the still-damp water wash — they should be softer and slightly darker than the actual trunks above
  • Mud banks are Burnt Umber at full strength, with Raw Umber at the water’s edge where wet mud is lighter — the value shift tells you where the bank meets the water
  • Touch Potter’s Pink into the water reflections of the nearest trunks — water reflections pick up subtle colors that the eye misses when looking at the tree directly
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Titanium White (PW6) is fully opaque and can be used to paint light subjects over dark backgrounds — essential for the bone-white bark technique. Chinese White (PW4) is semi-transparent zinc white, useful for lichen and translucent light effects. Lunar Black is a PrimaTek pigment — ground mineral stone — with dramatic granulation that separates into visible particles on wet paper. Potter’s Pink (PR233) is a subtle, slightly granulating pink that reads as warmth rather than color. Neutral Tint is a convenience mix designed to darken without shifting color temperature.
