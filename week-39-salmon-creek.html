Week 39 — PNW Watercolor Series

Salmon Creek

The creek runs red in October. Chum and coho push upstream through water barely deep enough to cover their backs, flanks splitting open with color — crimson, rust, a gold that looks forged rather than grown. They die in the same gravel where they were born. The forest closes in overhead, dark and tangled, and the water carries the smell of something finishing and something starting at the same time.

Primary Trio

Deep Scarlet

The intense crimson of a fully spawning salmon, deep and concentrated; a staining red that builds to almost unbearable saturation

Perylene Green

Dark forest corridor closing over the creek; deep, cool, almost black-green in shadow, the canopy the salmon swim beneath

Aussie Red Gold

The distinctive red-gold shimmer of salmon skin catching light through shallow water; warm, granulating, unique to Daniel Smith

Mixes & Ratios

Spawning Crimson

2 Deep Scarlet : 1 Aussie Red Gold : ¼ Transparent Red Oxide

The impossible red of a salmon at peak spawn — crimson so saturated it looks arterial, shot through with gold.

Deep Scarlet
Aussie Red Gold
Trans. Red Oxide
Deep Scarlet is the structural red — a staining pyrrole pigment that delivers the saturated crimson visible on a spawning salmon’s flank from twenty feet away. Aussie Red Gold brings the metallic warmth, the shimmer that makes salmon color different from any other red in nature. Its granulation gives the mix a textured quality that reads as scales catching light. Transparent Red Oxide at the edges darkens and oxidizes the red where the fish’s color is breaking down, the transition from living red to dying rust. Wet the paper first, drop Deep Scarlet into the body, then touch Aussie Red Gold along the dorsal line and let it bloom downward. The granulation will separate naturally.

Creek Corridor

2 Perylene Green : 1 Sap Green : ½ Neutral Tint

The dark, tangled tunnel of alder and cedar that closes over a salmon creek — green so dense it swallows light.

Perylene Green
Sap Green
Neutral Tint
Perylene Green is one of Daniel Smith’s darkest greens — almost black at full strength, but with an unmistakable green undertone that keeps it reading as forest rather than void. Sap Green lightens the canopy where light barely filters through, providing the mid-value green that makes the darker areas feel like depth rather than paint. Neutral Tint in the deepest recesses pushes the shadows toward colorless darkness, the way real creek corridors go essentially black under overhanging salmonberry and devil’s club. Build from dark to light: lay Perylene Green across the full canopy area, then lift out lighter passages with a thirsty brush while still damp. Touch Neutral Tint into the deepest shadow pockets last.

Gravel Bed

2 Sedona Genuine : 1 Burnt Umber : ¼ French Ultramarine

Creek bottom where eggs are laid — mineral gravel seen through cold, clear water, granulation doing the work of a thousand stones.

Sedona Genuine
Burnt Umber
French Ultramarine
Sedona Genuine is ground mineral stone — actual red rock from Sedona — and it granulates on paper the way real gravel settles on a creek bed. You cannot replicate this with synthetic pigments. The particles separate and cluster, creating texture that reads as individual stones without painting a single one. Burnt Umber darkens the spaces between stones, the organic sediment that accumulates in the gaps. French Ultramarine is the cold water itself — a whisper of it over the entire mix shifts the gravel behind a veil of creek water, pushing it visually underwater. Apply Sedona Genuine to very wet paper and tilt the board to let the mineral particles migrate. Once dry, glaze the thinnest possible wash of French Ultramarine over the top to put the gravel under water.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built around the collision of red and green that defines a salmon creek in October — the most saturated reds in nature pushing upstream through the darkest greens. Deep Scarlet delivers the intense crimson of peak-spawn fish, the red that stops you in your tracks. Aussie Red Gold is the pigment that makes this palette different from any other red-green combination — its unique red-gold shimmer and granulation capture the metallic quality of salmon skin that no other pigment can match. Perylene Green closes the forest corridor over the creek, dark enough to make the reds burn by contrast. Transparent Red Oxide handles the dying fish, the ones going rust and falling apart at the edges. Sap Green softens the banks with moss and fern. Burnt Umber grounds everything in the creek bottom. French Ultramarine is the cold water itself, the element the salmon are fighting through. Neutral Tint deepens the shadows under cut banks where fish rest. Quinacridone Burnt Orange catches the lateral stripe, that copper flash along the flank. And Sedona Genuine — ground mineral stone — gives you the gravel redd where the whole cycle begins and ends, its granulation building creek-bottom texture that no synthetic pigment can replicate.

Painting Prompts

These subjects lean into the drama of the spawn — red against green, life against death, the ancient cycle playing out in creeks you can walk to from the trailhead.

Prompt 1

Spawning Salmon — Shallow Riffle

Subject: Two or three salmon pushing through ankle-deep water over a gravel riffle, backs breaking the surface. Their bodies are crimson and gold, the water splits around them in white Vs. Dark forest walls on both sides. Viewed from above, looking down into the creek.

The Spawning Crimson mix (Deep Scarlet + Aussie Red Gold + Transparent Red Oxide) defines each fish. The Gravel Bed mix (Sedona Genuine + Burnt Umber + French Ultramarine) builds the creek bottom they’re swimming over. Perylene Green walls the scene in on both sides.

  • Paint the gravel bed first with the Sedona Genuine mix on very wet paper — tilt and let the mineral particles settle, then dry completely before adding anything else
  • Glaze dilute French Ultramarine over the entire creek area to put the gravel underwater — leave dry-paper gaps where the salmon’s backs break the surface
  • Build each fish with the Spawning Crimson mix: Deep Scarlet for the body mass, Aussie Red Gold along the dorsal ridge where light catches scales, Transparent Red Oxide at the tail and gill edges where color is breaking down
  • The white V-shaped wakes around each fish are preserved white paper — plan these carefully before painting the water, using masking fluid or careful negative painting
Prompt 2

Creek Corridor — Looking Upstream

Subject: The view looking upstream into a narrow salmon creek, alder and cedar closing in overhead to form a green tunnel. Dappled light hits the water in patches. A single red shape — a salmon — is visible in the middle distance. The creek bends and disappears into darkness.

The Creek Corridor mix (Perylene Green + Sap Green + Neutral Tint) builds the canopy tunnel. Deep Scarlet makes the distant salmon a single red note against overwhelming green. Burnt Umber for the creek banks and fallen logs.

  • Establish the canopy tunnel first with Perylene Green at full strength along both sides, grading to Sap Green where light filters through — the tunnel shape is the composition
  • The creek surface is French Ultramarine diluted heavily, with patches of white paper left for reflected sky — these light patches on water are what create the sense of dappled light
  • The salmon in the middle distance is a single stroke of Deep Scarlet, small but saturated — it should be the most chromatic thing in the painting and draw the eye immediately
  • Push Neutral Tint into the deepest background where the creek bends out of sight — let the scene dissolve into formless dark, suggesting the creek continues beyond what you can see
Prompt 3

Salmon Carcass — Creek Bank

Subject: A spent salmon carcass on the mossy creek bank, half in and half out of the water. The body is breaking down — pale, oxidized, returning to the earth. Moss and fallen leaves surround it. The creek runs past, still carrying the living.

Transparent Red Oxide and Quinacridone Burnt Orange for the oxidized, fading body. Sap Green for the mossy bank. Burnt Umber for decomposing flesh meeting earth. This is the death side of the palette — the warm accents rather than the vivid primaries.

  • The carcass is primarily Transparent Red Oxide glazed thinly — the fish has lost its vivid red, so keep the pigment dilute and let the paper show through as pale, spent flesh
  • Quinacridone Burnt Orange along the lateral line and fins, where the last traces of living color remain — touch it in sparingly, this fish is mostly done
  • The mossy bank is Sap Green with Burnt Umber worked in wet-in-wet — the organic decay of the creek bank, soft and decomposing itself
  • The creek water running past the carcass gets a thin wash of French Ultramarine — the cold indifference of the water continuing while the fish has stopped
Prompt 4

Gravel Redd — Eggs in the Stones

Subject: An underwater view of a salmon redd — the cleared gravel nest where eggs glow orange among dark stones. Water flows over the top, bending light. The creek bottom fills the frame, intimate and close. New life hidden in the streambed.

The Gravel Bed mix (Sedona Genuine + Burnt Umber + French Ultramarine) is the foundation. Aussie Red Gold for the eggs themselves — small dots of concentrated warmth among cool, dark stone. Neutral Tint for the shadows between cobbles.

  • Lay down Sedona Genuine on saturated paper and let it granulate freely — the mineral particles will build the gravel texture. Tilt the board and let gravity do the detail work
  • While the Sedona wash is still damp, drop Burnt Umber between the stones and Neutral Tint into the deepest gaps — these shadows define the three-dimensional space between cobbles
  • The eggs are small dots of concentrated Aussie Red Gold, placed after the gravel is fully dry — their orange glow should read as the warmest, brightest elements in the painting
  • Glaze the entire surface with very thin French Ultramarine once everything is dry to unify the scene underwater — the water layer should barely be visible, just enough to shift the color temperature cool
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Deep Scarlet (PR175) is a deeply staining pyrrole red with exceptional lightfastness and intensity. Aussie Red Gold (PO73) is unique to Daniel Smith — a red-gold pigment that granulates and has no equivalent from other manufacturers. Perylene Green (PBk31) is one of the darkest greens available, nearly black at full strength but unmistakably green in dilution. Sedona Genuine is a PrimaTek pigment — ground red rock from Sedona, Arizona — and its mineral granulation creates creek-bed texture that cannot be replicated by mixing synthetic pigments.
