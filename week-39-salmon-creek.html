Salmon Creek

Dinner-plate maple leaves plaster every surface. Spawning salmon turn creeks into triptychs — red flanks, green heads, white fungal patches on the dying. Matsutake hide under shore pines, their smell sharp as cinnamon and dirt. Peak leaf fall means the forest is giving everything back.

Mixes & Ratios

Spawning Triptych

1 Cadmium Red Medium Hue : 1 Hooker’s Green : touch Chinese White

Three colors of a salmon creek — red flank, jade head, white fungal patch, all on one body.

Cad. Red Med. Hue
Hooker's Green
Chinese White
Three zones on wet paper. Let the red and green meet at the gill plate without pre-mixing — the boundary should be ragged and organic. The Chinese White should blob irregularly for fungal patches. Decay is not symmetrical. These are not stripes; they are territories.

Matsutake Earth

2 Raw Sienna : 1 Olive Green : touch Burnt Umber

Pine duff hiding the mushroom — the duff is the subject, the mushroom is negative space.

Raw Sienna
Olive Green
Burnt Umber
The duff surrounds the mushroom; the mushroom is negative space. Leave the cap as untouched white paper — its pale dome emerging from the earth is revealed by what you don’t paint. Raw Sienna and Olive Green mix to a warm forest-floor tone. Burnt Umber darkens the deepest shadows between needles.

Plate Leaf

2 Quinacridone Gold : 1 Transparent Red Oxide

Bigleaf maple leaf dying big — gold center rusting from the margins inward, both pigments transparent enough to glow.

Quin. Gold
Trans. Red Oxide
Start with a full leaf-shaped wash of Quinacridone Gold. While soaking wet, feed Transparent Red Oxide from margins inward. Both pigments are transparent, so overlapping zones glow rather than muddy. The gold center stays pure; the rust creeps. Let the paper do the work.

Why These Colors?

This palette is a creek in late October. Cadmium Red Medium Hue and Hooker’s Green are chosen for their opacity — spawning salmon are solid objects in solid color, not atmospheric suggestions. Quinacridone Gold’s transparency works for maple leaves that transmit light rather than blocking it. Chinese White does grim duty as fungal growth on dying fish — opaque, irregular, clinical. Ivory Black handles the deep pools where salmon hold before their final push. Moonglow is Daniel Smith’s granulating convenience mix, perfect for the diffused overcast light on wet creek stones. Raw Sienna and Olive Green serve the matsutake passages. This is a palette of solid things: fish, leaves, stones, mushrooms. No atmosphere, all object.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Spawning Salmon Triptych

Subject: A single spawning salmon — red flank, jade-green head, white fungal patches on the body, the whole life cycle in one fish.

The Spawning Triptych approach (Cadmium Red Medium Hue 1:Hooker’s Green 1:Chinese White touch) paints the fish as three territories meeting on wet paper — decay is not symmetrical.

  • Three zones on wet paper — red flank, green head, white patches — let them meet without pre-mixing
  • Chinese White applied irregularly for fungal patches, not uniform
  • Ivory Black for the deep pool beneath the fish
  • Burnt Umber dry-brushed for creek gravel in the shallows
Prompt 2

Matsutake Hidden Under Shore Pines

Subject: A matsutake cap barely visible under pine duff — the forest hiding its most valuable thing in plain sight.

The Matsutake Earth mix (Raw Sienna 2:Olive Green 1:Burnt Umber touch) paints the duff as subject — the mushroom is what you leave unpainted.

  • Paint the duff surrounding the cap shape — leave the cap as white paper
  • Olive Green overhead for the pine canopy, dappled and dark
  • Quinacridone Gold maple leaves scattered for seasonal context
  • The mushroom’s pale dome emerges from what you don’t paint
Prompt 3

Dinner-Plate Maple Leaf on Wet Asphalt

Subject: A single bigleaf maple leaf plastered to wet asphalt — gold center rusting from margins, reflected sky on the wet surface around it.

The Plate Leaf mix (Quinacridone Gold 2:Transparent Red Oxide 1) captures the dying-big quality — gold center with rust creeping inward, both transparent pigments glowing.

  • Start with Quinacridone Gold wash in leaf shape, feed Transparent Red Oxide from margins while wet
  • Gold center stays pure, rust creeps inward — let the pigments find their own boundary
  • Ivory Black thinned for wet asphalt surrounding the leaf
  • Moonglow for reflected sky on the wet pavement surface
Prompt 4

Creek-Side Autumn Panorama

Subject: A full salmon creek scene — spawning fish, fallen leaves, wet stones, overcast light, the whole late-October package.

Every color in the palette earns its place here — salmon are opaque objects, leaves transmit light, stones diffuse it, the pool absorbs it.

  • Work background to foreground — establish the overcast with Moonglow first
  • Moonglow on wet paper for creek stones — let the granulation do the texture work
  • Salmon are solid opaque objects: Cadmium Red Medium Hue and Hooker’s Green at full concentration
  • Chinese White for both fungal patches on fish and foam where the current breaks over stones
Notes: All pigments confirmed Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. Cadmium Red Medium Hue is cadmium-free — same intensity, no toxicity concerns. Moonglow is a Daniel Smith convenience mix that separates beautifully on cold-press paper, granulating into its component pigments for complex, diffused effects. Ivory Black has two behaviors: use it wet for deep pools (glossy, reflective) and dry-brush it for gravel shadow (matte, textured). Same pigment, two personalities.
