Rain Shadow Split

Cross Snoqualmie Pass and the world cracks open. Behind you: Hooker’s Green forest under Payne’s Gray ceiling, rain on the windshield. Ahead: Hansa Yellow light on golden hills, Olive Green sage, Buff Titanium sky so bright you squint. Same state, same hour, two entirely different paintings.

Mixes & Ratios

Westside Drench

2 Payne’s Gray : 1 Hooker’s Green

The wet side of the pass — sodden Douglas fir under a sky that forgot what blue looks like.

Payne's Gray
Hooker's Green
Payne’s Gray is PB29/PBk6/PV19 — it already leans blue-violet, so adding Hooker’s Green (PG36/PY150) pushes it into that specific dark-forest-under-overcast temperature. The mix stays clean because neither pigment is opaque. Use it wet-in-wet for the tree masses flanking I-90 before the pass — the kind of forest that’s so dark and dense it reads as a single value from the car. For variation, tilt the ratio toward more Hooker’s Green where light catches the canopy, more Payne’s Gray where the trees merge into cloud.

Kittitas Gold

1 Hansa Yellow Deep : 1 Raw Sienna : touch Buff Titanium

Dry-side golden hills in February light — dormant grass glowing like the sun lives inside the ground.

Hansa Yel. Dp
Raw Sienna
Buff Ti.
Hansa Yellow Deep (PY65) is transparent and saturated; Raw Sienna (PBr7) is transparent and granulating. Together they produce a luminous gold that granulates just enough to suggest dry grass texture without going muddy. The touch of Buff Titanium (PW6:1) lifts the value and adds the slightest opacity — that bright, bleached quality of winter sun on east-side hills. Apply in a graded wash from the horizon up, letting Raw Sienna dominate the foreground (closer, warmer, more texture) and Hansa Yellow Deep take the mid-distance where the light concentrates.

Pass Divide

1 Cobalt Blue : 1 Olive Green

The transition zone at Snoqualmie Pass itself — where wet-side blue and dry-side sage exist in the same breath.

Cobalt Blue
Olive Green
This is the color of the transition — neither fully wet-side nor dry-side. Cobalt Blue (PB28) granulates; Olive Green (PO49/PG7/PY42) is smooth and warm. Mixed equally they produce a complex blue-green-gray that shifts depending on the paper’s moisture. Use it for the snow-patched ridgeline at the pass summit, for the moment where the forest starts thinning and the first golden hills appear in the distance. Let the two pigments separate slightly on the paper — the granulation tells the story of two climates colliding.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built on a fault line. One side is wet, cool, and dense: Payne’s Gray for the overcast, French Ultramarine for the heavy rain-cloud blue, Cobalt Blue for the rare break, Hooker’s Green for the soaked forest, Neutral Tint for the veil that hangs over everything. The other side is warm, open, and golden: Hansa Yellow Deep for the light itself, Raw Sienna for the dormant hills, Olive Green for the sage and bunchgrass, Burnt Sienna Light for the warm ranch earth, Buff Titanium for the bright sky you squint into. Ten pigments, two climates. The split isn’t just thematic — it’s structural. The wet-side pigments are cooler, more transparent, built for layering gray on gray. The dry-side pigments are warmer, include more earth tones, and Buff Titanium brings the only semi-opaque body to the palette — because the dry side has substance, weight, visible light.

Painting Prompts

Four subjects that live inside this palette’s split personality. Each one asks you to commit to a side — or paint the line where they meet.

Prompt 1

Snoqualmie Pass Crossing

Subject: The view from your windshield cresting the pass on I-90. Rearview: dark forest and low cloud. Ahead: the valley opens, golden and bright, and the road drops toward Ellensburg. The split happens in real time.

The Westside Drench mix handles everything behind you. Kittitas Gold handles everything ahead. The Pass Divide mix lives right at the summit, where the two worlds overlap and the snow-patched ridgeline can’t decide which side it belongs to.

  • Split your paper vertically — wet side on the left, dry side on the right, the pass summit as the dividing line
  • Paint the left half with Westside Drench (Payne’s Gray + Hooker’s Green) wet-in-wet, letting the forest and cloud merge into a single dark mass
  • Paint the right half with Kittitas Gold (Hansa Yellow Deep + Raw Sienna + Buff Titanium) as a graded wash, brighter and more open
  • The road itself is Neutral Tint — a thin dark line pulling you from one world into the other
Prompt 2

Wet-Side Forest, North Bend

Subject: Douglas fir and western red cedar in the rain, just west of the pass. Everything is dark, vertical, dripping. The sky is indistinguishable from the canopy. A February afternoon that never quite becomes day.

This is the cool side of the palette doing its best work. Hooker’s Green for the canopy, Payne’s Gray for the sky and shadows, French Ultramarine for the deep blue in the rain clouds, Neutral Tint for the overall atmospheric weight.

  • Wet the entire paper and lay in French Ultramarine at the top, letting it bleed into Payne’s Gray — the sky and cloud are one continuous wash
  • While still damp, drop Hooker’s Green for the tree masses, letting it merge upward into the sky — the boundary between forest and cloud should be lost
  • Once dry, use Neutral Tint for the trunks — vertical strokes, slightly varied, suggesting depth without detail
  • Leave no warm colors in this painting. The absence of gold is the point — that warmth is forty miles east, on the other side
Prompt 3

Dry-Side Ranch Country, Kittitas Valley

Subject: A ranch fence line running toward golden hills east of Ellensburg. Dormant bunchgrass, sage, open sky, a barn the color of Burnt Sienna Light. February sun low and warm, casting long shadows that are actually blue.

Kittitas Gold for the hills. Olive Green for the sage. Burnt Sienna Light for the barn and warm earth. Buff Titanium for the wide sky. Cobalt Blue for the shadows — because on the dry side, even the shadows carry color.

  • Paint the sky with Buff Titanium mixed with a whisper of Cobalt Blue — bright, pale, the kind of winter sky that makes you reach for sunglasses
  • Build the hills with Kittitas Gold as a base wash, then add Olive Green in drybrush strokes for the sage clumps and bunchgrass
  • The barn is pure Burnt Sienna Light — lighter and more orange than regular Burnt Sienna, perfect for weathered east-side wood
  • Cast shadows on the golden ground are Cobalt Blue, diluted — they granulate on the paper and read as cold air pooling in low spots
Prompt 4

The Transition — Hyak to Keechelus

Subject: The stretch of I-90 between Hyak and Keechelus Lake where the forest starts thinning, patches of snow appear on south-facing slopes, and the light changes. Not yet the dry side, but no longer the wet side. The in-between.

The Pass Divide mix (Cobalt Blue + Olive Green) owns this painting. It’s the color of uncertainty — part rain-forest blue, part sage-country green. Raw Sienna starts appearing in the ground. Neutral Tint still hangs in the air.

  • Use the Pass Divide mix for the middle-distance ridges, letting Cobalt Blue and Olive Green separate on the paper to suggest the climate shifting
  • Paint the lake with Cobalt Blue alone, flat and cold — Keechelus is alpine, steel-colored, not yet touched by the warmth ahead
  • Add Raw Sienna to the exposed hillsides where snow has melted — the first hint of the golden landscape to come
  • Keep Neutral Tint in the sky but thinned out, breaking up — the overcast is losing its grip, and the dry-side light is starting to win
Rain shadow effect: The Cascades and Olympics force moisture-laden Pacific air upward, where it cools and drops its rain on the western slopes. The eastern side sits in the “shadow” — dry, sunny, sometimes receiving less than 10 inches of rain annually while the west side gets 80+. It’s one of the most dramatic climate transitions in North America, and it happens over the span of a single mountain pass.

Burnt Sienna Light vs. Burnt Sienna: Daniel Smith’s Burnt Sienna Light (PBr7) is lighter-valued and more orange than their regular Burnt Sienna. It’s closer to a terracotta — warmer, less red-brown, more golden-brown. Perfect for the sun-baked wood and earth of the dry side, where the regular Burnt Sienna would read too heavy and dark.

Pigment notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Neutral Tint (PBk6/PV19/PB15) is a convenience mix that reads as a true neutral — useful for atmospheric weight without color bias. Olive Green (PO49/PG7/PY42) is warm and muted, ideal for the sagebrush and bunchgrass east of the Cascades. Cobalt Blue (PB28) granulates beautifully; French Ultramarine (PB29) less so but offers deeper blue saturation for storm clouds.
