Week 6 — PNW Watercolor Series

Rain Shadow Split

Sequim gets sun while Seattle gets hammered — a 40-mile gap in weather. Pacific tree frogs calling from ditches at 7 PM, hazelnut catkins swinging pale against wet bark. The tension: the Olympics as wall — abundance on one side, drought memory on the other.

Primary Trio

Cobalt Blue

Sequim sky, rare and real; granulating, clean, the blue that earns its presence by being withheld all winter

Raw Umber

Ditch bank where tree frogs call; transparent, granulating, the substrate of every lowland edge

Quinacridone Gold

Hazelnut catkin backlit by low sun; transparent, warm, luminous even in small quantities

Mixes & Ratios

Shadow Sun

1 Cobalt Blue : 2 Buff Titanium

Rain shadow sky — thin but real, the blue that Sequim earns while Seattle drowns.

Cobalt Blue
Buff Titanium
This is a sky that’s more white than blue — the Buff Titanium dominates because winter rain-shadow sun is pale, washed-out, tentative. Mix on the palette and apply as a graded wash, heavier Cobalt at zenith, nearly pure Buff at the horizon. Cobalt Blue’s granulation will add a subtle texture that reads as thin atmosphere. Don’t overload — this sky is precious because it’s barely there. For the Olympic mountains below, switch to Payne’s Gray and let the contrast tell the rain shadow story.

Frog Ditch

2 Hooker's Green : 1 Raw Umber

Standing water with algae bloom — the drainage ditch where tree frogs sing their entire spring into being.

Hooker's Green
Raw Umber
Hooker’s Green is a convenience mix — it’s already complex. Adding Raw Umber earths it into ditch-water territory. Apply wet to the paper and let them mingle rather than premixing completely; the partial separation reads as algae floating over silty bottom. For the water surface, keep it smooth. For the bank, add more Raw Umber and use dry brush for the grass stubble. The frogs are calling from here. You won’t paint them — they’re thumbnail-sized and invisible. Paint the place they choose.

Catkin Swing

2 Quinacridone Gold : 1 Hansa Yellow Light

Hazelnut catkins in low-angle sun — pale gold pendulums swinging against dark wet bark.

Quin. Gold
Hansa Yellow Lt
Quinacridone Gold is transparent and warm; Hansa Yellow Light lifts it toward the pale, pollen-dusty quality of hazelnut catkins. Paint each catkin as a single stroke with a rigger — they hang in clusters of three to five, each about two inches long. Apply over a dried dark wash of Burnt Umber (the bark) so the transparency lets the dark show through at the attachment point. Hansa Yellow Light is the sun-catching tip; Quinacridone Gold is the body. For catkins in shadow, drop the Hansa entirely and add Raw Umber.

Why These Colors?

The rain shadow is fundamentally a story about two sides of a mountain, and this palette holds both. Cobalt Blue and Cerulean Blue split the sky duties — Cobalt for the Sequim side (warmer, granulating, earned), Cerulean for the wider horizon (cooler, smoother, more confident). Payne’s Gray is Seattle’s sky, visible as a distant wall to the west. Quinacridone Gold and Hansa Yellow Light are both transparent, giving the catkins a backlit luminosity that opaque yellows can’t achieve. Raw Umber and Yellow Ochre granulate together beautifully for the dry-grass, rain-shadow-floor passages. Hooker’s Green is deliberately vivid — the ditch algae is the most saturated thing in the scene, thriving in standing water while everything else waits for spring.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Rain Shadow Split — Two Skies

Subject: The view from Hurricane Ridge or a Sequim overlook: Cobalt Blue sky overhead, the Olympic wall behind you, and Seattle’s gray rain curtain visible 40 miles to the east. Two weathers in one frame.

Cobalt Blue and Cerulean Blue handle the rain-shadow sky (warm and granulating vs. cool and smooth). Payne’s Gray is the distant rain wall. The palette literally holds both sides of the mountain.

  • Divide the paper roughly 60/40 — the rain shadow sky gets the larger share, because this painting is about the gift of sun
  • Paint the rain-shadow sky with the Shadow Sun mix (Cobalt Blue + Buff Titanium), grading from more Cobalt at zenith to nearly pure Buff at the horizon
  • Paint the distant rain curtain with Payne’s Gray on damp paper, letting it bleed and soften — rain seen from 40 miles away is a veil, not a wall
  • The Olympic ridge between the two skies is Burnt Umber + Payne’s Gray — it should be the hardest edge in the painting, the dividing line
Prompt 2

Frog Ditch at Dusk

Subject: A drainage ditch in the Sequim lowlands at 7 PM, standing water bright with algae, Pacific tree frogs calling from invisible positions. The sound is everywhere; the frogs are nowhere.

The Frog Ditch mix (Hooker’s Green + Raw Umber) is deliberately vivid — ditch algae is the most saturated thing in the scene. Raw Umber earths the banks. You won’t paint the frogs, but you’ll paint exactly where they choose to be.

  • Paint the standing water with Hooker’s Green + Raw Umber, applying wet and letting them mingle on the paper — the partial separation reads as algae floating over silty bottom
  • Build the banks with Raw Umber + Yellow Ochre, using dry brush for the grass stubble that lines the ditch edge
  • Add a reflection of the rain-shadow sky in the water with a thin glaze of Cobalt Blue over the dried green — just enough to show there’s sky above
  • Leave the painting without any visible wildlife — the frogs are thumbnail-sized and invisible. Paint their habitat so well that the viewer can hear them
Prompt 3

Hazelnut Catkins Against Wet Bark

Subject: A close study of hazelnut catkins in low-angle February sun — pale gold pendulums swinging against dark, fissured bark. The contrast between warm catkin and cold bark is the whole story.

The Catkin Swing mix (Quinacridone Gold + Hansa Yellow Light) gives you transparent, pollen-dusty catkins. Burnt Umber provides the dark wet bark they hang against. Both yellows are transparent, so the catkins glow like backlit pendants.

  • Paint the bark first with Burnt Umber, building up the fissured texture with dry brush on rough paper. Let it dry completely
  • Paint each catkin as a single rigger stroke over the dried bark — the transparency of the Catkin Swing mix lets the dark bark show through at the attachment point
  • Hansa Yellow Light goes at the sun-catching tip; Quinacridone Gold is the body. For catkins in shadow, drop the Hansa and add Raw Umber
  • Keep the background out of focus with a soft wash of Payne’s Gray — the catkins and bark should be the sharpest things in the painting
Prompt 4

Sequim Fieldstone in Pale Sun

Subject: A fieldstone wall or fence corner in the rain shadow, pale winter sun warming the stone, dried grass at the base, the wide-open horizon behind.

Buff Titanium is the pale sun on the stone — semi-opaque and quiet. Yellow Ochre and Raw Umber give you the dried grass and earthen base. Cerulean Blue handles the wide-open horizon behind, smoother and more confident than the intimate Cobalt overhead.

  • Paint the stone with Raw Umber + Buff Titanium, varying the ratio for each stone — more Buff for the sunlit face, more Raw Umber for the shadowed edges
  • The grass at the base is Yellow Ochre in thin, dry-brush strokes — straw-pale and winter-beaten
  • Paint the horizon sky with Cerulean Blue, keeping it flat and opaque — this is the rain shadow at its most expansive, nothing between you and the sky
  • Add a distant Payne’s Gray rain curtain at one edge of the horizon to remind the viewer what the other side of the mountain looks like
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Temperature splits the palette down the middle: the rain-shadow side runs warm (Quinacridone Gold, Yellow Ochre, Hansa Yellow Light) while the wet side runs cool (Payne’s Gray, Cobalt Blue). Cerulean Blue is opaque — use it for the horizon, not for glazing. Hooker’s Green is a convenience mix; it’s already balanced and doesn’t need much adjustment. Two blues on the palette (Cobalt and Cerulean) serve different atmospheric roles — don’t substitute one for the other. The palette works best when you commit to the split: warm light on the right, cool rain on the left, the Olympics as the dividing line.
