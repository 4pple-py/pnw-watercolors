Week 49 — PNW Watercolor Series

Year’s Last Breath

The year exhales and doesn’t inhale again. Everything is fog, mud, spent leaves turning to mulch underfoot. The palette is exhausted — grays and browns and blues that have forgotten what warmth feels like. Buff Titanium light barely registers through the overcast. You paint with what’s left, and what’s left is honest. December doesn’t perform. It just ends.

Primary Trio

Neutral Tint

Exhaustion made visible; the fog that flattens everything, the gray that has no opinion, the color of a year that’s used itself up

Burnt Umber

Spent earth; the dark brown of sodden ground, decomposing leaves, mud that was once a trail — rich, warm, exhausted

French Ultramarine

Cold deep blue; the temperature of December water, the color behind the overcast that you know is there but cannot reach

Mixes & Ratios

Year’s Exhale

2 Neutral Tint : 1 Payne’s Gray : ½ Shadow Violet

The color of fog that has given up pretending it might clear — gray with a violet undertow of melancholy.

Neutral Tint
Payne’s Gray
Shadow Violet
Neutral Tint flattens. That’s what it does — it removes temperature, removes personality, removes the will to be a color. Payne’s Gray adds just enough blue-gray lean to prevent the mix from going dead on paper, because even December fog has a direction it leans. Shadow Violet is the emotional ingredient: it adds a violet-gray undertone that reads as melancholy rather than mere grayness. The difference between a gray sky that’s boring and one that’s beautiful is this violet. Apply as a wet-into-wet wash across the entire sky area, allowing Neutral Tint to dominate most of the surface. Touch Shadow Violet into the lower sky where it meets the treeline or horizon. Let it bloom and spread without interference.

Spent Earth

2 Burnt Umber : 1 Raw Umber : ½ Transparent Brown Oxide

The ground after everything has fallen, rotted, and been rained on for weeks — rich mud that smells like endings.

Burnt Umber
Raw Umber
Trans. Brown Oxide
Burnt Umber is the backbone — a dark, warm brown that reads as saturated earth. It’s the color of ground that has absorbed months of rain and decomposing leaves. Raw Umber cools and lightens the mix at the edges where mud thins out toward gravel or grass, providing the transition from dark wet ground to lighter dry patches. Transparent Brown Oxide is the glazing layer — fully transparent, it goes over dry Burnt Umber to add depth and the rich, warm undertone of decomposing organic material. The transparency means it deepens without muddying. Apply Burnt Umber as the base to wet paper, charge Raw Umber into the lighter zones, let it dry completely, then glaze Transparent Brown Oxide over the darkest areas where leaves have composted into the soil.

Winter Depth

2 Indanthrone Blue : 1 French Ultramarine : ¼ Moonglow

The color at the bottom of a December river — deep, cold, unreachable, where the year sinks and settles.

Indanthrone Blue
French Ultramarine
Moonglow
Indanthrone Blue at full strength approaches black but holds onto its blue identity — it’s the color of deep water in winter, cold and definitive. French Ultramarine is brighter and warmer by comparison, and it lifts the mix just enough to read as water rather than void — there’s still light in there somewhere, deep down. Moonglow adds the granulating, separating quality of disturbed sediment: its three component pigments drift apart on paper the way silt drifts in slow current. Apply Indanthrone Blue at full concentration to the deepest zones, grade to French Ultramarine where the water shallows or catches reflected sky, and float Moonglow into the transition zones where depth becomes uncertain. The granulation will suggest suspended sediment without any brushwork.

Why These Colors?

An exhausted palette for an exhausted year. Neutral Tint and Payne’s Gray flatten everything the way December flattens everything — they remove the drama, the contrast, the will to be vivid. Burnt Umber and Raw Umber are the spent earth: trails turned to mud, leaves composted to mulch, the ground absorbing everything the trees dropped and turning it back into soil. French Ultramarine and Indanthrone Blue bring the cold — real cold, the kind that gets into river water and stays. Shadow Violet and Moonglow add the melancholy that separates December from mere grayness: Shadow Violet’s violet-gray undertone is the emotional color of endings, and Moonglow’s three-pigment separation on paper mimics the way twilight breaks everything apart at four in the afternoon. Transparent Brown Oxide glazes the decomposing leaves with a richness that the opaque earth tones can’t achieve alone. Buff Titanium is barely here — the light is barely here. This palette doesn’t fight the darkness. It sits with it.

Painting Prompts

The year is almost done, and so is the impulse to make things pretty. These prompts lean into the quiet, the gray, the honest ugly-beauty of a Pacific Northwest December. Paint what you see, not what you wish you saw.

Prompt 1

Fog Over the Skagit Flats

Subject: Agricultural flats in the Skagit Valley, fog erasing everything beyond fifty yards. Bare fields, a few fence posts, a treeline that’s more suggestion than silhouette. The ground is mud. The sky is the same color as the ground. Almost nothing is happening, and that’s the painting.

The Year’s Exhale mix (Neutral Tint + Payne’s Gray + Shadow Violet) for the sky and fog. Spent Earth mix (Burnt Umber + Raw Umber + Transparent Brown Oxide) for the fields. Buff Titanium for whatever passes as light.

  • Wet the entire paper and apply the Year’s Exhale mix in a single pass from top to bottom — sky and ground should be nearly the same value, because in fog they are
  • While still wet, charge Burnt Umber into the lower third for the muddy fields — the boundary between sky and ground should be indistinct, a soft bleed rather than a horizon line
  • Fence posts are Payne’s Gray applied with a rigger brush after the background is just damp enough to soften edges slightly — they should half-disappear into the fog
  • The distant treeline is a single stroke of dilute Neutral Tint with Shadow Violet, barely darker than the sky behind it — if you can clearly see the trees, you’ve made them too dark
Prompt 2

December River — Nooksack or Stillaguamish

Subject: A PNW river in late December, running high and gray-brown with winter runoff. Gravel bars exposed, bare cottonwoods and alders on the banks, the water moving with a slow, heavy authority. Overcast pressing down on everything.

Winter Depth mix (Indanthrone Blue + French Ultramarine + Moonglow) for the deep water. Raw Umber for the gravel bars. Year’s Exhale for the sky. Payne’s Gray for the bare trees reflected in the water.

  • The river is the Winter Depth mix graded from Indanthrone Blue in the main channel to French Ultramarine at the shallower edges — let Moonglow granulate in the transition zones to suggest suspended sediment
  • Gravel bars are Raw Umber with spots of Buff Titanium for the lightest stones — keep the values close together, because December light doesn’t create strong contrasts on gravel
  • Bare trees on the banks are Payne’s Gray, and their reflections in the water are the same Payne’s Gray painted into the still-wet river wash so they blur and soften
  • The sky is Year’s Exhale at its most dilute — barely a wash, because the sky should be the lightest thing in the painting but only barely
Prompt 3

Forest Floor — Decomposing Leaves

Subject: Close-up of the forest floor in a second-growth PNW forest. Maple and alder leaves in various stages of decomposition, from recently fallen to skeletal. Wet, dark earth showing between them. A few mushrooms. No sky visible.

Transparent Brown Oxide for the richest leaf colors. Burnt Umber for the darkest decomposed areas. Raw Umber for the drier leaves. Shadow Violet for the blue-purple tones of wet decomposing organic matter. Buff Titanium for mushroom caps.

  • Start with a Burnt Umber base wash over the entire surface — the forest floor is dark and wet, and every leaf sits on top of this darkness
  • Transparent Brown Oxide glazed over dry Burnt Umber creates the rich, complex brown of recently fallen leaves — the transparency lets the dark base show through, building depth
  • Leaf skeletons where veins remain but tissue has rotted are negative painting: leave thin lines of the lighter base showing through darker glazes to suggest the vein network
  • Mushroom caps are Buff Titanium with a touch of Raw Umber — they should be the lightest thing in the composition, small points of pale relief against the dark floor
Prompt 4

Four O’Clock Twilight

Subject: A suburban PNW street at four in the afternoon in late December. The light is already failing. Streetlights just coming on, their glow barely competing with the remaining daylight. Wet pavement reflecting everything. A figure walking away.

Moonglow for the twilight sky, separating on paper into its blue-brown-green components. Indanthrone Blue for the darkening east. Payne’s Gray for the wet pavement. Buff Titanium for the streetlight glow. Shadow Violet for the shadows that are starting to win.

  • The sky is Moonglow applied to very wet paper — let the pigment separate into its three components across the sky. This granulating separation is what four o’clock December twilight actually looks like: not one color but several, drifting apart
  • Charge Indanthrone Blue into the eastern sky while the Moonglow is still wet — night is coming from that direction, and the deep blue should bleed into the separating Moonglow
  • Wet pavement is Payne’s Gray at full strength with reflections pulled down vertically — reserve small spots of Buff Titanium for streetlight reflections on the wet surface
  • The walking figure is a simple silhouette in Payne’s Gray or Indanthrone Blue, small and slightly blurred at the edges — they are leaving, and the painting should let them
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Neutral Tint is a convenience mix of lamp black and blue pigments, designed to flatten and darken without adding obvious color — the point is the absence of color. Shadow Violet (PV15+PBk7) is a granulating violet-gray that adds melancholy without drama. Transparent Brown Oxide (PBr41) is one of Daniel Smith’s most transparent earth pigments — it glazes over other colors without muddying, adding depth and warmth to browns. Moonglow separates into three distinct pigment components on paper — the granulation and separation are the point, not a side effect. Indanthrone Blue (PB60) is the darkest transparent blue available.
