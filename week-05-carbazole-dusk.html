Week 5 — PNW Watercolor Series

Carbazole Dusk

The sun drops behind the Olympics at 4:15 and the sky turns violet. Not purple — violet. That clean, cold, staining color that only happens in winter when the air has nothing in it. Bare cottonwoods along the Snoqualmie stand black against it. A thin crimson line at the horizon lasts maybe ten minutes, then it’s gone.

Primary Trio

Carbazole Violet

The sky itself at 4:30 — that ten-minute violet window between daylight and dark that only winter delivers.

Indanthrone Blue

The deep overhead sky where violet shades into blue-black — the color directly above you when you look up through bare branches.

Alizarin Crimson

That thin band of red-violet at the horizon where the sun was — the last warm thing in the sky.

Mixes & Ratios

Horizon Shiver

1 Carbazole Violet : 2 Moonglow : touch of Buff Titanium

The unstable zone where the violet sky meets the last warm light — color breaking apart as you watch it.

Carbazole Violet
Moonglow
Buff Titanium
This is where the magic of Moonglow earns its place. Moonglow is a convenience mix of Ultramarine Blue (PB29), Viridian (PG7), and Alizarin Crimson (PR206) — and on wet paper those three pigments separate. Drop it into a wash of Carbazole Violet and the granulation creates a horizon that shifts between blue, violet, and gray in ways you cannot control. That’s the point. The Buff Titanium lifts the value just enough to read as lingering light. Apply wet-into-wet and walk away. Touching this while it dries will kill the separation.

Afterburn Violet

2 Carbazole Violet : 1 Quinacridone Rose : touch of Alizarin Crimson

The ten-minute window after sunset when the sky is neither red nor violet but something electric between them.

Carbazole Violet
Quin. Rose
Alizarin Crimson
All three pigments are transparent stainers, so this mix glows from within — light passes through to the paper and bounces back through the pigment. Carbazole Violet (PV23) provides the deep violet structure. Quinacridone Rose (PV19) pushes it warm without muddying. The touch of Alizarin Crimson (PR83) adds a red-violet depth at the horizon edge. This mix stains permanently on contact, so lay it confidently in one pass. Use it for that specific band of sky just above where the sun was — the part that makes you stop walking and look west.

Branch Bone

2 Payne’s Gray : 1 Lamp Black : touch of Transparent Brown Oxide

Bare cottonwood limbs against the violet — not pure black, because nothing in nature is. There’s bark in there.

Payne’s Gray
Lamp Black
Trans. Brown Oxide
Payne’s Gray (PBk6, PB29, PV23) already leans blue-violet, which means these silhouettes belong to this sky without forcing them. Lamp Black deepens the value for the thickest trunks and main forks. The touch of Transparent Brown Oxide is crucial — it’s the warmth of actual bark showing through, the thing that tells your eye these are real trees and not cutout shapes. Use a round brush, not a rigger — bare winter branches have weight and thickness. Paint from trunk outward, letting each stroke thin naturally as the branch narrows. The finest twigs can be scratched out with a palette knife while the wash is still damp.

Why These Colors?

Carbazole Violet is the palette’s reason for existing. It is the single most accurate pigment for that specific violet phase of PNW winter dusk — not purple, not lavender, but that deep, clean, electric violet the sky produces when the atmosphere is cold and clear. It stains permanently, which means the sky wash locks in and everything else works around it. Indanthrone Blue deepens the overhead sky where violet shades toward blue-black, and together these two staining pigments build the full range of the winter sky from horizon to zenith. Alizarin Crimson provides the thin warm band at the horizon — fugitive in sunlight but honest in a sketchbook. Payne’s Gray and Lamp Black build the bare branch silhouettes, with Transparent Brown Oxide warming them just enough to read as real bark rather than flat shapes. French Ultramarine granulates at the upper sky edge where Carbazole Violet is smooth, and that textural contrast matters. Moonglow separates into its component pigments at the horizon, creating color shifts you couldn’t mix on purpose. Quinacridone Rose bridges the gap between the violet sky and the crimson horizon. Buff Titanium is the last pale light — use it sparingly, because at 4:30 in January, there isn’t much light left.

Painting Prompts

Late January, early February. The sun sets at 4:30. You have ten minutes of violet sky before it goes dark. These prompts are built for that window and this palette.

Prompt 1

Cottonwood Silhouette, 4:20 PM

Subject: A bare black cottonwood against the full violet sky, seen from a river trail. The tree’s crown is a web of branches — no leaves, just structure. The sky behind it goes from Indanthrone Blue overhead through Carbazole Violet to a thin line of Alizarin Crimson at the horizon.

The Branch Bone mix gives you silhouettes that belong to this sky. Carbazole Violet and Indanthrone Blue build the graduated sky wash. That thin crimson horizon line is pure Alizarin Crimson, barely diluted.

  • Paint the sky first as a continuous graded wash — Indanthrone Blue at the top, blending into Carbazole Violet at center, with Alizarin Crimson at the very bottom. Both top pigments stain, so work quickly and don’t go back
  • Let the sky dry completely. Carbazole Violet stains on contact — if you paint branches into a wet sky, you lose the crisp silhouette edge
  • Paint the tree with the Branch Bone mix, starting from the trunk base. Use a #8 round, not a rigger — winter branches have real thickness
  • For the finest twigs at the crown edges, scratch into the damp Branch Bone wash with a palette knife or credit card edge — this lifts the Payne’s Gray slightly and reveals the violet sky beneath
Prompt 2

Olympics at Last Light

Subject: The Olympic Mountains seen across the Sound at the moment the sun drops behind them. The peaks are dark shapes — Lamp Black and Payne’s Gray — against a sky that runs the full violet spectrum. A band of Buff Titanium at the horizon is the last daylight.

The Afterburn Violet mix gives you that electric sky band just above the mountains. Moonglow at the horizon will granulate and separate into shifting color. The mountains themselves are simple dark shapes — Lamp Black with a breath of French Ultramarine for atmospheric distance.

  • Wet the entire paper. Lay Buff Titanium at the horizon, then work upward through Afterburn Violet into pure Carbazole Violet, finishing with Indanthrone Blue at the top
  • While the horizon zone is still wet, drop in Moonglow and let it granulate — the pigment separation creates the atmospheric shimmer of distant air over water
  • Let everything dry. Paint the Olympic ridge as a single silhouette shape with Lamp Black plus a touch of French Ultramarine — the blue suggests distance without announcing it
  • The mountains should have soft top edges (snow-covered peaks catch the last light) and hard bottom edges where they meet the sky band
Prompt 3

Streetlights Coming On

Subject: A residential street at 4:35 PM — streetlights just flickering to life, their sodium glow against the violet sky. Bare bigleaf maples line the sidewalk. The sky is still bright enough to silhouette the branches but dark enough that the lights register.

Buff Titanium dropped into wet Carbazole Violet creates the streetlight glow — the semi-opaque titanium pushes back against the staining violet. Transparent Brown Oxide warms the light poles and maple bark. The Branch Bone mix handles the tree silhouettes.

  • Mask the streetlight globes with masking fluid before painting the sky. Lay the sky wash with Carbazole Violet, grading darker at the top
  • Once the sky is dry, remove the masking and paint the light globes with Buff Titanium tinted with a whisper of Quinacridone Rose — sodium lights aren’t white, they’re warm
  • Paint the glow halos by wetting the area around each light and dropping in dilute Buff Titanium — it will bloom into the dampened violet sky
  • The maples and light poles are Branch Bone mix, but add more Transparent Brown Oxide for the trunks closest to the viewer where bark texture is visible
Prompt 4

The Violet Window

Subject: Pure sky study — no trees, no mountains, just the ten-minute violet phase from zenith to horizon. The subject is the color transition itself: Indanthrone Blue to Carbazole Violet to Quinacridone Rose to Alizarin Crimson to Buff Titanium.

This is a palette exercise disguised as a painting. Every primary and accent pigment appears in its natural position in the sky. French Ultramarine granulates against the smooth staining pigments around it, creating texture where the atmosphere thickens near the horizon.

  • Work on a full sheet, landscape orientation. Wet the entire surface evenly. Speed matters — you’re racing the drying time just like the sky is racing the clock
  • Start at the top with Indanthrone Blue, then Carbazole Violet in the upper-middle, French Ultramarine just below that (its granulation will create visible texture against the smooth staining pigments), then Quinacridone Rose, Alizarin Crimson, and finally Buff Titanium at the bottom
  • Drop Moonglow into the transition zone between Carbazole Violet and the warm colors — let it separate on the wet paper into its component blues, violets, and grays
  • Do not touch it once it’s laid down. Every brushstroke after the initial pass will destroy the wet-into-wet transitions that make this study work. Set it flat and walk away for thirty minutes
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Carbazole Violet (PV23) is one of the most intensely staining pigments in watercolor — it bonds to paper fibers on contact and cannot be fully lifted. Plan your lights before it touches the surface. Indanthrone Blue (PB60) stains similarly. Alizarin Crimson (PR83) is a classic that’s somewhat fugitive in prolonged light exposure but perfectly stable in a sketchbook. Moonglow is Daniel Smith’s convenience mix of PB29/PG7/PR206 — its value is in the granulating separation of its component pigments on wet paper, producing color shifts you cannot premix. French Ultramarine (PB29) granulates visibly, which provides textural contrast against the smooth staining violets. Payne’s Gray (PBk6/PB29/PV23) already contains Carbazole Violet, so branch silhouettes painted with it naturally harmonize with the sky. Lamp Black (PBk7) is opaque and dense — reserve it for the darkest darks. Temperature runs overwhelmingly cool in this palette; Transparent Brown Oxide (PR101) and Buff Titanium (PW6:1) are the only true warms.
