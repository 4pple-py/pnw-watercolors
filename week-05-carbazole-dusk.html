Carbazole Dusk

Red alder catkins dangle over the Stillaguamish at 5:15 PM — the sky a single bruise from Carbazole to nothing. Varied thrush whistles once from deep second-growth. Light gains two minutes but you can’t feel it yet. The tension: lengthening that registers only in data, not in the body.

Primary Trio

Carbazole Violet

Sky at civil twilight, the specific violet that lives between daylight and dark; deep-staining, transparent, absolute

Transparent Red Oxide

Alder catkin dangle, rust-red and backlit; clean transparency, warm against the violet

Indanthrone Blue

River surface at last light, holding the sky’s color but darker; staining, deep, non-granulating

Mixes & Ratios

Catkin Rust

2 Transparent Red Oxide : 1 Quinacridone Burnt Orange

Pendulous alder catkins backlit against the fading west — rust-red fingers reaching for water they’ll never touch.

Trans. Red Oxide
Quin. Burnt Orange
Both pigments are transparent, so the backlit quality is built into the chemistry. Mix on the palette and use a rigger or fine round to paint each catkin as a single downward stroke — they hang, so paint them hanging. Transparent Red Oxide provides the body; Quinacridone Burnt Orange gives the pollen-dust warmth at the tips. For catkins in full shadow, drop the Burnt Orange and add a touch of Carbazole Violet instead. The transparency means you can glaze these over a dried sky wash and the twilight will show through.

River Bruise

2 Carbazole Violet : 1 Indanthrone Blue

Stillaguamish surface at dusk — the river wearing the sky’s bruise, darker and less forgiving.

Carbazole Violet
Indanthrone Blue
Both pigments stain permanently. This is not a mix you lift or correct — commit to it. Lay a graduated wash from Carbazole-heavy at the top (reflecting sky) to Indanthrone-heavy at the bottom (river’s own darkness). Neither pigment granulates, so the surface will be smooth — which is what slow, deep river water looks like at dusk. For current ripples, drag a damp brush through while still wet. For the far bank’s reflection, reverse the ratio.

Thrush Wood

2 Lunar Black : 1 Perylene Green

Second-growth interior, no sky — the dark that a varied thrush whistles from, seen from outside, impenetrable.

Lunar Black
Perylene Green
Lunar Black granulates heavily; Perylene Green stains smoothly. Together they create a dark with internal texture — the granulation reads as branch density while the staining green keeps it from going neutral. Apply to damp paper and let the Lunar Black particles settle. Don’t try to paint individual trees inside this mass — the point is that you can’t see them. The thrush is in there. You’ll have to trust the sound. For the edge where forest meets sky, soften with a clean damp brush.

Why These Colors?

Carbazole Violet is the palette’s reason for existing — it’s the single most accurate pigment for Pacific Northwest civil twilight, that ten-minute window when the sky goes violet before it goes black. It stains absolutely, which means the sky wash is permanent and everything else layers on top of or beside it. Transparent Red Oxide and Quinacridone Burnt Orange are both transparent warm stainers, giving the catkins their backlit quality against the cold sky. Indanthrone Blue and Carbazole Violet are staining partners that mix to a deep, smooth river surface — no granulation, no texture, just depth. Lunar Black provides the only granulation in the palette, reserved for the conifer mass where texture equals density. Moonglow at the horizon shifts its tricolor granulation against the smooth staining sky above — instability where the light is dying.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Alder Catkins Over the Stillaguamish

Subject: Red alder catkins dangling over the river at 5:15 PM, backlit against a Carbazole Violet sky. The catkins are rust-red fingers reaching downward; the water below mirrors the sky’s bruise.

The Catkin Rust mix (Transparent Red Oxide + Quinacridone Burnt Orange) gives you backlit catkins that glow with transparency. The River Bruise mix (Carbazole Violet + Indanthrone Blue) is the water and sky in one smooth, staining wash. Warm over cold, small against vast.

  • Paint the sky first as a graduated wash of Carbazole Violet — heavier at zenith, fading toward Buff Titanium at the horizon. This stains permanently, so commit
  • Once dry, paint the river with the River Bruise mix, grading from Carbazole-heavy (reflecting sky) to Indanthrone-heavy (river’s own dark)
  • Glaze the catkins over the dried sky with the Catkin Rust mix using a rigger — each catkin is a single downward stroke. The transparency lets the violet show through at the attachment points
  • Add Moonglow at the horizon line where sky meets land for that shifting, dying glow
Prompt 2

Varied Thrush in Second Growth

Subject: The view from a trail looking into impenetrable second-growth conifer forest at dusk. You can’t see the varied thrush. You can hear it — one clear whistle from deep inside the dark.

The Thrush Wood mix (Lunar Black + Perylene Green) creates a dark with internal texture — granulation reads as branch density, staining green keeps it from going dead neutral. This is a painting about darkness that isn’t empty.

  • Paint the entire forest mass with Lunar Black + Perylene Green on damp paper — let the Lunar Black granulate for at least five minutes without touching it
  • Soften the top edge where forest meets sky with a clean damp brush — the transition should be gradual, not hard
  • The sky above is Carbazole Violet grading to Buff Titanium, already mostly gone
  • Leave one small branch silhouette unpainted at the forest edge — that’s where the thrush might be sitting. The negative space suggests the bird without painting it
Prompt 3

Civil Twilight River Reflection

Subject: The Stillaguamish at the ten-minute window of civil twilight — the sky a single violet bruise reflected in slow, deep water. A bridge abutment anchors one side. No sun, just the afterglow.

Carbazole Violet and Indanthrone Blue are both staining and non-granulating, which gives you the smooth, deep surface of slow river water at dusk. Perylene Green on the bridge abutment’s moss provides the one grounded, earthly element.

  • This is a painting of two smooth surfaces — sky and water — so work on hot-pressed or smooth cold-pressed paper to minimize texture
  • Lay the sky as a single graded wash: Carbazole Violet at zenith to Indanthrone Blue at the horizon. Neither pigment granulates, so the transition will be seamless
  • Mirror the sky in the water, but darker and slightly shifted toward Indanthrone Blue — rivers always read darker than the sky they reflect
  • Paint the bridge abutment with Perylene Green (moss) and Raw Sienna (concrete) as the hard geometric shape that anchors the composition against all that soft atmosphere
Prompt 4

Last Blue in the West

Subject: A narrow band of French Ultramarine blue at the western horizon, the last true color before the sky goes fully violet-to-black. Above it, Carbazole Violet. Below it, the dark land.

French Ultramarine is the only granulating pigment in an otherwise smooth sky — its texture at the horizon reads as the atmosphere’s last hold on daylight. Carbazole Violet above is smooth and absolute. The contrast between granulating blue and staining violet IS the subject.

  • Wet the top two-thirds and lay Carbazole Violet as a smooth graded wash — darkest at zenith, lighter toward the center
  • While still damp at the bottom edge, touch in French Ultramarine pure and unmixed — let it bloom slightly into the violet above
  • The granulation of the Ultramarine against the smooth Carbazole is the visual event — don’t blend them together
  • Paint the land silhouette with Lunar Black + Perylene Green once everything is dry — hard edge against the soft sky
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. This palette stains hard — Carbazole Violet, Indanthrone Blue, Transparent Red Oxide, and Perylene Green are all permanent on contact. Plan your whites and lights before touching brush to paper. Temperature runs cool-to-violet with the catkin pigments as the only true warms. Lunar Black and Moonglow provide the palette’s granulation; everything else is smooth. French Ultramarine appears only for the last wedge of blue in the western sky — use it pure, unmixed, at the horizon. Buff Titanium is the lightest note and should be applied sparingly, because at civil twilight, there isn’t much light left to depict.
