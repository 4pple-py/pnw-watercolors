Neon Moss

Noon, and the moss is almost fluorescent — weeks of rain have pumped it full of impossible green against charcoal trunks. Trumpeter swans land on Skagit flats like white freight. The world shrinks to nine hours of light but the green gets louder.

Primary Trio

Hooker’s Green

Moss screaming from every surface; the green that has absorbed weeks of rain and is now louder than it has any right to be

Lunar Black

Wet bark, granulated and ancient; the mineral dark that makes the green glow by sheer contrast

Raw Umber

River opacity, clay and runoff; the Skagit running brown after days of rain, carrying the mountains downstream

Mixes & Ratios

Swan Landing

3 Titanium White : touch Cobalt Teal Blue : touch Lunar Black

White bird on pewter slough — not warm white, not cold white, but the specific white of a living thing on gray water.

Titanium White
Cobalt Teal
Lunar Black
Cobalt Teal Blue cools the white — swans are never warm white. Lunar Black only in shadow areas. Apply thickly enough to sit on paper’s surface; opacity is the point.

Drunk Moss

2 Hooker’s Green : 1 Hansa Yellow Light

Neon carpet on nurse log — the green that makes you check whether you’re seeing things or whether the forest really is glowing.

Hooker's Green
Hansa Yellow Lt
Hansa Yellow Light lifts Hooker’s Green toward fluorescence. Apply in thick, wet strokes onto damp paper. Load the brush until it drips. This is not a subtle mix.

Silt River

3 Raw Umber : 1 Lunar Black

Skagit running opaque after days of rain — carrying the mountains downstream one particle at a time.

Raw Umber
Lunar Black
Raw Umber’s opacity does the work. Lunar Black granulates dramatically. Apply wet on dry paper and do not touch it again — granulation needs to settle undisturbed.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built on the contrast between fluorescent life and mineral inertia. Hooker’s Green paired with Hansa Yellow Light produces almost-unnatural neon. Lunar Black’s extreme granulation mimics bark, stone, and wet wood. Indanthrone Blue provides deep shade. Potter’s Pink and New Gamboge are calendar markers. Titanium White earns its place because trumpeter swans demand true opacity.

Painting Prompts

Nine hours of light and the green gets louder — these prompts are about painting what happens when the world simplifies down to contrast and saturation.

Prompt 1

Moss-Draped Big-Leaf Maple

Subject: A single trunk smothered in moss, one last golden leaf clinging — the tree wearing more green than it grew, the leaf a flag of surrender.

Drunk Moss (Hooker’s Green 2:Hansa Yellow Light 1) is the headline. Lunar Black’s granulation provides the bark beneath. New Gamboge gets one confident leaf shape.

  • Paint trunk first with Lunar Black, let it granulate
  • Load brush to dripping with Drunk Moss mix
  • Drop pure Hansa Yellow Light for brightest highlights
  • Paint single New Gamboge leaf last with crisp edge
Prompt 2

Trumpeter Swans on Skagit Flats

Subject: A raft of trumpeter swans on pewter slough — white freight on gray water, the heaviest grace note in the landscape.

Swan Landing (Titanium White 3:Cobalt Teal Blue touch:Lunar Black touch) is applied thick because opacity is the subject. The water beneath is Undersea Green and Raw Umber.

  • Paint water first with Undersea Green and Raw Umber
  • Apply Swan Landing mix thickly
  • Add Lunar Black touches to shadow sides
  • Landing swan gets hard-edged white wings, softened wingtips
Prompt 3

Nurse Log Close-Up

Subject: Cross-section of nurse log hosting an ecosystem — death feeding life in miniature, the forest’s recycling made visible.

Lunar Black for the log’s mineral dark, Drunk Moss for the living carpet, and a rigger brush for the tiny hemlock seedlings that prove the cycle works.

  • Block in log with Lunar Black
  • While damp, charge Hooker’s Green into top
  • Build moss with Drunk Moss mix in stippled strokes
  • Paint tiny hemlock seedlings with rigger brush
Prompt 4

Silt River at Dawn

Subject: Skagit running opaque with first pink light — three horizontal bands of sky, mountains, and river, the simplest composition and the hardest to pull off.

Potter’s Pink earns its place in the palette right here — dawn behind the Cascades. Silt River (Raw Umber 3:Lunar Black 1) is one confident wash.

  • Three horizontal bands: sky, mountains, river
  • Sky: Indanthrone Blue fading to Potter’s Pink
  • Mountains: silhouettes in Indanthrone Blue
  • River: Silt River mix in single confident wash
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. Lunar Black is a PrimaTek pigment with extraordinary granulation. Undersea Green is a Daniel Smith convenience mix. Titanium White is opaque by design — use only where opacity is intentional.
