Week 43 — PNW Watercolor Series

Neon Moss

First real soaking rain of fall and every surface that spent summer drying out wakes up overnight. Moss on bigleaf maples goes from dusty olive to electric green in forty-eight hours. The color is almost aggressive — fluorescent, radioactive, the kind of green that makes visitors from other places stop walking and stare. Every branch, every fence rail, every north-facing rock face becomes a broadcast. The forest is recharging, and it wants you to know.

Primary Trio

Cascade Green

That specific cool, bright moss green unique to the PNW — the color that makes out-of-towners reach for their phones; clean, vivid, unmistakable

Sap Green

Structural mid-tone moss — the green that holds the composition together between the electric highlights and deep shadows; warm, natural, reliable

Phthalo Green (BS)

Intense wet moss at maximum saturation — staining, transparent, overwhelming if unchecked; the nuclear reactor in the palette that powers the neon effect

Mixes & Ratios

Recharge Green

2 Cascade Green : 1 Phthalo Green (BS) : ½ Nickel Azo Yellow

The electric moment when moss wakes up — that first morning after rain when the green is so vivid it looks backlit, even in overcast.

Cascade Green
Phthalo Green BS
Nickel Azo Yellow
Cascade Green is the structural color — it reads as “PNW moss” immediately, with the cool, clean quality that separates our moss from the yellowish moss of other climates. Phthalo Green BS intensifies the chroma past what feels natural, which is exactly right — rehydrated moss IS unnaturally vivid. It stains, so commit to your placement. Nickel Azo Yellow pushes the highlights toward that acidic, almost toxic brightness on the outermost moss tips where water beads catch light. Apply Cascade Green as the base wash, then drop Phthalo Green BS into the wettest, most saturated areas while still damp. Touch Nickel Azo Yellow onto the highest points while the under-layers are barely damp — it will bloom slightly, mimicking the fuzzy edges of moss filaments.

Old Growth Carpet

2 Sap Green : 1 Hooker’s Green : ¼ Raw Umber

The thick moss mantle on a bigleaf maple branch — years of growth compressed into a living carpet, deeper and darker where the branch curves away from light.

Sap Green
Hooker's Green
Raw Umber
Sap Green is the workhorse mid-tone — warm enough to read as organic, structured enough to hold the moss’s form without dissolving into abstraction. Hooker’s Green deepens the shadows where moss layers stack so thick the bottom layers never dry, creating the dark recesses between clumps that give the carpet its three-dimensional texture. Raw Umber anchors the mix to the bark beneath — moss is never floating in space, and this earth tone grounds the greens to the branch. Apply Sap Green first as an even mid-tone, then work Hooker’s Green into the shadowed undersides and deep crevices. The Raw Umber goes at the edges where moss thins and bark peeks through. Let the transitions happen on damp paper so the greens bleed softly into one another the way real moss layers do.

Acid Fringe

2 Green Gold : 1 Nickel Azo Yellow : ¼ Cascade Green

The luminous yellow-green at the growing edge of moss colonies — new growth so bright it looks like it’s generating its own light.

Green Gold
Nickel Azo Yellow
Cascade Green
Green Gold is the warm side of moss — where new filaments reach outward and catch ambient light, they shift from the cool green of the moss body toward this golden-green that’s almost more yellow than green. Nickel Azo Yellow is the most acidic, high-key pigment in the palette — a controlled dose of it makes the fringe glow without tipping into chartreuse. Cascade Green at the edges ties the fringe back to the parent moss colony, preventing the warm tones from floating free. Use this mix only at the outermost edges of moss patches and on the tips of individual fronds. Apply with the tip of a small round brush in tiny, irregular dabs — moss grows in random directions and any pattern will look painted rather than grown. The Green Gold’s transparency lets the paper glow through, reinforcing the backlit effect.

Why These Colors?

Five greens is not excessive — it’s accurate. Walk up to a moss-covered bigleaf maple after an October rain and count the greens you actually see: the cool, vivid green of the main moss body (Cascade Green), the warm structural mid-tone where old growth thickens (Sap Green), the intense, almost unreal saturation of thoroughly soaked patches (Phthalo Green BS), the golden undertone where moss catches warm light (Green Gold), and the deep shadow green where layers compress in the crevices (Hooker’s Green). Each one does something the others cannot. Nickel Azo Yellow is the high note — that acidic, almost uncomfortable brightness on the newest growth tips that makes the whole scene vibrate. Without it, the greens are beautiful but calm; with it, they’re electric, which is the truth of rehydrated PNW moss. French Ultramarine provides the rain-wet surface sheen on bark and rock, the blue reflection that proves everything is soaked. Raw Umber is the bark architecture beneath the moss — without visible structure, moss floats like a texture applied to nothing. Payne’s Gray darkens the wet trunks and creates the shadows that make the neon greens pop by contrast. Neutral Tint handles the rain-filled air between trees, the atmospheric depth that says this forest goes back farther than you can see.

Painting Prompts

Every surface is a canvas this week. The rain has turned the forest into a green light show — your job is to capture the intensity without it reading as fake. Restraint in value, abandon in chroma.

Prompt 1

Bigleaf Maple Branch — Moss Portrait

Subject: A single horizontal bigleaf maple branch, so thickly coated in moss it’s more green than bark. Ferns growing from the moss. Rain dripping from the branch tips. The branch fills the frame like a landscape in miniature.

The Old Growth Carpet mix (Sap Green + Hooker’s Green + Raw Umber) builds the moss body. Recharge Green (Cascade Green + Phthalo Green BS + Nickel Azo Yellow) for the most vivid wet patches. Raw Umber for the rare bark visible at the branch’s ends.

  • Start with the branch structure in Raw Umber — even though most of it will be covered by moss, knowing where the bark is underneath gives the moss something to cling to and prevents it from looking like a green cloud
  • Build the moss in layers: Old Growth Carpet mix as the broad mid-tone first, then Recharge Green into the areas facing upward where rain hits directly and saturation is highest
  • Ferns growing from the moss are Sap Green with touches of Green Gold at their tips — they need to read as separate plants, so paint them with crisper edges than the surrounding moss
  • Rain drops on the branch tips are negative painting: leave tiny circles of white paper or lift them out with a damp brush, then add the faintest ring of Payne’s Gray shadow beneath each drop
Prompt 2

Moss on a Stone Wall — Roadside Near Astoria

Subject: An old stone retaining wall along a coastal highway, every mortar joint packed with moss. Rain darkens the stone to near-black while the moss blazes green. Ferns cascade from the top. Mist in the background.

Payne’s Gray for rain-darkened stone. The Acid Fringe mix (Green Gold + Nickel Azo Yellow + Cascade Green) for the brilliant moss in the mortar joints. Neutral Tint for the coastal mist behind everything.

  • Paint the stone wall first in Payne’s Gray, varying the value block by block — wet stone is not uniform, and each stone absorbs water differently. Leave the mortar joints as thin white lines initially
  • Once the stone is dry, fill the mortar joints with the Acid Fringe mix applied with a rigger brush — the thin, bright lines of moss against dark stone create the maximum contrast that makes this subject so striking
  • French Ultramarine glazed thinly over select stones adds the blue sheen of wet surfaces reflecting overcast sky — apply only to the most prominent stones to suggest rain-slick surfaces
  • The background mist is Neutral Tint at extreme dilution, applied to wet paper and left alone — the mist should have no edges, no brushwork, just a gradual dissolution of detail behind the sharp-focused wall
Prompt 3

Forest Floor Mosscape — Hoh Rain Forest

Subject: Looking down at the forest floor where every fallen log, rock, and root is blanketed in moss of varying ages and species. A nurse log with seedlings growing from its mossy back. Tiny mushrooms. The green goes on forever.

All five greens get used here in different zones: Hooker’s Green for the oldest, darkest moss in the shadows; Cascade Green for the dominant carpet; Phthalo Green BS for the wettest spots; Green Gold and Nickel Azo Yellow for the highlights and new growth. Raw Umber for the nurse log’s exposed wood.

  • Map the composition by value before thinking about green: the darkest greens (Hooker’s Green) go in the crevices between logs, the mid-tones (Sap Green, Cascade Green) cover the main surfaces, and the brightest greens (Acid Fringe mix) hit only the uppermost surfaces catching light
  • The nurse log is Raw Umber where bark shows through, transitioning to Old Growth Carpet mix where moss has taken over — the gradient from bark to moss is gradual and should be painted wet-into-wet
  • Tiny mushrooms are lifted white paper with Payne’s Gray shadows — these small non-green accents give the eye places to rest in the sea of green
  • Depth in a mosscape comes from aerial perspective within inches: the moss nearest the viewer has sharp texture and vivid chroma (Recharge Green), while moss even a few feet away softens and grays (add Neutral Tint to any green to push it back in space)
Prompt 4

Moss-Draped Alders in Rain — Quinault Valley

Subject: A stand of red alders with moss hanging from every branch like green drapery. Rain falling through the canopy. The trunks are dark with moisture. Light filters through from behind, backlighting the moss so it glows.

Payne’s Gray for the rain-soaked alder trunks. Recharge Green for the backlit moss. Neutral Tint for rain in the air between trees. Green Gold for the warm glow where light penetrates the moss canopy from behind.

  • The backlighting is the key to this composition: paint the moss greens warmer and more yellow (Green Gold dominant) where light comes through, and cooler and darker (Hooker’s Green, Sap Green) on the side facing the viewer
  • Alder trunks in rain are Payne’s Gray with Raw Umber — alders have pale bark when dry, but rain turns them dramatically dark, creating strong contrast against the bright moss
  • Hanging moss is painted as irregular curtains using the Recharge Green mix applied to damp paper with downward strokes — let the pigment run and feather at the bottom edges to suggest the wispy, dripping quality of saturated moss
  • Rain between the trees is Neutral Tint glazed over everything in the middle distance after all layers are dry — this single glaze pushes the background trees back and creates the wet-air atmosphere that makes the nearest moss-draped alder pop forward
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Cascade Green (PG7, PY150) is a Daniel Smith signature color — cool, clean, and unmistakably PNW. Phthalo Green Blue Shade (PG7) is extremely staining and intense; use it diluted unless you want permanent neon. Sap Green (PG36, PY150) is warmer and more natural than Cascade Green, providing the structural mid-tone. Green Gold (PY129) bridges green and yellow with a unique transparency. Hooker’s Green (PG36, PY3, PG7) is the darkest green here — use it for shadow and depth. Nickel Azo Yellow (PY150) has the specific acidic quality that matches new moss growth; no other yellow captures it.
