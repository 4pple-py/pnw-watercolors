Week 13 — PNW Watercolor Series

Cherry Camas Foam

UW Quad cherry blossoms at peak — Opera Pink petals falling like confetti into standing water. Drive an hour south to the Chehalis prairies and camas turns the meadows ultramarine. Two completely different flowers, two completely different blues-and-pinks, happening the same week. The PNW doesn’t coordinate, it just detonates.

Mixes & Ratios

Cherry Petal

2 Opera Pink : 1 Quinacridone Rose : touch Buff Titanium

A single cherry blossom held up to spring light — fluorescent pink at the edges, deepening to rose at center, warm cream where sun hits the petal.

Opera Pink
Quin. Rose
Buff Titanium
Opera Pink provides the fluorescent glow that makes cherry blossoms look unreal — literally unreal, because this pigment (BV10:1) contains a fluorescent brightener that reflects UV light as visible pink. Quinacridone Rose (PV19) grounds it with transparent warmth, the deeper flush at the petal’s center where the veins converge. Layer, don’t premix: wash Opera Pink first, let it dry, then drop Quinacridone Rose into the center wet-on-dry. Touch Buff Titanium where direct sun bleaches the petal edge. The two pinks are both transparent, so layering produces luminous depth rather than opacity. This is exactly how cherry petals work — light passes through them twice, bouncing off the branch behind.

Camas Meadow

2 Ultramarine Blue : 1 Cobalt Violet : touch Lavender

The prairie at middle distance — ten thousand camas blooms resolved into a single shimmering blue-violet field that no photograph ever gets right.

Ultramarine Blue
Cobalt Violet
Lavender
Ultramarine Blue (PB29) and Cobalt Violet (PV14) both granulate, and together they produce a blue-violet field that shimmers with mineral texture — exactly the way a camas prairie looks at middle distance, where individual flowers blur into a single impossible hue. Apply to damp paper in broad horizontal strokes, letting the two pigments intermingle and separate on their own. The granulation does the work of ten thousand individual blooms. A touch of Lavender at the far edges creates the haze where the field meets the horizon. For foreground camas, switch to pure Cobalt Violet and paint each bloom as a six-petaled star on a single green stem. Keep the middle-distance mix confident and saturated — this blue only happens once a year.

Spring Ground

1 Sap Green : 1 Burnt Sienna : touch Hansa Yellow Light

The earth beneath both spectacles — muddy prairie path and rain-wet garden soil, the ground that grows all this absurd color without asking for credit.

Sap Green
Burnt Sienna
Hansa Yellow Lt
Sap Green (a phthalo-based convenience mix) and Burnt Sienna (PBr7) produce a range of warm, natural greens and olive-browns depending on ratio — lean green for new grass, lean sienna for bare earth, equal parts for the muddy path between cherry trees. Touch of Hansa Yellow Light warms the mix toward the fresh chartreuse of new growth pushing through last year’s thatch. This is the ground mix that anchors both the cherry scene and the camas prairie. Without it, all those pinks and blues float. Vary the ratio across a single wash to suggest the patchwork of grass, mud, and new shoots that defines a PNW meadow in late March.

Why These Colors?

Opera Pink is a fluorescent pigment (BV10:1) — it literally glows, reflecting UV light as visible pink. Cherry blossoms at peak bloom have the same quality: they look backlit even in overcast, almost digitally saturated. No other watercolor pigment captures this. Quinacridone Rose gives the blossoms their warm interior depth, the transparent staining quality that Opera Pink lacks. On the camas side, Ultramarine Blue and French Ultramarine are both PB29 but lean differently — Ultramarine Blue runs slightly more violet, perfect for the blue-violet of camas flowers, while French Ultramarine stays cooler and cleaner for sky. Having both lets you separate flower from sky without mixing. Cobalt Violet (PV14) granulates on the paper, giving close-up camas blooms a mineral texture that no amount of careful brushwork could replicate. Lavender softens the far field into haze. Sap Green and Burnt Sienna handle the ground — meadow grass and wet earth, the unglamorous foundation that makes the pinks and blues possible. Buff Titanium catches the soft spring light on petal edges. Hansa Yellow Light is the first real sunshine of the year, warm and uncomplicated, the color that convinces you winter is done.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

UW Quad Cherry Blossoms

Subject: The Yoshino cherry canopy at peak — looking up through layers of Opera Pink blossoms against a French Ultramarine sky, petals drifting down past Gothic Revival stonework darkened with rain.

The Cherry Petal mix (Opera Pink layered with Quinacridone Rose) captures the fluorescent glow that makes these trees look unreal. French Ultramarine provides the clean cool sky visible through the canopy gaps. Burnt Sienna for the wet stonework gives the painting its anchor.

  • Paint the French Ultramarine sky first, leaving negative shapes for blossom clusters — irregular, overlapping, dense at center
  • Wash Opera Pink into the negative spaces, then drop Quinacridone Rose into petal centers while still damp for depth
  • Falling petals: tiny flicks of Opera Pink on dry paper, scattered, some sharp and some soft-edged
  • Burnt Sienna for the Gothic arches at bottom — keep it dark and understated, the architecture is secondary to the bloom
Prompt 2

Chehalis Camas Prairie

Subject: A south Puget Sound prairie at the camas peak — the entire middle ground a sweep of blue-violet, framed by Sap Green grass and scattered Garry oak with Hansa Yellow Light new leaves.

The Camas Meadow mix (Ultramarine Blue 2:Cobalt Violet 1:Lavender touch) produces a granulating blue-violet field that shimmers like the real thing. Sap Green and Hansa Yellow Light frame the prairie with spring greens.

  • Wet the middle ground and apply the Camas Meadow mix in broad horizontal strokes — let granulation happen, don’t fuss
  • Foreground camas: switch to pure Cobalt Violet, paint each bloom as a six-petaled star on a Sap Green stem
  • Lavender at the far edge where the field meets distance — the haze of a thousand blooms becoming atmosphere
  • Oak branches in Burnt Sienna, new leaves in Hansa Yellow Light — warm frame around the cool prairie
Prompt 3

Cherry Petals on Standing Water

Subject: Close-up of cherry petals floating on a rain puddle beneath the trees — pink confetti on dark reflective water, a reflected branch visible through the surface, petals collecting at the puddle’s edge in pale foam.

Opera Pink petals on a dark Ultramarine Blue reflection surface. Buff Titanium for the foam where petals accumulate at the puddle edge. Burnt Sienna for the reflected branch. This is a painting about surface tension — things resting on top of things.

  • Paint the puddle first: Ultramarine Blue and French Ultramarine mixed for a deep reflective surface, with a Burnt Sienna reflected branch
  • Let it dry completely, then float Opera Pink petals on top — small irregular ovals, some clustered, some solitary
  • Buff Titanium at the puddle edges where petals collect in a pale line of foam
  • Leave a few Quinacridone Rose petals sinking below the surface — slightly blurred, slightly muted, translucent beneath the water
Prompt 4

The Pink-and-Blue Overlap

Subject: A split composition — cherry-lined residential street on the left, camas meadow on the right, connected by a continuous spring sky. The moment when both spectacles peak simultaneously, an hour’s drive apart.

This painting uses the full palette. Opera Pink and Quinacridone Rose own the left half. Ultramarine Blue and Cobalt Violet own the right. French Ultramarine sky unifies both. Hansa Yellow Light spring sun falls equally on city and prairie.

  • Paint a continuous French Ultramarine sky across both halves first — this is what connects two scenes an hour apart
  • Left half: Opera Pink canopy above, Burnt Sienna trunks, petals falling onto a Sap Green lawn
  • Right half: Camas Meadow mix as a broad horizontal sweep, Cobalt Violet foreground blooms, Lavender at the far edge
  • Glaze Hansa Yellow Light thinly across both halves after everything dries — same spring sun, same impossible week
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. Opera Pink (BV10:1) contains a fluorescent brightener that reflects UV light as visible pink — stunning on paper, but this fluorescence fades faster than conventional pigments. Lightfastness is rated II (good, not excellent). For archival work, Quinacridone Rose is the safer choice; for capturing what cherry blossoms actually look like, nothing else comes close. Use Opera Pink generously and photograph the painting while it’s fresh. Ultramarine Blue and French Ultramarine are both PB29 (synthetic lapis lazuli), but they lean differently: Ultramarine Blue runs warmer and more violet, better for camas flowers; French Ultramarine stays cooler and greener, better for sky. Having both on the palette lets you separate flower from sky without any mixing. Cobalt Violet (PV14) is a genuine cobalt pigment — granulating, semi-transparent, and expensive. Its granulation gives individual camas blooms a mineral texture at close range that synthetic violets cannot replicate.
