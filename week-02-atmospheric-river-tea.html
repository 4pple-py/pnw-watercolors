Week 2 — PNW Watercolor Series

Atmospheric River Tea

Third atmospheric river in ten days. Licorice fern grips moss-sleeved maples while creeks run the color of steeped bark. Midday reads like dusk — the cloud deck isn’t a ceiling, it’s a floor pressed down. The tension: saturation without color — everything soaked, nothing bright.

Primary Trio

Moonglow

Bruise-light filtering through the cloud deck; granulating, tricolor-shifting, it does the atmospheric work of three pigments in one

Transparent Brown Oxide

Creek tannin, pure and clean; transparent enough to layer without muddying

Indanthrone Blue

Deep wet bark at full saturation; staining, non-granulating, the darkest blue that still reads as blue

Mixes & Ratios

Tannin Creek

3 Transparent Brown Oxide : 1 Moonglow

Tea-brown water over cobble — the creek running with a week’s worth of dissolved forest floor.

Trans. Brown Oxide
Moonglow
Both pigments are transparent, so this mix glows even in heavy application. Lay Transparent Brown Oxide first, wet, then touch Moonglow into the deeper pools — the granulation in Moonglow will settle into the crevices while the oxide stays smooth on the surface. This separation is the visual difference between shallow flow and standing depth. For faster water, use more oxide; for still pools, more Moonglow.

Bark in Rain

2 Indanthrone Blue : 1 Lunar Black

Bigleaf maple trunk mid-downpour — so saturated with water it’s lost its brown entirely and gone to blue-black.

Indanthrone Blue
Lunar Black
Lunar Black granulates dramatically — let it. Mix loosely on the palette and apply to damp paper so the granulation has room to separate. Indanthrone Blue anchors the color while Lunar Black provides the particulate texture of wet bark. This is a dark that still has temperature (cool) and texture (rough). For the wettest surfaces, add more water and let the granulation do the bark work. Don’t scrub.

Moss Sleeve

2 Perylene Green : 1 Raw Sienna

Epiphyte mat dripping — the moss that sleeves every maple branch, heavier now than the wood beneath it.

Perylene Green
Raw Sienna
Perylene Green stains and Raw Sienna granulates — together they give you the two-texture reality of moss over bark. Apply the mix to dry paper for defined moss edges, or to damp paper for the soft mass of an entire sleeved branch. Raw Sienna warms the green just enough to read as organic growth rather than paint. More Raw Sienna for older, browner moss; more Perylene Green for the actively growing tips.

Why These Colors?

The palette is engineered around transparency — seven of the ten pigments are fully transparent, because an atmospheric river turns the entire landscape into a lightbox of layered veils. Moonglow’s tricolor granulation (it separates into blue, red, and yellow on textured paper) mimics the strange shifting light under deep cloud cover. Transparent Brown Oxide is the cleanest tannin-colored pigment available — no opacity, no granulation, just pure dissolved-bark color. Lunar Black’s extreme granulation provides the only texture in what is otherwise a smooth, soaked world. Indanthrone Blue stains permanently, which is exactly what sustained rain does to every surface it touches. This palette doesn’t fight the monochrome — it finds the variation within it.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Moss-Sleeved Maple in Downpour

Subject: A single bigleaf maple in full rain, every branch wrapped in dripping moss, the trunk gone blue-black with saturation. The background is fog. The foreground is puddle.

The Bark in Rain mix (Indanthrone Blue + Lunar Black) gives you that rain-saturated trunk, while Moss Sleeve (Perylene Green + Raw Sienna) handles every epiphyte. Buff Titanium is the fog — the brightest value in a scene with no sun.

  • Wet the entire sheet and lay Buff Titanium + Moonglow as the fog ground first — keep it pale and let Moonglow’s granulation do the atmospheric shifting
  • Paint the trunk wet-into-wet with Indanthrone Blue + Lunar Black while the fog wash is still damp, so the edges bleed into mist
  • Once dry, add the moss sleeves with Perylene Green + Raw Sienna on dry paper for defined edges where moss meets air
  • Use a spray bottle to flick clean water onto the finished painting for rain-drop blooms — the transparent pigments will push aside beautifully
Prompt 2

Tannin Creek Over Cobble

Subject: A forest creek running tea-brown over smooth cobblestones, licorice ferns on the bank, the water surface broken by a single half-submerged log.

The Tannin Creek mix (Transparent Brown Oxide + Moonglow) is literally this water — transparent, glowing, with granulation settling into the deep pools. Burnt Sienna gives you the licorice fern spore lines as warm punctuation.

  • Paint the cobblestones first with Raw Sienna, leaving white paper gaps for water highlights — let them dry completely
  • Glaze the Tannin Creek mix over the cobbles in a single confident wash — the transparency will let the stones show through, which is how clear tannin water actually looks
  • Add deeper pools with more Moonglow, letting it granulate and settle where the water slows
  • Paint licorice ferns on the bank with Perylene Green + Raw Sienna, then add Burnt Sienna dots along the underside of each frond for the spore sori
Prompt 3

Road Runoff Joining the Creek

Subject: Where a gravel road’s runoff ditch meets the forest creek — two colors of water merging, the gray road water bleeding into the brown forest water. Rain is falling.

Payne’s Gray is the road runoff — cool, mineral, urban. Transparent Brown Oxide is the creek — warm, organic, forest. Where they meet on wet paper, the mixing happens naturally.

  • Divide the paper into two zones and wet the whole surface — lay Payne’s Gray on the road side and Transparent Brown Oxide on the creek side
  • Let them find each other at the confluence while the paper is still wet — the blending zone IS the subject, so don’t direct it too much
  • Once dry, add the road gravel with Lunar Black dry-brushed, and the creek bank with Raw Sienna
  • Quinacridone Burnt Orange makes a tiny alder catkin accent at the top of the frame — barely there, but it places the season
Prompt 4

Root Wad in Shadow

Subject: An uprooted tree’s root wad at the creek bank, the tangled roots holding a cave of darkness beneath. Moss and mud and pooling shadow.

Lunar Black’s extreme granulation gives you the shadow under the root wad with real texture — not flat dark, but particulate dark. Indanthrone Blue deepens it. Perylene Green + Raw Sienna handle the moss.

  • Paint the root wad’s dark interior first with Lunar Black on damp paper — let the granulation separate and settle for at least five minutes
  • Add Indanthrone Blue into the deepest recesses while the Lunar Black is still slightly damp for a dark that has both texture and depth
  • Paint the roots themselves with Transparent Brown Oxide for the exposed wood, Perylene Green + Raw Sienna for the mossy surfaces
  • The water pooling beneath the root wad is Moonglow at its most dilute — a pale, shifting wash that catches light the darkness above doesn’t
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Temperature runs cool-to-neutral with Burnt Sienna and Quinacridone Burnt Orange as the only true warms — use them sparingly or the rain feeling breaks. Moonglow and Lunar Black both granulate heavily; give them rough paper and time. Transparent Brown Oxide and Raw Sienna are close in hue but completely different in behavior — oxide is smooth and staining, sienna is granulating and liftable. Don’t substitute one for the other. This is a palette that rewards wet-in-wet work and patience. Let gravity and water do the mixing. The landscape is already doing the same thing outside your window.
