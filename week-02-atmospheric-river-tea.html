Mixes & Ratios

Rooibos Current

2 Transparent Brown Oxide : 1 Quinacridone Gold : touch Sedona Genuine

Mid-river flood water catching a break in the clouds — brown gone briefly gold.

Trans. Brown Oxide
Quin. Gold
Sedona Genuine
Transparent Brown Oxide and Quinacridone Gold are both fully transparent and staining, so this mix glows from the paper up even at heavy concentration. The Quinacridone Gold shifts the brown toward a warm amber that reads as light hitting tannin water — not yellow, not orange, but that specific golden-brown of rooibos held up to a window. Touch Sedona Genuine into the wet mix and its mineral granulation settles into the paper valleys, mimicking sediment suspended in current. Apply to wet paper for soft-edged flow; dry paper for the sharp line where fast water meets bank.

Low Ceiling

2 Payne’s Gray : 1 French Ultramarine : touch Raw Umber

The sky that isn’t a sky anymore — just weight pressing down with rain in it.

Payne’s Gray
French Ultramarine
Raw Umber
French Ultramarine granulates heavily and Payne’s Gray is smooth — together they produce a sky that has visible texture where rain is thickest and smooth passages where the cloud thins. The Raw Umber touch warms the gray just enough to keep it from reading as cold neutral; atmospheric river clouds carry subtropical moisture and they look it. Lay this mix in a single graded wash across the top third of your painting, tilting the board to let the granulation settle downward. Where you want the heaviest rain, add more Ultramarine while wet.

Bark Liquor

2 Burnt Sienna : 1 Burnt Umber : touch Yellow Ochre

Cedar bark steeping in standing water — the concentrated tannin at the bottom of every puddle.

Burnt Sienna
Burnt Umber
Yellow Ochre
Burnt Sienna is semi-transparent and warm; Burnt Umber is more opaque and cooler. Mixed, they produce a rich dark brown that still has internal temperature variation rather than going flat. The Yellow Ochre touch lifts the edges where this dark water catches ambient light — drop it in while the mix is still wet and it will bloom outward naturally. This is your darkest water color. Use it for standing puddles, backed-up culverts, and the still pools behind logjams where tannin concentrates. Burnt Sienna granulates slightly on rough paper, which adds sediment texture without effort.

Why These Colors?

This palette is built around browns on purpose. An atmospheric river turns the entire watershed into a steeping pot, and the visual range of tannin water — from pale amber to near-black — is wider than most people realize. Transparent Brown Oxide is the cleanest tannin pigment available: fully transparent, non-granulating, it layers without muddying. Raw Sienna and Yellow Ochre occupy similar warm territory but behave differently — Raw Sienna granulates and lifts easily, while Yellow Ochre is opaque and stays put, giving you both soft and hard edges in the amber range. French Ultramarine earns the only blue slot because its heavy granulation mimics rain-loaded clouds better than any smooth blue could. Quinacridone Gold is the surprise — it transforms the browns from muddy to luminous when glazed over them, the way actual light transforms actual floodwater. Sedona Genuine is a Primatek mineral pigment that granulates with real ground earth, settling into paper texture the way suspended sediment settles in slow current.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are built for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and what’s happening outside right now.

Prompt 1

Tannin River at Flood Stage

Subject: A PNW river running tea-brown at high water, carrying foam and debris. Overhanging alders on the far bank, the sky a solid gray pressing down to meet the treeline.

The Rooibos Current mix is the river surface — Transparent Brown Oxide glowing with Quinacridone Gold where light hits, Sedona Genuine granulating in the slow eddies. Low Ceiling handles the entire sky in one wash.

  • Paint the sky first with the Low Ceiling mix as a single graded wash, stronger at the top — let French Ultramarine’s granulation settle before touching anything else
  • The river is the Rooibos Current mix applied to wet paper in horizontal strokes, leaving thin white lines for foam streaks
  • Alders on the far bank are Raw Umber trunks with Payne’s Gray branches — keep them simple silhouettes against the sky
  • Drop Quinacridone Gold into the wet river wash where the eye should travel — flood water catches light in specific places, not everywhere
Prompt 2

Culvert Outfall After 48 Hours of Rain

Subject: A road culvert pouring brown water into a ditch, the concrete pipe stained with years of tannin. Gravel shoulder above, blackberry canes arching over the outfall.

Bark Liquor gives you the concentrated dark water pouring from the pipe. Raw Umber is the concrete — stained, cold, structural. Payne’s Gray handles the wet gravel road surface above.

  • Paint the culvert pipe with Raw Umber, leaving a hard-edged circle of white paper for the water coming out — fill that circle with Bark Liquor mix while the umber is still damp
  • The ditch water below is Transparent Brown Oxide thinned to a pale wash, getting darker toward the culvert with more Burnt Sienna
  • Wet gravel is Payne’s Gray dry-brushed over dry paper for texture, with Burnt Umber in the tire ruts
  • Blackberry canes are Burnt Umber with thorns scratched out using a palette knife while the paint is wet
Prompt 3

Alder Leaves in the Current

Subject: Close-up of tannin water flowing over cobblestones, carrying alder leaves. Some leaves are pinned against rocks, others spinning in small eddies. The water is clear enough to see the stones but stained deep amber.

Raw Sienna and Yellow Ochre build the cobblestones under water. Transparent Brown Oxide glazed over them IS the tannin water — transparent enough to let the stones read through. Quinacridone Gold catches where the surface breaks over a rock.

  • Paint the cobblestones first with Raw Sienna and Yellow Ochre, varying warm and cool, and let them dry completely
  • Glaze Transparent Brown Oxide over the entire stone field in one confident pass — the transparency lets every stone show through, which is exactly how tannin water looks
  • Alder leaves are Burnt Sienna with Burnt Umber veins, painted on top of the glaze once dry — some flat, some foreshortened as they spin
  • Use Quinacridone Gold for the bright crescent where water accelerates over a stone — a thin line of reflected sky light on moving water
Prompt 4

Flooded Pasture at Dusk

Subject: A low-lying farm field holding six inches of standing water, fence posts emerging from the flood. One bare oak silhouetted against the last amber light on the horizon. Everything else is gray.

The horizon band is Quinacridone Gold and Yellow Ochre — the only warm light in the scene. Payne’s Gray and French Ultramarine build the rest of the sky. The standing water reflects everything above it, giving you a mirror-image painting.

  • Wet the entire sheet and lay the sky: Payne’s Gray overhead grading to a narrow band of Quinacridone Gold and Yellow Ochre at the horizon
  • While still damp, paint the oak silhouette with Burnt Umber — let the edges bleed slightly into the wet sky for atmosphere
  • The standing water is the sky reflected: flip the same color sequence, Quinacridone Gold near the horizon line grading to Payne’s Gray in the foreground
  • Fence posts are Burnt Umber dry-brushed on once everything is dry, with Sedona Genuine at the waterline where mineral deposits and mud mark the flood level
Notes: All ten pigments are Daniel Smith extra fine watercolors. Sedona Genuine is a Primatek mineral pigment made from actual ground earth — it granulates with real mineral particles, settling into paper texture in ways synthetic pigments cannot replicate. Transparent Brown Oxide (PBr7) and Raw Sienna (PBr7) share a pigment number but behave very differently: the oxide is smooth and staining, the sienna granulates and lifts easily. Don’t substitute one for the other. French Ultramarine (PB29) granulates heavily — give it rough paper and time to settle. Quinacridone Gold (PO49) is transparent and staining; once it’s down, it stays. This palette rewards glazing in thin layers — build depth by stacking transparent washes rather than mixing darker on the palette. The browns look close in the tube but spread wide on paper.
