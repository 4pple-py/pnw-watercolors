# Pacific Northwest Watercolor Palettes — 12 Months

A complete set of twelve 10-color watercolor palettes representing each month of the year in the Pacific Northwest, built exclusively from Daniel Smith watercolors. Each palette includes a primary trio, a full 10-color table, three mixes with ratios, a color-choice explanation, and notes.

## 🎨 January — Iron Creek Winter

**Mood:** Heavy, saturated stillness — the deep Pacific Northwest winter when the sky presses down like wet wool and the only colors left are iron, moss, and the bruised violet of dusk at 4pm. Rain-blackened bark, steel-colored puddles reflecting nothing, and the occasional shock of rust where a sword fern has turned. This is a palette of weight and atmosphere, not sparkle.

### Primary Trio

A warm-leaning split-primary set that yields rich darks and granulating earth tones — chosen to paint the heaviness of midwinter rather than its cold clarity.

- 🔴 **Alizarin Crimson** — a deep, cool-warm red that granulates slightly; mixes powerful darks with blues and muted plums with violets
- 💛 **Yellow Ochre** — an opaque, earthy yellow; grounds every mix in PNW soil tones and keeps greens from going tropical
- 🔵 **Indanthrone Blue** — a deep, granulating near-black blue; mixes velvety darks and moody blue-grays without the intensity of Phthalo

**3. 🌾 Dead Bracken**

| Raw Umber | 1 |

### Why These Colors?

January in the PNW lowlands isn't about crisp snow — it's about saturation without chroma, where everything is soaked dark but nothing is truly bright. I built this palette around Indanthrone Blue and Moonglow because both granulate beautifully, giving you built-in texture that mimics the mottled quality of winter skies and wet stone without any extra effort. Alizarin Crimson and Yellow Ochre anchor the warm side with enough subtlety to avoid pulling the palette toward autumn — Ochre's opacity keeps washes grounded in earth rather than floating into candy territory, and Alizarin's blue lean means your darks stay rich instead of muddy. Perylene Green and Lamp Black give you access to near-blacks that still feel alive with color, while Cobalt Violet and Burnt Sienna provide the two temperature accents you need for twilight skies and dead fern detail. Buff Titanium is your fog and highlight workhorse — it lifts values without bleaching out the mood.

### Notes

> This palette leans heavier and warmer than a typical January selection — it's designed to capture the sodden, mossy, iron-earth quality of lowland PNW winter rather than alpine snow and ice. Moonglow is the secret weapon: its built-in granulation gives atmospheric effects for free. Every color here mixes cleanly with every other because the palette avoids opposing staining pigments. ## 🎨 February — Thaw & Thorn

**Mood:** The slow exhale between storms — February in the Pacific Northwest is winter beginning to doubt itself. Skunk cabbage unfurls violent yellow in black bog water, the first crocus needles through cold soil, and on rare clear afternoons the Olympic Mountains go sharp-edged and blue-white against a sky that hasn't been that color in months. But the rain always comes back. This palette holds the tension between dormancy and the first reckless reach toward spring.

### Primary Trio

A split-primary set tuned for the collision of lingering cold and first warmth — cool blue for persistent winter sky and water, a transparent warm yellow for the shock of early bloom, and a versatile red that bridges both temperatures.

- 🔴 **Quinacridone Rose** — a cool, transparent pink-red; mixes luminous violets with blues and clean oranges with yellows without muddying
- 💛 **New Gamboge** — a warm, transparent golden yellow; captures the punch of skunk cabbage and crocus without the opacity of earth yellows
- 🔵 **French Ultramarine** — a warm-leaning granulating blue; mixes rich purples with reds and soft greens with yellows, with built-in texture

### Why These Colors?

February is the first month where you need both warmth and cold on your palette simultaneously — January can get away with all cool, but February demands the violent yellow of skunk cabbage against steel-blue storm light. I chose New Gamboge over the earth yellows because February's first blooms are transparent and luminous, not opaque and dusty. Quinacridone Rose pairs with French Ultramarine to produce the rich violets you see in late-afternoon storm clouds, and both are transparent enough that layered washes stay glowing. Cobalt Teal Blue captures the specific icy clarity of a rare February clear sky — no other blue in your collection has that minty, glacial quality. Potter's Pink is the secret pick here: its gentle granulation gives you mountain alpenglow and dawn clouds that no amount of mixing Quinacridone Rose with white could replicate. Carbazole Violet and Indigo handle the darks with enough color to stay interesting, and Raw Sienna bridges the gap between the dead-grass earth tones still lingering from January and the new growth pushing through.

### Notes

> February's palette runs wider in temperature range than January's — you need access to both genuine warmth (skunk cabbage, crocus, alpenglow) and genuine cold (storm sky, snowmelt, bare branches). The transparency of the primary trio keeps layered washes luminous, which matters because February light in the PNW has a crystalline quality on clear days that opaque pigments would kill. All ten colors are from your Daniel Smith collection.

## 🎨 March — Green Returning

**Mood:** Explosive, tender, and overwhelmingly green — March in the Pacific Northwest is when the land remembers it's alive. New moss glows electric on every surface, fiddlehead ferns uncurl in slow spirals, and the willows along river banks go gold-green seemingly overnight. Rain is constant but warmer now, and the light through clouds has shifted from pewter to pearl. Cherry blossoms erupt in neighborhoods while the mountains still hold snow. This palette captures the first real surge of life pushing through winter's holdout.

### Primary Trio

A high-key, transparent trio built for layering luminous greens and soft atmospheric washes — the engine of early spring.

- 🔴 **Pyrrol Scarlet** — a warm, clean red; mixes vibrant oranges with yellows and serves as the complement to knock greens back when needed
- 💛 **Hansa Yellow Medium** — a bright, transparent mid-yellow; the workhorse for mixing every shade of spring green without going chalky
- 🔵 **Cobalt Blue** — a true-neutral blue with gentle granulation; mixes clean greens with yellows and soft violets with reds, never overpowering

### Why These Colors?

March is the month where green takes over the entire conversation, so I gave you three different ways to get there: Hansa Yellow Medium mixed with Cobalt Blue for custom greens, Sap Green as a ready-made convenience, and Cascade Green and Green Gold as cool and warm accents at opposite ends of the green spectrum. Cobalt Blue was chosen over the Phthalos and Ultramarines because its moderate tinting strength won't bulldoze your yellows in green mixes — you stay in control of the temperature. Pyrrol Scarlet serves double duty as the red primary and as the complement you reach for when a green passage gets too intense and needs to be grayed down. Opera Pink is a deliberate choice for cherry blossoms: it's the only pigment in your collection that truly captures that fluorescent, almost unreal pink, and its impermanence mirrors the flowers themselves. Raw Sienna keeps the earth present beneath all that new growth, and Payne's Gray with Buff Titanium handle the sky without competing with the greens below.

### Notes

> March's palette is the widest in chroma range so far — you need access to electric greens, hot pinks, and soft atmospheric grays all in the same painting. The primary trio is deliberately moderate in tinting strength so that mixing stays controllable even when you're layering fast in wet conditions. Opera Pink will fade over months in strong light; if permanence matters, substitute Quinacridone Rose at lower concentration, but you'll lose that specific fluorescent quality. All ten colors are from your Daniel Smith collection.

## 🎨 April — Trillium Light

**Mood:** Soft, radiant, and teeming — April in the Pacific Northwest is spring arriving with both hands full. Trillium carpets the forest floor in white and deep rose, bigleaf maples finally leaf out in translucent lime, and the rain shifts from cold and punishing to warm and almost welcome. Light filters through new canopy in dappled gold-green, streams run high and milky with snowmelt, and every trail smells like wet earth and crushed salal. The palette is brighter and softer than March — less shock, more radiance.

### Primary Trio

A luminous, transparent trio designed for layering light through leaves — warm enough for dappled sun, cool enough for forest shade.

- 🔴 **Rose Madder Permanent** — a soft, granulating rose-red; mixes gentle pinks and muted mauves without the intensity of Quinacridone Rose
- 💛 **Hansa Yellow Light** — a cool, transparent lemon-yellow; the ideal base for the pale, acidic greens of brand-new leaves
- 🔵 **Cerulean Blue Chromium** — a granulating, opaque sky blue; holds its own in wet washes and mixes soft, natural greens with yellows

### Why These Colors?

April's light in the PNW is fundamentally different from the previous months — it comes through things rather than bouncing off them. New leaves are translucent, petals glow from within, and even the rain feels illuminated. That's why I anchored this palette on transparency: Rose Madder Permanent, Hansa Yellow Light, and Cerulean Blue Chromium all let the paper breathe through the paint. Cerulean Blue Chromium over Cobalt Blue was a deliberate choice — its granulation mimics the broken texture of spring sky seen through branches, and its slight opacity means it holds its ground in wet-in-wet washes where a more transparent blue would diffuse into nothing. Green Gold is essential this month because no mixture of yellow and blue quite captures the warm, pollen-dusted quality of new growth. Quinacridone Gold and Transparent Brown Oxide handle the forest floor with warmth and luminosity — opaques would deaden the duff. Chinese White earns its place for trillium and mist effects where you need petals to sit on top of dark washes. Indigo provides the only real dark, keeping the palette's value range wide enough to paint convincing forest interiors.

### Notes

> April's palette is the most luminous so far — almost every color is transparent or semi-transparent, reflecting the quality of light that defines PNW spring. The exception is Cerulean Blue Chromium and Chinese White, both opaque, which serve specific purposes: sky texture and petal opacity. This palette pairs especially well with rough or cold-pressed paper, where the granulating pigments (Rose Madder Permanent, Cerulean Blue Chromium, Cobalt Violet) will separate and create texture automatically. All ten colors are from your Daniel Smith collection.

## 🎨 May — Rhododendron Fire

**Mood:** Lush, saturated, and triumphant — May in the Pacific Northwest is the crescendo of spring before summer simplifies everything. Rhododendrons detonate in magenta, coral, and white along every shaded road. The greens have deepened from April's tender lime into full-bodied emerald, and the understory is so thick you can't see ten feet into the woods. Evenings stretch past 8pm now, and the light turns amber-gold through the canopy. Streams still run strong with the last snowmelt. This is the month where the PNW earns its reputation — everything alive, everything growing, everything almost too much.

### Primary Trio

A high-saturation trio with real punch — built to handle the intense blooms and deep greens of peak spring without washing out.

- 🔴 **Deep Scarlet** — a rich, warm red with excellent transparency; bridges the gap between the hot magentas of rhododendron and the warm tones of evening light
- 💛 **Nickel Azo Yellow** — a deep, earthy golden yellow; less acidic than Hansa, it captures late-afternoon light filtering through mature canopy

### Why These Colors?

May demanded a palette with more saturation and more darks than any month so far. I chose Phthalo Blue (Red Shade) over its green-shade sibling because May's greens have deepened past the cool, acidic stage — you need warmer, richer greens now, and PB(RS) mixed with Nickel Azo Yellow gives you exactly that dark emerald. Deep Scarlet is the red primary because it straddles the warm side of the rhododendron spectrum while still being transparent enough to layer over other washes. Opera Pink and Quinacridone Rose together give you the full magenta range rhododendrons demand — no single pigment covers it. Nickel Azo Yellow was chosen over brighter yellows because May's light has shifted from spring's pale lemon to a richer, more golden quality, especially in the long evenings. Undersea Green and Carbazole Violet handle the deep forest darks with color rather than blackness, and Quinacridone Burnt Orange captures the specific warm glow of Pacific madrone bark and evening-lit tree trunks. Hooker's Green and Green Gold give you the mid-range foliage without constant mixing.

### Notes

> This is the most saturated palette in the series so far — May in the PNW is genuinely intense, and trying to paint it with muted colors reads as dishonest. The Phthalo Blue (Red Shade) is powerful, so use it sparingly in mixes; a little goes a long way. The three-red approach (Deep Scarlet, Opera Pink, Quinacridone Rose) might seem excessive, but rhododendrons genuinely span that range from warm scarlet through hot magenta to cool rose, often on the same bush. All ten colors are from your Daniel Smith collection.

- 🔴 **Transparent Brown Oxide** — a warm, transparent iron oxide; the exact color of wet deciduous bark and fallen leaf mulch — mixes soft warm grays and natural earth tones
- 💛 **Raw Sienna** — a warm, semi-transparent golden earth; the last color left in the landscape — dead grass, remaining oak leaves, muddy puddle reflections
- 🔵 **Ultramarine Blue** — a warm, granulating blue; mixes the soft blue-grays of November sky and the muted violets in bare-branch shadows

### Mixes & Ratios

**1. 🪨 Bare Alder Grove**
**Ratio:** 3 Payne's Gray : 2 Buff Titanium : 1 Lavender
*The pale, ghostly trunks and tangled branches of a leafless red alder stand against November overcast — silver-white bark against silver-gray sky, barely distinguishable*

| Color | Parts |
|-------|-------|
| Payne's Gray | 3 |
| Buff Titanium | 2 |
| Lavender | 1 |

> This mix produces the specific silver-gray that defines November in the PNW — not warm, not cold, just flat and honest. Payne's Gray establishes the value, Buff Titanium lifts it into the pale range of alder bark and overcast sky, and Lavender adds the faintest cool violet shift that separates November light from January's iron. Use diluted for sky, slightly more concentrated for bark, and you can paint trunk against sky with almost no value change — just temperature.

**2. 🍂 November Floor**
**Ratio:** 3 Transparent Brown Oxide : 2 Burnt Umber : 1 Perylene Green
*The sodden carpet of decomposing leaves, fir needles, and dark earth that covers every trail and sidewalk — November underfoot*

| Color | Parts |
|-------|-------|
| Transparent Brown Oxide | 3 |
| Burnt Umber | 2 |
| Perylene Green | 1 |

> Transparent Brown Oxide's warmth gives the leaf litter its remaining color while Burnt Umber pushes it into the dark, saturated-wet state of leaves that have been rained on for weeks. Perylene Green is the persistent moss and the green stain of decomposition — even in November, the PNW forest floor is never purely brown. Vary the Perylene Green for mossy versus leaf-dominant areas.

**3. 🌁 River in Flood**
**Ratio:** 3 Neutral Tint : 2 Ultramarine Blue : 1 Raw Sienna
*The high, fast, brown-gray water of a November river after a week of rain — carrying silt, leaves, and broken branches, running bank to bank*

| Color | Parts |
|-------|-------|
| Neutral Tint | 3 |
| Ultramarine Blue | 2 |
| Raw Sienna | 1 |

> Neutral Tint provides the dark, opaque quality of silt-heavy water. Ultramarine Blue's granulation adds the movement and texture of current, and Raw Sienna is the suspended clay and sediment that turns every PNW river brown in November. This mix should look murky and heavy — that's honest. For white water over rapids, leave paper white and glaze this over the surrounding water to make the foam pop by contrast.

### Why These Colors?

November demanded the most restrained palette in the series — a deliberate pulling-back after October's blaze. I chose Transparent Brown Oxide over Burnt Sienna as the red primary because November's warmth is subdued and transparent, not orange or flashy — it's wet bark and decomposing leaves, not turning foliage. Raw Sienna won the yellow slot because it's the only warm color the landscape still offers: dead grass, lingering oak leaves, muddy puddles. Ultramarine Blue returns because its granulation is essential for the soft, broken texture of November skies and wet surfaces. Payne's Gray and Neutral Tint together give you the full value range of November's flat light — Payne's for the mid-tone atmosphere, Neutral Tint for the true darks. Lavender is the subtle choice that makes this palette work: November mist and distance in the PNW carry a specific cool violet cast that pure gray can't capture. Perylene Green is the only green because the conifers are the only green thing left, and they've darkened to near-black. Ivory Black earns its place for the graphic, structural elements — bare branches against sky, power lines, crows — that define November's stripped-down visual language. Buff Titanium handles the narrow range of highlights in a month where nothing is truly bright.

### Notes

> November is the palette that tests your comfort with restraint. After the full-saturation months of May through October, it can feel like something is missing — but that's November. The challenge is painting beauty with almost no chroma, relying instead on value, temperature, and texture. Ultramarine Blue's granulation and Lavender's cool tint do most of the atmospheric work. If you find yourself reaching for brighter colors, resist — November in the PNW earns its gray. This palette works especially well for urban-landscape paintings: wet streets, bare street trees, rain on windows, the glow of early-evening lights against dark sky. ## 🎨 December — Solstice Dark

**Mood:** Dark, intimate, and quietly luminous — December in the Pacific Northwest is the deepest inward turn of the year. The sun sets before 4:30pm and the world contracts to what's lit: headlights on wet roads, warm windows in rain, the faint glow of moss-covered stumps in twilight. The first lowland snow may come and go in a single night, leaving everything slicked and silver. Conifers stand black against indigo sky, frost rims every surface at dawn, and the rivers run cold and clear again after November's floods. This is a palette of darkness punctuated by small, specific lights.

### Primary Trio

A deep, cool trio built for painting the darkest month — anchored in shadow with just enough warmth to capture the small fires that keep December bearable.

- 🔴 **Pyrrol Red** — a warm, opaque true red; the specific red of holly berries, winter finch breasts, and holiday lights glimpsed through rain-streaked windows
- 💛 **Hansa Yellow Medium** — a bright, transparent mid-yellow; the color of artificial warmth — porch lights, candles, window glow against blue-black evening
- 🔵 **Indanthrone Blue** — a deep, granulating near-black blue; the dominant color of December — predawn sky, wet pavement, conifer silhouettes, the long dark

**1. 🌃 Solstice Sky**
**Ratio:** 3 Indanthrone Blue : 2 Moonglow : 1 Cobalt Violet

| Color | Parts |

> Indanthrone Blue provides the dominant darkness while Moonglow's granulation breaks it into a living, breathing sky rather than a flat wash. Cobalt Violet shifts the shadow side of the sky toward the specific red-violet you see at the horizon after sunset in December. Apply wet and let the granulating pigments separate on the paper — the texture they create is the texture of actual December sky. Leave small gaps of dry paper for stars on clear nights.

**2. 💡 Window Glow**
**Ratio:** 3 Hansa Yellow Medium : 1 Pyrrol Red : ½ Buff Titanium

| Color | Parts |
|-------|-------|
| Hansa Yellow Medium | 3 |
| Pyrrol Red | 1 |
| Buff Titanium | ½ |

> Paint the dark surroundings first and leave the window shape as white paper, then drop this mix in while the edges of the dark wash are still slightly damp. The color will bloom outward into the dark — that halo is the glow. Hansa Yellow Medium provides the warm light, Pyrrol Red shifts it toward incandescent warmth, and Buff Titanium gives the glow enough body to read as light hitting curtains or glass rather than empty air. For holiday string lights, use tiny dots of this mix at full concentration.

Apply on dry paper with a fairly dry brush for the sharpest frost texture, or on damp paper for a softer, melting-frost effect.

### Why These Colors?

December closes the year by returning to the deep, cool palette territory of January — but with key differences that reflect the month's unique character. Indanthrone Blue returns from January as the blue primary because December is fundamentally about darkness, and Indanthrone's granulation gives that darkness life. But where January's palette was all earth and iron, December gets Pyrrol Red and Hansa Yellow Medium for the human-made warmth that punctuates the natural dark: window light, holiday decorations, candles. These are the only truly bright, saturated colors in the palette, and their rarity makes them powerful — a single warm window against a full sheet of Indanthrone Blue tells the whole story of December. Moonglow returns for atmospheric granulation, and Cobalt Violet provides the twilight horizon color that December owns more than any other month. Lunar Black is the textural choice over Ivory Black or Lamp Black — its heavy granulation creates the visual roughness of wet stone and frozen gravel. Mayan Blue Genuine is the most unusual selection in the entire twelve-month series: a Primatek pigment ground from real minerals that granulates into crystalline patterns genuinely reminiscent of frost and ice. Perylene Green holds down the conifer darks, and Burnt Sienna provides the only earth warmth for the few remaining organic textures — wet cedar, clinging oak leaves, bark caught in streetlight.

### Notes

> December's palette is a bookend to January's — both are dark and cool, but December adds the element of human warmth against natural cold that January's purely landscape-focused palette didn't need. The Pyrrol Red and Hansa Yellow Medium are deliberately restricted to small, specific uses (windows, lights, berries) rather than broad washes — December's power comes from how little warmth there is, not how much. Mayan Blue Genuine is the most expensive pigment in any of the twelve palettes, but its frost effects are irreplaceable. If you prefer not to use it, substitute Cobalt Teal Blue with a touch of Chinese White, though you'll lose the crystalline granulation. This palette works especially well for urban nocturnes — wet streets, holiday shopfronts, headlights in rain. All ten colors are from your Daniel Smith collection.
