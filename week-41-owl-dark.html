Owl Dark

October dark comes early and stays. The barred owl calls from a Doug fir snag at 4:47 p.m. and it’s already dusk — night doesn’t fall here, it rises from the ground, pooling under hemlocks, climbing trunks, until only the last gray ribbon of sky remains. You hear wings before you see anything. Then the silence after, which is worse.

Primary Trio

Indanthrone Blue

Deep night blue that holds its hue even at full concentration; the sky after civil twilight ends, blue-black with memory of color

Lunar Black

Granulating blackness that separates on paper into warm and cool particles; owl feather darkness, textured and alive

Burnt Sienna

Owl feather warmth — the brown-orange barring on breast and belly, warm as a heartbeat in all that cold dark

Mixes & Ratios

Feather Night

2 Indanthrone Blue : 1 Lunar Black : ¼ Burnt Sienna

The darkness between an owl’s wings — not empty black but a living dark, blue-shifted and textured with warmth.

Indanthrone Blue
Lunar Black
Burnt Sienna
Indanthrone Blue provides the structural darkness — a blue so deep it reads as black at full concentration, but holds a blue undertone that keeps the dark alive. Lunar Black granulates as it dries, its particles separating into warm and cool clusters that mimic the micro-texture of owl flight feathers. The whisper of Burnt Sienna prevents the mix from going cold and clinical; it’s the metabolic warmth in a living creature, detectable even in darkness. Apply heavily wet to cold-pressed paper and tilt the board — let the Lunar Black granulation settle into the paper’s valleys. The result is a dark that has depth and direction, not a flat hole in the painting.

Actual Moonlight

2 Moonglow : 1 Buff Titanium : ¼ French Ultramarine

The color of moonlight on wet bark — granulating violet-blue that separates into its component hues like light through mist.

Moonglow
Buff Titanium
French Ultramarine
Moonglow is Daniel Smith’s granulating triad mix — it separates on paper into blue, violet, and warm tones, mimicking the way actual moonlight fractures through atmosphere. Buff Titanium lifts the value without killing the color; it’s a warm, creamy semi-opaque that reads as diffused light rather than white paint. French Ultramarine deepens the blue component where moonlight hits wet surfaces and concentrates. Apply to damp paper and watch the Moonglow granulate into its constituent pigments — you’ll see blues pull away from violets, exactly the way moonlit surfaces shimmer between colors. This is not a mix you can fully control, and that’s the point.

Owl Bark

2 Raw Umber : 1 Transparent Brown Oxide : ½ Lunar Black

The bark the owl sits against — Doug fir roughness in near-darkness, where bark and bird become the same surface.

Raw Umber
Trans. Brown Oxide
Lunar Black
Raw Umber is the structural earth tone — cool, muted, the color of bark in low light when warmth drains from surfaces. Transparent Brown Oxide glazes over the Raw Umber to build depth without opacity, adding a red-brown warmth that reads as texture in bark furrows. Lunar Black darkens the shadow side and contributes its signature granulation, which settles into the paper’s texture to suggest bark ridges. This mix is designed for the tree trunk the owl perches on — apply in vertical strokes, varying the ratio to create the irregular pattern of Doug fir bark. Where the owl sits against the trunk, let this mix bleed into the Feather Night mix so bird and tree merge into one dark form.

Why These Colors?

Owl Dark demands darks with character — not just low values but textured, living darkness. Lunar Black granulates beautifully; it settles into the paper’s surface the way owl feathers settle, each particle finding its place. Indanthrone Blue provides the deep night sky that isn’t quite black — it holds just enough blue to read as sky rather than void, and at full concentration it’s one of the darkest transparent pigments Daniel Smith makes. Moonglow, named for this exact light, granulates into blues and violets like actual moonlight splitting through wet air. Burnt Sienna and Raw Umber carry the owl feather tones — the warm barring on breast feathers, the earth-brown of back and wing. Transparent Brown Oxide layers bark texture without going opaque. Ivory Black handles the absolute darks where even Indanthrone can’t go deep enough — the inside of a tree cavity, the gap between branches. French Ultramarine pushes the upper sky toward its cooler, redder blue. And Yellow Ochre — one bright note in all this darkness — is the owl’s eye, the thing you see when everything else has disappeared. Buff Titanium is moonlight itself, catching the owl’s pale breast and the smooth spots on birch bark.

Painting Prompts

These are paintings you make in the quiet hours. Low light, slow looking. The palette is built for darkness that breathes — lean into the granulation and let the pigments do what they do.

Prompt 1

Barred Owl on a Doug Fir Snag

Subject: A barred owl perched on a broken-top Doug fir snag, facing the viewer. Last light behind. The owl’s breast feathers catch the faintest glow while the rest of the bird merges with the dark trunk. Forest floor invisible below.

The Feather Night mix (Indanthrone Blue + Lunar Black + Burnt Sienna) builds the owl’s dark upper body, while Buff Titanium and Burnt Sienna handle the pale, barred breast. Yellow Ochre for those forward-facing eyes — the focal point of the entire painting.

  • Wet the entire sheet and lay in a graded wash of Indanthrone Blue from top to bottom, darker at the edges — establish the night before you establish the subject
  • The owl’s body is the Feather Night mix applied to still-damp paper, letting Lunar Black’s granulation build feather texture automatically — resist the urge to paint individual feathers
  • Breast barring is Burnt Sienna stripes over dry Buff Titanium — use a rigger brush and quick horizontal marks, slightly irregular, wider apart at the belly than at the throat
  • Yellow Ochre eyes go in last, at full concentration, on completely dry paper — the sharpest edges in the painting should be the eyes, everything else soft and dark
Prompt 2

Moonlit Forest Floor

Subject: Looking down through hemlock branches at a moonlit forest floor. Patches of silver light between tree trunks. Sword ferns catching the glow. Everything else in deep shadow. The ground itself seems to emit light.

The Actual Moonlight mix (Moonglow + Buff Titanium + French Ultramarine) paints the silver patches on the ground. The Owl Bark mix darkens the trunks. Indanthrone Blue overhead. The granulation in both Moonglow and Lunar Black does the heavy lifting.

  • Reserve the moonlit patches with masking fluid before painting — these will be the Actual Moonlight mix applied later, and they need to glow against the surrounding dark
  • Build the forest dark in layers: Indanthrone Blue wash first, then Lunar Black into the deepest shadows while the blue is still damp — the granulation will create the illusion of depth between trees
  • Sword fern fronds in the light patches are Raw Umber with a touch of Moonglow — moonlight drains color from green things, shifting them toward blue-gray
  • Tree trunks are the Owl Bark mix applied with a flat brush in single vertical strokes — don’t go back in. The granulation of Lunar Black will texture the bark for you
Prompt 3

Owl in Flight — Silent Approach

Subject: A great horned owl mid-flight, wings fully spread, approaching through a gap between trees. Head-on angle, talons extended. The background is a blur of dark forest; the owl is the sharp thing moving through it.

Lunar Black for the wings — its granulation separates into the warm and cool zones of flight feathers. Burnt Sienna and Raw Umber for the face and ear tufts. Ivory Black in the deepest wing shadows. Buff Titanium for the pale chin and underwing patches.

  • Paint the forest background first as a soft, out-of-focus wash of Indanthrone Blue and Lunar Black — keep everything blurred and undefined to push it behind the sharp-edged owl
  • The owl’s wings are Lunar Black at various dilutions — full strength at the leading edge, diluted toward the trailing edge where light passes through. Let the granulation separate naturally
  • Burnt Sienna facial disk and ear tufts go in with a small round brush on dry paper — these warm tones against the surrounding blue-black create the focal contrast
  • Talons are Raw Umber darkened with Ivory Black, painted with sharp, precise strokes — these and the Yellow Ochre eyes are the only truly hard edges in the composition
Prompt 4

Dusk Treeline — First Owl Call

Subject: A PNW treeline at dusk, silhouetted against a sky that’s transitioning from deep blue to blue-black. The last color in the sky is a thin band at the horizon. One tree has a dark shape on a high branch — the owl, barely distinguishable from the tree itself.

French Ultramarine and Indanthrone Blue grade the sky from upper darkness to horizon light. Ivory Black and Lunar Black build the treeline silhouette. Moonglow adds the first hint of moonrise at the horizon. Buff Titanium is the thin bright band where sky still holds light.

  • This is a sky painting first — wet the upper two-thirds and grade from French Ultramarine at the top through Indanthrone Blue in the middle to Buff Titanium thinned at the horizon
  • While the sky is still damp, drop Moonglow along the horizon where moonrise begins — its granulation will separate into violet and blue, suggesting the complex color of early moonlight through atmosphere
  • The treeline silhouette is Ivory Black with Lunar Black, applied to dry paper in one confident pass — Doug firs are pointed, hemlocks are droopy, alders are rounded. Vary the shapes
  • The owl on the branch is just a subtle thickening of the silhouette — suggest, don’t detail. If viewers have to look twice to find the owl, you’ve painted it right
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Indanthrone Blue (PB60) is one of the darkest transparent blues available — it holds its blue character even at masstone. Lunar Black is a granulating black made from genuine mineral pigments; it separates into warm and cool particles as it dries, creating texture no carbon black can match. Moonglow is Daniel Smith’s proprietary granulating convenience mix of Ultramarine Blue, Viridian, and a quinacridone — it separates on paper into its component hues. Transparent Brown Oxide (PBr7) is a fully transparent iron oxide, ideal for layered bark glazes. Buff Titanium (PW6:1) is a warm, semi-opaque white tint — it lifts values without the chalkiness of Chinese White.
