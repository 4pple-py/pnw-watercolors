Alpine Blaze

Mountain huckleberries ripen under hard bright light. Alpine meadows at peak — lupine blue, paintbrush red, everything saturated by thin air and UV. Sharp shadows on scree. Summer at altitude is not gentle; it’s compressed and fierce, burning through a short window. The meadow has weeks, not months, and it knows it.

Lemon Yellow

Alpine buttercup and glacier lily, the sharp cool yellow of flowers at timberline; transparent, cool, clean as altitude

Cadmium Red Medium Hue

Indian paintbrush, the scarlet bracts that out-compete everything; opaque, strong, unyielding

Phthalo Blue GS

Lupine field at distance, blue concentrated by altitude; transparent, staining, dangerously strong

Mixes & Ratios

Paintbrush Blaze

2 Cadmium Red Medium Hue : 1 Transparent Pyrrol Orange : ¼ Lemon Yellow

Scarlet bracts that out-compete everything in the meadow — the color that draws your eye from a quarter-mile out.

Cad Red Med Hue
Pyrrol Orange
Lemon Yellow
Cadmium Red Medium Hue is opaque by nature — it covers, it dominates, it doesn’t negotiate. That’s paintbrush. Transparent Pyrrol Orange fires up the bract edges where the sun catches them. Lemon Yellow at the base where the bracts transition to stem. Apply with conviction; paintbrush is the alpine meadow’s loudest resident. Use minimal water — the opacity is the point. These bracts don’t glow from within; they shout from the surface.

Lupine Depth

2 Phthalo Blue GS : 1 Cobalt Violet : ½ Indanthrone Blue

Banner petals catching thin-air UV — lupine blue intensified by altitude into something almost unreal.

Phthalo Blue GS
Cobalt Violet
Indanthrone Blue
Phthalo Blue GS is the backbone — staining, transparent, the blue that defines lupine at distance. Cobalt Violet adds the true violet of individual flowers seen up close, and its granulation creates the texture of clustered petals on the raceme. Indanthrone Blue deepens the shadow between whorls of flowers. Apply the Phthalo first, then touch Cobalt Violet into it while wet — the granulation of the violet against the smooth phthalo creates the perfect lupine spike texture.

Huckleberry Ripe

2 Indanthrone Blue : 1 Cobalt Violet : ½ Transparent Pyrrol Orange

Dark blue-purple berry with dusty bloom — the fruit you eat faster than you can pick.

Indanthrone Blue
Cobalt Violet
Pyrrol Orange
Huckleberry is darker than you think — Indanthrone Blue gets the value right, that near-black blue-purple. Cobalt Violet adds the violet shift visible in direct light, and its granulation mimics the dusty bloom on the berry’s skin. Transparent Pyrrol Orange is the hidden warmth — huckleberries taste warm even though they look cold, and this trace of orange keeps the mix alive. Apply as small, round, individual berry forms. Let the Cobalt Violet granulate — that granulation is your bloom.

Why These Colors?

Alpine meadows demand maximum chroma because UV-saturated light at altitude makes everything more vivid than lowland eyes expect. Cadmium Red Medium Hue’s opacity mirrors the physical opacity of paintbrush bracts — they’re not translucent flowers, they’re solid color. Phthalo Blue GS at altitude reads differently than in the lowlands; it’s concentrated and hard, like the sky itself. Cobalt Violet is the alpine specialist — its granulation serves both lupine and huckleberry. Indanthrone Blue handles darks at altitude, where shadows are sharper and the contrast range is extreme. Lemon Yellow is the coolest yellow available, appropriate for flowers adapted to snowmelt conditions.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Paintbrush and Lupine Meadow

Subject: An alpine meadow split between red paintbrush and blue lupine, with a snowfield remnant and dark krummholz conifers in the background. The classic PNW subalpine postcard, but you’re painting it because it actually looks like this.

Cadmium Red Medium Hue’s opacity is physically correct for paintbrush bracts — they’re solid surfaces, not translucent petals. Phthalo Blue GS at altitude dilution gives you lupine fields. The two colors fight for attention exactly the way the flowers do.

  • Paint the lupine field first with dilute Phthalo Blue GS — at distance, lupine reads as a blue haze, not individual flowers. Let it dry completely
  • Paintbrush goes in with Cadmium Red Medium Hue at near-full concentration, minimal water — the opacity is the point. Each cluster is a few confident dabs, not careful brushwork
  • The meadow grass between flowers is Sap Green mixed with Lemon Yellow — keep it lighter and cooler than lowland greens to read as alpine
  • Krummholz trees in the background are Indanthrone Blue + Sap Green, twisted and wind-bent — small, dark shapes that anchor the bright meadow
Prompt 2

Lupine Spike Close-Up

Subject: A single lupine raceme filling the page vertically — individual banner petals visible, the whorled structure clear, with a bumblebee working the lower flowers.

The Lupine Depth mix (Phthalo Blue GS + Cobalt Violet + Indanthrone Blue) was built for exactly this level of detail. Cobalt Violet’s granulation creates petal texture that you couldn’t paint by hand.

  • Paint the spike from top (buds, lighter) to bottom (open flowers, darker) — lay Phthalo Blue GS first, then touch Cobalt Violet into each whorl of open flowers while wet
  • The granulation of Cobalt Violet against smooth Phthalo Blue creates the clustered-petal texture automatically — don’t fight it
  • Indanthrone Blue in the deep shadows between whorls, where flowers overlap and light can’t reach
  • The bee is Hansa Yellow Light body with Indanthrone Blue wings — keep it small and slightly out of focus so the lupine stays the subject
Prompt 3

Huckleberry Picking at Timberline

Subject: A hand reaching into a huckleberry bush, fingers already stained purple — the berries dark against bright green leaves, with an alpine vista dropping away behind.

The Huckleberry Ripe mix (Indanthrone Blue + Cobalt Violet + Transparent Pyrrol Orange) captures the near-black blue-purple of ripe berries, while Cobalt Violet’s granulation mimics the dusty bloom. Cerulean Blue Chromium and Hansa Yellow Light handle the alpine backdrop.

  • The berries are small and round — use the Huckleberry Ripe mix with a round brush, one berry per stroke. Let the Cobalt Violet granulate for the bloom effect
  • Fingers and hand in dilute Raw Sienna or Burnt Sienna (borrow from the scree trail color), with Indanthrone Blue-Cobalt Violet stains at the fingertips
  • Huckleberry leaves are Sap Green with Lemon Yellow at the edges where sun hits — small, oval, arranged alternately on wiry branches
  • The background alpine vista is a graded wash: Cerulean Blue Chromium sky, then distant ridges in very dilute Indanthrone Blue, soft and out of focus
Prompt 4

Scree Trail at Noon

Subject: A rocky alpine trail crossing a scree field, sharp shadows on angular rocks, with scattered wildflowers at the trail edges and a hard blue sky overhead.

Burnt Sienna and Hansa Yellow Light are the scree — iron-red rock and sun-blasted granite. Cerulean Blue Chromium gives you the deeper, harder alpine sky. The wildflower accents are already mixed and ready.

  • Block in the scree with a variegated wash of Burnt Sienna and Hansa Yellow Light, letting them mingle on wet paper — the irregular mixing creates the natural color variation of exposed rock
  • Shadows are sharp at altitude — use Indanthrone Blue at medium concentration with hard edges, laid in after the scree wash is completely dry
  • Trail flowers at the margins: dabs of the Paintbrush Blaze mix for paintbrush, single strokes of dilute Phthalo Blue GS for lupine, dots of Lemon Yellow for buttercups
  • Cerulean Blue Chromium sky in a single graded wash — chalky and granulating, exactly how alpine sky reads at noon
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Cadmium Red Medium Hue (Daniel Smith’s version) is cadmium-free — it uses Pyrrol pigments to match cadmium’s opacity and hue. Phthalo Blue GS at full concentration is nearly black; dilute heavily for lupine field distance work. Cobalt Violet’s price per tube reflects the genuine cobalt content — budget accordingly, but accept no substitutes for the granulation. Indanthrone Blue dries significantly lighter than it looks wet; build value in layers.
