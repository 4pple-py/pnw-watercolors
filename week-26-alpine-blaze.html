Week 26 — PNW Watercolor Series

Alpine Blaze

Above treeline the meadows detonate. Fireweed in magenta sheets, lupine in dense blue spikes, glacier lilies nodding yellow between boulders still streaked with snowmelt. Indian paintbrush jabs scarlet into the chaos. The air is thin and the colors are not — everything cranked to full saturation because the growing season is eight weeks and these flowers know it.

Primary Trio

Opera Pink

The searing magenta of fireweed in full bloom; fluorescent, fugitive, and unapologetically loud — exactly like the real thing

Cobalt Teal Blue

The specific blue-green of alpine lupine; granulating and opaque, it sits on the paper like a petal sits on a stem

Hansa Yellow Medium

Glacier lily yellow, buttercup yellow — clean, transparent, and warm enough to hold its own against all that pink and blue

Mixes & Ratios

Paintbrush Fire

2 Pyrrol Scarlet : 1 Opera Pink : ¼ Hansa Yellow Medium

The traffic-cone orange-red of Castilleja in full bloom — opaque, confrontational, impossible to ignore against green meadow.

Pyrrol Scarlet
Opera Pink
Hansa Yellow Med
Pyrrol Scarlet is the structural pigment here — opaque, intense, and hot enough to read as Indian paintbrush without any help. Opera Pink shifts the scarlet toward the magenta undertone you see in certain Castilleja varieties at altitude, where UV intensity deepens the reds. A whisper of Hansa Yellow Medium warms the tips where the bracts catch direct sun. Apply Pyrrol Scarlet to dry paper for maximum opacity, then touch Opera Pink into the shadow side while it’s still damp. The Hansa goes in last, just at the sunlit tips, with a nearly dry brush.

Lupine Spike

2 Phthalo Blue (Red Shade) : 1 Cobalt Teal Blue : ½ French Ultramarine

The dense blue of a lupine raceme — red-shifted at the base where flowers have opened, greener and cooler at the tip where buds are still tight.

Phthalo Blue RS
Cobalt Teal Blue
French Ultramarine
Phthalo Blue Red Shade is the staining backbone — the specific red-shifted blue that separates lupine from every other blue wildflower. Cobalt Teal Blue brings the greener, cooler note you see at the top of each spike where buds haven’t opened yet. French Ultramarine granulates slightly into the shadows between individual flowers on the raceme, giving the spike its three-dimensional texture. Paint the full spike with Phthalo Blue RS first, then drop Cobalt Teal Blue into the tip while wet, and touch French Ultramarine into the lower shadow pockets. The Phthalo stains immediately; the Cobalt and Ultramarine will granulate on top.

Meadow Floor

2 Sap Green : 1 Hansa Yellow Medium : ¼ Transparent Red Oxide

The vivid, sun-soaked green of subalpine meadow grass in July — warm enough to feel like summer, green enough to anchor all those wildflower accents.

Sap Green
Hansa Yellow Med
Trans. Red Oxide
Sap Green is the workhorse meadow green — warm-leaning, transparent, and mixable without going muddy. Hansa Yellow Medium pushes it toward the sun-struck chartreuse of new alpine grass, which grows fast and bright in the short season. Transparent Red Oxide at the base of the grass clumps grounds the green into the earth, adding the reddish-brown of exposed soil between tussocks. Lay the Sap Green + Hansa mix broadly, then drop Transparent Red Oxide into the base while still wet — it will bloom upward into the green naturally, mimicking how alpine meadow grass transitions from soil to blade.

Why These Colors?

Alpine meadows are nature running its saturation slider to the right. Opera Pink is the only pigment that matches fireweed’s fluorescent intensity — yes, it’s fugitive, and so are the flowers. Eight weeks of bloom, then gone. Cobalt Teal Blue nails the specific blue-green of subalpine lupine, and its granulation gives each painted spike the textured look of individual florets. Hansa Yellow Medium is the clean, warm yellow of glacier lilies nodding between rocks — transparent enough to glow against green. Pyrrol Scarlet is pure Indian paintbrush: opaque, confrontational, the orange-red that makes hikers stop mid-stride. Phthalo Blue Red Shade handles the deeper, red-shifted lupine varieties that grow in the partial shade at meadow edges. French Ultramarine provides the mountain sky and the blue of distant ridges above the flower line. Sap Green is the meadow itself, lush and fast-growing. Quinacridone Rose covers penstemon and the cooler pink wildflowers between the dominant fireweed. Transparent Red Oxide grounds the palette in trail dirt and exposed rock — without earth tones, alpine paintings float. Titanium White for the snowfields still lingering behind the flowers, the white that makes all those colors pop by contrast.

Painting Prompts

The meadows are screaming color right now. These prompts are designed to harness the full chromatic chaos of this palette — let the pigments be as loud as the flowers.

Prompt 1

Fireweed Slope — Mount Rainier Meadow

Subject: A hillside of fireweed in full bloom, magenta spikes filling the middle ground. Snowfields on Rainier visible above. A dirt trail cuts diagonally through the flowers. Late July light, everything saturated.

Opera Pink is the star — this is its moment. Sap Green + Hansa Yellow Medium for the meadow, Titanium White mixed with French Ultramarine for the snowfield, Transparent Red Oxide for the trail.

  • Lay in the snowfield first with Titanium White tinted with a breath of French Ultramarine — let it dry completely before painting the meadow below
  • The fireweed goes in as vertical dabs of Opera Pink at varying concentrations — full strength for foreground flowers, diluted for the middle distance. Let the vertical rhythm of the brush suggest the spike shape
  • The trail is Transparent Red Oxide applied in a single confident stroke, then softened at the edges with a damp brush while still wet — alpine trail dirt is compacted and reddish
  • Sap Green meadow grass goes in last around and between the fireweed — negative painting that makes the pink shapes pop forward
Prompt 2

Lupine and Paintbrush — Mixed Meadow

Subject: A close-up section of meadow where blue lupine spikes and red-orange Indian paintbrush grow side by side. The color contrast is almost aggressive. Green stems and leaves fill the gaps. A single boulder anchors the composition.

The Lupine Spike mix (Phthalo Blue RS + Cobalt Teal Blue + French Ultramarine) against the Paintbrush Fire mix (Pyrrol Scarlet + Opera Pink + Hansa Yellow Medium). Maximum chroma contrast, held together by Sap Green.

  • Paint the lupine spikes first using the Lupine Spike mix — Phthalo Blue RS stains the paper permanently, giving you a committed blue that won’t lift when you paint around it
  • Let the lupine dry completely, then paint the paintbrush with Pyrrol Scarlet. The opacity of Pyrrol Scarlet means it will hold its own against the staining Phthalo without losing intensity
  • Sap Green stems and leaves go in between the flower masses, connecting them. Add Hansa Yellow Medium to the green on the sunlit side of stems
  • The boulder is Transparent Red Oxide with a touch of French Ultramarine for the shadow side — warm enough to belong in the scene, cool enough to anchor it
Prompt 3

Glacier Lily Cluster — Snowmelt Edge

Subject: Three or four glacier lilies nodding from a mossy patch right at the edge of retreating snow. The flowers are bright yellow against the white. Green moss and wet rock below. A study in fragility at the margin of seasons.

Hansa Yellow Medium for the lily petals, Sap Green for the moss, Titanium White for the snow edge. Quinacridone Rose for the subtle pink tinge at the petal bases where the stamens show through.

  • Paint the yellow petals with Hansa Yellow Medium at full strength on dry paper — the transparency means the paper light shines through, giving the lilies their characteristic inner glow
  • While the yellow is still damp, touch Quinacridone Rose into the base of each petal where it meets the stem — glacier lilies have a warm pink flush at their center that makes them read as alive
  • The snow edge is unpainted paper with Titanium White only at the thickest parts — the boundary where snow meets exposed ground should be ragged and irregular, painted with a dry-brush drag
  • Wet the moss area and drop in Sap Green mixed with a touch of Cobalt Teal Blue — the granulation of the Cobalt Teal will suggest the texture of saturated alpine moss
Prompt 4

Wildflower Panorama — North Cascades Ridge

Subject: A wide-format panorama of an alpine ridge with meadows cascading down both sides. Multiple flower species visible as masses of color. Distant peaks in blue haze. The scale of an entire mountainside in bloom.

Every color in the palette gets used here. French Ultramarine for distant peaks, Opera Pink for fireweed masses, Cobalt Teal Blue for lupine drifts, Hansa Yellow Medium for buttercup patches, Pyrrol Scarlet for paintbrush accents. Sap Green holds it all together.

  • Start with the sky and distant peaks — French Ultramarine graded from deep at the zenith to pale at the horizon, with the far ridges painted wet-into-wet so they blur into atmospheric haze
  • The meadow is a patchwork: lay in large areas of the Meadow Floor mix first, then drop flower colors into it while still wet. Opera Pink in broad swathes, Cobalt Teal Blue in vertical clusters, Hansa Yellow Medium in scattered patches
  • Let the colors bleed into each other at the edges — from a distance, alpine meadows are masses of color, not individual flowers. Only the immediate foreground gets distinct shapes
  • Pyrrol Scarlet paintbrush accents are small, sharp dabs on dry paper — the opacity reads as the flowers popping forward from the softer washes behind them
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Opera Pink (BV10:1) is a fluorescent pigment — brilliant but fugitive; protect finished paintings from direct light. Cobalt Teal Blue (PG50) granulates beautifully and is genuinely opaque. Pyrrol Scarlet (PR255) is one of the most opaque reds available in watercolor. Phthalo Blue Red Shade (PB15:6) is the red-shifted version of Phthalo Blue, staining and intense. Hansa Yellow Medium (PY97) is a clean, warm, transparent yellow. Titanium White (PW6) is opaque gouache-like body color — use sparingly for snow and highlights.
