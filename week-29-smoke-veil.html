Smoke Veil

Wildfire smoke rolls west and the sun turns copper coin. Fireweed blooms top out — the old signal, six weeks to frost. Huckleberries stain fingers at 3,000 feet while the valley below disappears in amber gauze. Summer is burning its own calendar.

Mixes & Ratios

Smoke Sun

2 Transparent Red Oxide : 2 Buff Titanium : 1 Raw Sienna

The copper disk you can stare at directly — wildfire smoke as solar filter, stripping the sun of its authority.

Trans. Red Oxide
Buff Titanium
Raw Sienna
The Buff Titanium does the heavy lifting here — its opacity creates the dense, milky quality of smoke-filtered light. Transparent Red Oxide provides the copper hue, and Raw Sienna warms the edges. Paint the disk on dry paper, then immediately surround it with dilute Buff Titanium while the edge is still damp. The bleed should be soft, not sharp. Smoke doesn't have edges.

Fireweed Clock

2 Quinacridone Rose : 1 Olive Green

Magenta spire against a dulled green slope — the plant that counts down to frost in blooms, opening from bottom to top.

Quin. Rose
Olive Green
Side by side, not blended — Quinacridone Rose for the flower spike, Olive Green for the slope behind it. Where they meet, they'll create a muted mauve-gray that reads as atmospheric distance. The contrast between QR's vivid transparency and Olive Green's drab opacity is the whole point. Let them argue on the paper.

Haze Conifer

2 Olive Green : 1 Indigo : touch Buff Titanium

Tree shape dissolving in particulate — you know it's a fir because you've seen a thousand, not because you can see this one clearly.

Olive Green
Indigo
Buff Titanium
Build the tree shape with Olive Green and Indigo first — dark enough to read as conifer, drab enough to read as smoke-filtered. While still damp, drag Buff Titanium across the edges with a dry-ish brush. The tree should lose its boundaries into the haze. Sharp edges are the enemy of smoke painting.

Why These Colors?

Smoke demands muted transparency in the warm range and opacity for atmospheric effects — this palette delivers both. Transparent Red Oxide and Raw Sienna are iron-based pigments that granulate gently, perfect for the gritty quality of particulate-laden light. Olive Green already reads as compromised; it doesn't need to be dulled further to look smoke-choked. Buff Titanium is the palette's secret weapon: its opacity creates the physical density of smoke in a way transparent pigments cannot. The bioluminescence colors — Phthalo Green BS and Cascade Green — are retained from Week 28 but diminished in role, the glow fading as water cools. Quinacridone Rose is the single saturated note, because fireweed really does scream that loudly even through haze.

Painting Prompts

Stuck staring at a blank page? These subjects are tailor-made for this week’s palette — each one leans into the colors you already have mixed and the PNW moment happening right outside.

Prompt 1

Copper Sun Through Smoke

Subject: The sun as a copper disk low in a smoke-filled sky — no glare, no rays, just a flat circle you can stare at directly. The landscape below dissolves into amber layers of diminishing visibility.

The Smoke Sun mix (Transparent Red Oxide + Buff Titanium + Raw Sienna) was designed for this exact disk. Buff Titanium’s opacity creates the physical density of smoke that no transparent pigment can match. The whole palette is built around obstructed light.

  • Paint the sun disk on dry paper with the Smoke Sun mix — a small, flat circle. No glow, no rays. Smoke kills all that
  • While the disk edge is still damp, surround it with dilute Buff Titanium and let the bleed go soft. Smoke doesn’t have edges
  • The landscape below is layers of increasing Buff Titanium mixed with Raw Sienna — each ridge paler and warmer than the one in front, dissolving into the haze
  • No detail in the background. Smoke painting is about what you can’t see. The less you paint, the more smoke you create
Prompt 2

Fireweed Spire Against Haze

Subject: A single fireweed plant in full bloom — magenta flowers open from the bottom, buds still closed at the top — against a smoke-dulled green hillside. The frost countdown clock in flower form.

Quinacridone Rose is the only saturated note in this entire palette, and fireweed earns it. The Fireweed Clock mix (Quinacridone Rose + Olive Green, side by side) creates that electric contrast between vivid transparency and drab opacity. The palette’s muted quality makes the fireweed scream louder.

  • Paint the hillside first with Olive Green — drab, flat, smoke-choked. Let it dry completely
  • The fireweed spike is Quinacridone Rose applied with a narrow brush, working bottom (open flowers, more paint) to top (buds, less paint). The opening progression from bottom to top is the whole story
  • Where the magenta meets the olive green, don’t blend — let the edge be sharp. The contrast is the point
  • Add Indigo stems and Olive Green leaves along the spike. The leaves should look as tired as the hillside; only the flowers are defiant
Prompt 3

Haze Conifer Ridge

Subject: A ridge of conifers dissolving into wildfire smoke — the nearest trees dark and identifiable, the middle trees ghostly shapes, the farthest trees gone entirely. An exercise in atmospheric perspective compressed by particulate.

The Haze Conifer mix (Olive Green + Indigo + touch of Buff Titanium) handles all three depth zones by varying the ratio. More Indigo for the near trees, more Buff Titanium for the far ones. The mix was designed to lose its edges.

  • Work back to front. Farthest ridge is almost pure Buff Titanium with a whisper of Olive Green — barely there
  • Middle ridge adds more Indigo and Olive Green to the Buff Titanium base. While damp, drag a dry-ish brush of Buff Titanium across the treetops to lose them into the haze
  • Nearest trees are Olive Green + Indigo at medium concentration, painted on dry paper with defined (but not sharp) edges
  • The sky above is Buff Titanium with Raw Sienna — no blue. Smoke kills blue. If your sky reads as blue, you haven’t committed to the smoke
Prompt 4

Ghostly Bioluminescence Through Smoke

Subject: A nighttime bay scene where fading bioluminescence glows dimly through smoke haze — the glow from weeks 28–29 still present but filtered, ghostly, uncertain. Smoke above, faint light below.

Phthalo Green BS and Cascade Green carry over from the previous weeks but in diminished roles. Buff Titanium smoke haze sits over everything. The tension between the glow trying to reach you and the smoke blocking it is the entire painting.

  • Dark base wash of Indigo across the full sheet — this is your smoke-choked night
  • The glow is Cascade Green (not Phthalo Green this time — softer, more diffused) applied wet-into-wet in the lower third. It should be dim and uncertain
  • Over the top, while still damp, drag Buff Titanium in horizontal strokes across the glow zone — the smoke layer physically suppressing the light
  • Phthalo Green BS only at the very brightest point, and barely. This week, the glow is losing to the smoke. Let it.
Notes: All pigments confirmed Daniel Smith. For bioluminescence passages that carry over from the previous weeks, keep transparency high and avoid granulation in the glow zones — but note that the glow is fading, so you can afford to let it be less precise, more ghostly. Buff Titanium and smoke effects: try spraying clean water over a Buff Titanium wash before it's fully dry for a convincing particulate bloom. Indigo tends toward purple in thin washes — useful here, since smoke-twilight genuinely skews violet.
