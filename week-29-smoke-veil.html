Week 29 — PNW Watercolor Series

Smoke Veil

The sky forgot what color it was. Wildfire smoke rolled in from the east and everything shifted to a sepia photograph nobody asked for — the sun a flat orange coin, the mountains erased, the air thick enough to taste. You can smell char from two hundred miles away. Shadows lose their edges. The world goes matte and strange, and even noon feels like an old memory of itself.

Primary Trio

Transparent Pyrrol Orange

The eerie orange of a smoke-filtered sun; fully transparent, staining, an unnatural warmth that tints everything it touches

Neutral Tint

Smoke itself — neither warm nor cool, a gray that muffles everything. The veil between you and the landscape

Yellow Ochre

Ochre haze hanging in still air; opaque, ancient, the color of smoke-stained daylight pooling at the horizon

Mixes & Ratios

Ember Sun

2 Transparent Pyrrol Orange : 1 Cadmium Orange Hue : ½ Yellow Ochre

The smoke-filtered sun at 2 p.m. — orange, flat, staring at you like a coal through gauze.

Trans. Pyrrol Orange
Cad. Orange Hue
Yellow Ochre
Transparent Pyrrol Orange is the structural color here — fully transparent, it glazes the sun disc with that eerie, unnatural warmth that smoke days produce. Cadmium Orange Hue adds opacity at the center of the disc, because the smoke-sun is not luminous like a normal sun; it’s a flat, matte coin you can stare at directly. Yellow Ochre at the edges diffuses the boundary between sun and sky, where the disc dissolves into haze. Apply the Cadmium Orange Hue as a concentrated dot first, then surround it with Transparent Pyrrol Orange on damp paper so the edges bleed outward. The opacity-to-transparency gradient mimics how the real smoke-sun has a hard center and soft edge.

Particulate Haze

2 Neutral Tint : 1 Mayan Orange : ¼ Buff Titanium

The smoke itself — gray shot through with warm particulate, grainy and unsettled, thick enough to see.

Neutral Tint
Mayan Orange
Buff Titanium
Neutral Tint provides the achromatic smoke base — it’s the muffled, colorless quality of air you can’t see through. Mayan Orange is the key ingredient: a Primatek pigment ground from actual mineral, it granulates on the paper the way real smoke particulate settles unevenly in still air. You cannot replicate this texture with synthetic oranges. Buff Titanium lifts the value and adds a milky opacity to suggest the sheer density of the haze. Apply to very wet paper and tilt the board — let the Mayan Orange particles drift and settle into the paper’s valleys. The granulation IS the smoke texture.

Smoke Shadow

2 Payne’s Gray : 1 Neutral Tint : ¼ Burnt Sienna

Shadows on smoke days have no edges — they pool and spread, warm at the core, gray at the margins.

Payne’s Gray
Neutral Tint
Burnt Sienna
Payne’s Gray has a blue-black undertone that reads as atmospheric depth without going to true black — smoke-day shadows are never crisp enough for Ivory Black. Neutral Tint desaturates the blue bias, pushing the shadow toward the same achromatic quality as the air itself. Burnt Sienna at the shadow’s warm core reflects the orange light bouncing off smoke particles into every crevice. Apply with very soft edges: wet the paper first, then drop the mix in and let it spread on its own. Smoke shadows don’t have boundaries. Lift edges with a damp brush if they dry too hard.

Why These Colors?

Smoke veil is an orange-gray filter over everything. Transparent Pyrrol Orange captures the eerie tinted light — transparent enough to glaze entire washes with that apocalyptic warmth that makes midday look like dusk on another planet. Neutral Tint IS the smoke: achromatic, muffling, the opposite of color. Yellow Ochre sits between them, the haze itself made visible, opaque enough to suggest density in the air. Mayan Orange earns its place through mineral behavior — a Primatek pigment whose granulation mimics the way real smoke particulate settles unevenly in still air, creating that gritty, visible texture that synthetic pigments cannot reproduce. Cadmium Orange Hue provides the opaque intensity needed for the sun disc itself, which on smoke days is flat and staring rather than luminous. Burnt Sienna and Raw Sienna layer warm atmospheric depth at different distances — Burnt Sienna for the nearer, darker smoke layers, Raw Sienna for the amber air between you and the mid-ground. Payne’s Gray handles the peculiar shadows that smoke creates — soft-edged and blue-tinged, never sharp. French Ultramarine appears sparingly for the patches of sky barely visible through rifts in the smoke, a reminder that blue still exists somewhere up there. Buff Titanium washes out everything in the distance — mountains, treelines, buildings all go flat and pale when the AQI climbs past 150.

Painting Prompts

The palette was built for haze, muted light, and the uncanny monochrome of smoke season. Every prompt here leans into the limited visibility and warm-gray atmosphere that wildfire smoke brings to the Pacific Northwest.

Prompt 1

Smoke Sun Over the Cascades

Subject: The sun as a flat orange disc low in a featureless sky. The Cascades are barely visible — pale silhouettes with no detail, layered from light to slightly darker as they recede into the haze. No shadows, no contrast, just warm gray gauze.

The Ember Sun mix (Transparent Pyrrol Orange + Cadmium Orange Hue + Yellow Ochre) is purpose-built for that unreal sun disc. Buff Titanium and Neutral Tint handle the mountains that are barely there.

  • Paint the sun first — a concentrated dot of Cadmium Orange Hue at the center, then ring it with Transparent Pyrrol Orange on damp paper so the edges bleed out into the surrounding haze
  • The sky is Yellow Ochre heavily diluted with Neutral Tint — apply as a flat wash around the sun, keeping the value almost the same everywhere because smoke kills contrast
  • Mountain silhouettes are Buff Titanium with a touch of Neutral Tint — the closest range gets slightly more Neutral Tint, the farthest range is nearly pure Buff Titanium against the hazy sky
  • Resist the urge to add detail to the mountains. Smoke erases ridgelines, snowfields, trees — everything. Simple flat shapes with soft edges are more truthful than rendered peaks
Prompt 2

Backyard Through the Haze

Subject: A familiar scene — a fence, a tree, a house — made alien by smoke. Objects twenty feet away look like they’re behind frosted glass. The light is orange and directionless. Fine ash dusts every surface.

The Particulate Haze mix (Neutral Tint + Mayan Orange + Buff Titanium) as an overall atmosphere wash. Burnt Sienna for the fence and tree warmth. Payne’s Gray for what passes as shadow in this diffused light.

  • Lay the Particulate Haze mix as a full-sheet wash first and let the Mayan Orange granulate — this establishes the smoke atmosphere before any objects are painted
  • Paint objects into the still-damp haze wash so their edges dissolve — nothing in smoke has a hard edge. The tree trunk is Burnt Sienna bleeding into Neutral Tint at its margins
  • Shadows under the Smoke Shadow mix (Payne’s Gray + Neutral Tint + Burnt Sienna) should be barely darker than the midtones — smoke compresses the value range to a narrow band
  • Flick tiny dots of Buff Titanium mixed with a touch of Raw Sienna onto dry areas with a toothbrush for ash fall — the spatter should be irregular and barely visible
Prompt 3

Smoke Layers Over a Valley

Subject: Looking down into a river valley from a ridgeline. Multiple smoke layers visible — thinner above, denser in the valley bottom. Trees in the foreground still have some color; trees in the valley are ghost silhouettes. The smoke itself has visible strata.

Raw Sienna and Burnt Sienna for the foreground trees that still read as warm. Neutral Tint layered in graded washes for smoke strata. Mayan Orange for the particulate texture in the densest layers. French Ultramarine at the very top where blue sky almost shows.

  • Work in horizontal bands from top to bottom: a thin strip of French Ultramarine at the zenith, then Yellow Ochre and Neutral Tint for the upper smoke, increasing Neutral Tint concentration as you descend into the valley
  • Foreground trees are Raw Sienna and Burnt Sienna with actual form and branches — these are the only objects in the painting with any detail or color saturation
  • Mid-valley trees are Neutral Tint silhouettes, no detail, just flat triangular shapes losing definition as they go deeper into the smoke
  • Drop Mayan Orange into the densest smoke layer while wet and let it granulate — the mineral particles settling will suggest the visible particulate banding that real valley smoke produces
Prompt 4

Red Sun Reflection on Still Water

Subject: A PNW lake at dusk during smoke season. The sun’s orange reflection stretches across perfectly still water. Shoreline trees are dark shapes with no detail. The water is a mirror of the smoky sky — orange gradients and gray wash meeting at the treeline.

The Ember Sun mix reflected vertically down the paper. Payne’s Gray for the shoreline tree silhouettes. The water mirrors the sky, so every sky color appears again below the treeline — slightly darker and with Mayan Orange granulation suggesting surface texture.

  • Paint this as a symmetrical composition: sky on top, water below, a thin dark treeline strip dividing them. The sky and water use identical colors — the water is just slightly deeper in value
  • The sun reflection in water is Transparent Pyrrol Orange pulled straight down in a vertical stroke on damp paper — keep the brush moving in one direction only for that clean elongated reflection
  • Shoreline trees are a single stripe of Payne’s Gray applied with the side of a flat brush — irregular top edge for treetops, ruler-straight bottom edge at the waterline
  • Add the faintest horizontal streaks of Buff Titanium across the water reflection while still damp to suggest the barely-there ripples that break the mirror surface
Notes: All pigments are Daniel Smith watercolors. Transparent Pyrrol Orange (PO71) is intensely transparent and staining — it tints washes with warmth that won’t lift. Neutral Tint is Daniel Smith’s pre-mixed neutral gray, useful here because smoke is genuinely achromatic. Mayan Orange is a Primatek pigment — ground mineral stone — whose granulation cannot be replicated by synthetic oranges; it creates the visible particulate texture of smoky air. Buff Titanium (PW6:1) is an opaque, chalky warm white ideal for muting and washing out forms in the distance. Cadmium Orange Hue provides maximum opacity for the sun disc without the toxicity concerns of true cadmium.
